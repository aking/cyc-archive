Dear Matthew,

First let me say that it is not enough to thank you for clarifying the
record -- on further thought it is clear that I should apologize for
suggesting that you (or anyone) proposed EXPRESS as an alternative to KIF,
when you did not do so. I am sorry for this error.

Having said that, I would like to make the suggestion that the SUO group
should compare the representational features of EXPRESS with KIF, to decide
whether EXPRESS may in fact be a better starting point for representation of
SUO. What are the advantages and disadvantages of each language?

Also, I would like to make the suggestion that the SUO group should consider
leveraging EPISTLE and other models already developed in EXPRESS as a
starting point for developing SUO.

It seems to me there could be some promise in both these efforts. For
example, you have written that the following concepts recently listed by Pat
Hayes could probably fit into EPISTLE:

> an 'information-bearing object' (see my long comment in a
> recent message);
> a piece of a certain kind of cloth which was used in evidence
> in a trial;
> the color of that piece of cloth;
> the act of breaking an egg by hitting it on the side of a bowl;
> a style of dress used by a certain social class in
> 17th-century France;
> the shape of Marilyn Monroe's left ankle;
> the cause of the Concorde disaster;
> a dish (in the sense that Eggs Benedict is a dish, ie a something
> that could be a menu item; as opposed to any particular example of
> that dish, or the symbols on the menu describing it.)

EXPRESS, EPISTLE and the other models developed in EXPRESS, have the
advantage of having been developed over several years by many people in an
open, standards-based effort. My understanding is that they are publicly
available standards, which could be leveraged by SUO. Their development has
also been focused on practical, industry-based applications which may be
consonant with SUO's objectives. For all of these reasons, plus the general
expressiveness of EXPRESS, they may be better starting points and long term
approaches than either of the current alternatives, which seem to be: a)
starting with a relatively new "merged ontology" developed in KIF; b) going
with CYC, if a suitable copyright release letter can be obtained.

Sincerely,

Phil Jackson

> > Once again I will jump into the fray with a few comments and
> > suggestions for

> >
> > It seems to me that SUO has been making progress, yet three
> central issues
> > are unresolved:
> >
> > 1) What are the objectives for use of the SUO?
> >
> > 2) What are the requirements for the SUO?
> >
> > 3) What are the requirements for the language(s) in which the
> SUO will be
> > represented?
> >
> > Before we can decide what language to use we need to agree on the
> > requirements for the language. The requirements for the
> language depend on
> > the requirements for the SUO, which depend on the objectives
> for the SUO.
> >
> > Much of the discussion in SUO seems to involve these issues while
> > trying to
> > make progress on theoretical questions related to the SUO itself. There
> > needs to be a consensus on these issues before people can agree
> about the
> > value of any proposed candidate for the SUO, or any proposed
> > elements of an
> > SUO, though efforts such as Ian Niles' "merged ontology" are valuable at
> > least as strawmen to help identify issues.
> >
> > The objectives of the SUO are addressed briefly at the SUO website, in
> > sections on Scope and Purpose of the project, though questions
> still arise
> > about the objectives.
> >
> > Likewise the requirements for the SUO are addressed briefly in
> > the sections
> > on Scope and Purpose of the project, yet there are questions that are
> > unresolved about the requirements. E.g. the Scope discussion says
> > "an upper
> > ontology is limited to concepts that are meta, generic, abstract and
> > philosophical" yet we have discussions about whether the ontology should
> > include "semiotic concepts" or should only include things that "really
> > exist" in the world.
> >
> > The SUO website lists KIF or the simplified version as the
> > proposed language
> > for the standard, without giving a discussion of the
> requirements for the
> > language. We have proposals for alternative languages (e.g.
> EXPRESS), and
> > ongoing discussions about some of the requirements for the
> language. E.g.
> > does the language need to include reflection, does it need to represent
> > semiotic concepts, does it need to be a programming language?
> > Which elements
> > of an SUO should be built into the language, and which should
> be expressed
> > using the language? Does it need to be a higher-order language or
> > should SUO
> > be expressed at the level of first order predicate calculus, etc.?
> >
> > Many such questions have already been addressed at one time or
> another in
> > email threads among the participants. Yet it appears that there
> > is no way to
> > determine what has been resolved or agreed about these
> questions (and what
> > is still unresolved) without reviewing all the email
> correspondence, which
> > is not practical.
> >
> > To address these issues, I recommend that the SUO participants take a
> > standard software engineering approach, and collaborate on
> development of
> > concise yet complete requirements specifications for the SUO and for its
> > language.
> >
> > These requirements specifications should be located on the SUO
> > web site and
> > should be working documents that are iteratively developed,
> starting with
> > the current statements of scope and purpose. I'd recommend that these
> > specifications be presented in a "requirements outline" format,
> where each
> > requirement or issue is very concisely stated in a single sentence, and
> > sub-requirements and issues are added as the group develops them.
> > Hyperlinks could be used to give more complete discussions of the
> > requirements, issues and resolutions, with pointers to the email
> > correspondence about them. We may need to have votes about specific
> > requirements, to resolve their status.
> >
> > The precise format of the requirements outline is not important, but it
> > should be something that is very concise yet complete. E.g.,
> > something like
> > the indented table of contents outline for a standard FAQ would
> be useful.
> >
> > Also, email correspondence could try to follow a standard of having the
> > specific requirement or issue identified in the email subject line, in a
> > standard way, so that people can easily see which requirements
> and issues
> > are being discussed, find other relevant email, etc.
> >
> > Finally, it is clear that there are distinct differences of
> opinion among
> > the participants about the answers to questions related to
> > requirements for
> > the SUO and its language. Indeed, questions about requirements are
> > "inevitably intertwined"* with theoretical questions about the
> design and
> > implementation of the ontology, and with theoretical questions about
> > ontology and knowledge representation in general. On some of
> > these questions
> > we may not reach consensus, and the SUO effort may need to "fork" and
> > explore alternative answers. This would allow progress to be made on
> > multiple paths when it is not clear to everyone which is the
> best path to
> > pursue.
