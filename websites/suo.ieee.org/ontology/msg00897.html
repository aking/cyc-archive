Occurents and continuants:

I'm certainly new at this, but I was becoming comfortable with the idea
that occurents depend necessarily on contintinuents  (my life depends on
me),
but that the continuents are not parts.  (I am not part of my life).
Mary's smile
depends on Mary, but isn't part of Mary.   The stabbing of Ceasar is a
stabbing
(occurent) that depends on Ceasar, Brutus, and a certain knife, (all
continuants),
but these aren't  _parts_ of the specific stabbing.     This seems quite at
odds
with much of the continuant-occurent discussion I see.

I've been away most of January and February till now, so I've certainly not
read
everything in this thread, but the idea of an occurant being a _part_ of a
continuent
bothers me.  I'm not sure if this makes me a non-extensionist...

John Velman

I appreciate your objection to the Continuant/Occurrent terminology here,
but I think it would be wise if we didn't get hung up on the names of
concepts, at least not at this early juncture.  Names are, after all, only
names, and arguments about terminology could easily overshadow the
substantive issues of defining concepts and crafting axioms.  They could
also make it harder for us to communicate, if people find it difficult to
keep track of the currently preferred term for a particular concept.  I
suggest that we let the names stand for the time being, and that if a term
is found to be really objectionable by a majority of SUO participants, we
make the change at the end of the ontology design process.  Does this sound
reasonable?

This is a good point.  If we included 'Human' under 'ContinuantType' and
'Human-Life' under 'OccurrentType' in the merged ontology, then we would
violate the disjointness condition on ContinuantType/OccurrentType, because
'Human' and 'Human-Life' are co-extensional and we are assuming an
extensional interpretation of SUO-KIF.  I guess my response here would be
to
leave 'Human' under 'ContinuantType', to place 'Lifetime' (in the sense of
