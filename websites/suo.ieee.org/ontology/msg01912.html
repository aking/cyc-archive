> But there is another, technical, reason for keeping the 
> relation/extension distinction in the KIF semantics, which is that it

MW: Sorry, I don't understand what your getting at here. I don't see what

> This is a compelling reason

> ......
MW: I'm struggling with this KIF. It seems to say:

If nothing, for all tuples @x
  (If rel1 @x implies rel2 @x and vice versa)
  then rel1 = rel2

> Chris' view of 'existence', class-membership and time is 
> idiosyncratic and I think woefully confused, and it is not forced on 
> one by non-extensionality or by assuming that the future is 
> indeterminate. And as I said, this isn't the only, or in my view the 
> main, reason why the KIF model theory is the way it is:certainly one 
> should not think of KIF as endorsing these positions.  But his final 
> point is the main one: the logic makes no committment to 
> extensionality one way or the other. It does not assume it, but it 
> also doesn't deny it. The fewer philosophical assumptions imposed by 
> the underlying language, the better.

MW: Yes I agree. But I thought this was already a basic bit of ontology
on top of the language. A rather good bit actually.

> > >
> > > MW: OK - of course one of the benefits of a 4D approach

>

> >allow for the possibility that there could have been other things --

> >other, since, by definition, the class HUMAN comprises just

> >humans.

> It doesn't restrict one to considering only the actual humans. In 
> fact, by providing a uniform notion of 'enclosing' spatiotemporal 
> plenum, it actually elegantly supports an ontology of alternative 
> world-lines. The issues of 4d/3d, and of representing nondeterminate 
> futures or alternative possible worlds, are almost completely 
> orthogonal.

MW: Yes, this is actually the approach I think I have ended up with.
>
