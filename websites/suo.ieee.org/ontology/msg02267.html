Robert's (In No Particular Rules Of Order) --

Let me extend my earlier remarks on the chief dialects
of Graph Theory to tell you of an easy way that I have
found to discern the accents of the different GTphones:

1.  The Set-Theoretic Dialects
2.  The Graph-Theoretic Dialects
3.  The Category-Theoretic Dialects

But first, in the spirit of saving the best for last,
let me put aside my consideration of the CT dialects
for now, simply alluding to some sources of interest
that I have mentioned before as possible inspiration
or as potential materials for our future discussions:

| J. Lambek & P.J. Scott,
| 'Introduction to Higher Order Categorical Logic',
| Cambridge University Press, Cambridge, UK, 1986.

| Michael Barr & Charles Wells,
|'Category Theory for Computing Science',
| Prentice Hall, London, UK, 1990.

Okay, here's how to tell if your interlocutor on a topic of
graph theory is a native speaker of the GT or the ST family
of dialects.  Simply ask your informant to provide you with
an "example of a graph", in a setting, of course, where the
free choice of an illustrative medium is not so constrained
as to bias the freedom of exemplary expression.

If your informant responds with an unadorned figure like this:

|              o
|             /|\
|            / | \
|           /  |  \
|          o   |   o
|           \  |  /
|            \ | /
|             \|/
|              o
|
| Figure 1.  The Random Graph

then you can be relatively sure that you are talking to a native
or a thoroughly naturalized speaker of the GT linguisitic family.

If your informant responds with an elaborate laundry list like this:

  Table 1.  The Random Graph
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Points:  v in V(G)          |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
|          v1                 |
|          v2                 |
|          v3                 |
|          v4                 |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Lines:   x  = {u , v } in G |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
|          x1 = {v1, v2}      |
|          x2 = {v1, v3}      |
|          x3 = {v1, v4}      |
|          x4 = {v2, v4}      |
|          x5 = {v3, v4}      |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤

then you can be reasonably certain that your informant is
a native speaker of ST, coming to GT as a second language.

Many diverting tales there are to be told
about the contretemps that are occasioned
between these two regionalities of not so
well-tempered-or-timbred theorem-o-phones,
but Shirazad has taught me to split while
the splitting is good.

More, Later,

Jon Awbrey
