JA: my guess is that "x-like" means "having all of the discourse-relative
    or currently noted properties of x".  but we have in this discourse
    currently noted only the property Red of D, or properties that are
    immediate implications of that.  in effect, D serves merely as
    yet another exemplar of the quality Red, as that is all the
    information that we have of it.

JA: more generally, peirce's use of "particulars" and "properties" is more
    like particles and waves than the usual hard lines that the descent of
    mind that de-evolved from atomic logicism imagines it can draw between
    absolute individuals and absolute predicates.

JA: it's a thing i've been looking for an excuse to get back to,
    and the occasion of your remark provides as good a catalyst
    as any i've seen lately for getting around, back, down tuit.
    and let me invent a not-so-subtle subtitle for it that i'll
    design to startle if not to shock, and that is a little bit
    that i'd like to call "peirce-heisenberg uncertainty" (phu),
    and by this phu to say that c.s. peirce in 1865 had already
    discovered by sheer logical detection and lectured publicly
    on all of the principal principles of the later-to-be-great
    uncertainty principle.  in effect, it says that information
    is the only "symmetry invariant meaning" (sim) that we have,
    that it can be cast to moderate and relative extents, never
    to absolute degrees, as information about individuals or as
    information about qualities, but only in a trade-off within
    the quantity of information that avails itself at any given
    moment, though the quantity and quality of information will
    of course be free to vary from moment to moment in due time.

JA: i will copy this to the global brain list, because it bears
    on some discussion there about the "limits of localization".
    one more alias:  the "folly of particles and waves" (fopaw).

these popular accounts of "emerging science" are very valuable in attracting
attention to inherently fascinating subjects, and heaven knows that i have
soaked up enough of them in my time.  but they necessarily soft-peddle
an array of difficult issues of the more boring methodological sort.
just by way of my first impressions of the comments that you have
collected here, one such issue that i find missing in them is
any recognition of the problem of "system identification".
of course, i am just going by what you have quoted so far,
as i can remember reading only the waldrop book, or one
such book by him, and that was quite a few years ago.

the point that i'd mention on that account is that references to
systems and networks, along with their properties and parameters,
such as any number of ostensibly concrete measures of complexity,
like node degrees, number of cycles, graph-theoretic connectivity,
and so on, are references to a form of description that is being
used to describe a putative reality -- they are not directly the
form of reality itself.

the other point that comes to mind is a historical one.
c.s. peirce came to grips with a concept of information
very early in his lifelong quest to understand how the
"logic of science" really worked, and he gave lectures
on this "theory of information" throughout the years of
1865-1866 at harvard university and the lowell institute.
if you say that it is only very recently that the rest of
the scientific establishment has come to grips with what
c.s. peirce so long ago came to grips with, the i guess
that i'd be inclined to agree, but then, it's hardly
a unique instance of that.

i append a few excerpts from these lectures
to assist the e-stablishment in catching up
with the recent revolutions in information.

jon awbrey
