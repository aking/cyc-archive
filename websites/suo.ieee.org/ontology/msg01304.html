> Hi Fred,
> 
> 	I don't see that there's any inconsistency between the 
> avowed aim of
> not relying on assumptions about the real nature of the world and
> incorporating the organism taxonomy into the merged ontology. 
>  The typology
> of plants, animals, microorganisms, etc. specifies the 
> meaning of these
> concepts (though axioms and subsumption relations).  
> Accordingly, these
> axioms and subsumption relations would be true (albeit not 
> very useful) even
> if there were no actual organisms in the real world.  When I 
> spoke of trying
> not to adopt a "world viewpoint", I meant that I endeavored 
> not to build
> philosophical assumptions into the ontology.  For example, in 
> the exchange
> with Matthew, I recommended that we not assume in our ontology that
> everything is, in effect, an occurrent.   
> 
> 	As I see it, an ontology should be a framework for asserting
> propositions about the world.  The more that philosophical 
> claims about the
> world are built into the ontology, the less that can be said 
> in terms of the
> ontology.  In the limiting case, the goal of a formal lingua franca is
> completely undermined.
> 
> -Ian
