   Concerning contextualization and the heap paradox:   

[Pat Cassidy]
> >   I can imagine using such precise limits in definitions [like
> >heap and grain] as being problematic only in some rare cases of 
> >language understanding, but since multiple reiterative processes 
> >are seldom referred to in normal language, I suspect that using 
> >a limit will work in just about every practical situation.
> 
[Pat Hayes]
> Oh, come ON!  Engineers and practical folk have to reason about
> multiple repetition all the time. Ask a blacksmith. Think of issues
> of rate of wear of machine parts. Even biologists have to think about
> cumulative error rates in DNA replication.
>
   Yes, I left out the implied qualifier in the phrase, it should 
have been: "multiple reiterative processes **involving vague 
concepts like a heap** . . .".  The types of physical reiterative
processes you mention present no problem because the systems 
are among those "just about every practical situation" where
making vague concepts precise is beneficial. 

--
[Pat Cassidy]
>>  "large" is very dependent on context,
> >and might need many different definitons depending on context.
> 
[Pat Hayes]
> But then that begs the question. If truths can be dependent on
> 'context' then we can just say that 'large heap' depends on context.
> How do you cash out this contextuality in axioms?
>
   We can't avoid the contextuality of natural language; ignoring 
*that* would be begging the central question of natural
language understanding.  For axioms, see below.

[Pat Hayes]
> It seems quite obvious to me both that the
> antecedents of the paradox are correct - heaps are finite, may be
> large, and removing a single grain from a large heap never, in
> itself, makes a large heap small  - and that the conclusion is
> absurd, and yet that the reasoning has no obvious flaws in it.

  I think it's clear that both can't be correct if both heap and
grain have unchanging definitions.  If at each iteration, the
"heap" is still a heap, and the "grain" is still a grain, then for 
the statement to be true, something must change -- for example, 
at some point the "grain" size would necessarily be smaller:

(forall ?x ?y
  (implies 
    (and (heap ?x) (grain ?y))
      (greater-than
          (size-ratio ?x ?y) 100)
    )
  )
)

   In this case, the "context" which is taken into account is
the number of prior iterations which allow one to still
consider the "heap" to be a heap, relative to whatever
one is willing to call a "grain".  The statement can be true
iteratively only if a "grain" is allowed to vary with the 
size of the heap.
   This is somewhat similar to Zeno's paradox of Achilles and the
Hare. It is a paradox only if we assume that there is some
minimum size to the time interval at every iteration.  The
heap paradox is a paradox only if we assume that there is some
minimum size limit on the grain removed.
