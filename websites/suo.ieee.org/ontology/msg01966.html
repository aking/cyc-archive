John,

A)
Let L1 = English (or a formalized subset thereof in order to apply
(2))
Let L2 = English (or same formalized subset...)
Let the transformation be the identity transformation except that
'left' in L1 maps to 'right' in L2, and 'right' in L1 maps to 'left'
in L2.

I believe this transformation satisfies your definition of a
meaning-preserving transformation, but woudn't be very
meaning-preserving in the usual sense of the words.

B)
Thinking about what happens not so rarely when translating into
English a sentence that was translated into French from English (or
any other pair of natural languages), it seems to me that one can
preserve meaning and yet have t3 not be a single term, but an
expression involving multiple terms that might be provably equivalent
to t1, but neither vocabulary preserving, even taking into account
synonyms, nor structure preserving.

C)
I don't think these requirements do anything more than say that L2 and
the transformation are good "carriers" or "encodings" for sentences of
L1.  The sentences in L2 to which the sentences in L1 map can be utter
gibberish under the usual interpretation of sentences in L2.  To use
Jon's terminology, shoudn't <o,s,i> appear somewhere?

Where am I going wrong in understanding you?

Mark

[John F. Sowa wrote:]
>
[...]
>
>  1. Invertible:  If sentence s1 expressed in language L1 is
>     translated to a sentence s2 in language L2, it should be
>     possible to translate s2 to a sentence s3 in L1.
>
>  2. Proof preserving:  The sentence s3 in language L1 should
>     either be identical to the original sentence s1 or it
>     should be provably equivalent (in the sense of "if and
>     only if") according to whatever rules of inference are
>     used in language L1.
>
>  3. Vocabulary preserving:  The nonlogical vocabulary (i.e.,
>     the "content" terms) of the sentences s3 and s1 should be
>     the same or synonymous (i.e., if t1 in s1 is replaced by
>     t3 in s3, there should be some definition in L1 that says
>     that t1 is a synonym for t3).
>
>  4. Structure preserving:  When s1 and s3 are converted to
>     Peirce Normal Form (with negation, conjunction, and the
>     existential quantifier as the only logical operators),
>     the number of negations and quantifiers is the same, and
>     they are nested in isomorphic syntactic patterns.
>
