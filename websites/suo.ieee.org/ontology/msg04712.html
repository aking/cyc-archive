1.3.1.  Logic, Ethics, Aesthetics

The philosophy I find myself converging to most often lately is
the pragmatic thinking of Charles Sanders Peirce and John Dewey.
According to this account, logic, ethics, and aesthetics form a
concentric series of normative sciences, each the subdiscipline
of the next.

Logic tells how one ought to conduct one's reasoning
in order to achieve the goals of reasoning in general.
In this perspective, logic is a special case of ethics.

Ethics tells how one ought to conduct one's activities in general
in order to achieve the good that is appropriate to each enterprise.
What makes the difference, the critical reflective difference, between
a normative science and a prescriptive dogma is whether this telling is
based on actual inquiry into the relationship of conduct to result, or not.

From this standpoint, logic and ethics do not set goals, they merely serve them.
Of course, logic may examine the consistency of an arbitrary selection of goals
in the light of what science tells us about the likely repercussions in nature
of trying to actualize them all.  And logic and ethics may serve the critique
of certain goals by pointing out the deductive implications and the probable
effects of striving toward them, but it has to be some other science that
finds out and tells whether these effects are preferred and thus to be
encouraged, or else detested and thus to be discouraged in relation
to a particular form of being, a chosen way of life.

The science that examines individual goods, species goods, and generic goods
from an outside perspective must be an aesthetic science.  The capacity for
inquiry into a subject is grounded in the capacity for uncertainty about
that subject.  Aesthetics is capable of inquiry into the nature of the
good precisely because it is able to exist in states of question
about what is good.

Whether conceived as empirical science or as experimental art, it is the job
of aesthetics to determine what might be good for us, for our form of life.
Through the exploration of artistic media we find out what satisfies our
own form of being.  Through the expeditions of science we discover and
further the goals of own species' evolution.

Outriggers to these excursions are given by the comparative study
of biological species and the computational study of abstractly
specified systems.  These provide additional ways to find out
what is the sensible goal for an individual system and what
is the perceived good for a particular species of creature.
It is especially interesting to learn the relations that
can be represented internally to a system's development
between the good of the system and the system's own
perception, knowledge, intuition, feeling, or
whatever sense it may have of its goal.

This amounts to asking the questions:

1.  What good can a system be able to sense for itself?

2.  How can a system discover its own best interests?

3.  How can a system achieve, from the evidence of its experience,
    a cognizance, evidenced in behavior, of its own best interest?

Jon Awbrey
