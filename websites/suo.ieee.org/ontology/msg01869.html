> Some more questions and comments.

Appreciated, as always.  Thanks for the careful look.

> 1. Under class, relation is defined as a class.

Yes, it is the class of relations.

> Later under relation you define class as a unary relation.

Right, unlike, say, the OWNERSHIP relation, CLASS is not a relation that
holds among several things, but is true only of single things (namely,
classes).

> This seems to be inconsistent.

It is quite consistent.  You seem to be confusing the claim that
RELATION itself is a class with the (false) claim that relations (i.e.,
the instances of the class RELATION) are classes.

> 2. < applies only to integers. Why doesn't it apply to reals or (at least)
> rationals?

Reals and rationals are not axiomatized in the ontology, so, as in the
ontology as it stands, < only holds between integers.  The axiom for <
would have to be modified if you introduced further number systems and
you wanted < to be polymorphic.

> 3. < is not defined (not that I have any idea how to do it). By which
> I mean what being less than is.

What being less than *is* is exactly what the axioms tell us it is.
For primitive terms like "<", this is what you get instead of a
definition.  (In a true definition, the defined term is eliminable;
anything expressed in terms of the defined term can be expressed without
it, albeit more verbosely, in terms of the primitives of the language.)

> 4. I can't get the axiom for addition (or subtraction). Here is my attempt
> at an English translation - where have I gone wrong?
>
> For all integers i and j
>   i+0=0 and  <-- I think this should be i+0=i

Whoops, yes, serious typo; thanks for catching that.

>   i+(j+1)= i+j+1 and
>   i+(j-1)= i+j+1

Don't confuse the sum of some number i and the number 1 with the
application of the (primitive) successor operator "+1".  The idea here
is to axiomatize addition and subtraction in terms of addition in terms
of the two successor "+1" and predecessor "-1".  (Maybe it would be
better to use, e.g., the terms "scssr" and "prdssr" or the like here for
these operators to avoid confusion.)  There are only three cases to
consider:

1. The case where we add 0 to a number i;
2. The case where we add the successor (+1 j) of a number j to i; and
3. The case where we add the predecesor (-1 j) of a number j to i.

The base clause (= (+ i 0) i) gives us the first case.  The clause

                (= (+ ?i (+1 ?j)) (+1 (+ ?i ?j)))

gives us case 2; it says that the sum of i and the successor of a number
j is the successor of the sum and i and j.  So if we know the sum of i
and j, we can calculate the sum of i and the successor of j.  Similarly,
case 3 is given by the last clause:

                 (= (+ ?i (-1 ?j)) (-1 (+ ?i ?j))))))

which says that the sum of i and the predecessor of a number j is the
predecessor of the sum of i and j.  Eventually, due to the recursive
nature of the axioms, you will be able to unpack any sum in terms of
a finite number of successive operations of successor and predecessor
(and hence,, in virtue of the Cancellation Axiom, successive operations
of *either* successor or predecessor only).  Try, for example, to show
that (= (+ 2 3) 5) (using the definitions of "2", "3", and "5" given).

> 6. Sequences
>
> a) Here you find a need to introduce extensionality for some classes like
> sequence.

Sequences aren't classes, but yes, they are axiomatized to be extensional.
(They didn't *have* to be, but it is definitely simpler to make this
assumption.)

> Robert has suggested they should also be necessary for Top, or else
> there is no reason for Top to be unique.

I think we've cleared that one up.

> It is starting to look to me as if it would be better to introduce a
> "property" like class that can be non-extensional, and a "set" like class
> that is extensional before we get to this point.

Quite possibly.

> b) on the one hand you say that a sequence holds only for positive
> integers, and on the other hand you say that any object can be an
> element of a sequence. Which is it?

You are not understanding.  A sequence is a (partial) *function* (i.e.,
a binary relation that is functional) that *applies* to positive
integers and which can have *any object* as value.  The elements of a
sequence are its values on the integers on which it is defined.  We say
that a sequence s is defined on n iff, for some object x, (s n x), i.e.,
n bears s to x.

> c)
> (forall (?s)
>         (=> (sequence ?s)
>             (and (relation ?s)
>                  (binary ?s)
>                  (functional ?s)
>                  (exists (?n (posint n))  <-- missing ?

Yes, thanks.

>                          (and (not (exists (?x) (?s ?n ?x)))
>                               (forall (?m (posint ?m) (< ?m ?n))
>                                       (exists (?x)
>                                               (?s ?m ?x))))))))
>
> My translation is:
>
> For all s:
>   If s is a sequence then:
>     s is a relation and
>     s is binary and
>     s is functional and
>     for some positive integer n
>       there is no x such that s(n,x) <-- doesn't seem to have
>                                          a defined meaning

It most certainly does.  As discussed at some length, you can use a term
both as a predicate and as a function symbol; the context serves to
disambiguate what is meant.  Sequences are introduced as (functional)
binary relations.  Hence, s(n,x) means that the sequence s holds between
the number n and the object x.  Alternatively, one can write s(n)=x,
though, for reasons explained in the introductory material, one should
do so only when one knows that s is defined on n.

>       and
>       for all positive integers m where m<n
>         there is some x such that s(m,x) <-- meaning?

As above.  Here's a terse English translation of the axiom:  if s is a
sequence, then it is a binary, functional relation that is defined on
some initial segment of the positive integers.  Here's a more verbose
version:  if s is a sequence, then it is a binary, functional relation
such that, for some positive integer n, s is defined on exactly the
positive integers less than n (i.e., for each such integer m, there is
an x such that s(m,x)).

> c) you have used the term length to mean just the length of a
> sequence. This means it cannot be used for e.g. length as a basic
> dimension.

So let's use "seqlength" instead.  Let us not quibble.

> Aren't what you are defining here as sequences really tuples?

Yes, it is a precise rendering of the notion of an n-tuple.  It is not
the only possible rendering, it is simply a good -- and common -- one.
