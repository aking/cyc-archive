Pat,

Just one more comment about possible worlds semantics:

PH>It works for every modal formalism yet invented, as far as I know.
>In fact it is the ONLY thing that works.

Actually, that depends on what you mean by "ONLY".  There are
many different variations that are isomorphic to Kripke's models.  
As a mathematician, I am comfortable with the idea of considering
isomorphic constructions to be "the same" or at least "equivalent".
But different isomorphic variants may have various kinds of
advantages for implementation efficiency, pedagogical simplicity,
esthetics, etc.

For any Kripke model, it is possible to construct an isomorphic
model using

 1. Hintikka model sets in which every possible world is replaced
    by a collection of propositions (or sentences) that describe
    the world.

 2. Dunn's laws & facts, in which the facts are identical to the
    propositions in a Hintikka model set, but the laws are selected
    as a subset of the facts, which are used to define the accessiblity
    relation.

 3. A Tarski-style model, in which all the Kripke worlds are embedded
    in a single model with appropriate naming conventions to distinguish
    the original "world" from which each individual or relational tuple
    was derived.

For a brief summary of these variations with citations, see my paper
on contexts:

   http://www.bestweb.net/~sowa/ontology/contexts.htm

This paper is still unfinished, but I describe the Hintikka and Dunn
variations in Section 3 and the mapping to a nested graph model,
which can the be flattened to a Tarski model in Section 6.

The rest of the paper talks about the intuitive interpretations
of different variations.  If you don't want to read about P's
thoughts on the subject, you can skip Section 5.
