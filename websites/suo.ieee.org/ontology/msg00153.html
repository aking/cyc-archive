Graham

We may have misunderstood each other. Let me try to clarify my point of view.

At 20:52 +1100 00/12/12, Horn, Graham wrote:
>Norbert,
>	.	I can feel the fuzzy approach you are taking here.

I did not at all intend to advocate the fuzzy approach.

>	.	Nevertheless, if code (and the underlying language) is
>correctly written, the computer will consistently interpret it only one way,
>and only produce one result (with the exception of random number generators,
>or programs that involve aspects dependent on previous calculations)

Agreed, but you wrote "if I communicate to you in a computer language 
in which you are literate, surely you will understand precisely what 
I say?" and I took it that by "you" you meant a human being which is 
a completely different situation.

>	.	I am a strong supporter of the (now old fashioned) approach
>of documenting program code, including explanations of the reasonings behind
>approaches taken.
>
>	.	Yes, that documentation will be ambiguous, but it won't be
>actual code, will it? Therefore, the statement I made won't apply to it.
>
>	.	I am intrigued by amount of weight and emphasis many
>contributions to this project place on what others have said. Such
>contributions seem almost to take the line that the statements must be right
>because someone else wrote them.

I wish I had formulated Hall's statement because he expresses exactly 
my convictions that form an essential part of the philosophy that led 
to Attempto  Controlled English.

Please, note that ACE has a dual face: on the one side it is English, 
on the other side a formal language. Thus ACE can play at the same 
time the role of code (formal notation) and commentary (explaining 
the formal notation in human terms).

>	.	While I acknowledge that we are in an area that involves
>logical implications, some of which I will be unaware, I prefer statements
>that stand on the basis of well argued logic with demonstrable soundness,
>and I acknowledge refutations demonstrating superior soundness. It may not
>be possible to be black and white, but it should be possible to  at least be
>extremely dark grey or extremely light grey, with only the slightest tints
>of any colours.

Please recall that we try to formalise domains of the real world that 
are not formal at all. It remains our task to relate the 
formalisation to the domain (in humanly understandable ways), which 
as we know cannot be done in a unique way.

Best regards.
