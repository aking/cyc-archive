Pat,

I basically agree with your statement of the heap problem.

> However, consider the idea
> that any change that is invisible cannot change the truthvalue of any
> 'sensory' predicate applied to the thing changed.

This is a more general formulation of the problem, which applies to
many kinds of things; e.g., the sound of one drop of water on a sandy
beach is imperceptible, but the sound of a wave crashing down is
noticeable -- and if it's big enough, devastating.

And by the way, this reformulation brings the heap paradox (and all
of its related variants) into the granularity box.  You can say that
for any particular problem, there is some minimal grain size that is
below some threshold of perceptibility, measurability, significance,
or whatever.  That brings it within the realm of techniques that
engineers and experimental scientists of every stripe have addressed
with methods for representing and estimating "experimental error".
A methodology for using the SUO will have to deal with such techniques.

The following checklist is a useful summary:

> 1. Refuse to admit that the concepts we want are really vague, and
> replace those that are by suitably sharpened ones.  This might be
> called the standard ontological strategy, and it is probably the most
> useful in practice.  It is the one I have adopted myself for many
> years. But it really is not compatible with the goal of having the
> ontology be relevant to NL, since virtually every concept in natural
> discourse is 'vague' in this sense.
> 
> 2. Note (along with recent contributors to this thread) that context
> is central to NL and since we are going to have to deal with it
> eventually, we might as well toss this problem into the same "in"
> tray as the general NL comprehension problem, the general AI problem,
> and the general problem of improving the Human Condition, and so on,
> and solve it when we get around to those. This has the merit of
> acknowledging the problem as real, without having to actually do
> anything about it.
> 
> 3. Note that context is central to NL and that since the SUO is
> supposed to be relvant to NL, that therefore all the hard problems
> are either done or bound to get done soon, so by pointing out that
> vague concepts are context dependent, we have pretty much solved the
> problem, at least on our credit cards. This might be said to have the
> advantage of optimism over insight.
> 
> 4. Actually come up with a workable theory of context-dependence
> which can handle the problem. If anyone wants to try this, or has any
> useful ideas on how to get started, I would love to hear from them.

I agree that #1 is a very useful technique for dealing with any
single problem.  But by dealing with every problem in that way,
you develop very nice solutions for small systems that can never
"scale up" to big systems.

But point #2 is much more than just an "NL comprehension problem"
because it arises whenever we try to generalize any kind of technical
problem.  For example, suppose you write a sort routine to sort
files on your current OS -- which might be an Apple, Unix, Windows,
Amiga, etc.  It might work fine on the original machine, but it will
probably "blow up" when you try running it (even with a recompilation)
on a different OS -- even a different version of the "same" OS.

Your points #3 and #4 are well taken, but I think that the difficulty
lies in the word "context", which has so many diverse meanings that
the problem can never even be stated, much less solved.

The first step toward a theory of "context dependence" is to drop
the word "context".  Or at least restrict its meaning to something
very narrow, such as "container for a single well-defined theory",
which reduces it to the solvable point #1.

Then you can start addressing the issue of how different "containers"
or "modules" or whatever you want to call them are related to one
another.  And you can start talking about different theories, how
they are related (as in a lattice of all possible theories) and how
they can be modified (by theory or belief revision methods).  You can
also talk about namespaces for the various containers, etc.

I won't say that the problem becomes "easy", but once you start
breaking it up, you can at least begin to address the separate
issues one at a time, using techniques that have already been
well studied in the AI and comp. sci. literature.

John
