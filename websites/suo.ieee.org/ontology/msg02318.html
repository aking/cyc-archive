Strand 1.  General Issues

¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

Matthew,

Let us try for a bit to focus on a source of misunderstanding
that arises from a certain type of "category mistake", namely,
that of confusing the two main levels of description that are
used in describing relations, in extension, as sets of tuples.

For the sake of continuity I will repeat the remarks that I made earlier,
incidental to another topic, and then I will follow up in greater detail.

JA:  The basic point here is that there are two levels of description:

     1.  The level having to do with relations,
         which are sets of tuples.

     2.  The level having to do with ordered tuples,
         which can be seen as lists or as sequences,
         which are actually functions from an ordered
         set, like counting numbers, to a component set.

JA:  To describe a relation is to describe a set.
     To describe a relation as a set it is necessary
     to refer to either one of two sorts of things:

     a.  Properties of relations, which are properties of sets.
         Here one is choosing to "change horses mid-stream"
         and to continue the res on the intensional ponens.

     b.  Categories, classes, or sets of relations, which are
         categories, classes, or sets of sets.  Here one is
         choosing to "stay the course" and to keep betting
         on the horse of the initial extensional color.

JA:  To describe a relation as a set of tuples it is, of course,
     necessary to describe the tuples to some degree and extent,
     perhaps even to remind oneself of their structure as tuples,
     according to some definitions of the cartesian product, say,
     the ones that express it in terms of projections or of roles.
     But I said "necessary" not "sufficient".  There is much more
     to the structure of a relation, qua set, than the structure
     of its tuples, taken severally, one at a time, in isolation.

As it happens, I have noticed that this very same type
of category mistake arises fairly often, especially in
the communication gaps that develop between any one of
a variety of formal sciences and the rest of the world.

Compare & Contrast:

¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Set                    | Element                        |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Formal Language:  X    | Word, Sentence, Text:  x in X  |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Formal Problem:   Y    | Problem Instance:      y in Y  |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Formal Relation:  Z    | Relation Instance:     z in Z  |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤

In the formal sciences of formal language theory,
computational theory, and the logic of relations,
the constructs that provide the most fundamental
bases of ideas in each field are defined as sets.

A "formal language" is a set of strings that are
variously called "words", "sentences", "texts",
and so on, depending on the application context.

A "problem" in computer science is really a set
of "problem instances", and one is regarded as
having solved a problem only when one has given
a generic algorithm for solving every instance,
not just for chipping away at a special case.

Thus, it often occurs that folks who have not been
exposed to the basic "set-ups" in each formal area,
and who probably have no way of guessing the clues
to these admittedly peculiar ways of talking, will
usually misunderstand much of what the enthusiasts
for each formal exercise are really going on about.

I am beginning to suspect that a similar phenomenon is what has
been generating a lot of the misunderstanding that we find here.

Just my latest guess,

Jon Awbrey
