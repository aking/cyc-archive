>Can you go into any more detail? I am very much interested in this kind of
>restriction of KIF.

Well, current KIF (ANSI-KIf or KIF 3.0)is an unsorted first-order 
language, so all quantifications are unsorted. It allows for what 
looks like sorting restrictions on quantifieres, but they are only 
'macros' for the usual mapping of sort-restricted quantification into 
an unsorted logic, where one treats sorts as predicates and 
explicitly states the sortal restrictions as antecedents.

Your style of axiom is the 'dual' of this, in a sense: it is intended 
to say that productRelation takes arguments of a certain sort and 
produces results of a certain sort. But what happens if one writes an 
assertion using 'productRelation' which violates these sorting 
constraints? In a genuine sorted logic, this would be syntactically 
illegal. In an unsorted logic (like KIF) it would simply be a 
contradiction, or have unintended consequences.

>
> >SUO-KIF will have an explicit
> > sorting extension; details available shortly, but you can get the
> > flavor from the way your example would look:
> >
> > (SortOf productRelation ((UnaryRelation UnaryRelation) UnaryRelation) )
> >
> > ie the sort of 'productRelation' is a binary function from
> > UnaryRelation x UnaryRelation to UnaryRelation. (The language will
> > also permit more complex Boolean combinations to allow things like
> > sort overloading and multiple inheritance.)
>
>OK. Seems rather elaborate.

Yes, it is rather elaborate, since it has to do a variety of jobs. 
Mathematical tidyness is not the primary aim of a sorting syntax.

>Would this stuff be better placed in some
>"mathematically-oriented" ontologies? I would prefer that an absolute
>minimum be placed in core KIF and a core KIF ontology. What that minimum is,
>is another question.

This will be an *extension* to the core KIF. The intention is that 
the core will be an unsorted language.

>Again, can we not have some very basic sort-like ontologies where semantical
>typing is defined?

There is no single thing called 'semantical typing'. There are many 
different, rival, schemes for assigning sorts to the semantics. The 
simplest ones (often found in logic textbooks) are so restrictive 
that they are of no practical use. The most complex ones (often 
developed in programming langauge design) are mind-numbing. Most 
practical uses of sorting require mechanisms (such as defaults in 
inheritance heirarchies) which violate the basic assumoptions made by 
mathematical approaches to sorting. Given the many complexities and 
the rival claims made by people, my own approach is to ask of each 
feature, what its intended use is supposed to be, and how difficult 
it will be to implement. We have had some active debates along these 
lines already, and no doubt they will continue. For example, we have 
rejected defaults, in spite of their widespread utility, since they 
would make the underlying logic nonmonotonic, and the resulting 
complexity of the semantics was judged unacceptable.

>I will soon offer a KIF "ontology" for basic
>category-theoretic notions where a kind of semantical typing is defined.

I remain unconvinced that category-theoretic notions are of central 
importance for ontology design, but I wish you well in the enterprise.
