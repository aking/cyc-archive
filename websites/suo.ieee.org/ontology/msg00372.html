> I think I will defer this to the Sanskrit grammarians. But one
> possible response occurs to me: if you check the sentence,
> 'friendship' isnt a verb, which may be why it is not considered to
> need a case-like decomposition. One cannot of course expect that
> every relational predication be 'case-relationed', which would lead
> to an infinite regress along these lines:
>
> source, prehending, friendship
> source, prehended, A
> target, prehending, friendship
> target, prehended, B
>
> and then:
> prehending, prehending, source

Actually, I would expect this, in a sense. I expect every relational
predication to be 'case-framable', bottoming out in one step, where case
framing is the process of realizing the cases. I do this by distinguishing
between the initial 'relational predication', which by definition is not a
case frame (it may not have the explicit cases), and its resulting 'case
frames', where a case frame is a triple of the form
    3rdness, 2ndness, 1stness
So that in particular, the relational predication
    friendship(A, B)
has the case framing
    friendship, prehending, A
    friendship, prehended, B
But by definition the latter are not relational predications. So I have
bottomed out in one step. Of course, I could apply case-framing to case
frames themselves. But then you can stop at any stage, since the result
consists of case frames. And even if you continue further, you may
*semantically* bottom out very quickly.
