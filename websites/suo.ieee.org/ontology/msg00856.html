Well, we could distinguish ContinuantType from OccurrentType in much the
same way that Cyc distinguishes StuffType from ObjectType - ContinuantType
would be a class containing all the classes of continuants and OccurrentType
would be a class containing all the classes of occurrents.  The advantage of
this sort of structure is that it allows us to maintain the disjointness of
the high-level classes - there is no overlap between ContinuantType and
OccurrentType.  Furthermore, we can accomodate the observation that some
things are, e.g., process-like in some respects and object-like in others.
Furthermore, this approach would embody your general suggestion above about
categorizing aspects of things rather than things themselves, since classes
(or ways of describing things) are categorized as continuants/occurrents,
rather than the things themselves.

Well, if the "incoherence" of the subclassing of 'Schema' and 'Script' is
the "single most central conceptual bug" in the merged ontology, that's good
news, because I think it can be fairly easily debugged.  What if we just
replace the concept 'Abstract' by 'Informational Object' or
'ContentBearingObject' or some such notion?  Then we would have the
following relationships in the merged ontology.

(subclass-of SchemaObject ContentBearingObject)
(instance-of SchemaObject ContinuantType)
(subclass-of ScriptObject ContentBearingObject)
(instance-of ScriptObject OccurrentType)

Note that 'Schema' and 'Script' have been replaced by 'SchemaObject' and
'ScriptObject', since we are now concerned with the things that embody
schemas and scripts, as construed by Sowa.  Note too that the relationship
between 'SchemaObject' and 'ContinuantType' and between 'ScriptObject' and
'OccurrentType' is 'instance-of', rather than 'subclass-of'.  Finally, note
that 'ContentBearingObject' could be defined as follows, using Sowa's
original high-level notions.

(subclass-of ContentBearingObject Abstract)
(subclass-of ContentBearingObject Object)

At least we could do this as long as we're willing to concede that an
informational object is both a physical object and an abstract form (in some
sense).

OK, once again I think you are failing to bear in mind the engineering
perspective that we have to assume if we're going to get anything done here.
I know philosophers like to while away the hours wondering about whether
something is a merely linguistic categorization as opposed to a marker along
the noumenal contours of the world, but this shouldn't have any bearing
here.  We're trying to create a conceptual schema that, among other things,
promotes interoperability among information systems.  A linguistic
categorization may only reflect an "attitude", as you put it.  However, if
an attitude is culturally embedded (that is, if a large community assume
this attitude in their communications with one another), it matters a lot to
the culture, and, hence, it will probably matter a lot to the information
systems that they construct.  Therefore, a conceptual schema that does not
incorporate the categorization corresponding to this attitute will be
crippled in this respect.

Again, I understand that you philosophers are very prudish about the
epistemological/metaphysical distinction, and that's fine - every discipline
has its sacred cows.  However, I don't see that we have to be similarly
circumspect in this context.  We're trying to develop a formalization of
those categories which are most important to us.  Whether those categories

perceptual/cognitive machinery is irrelevant for the purposes of the SUO, I
would claim.  If a high-level category either directly or indirectly can be
expected to promote information-sharing, it should be included in our
developing conceptual schema.  If it's merely a product of the parlor games
of the 17th-century leisured class, then perhaps we can do without it.

> the basic rule of identity.  Now, how one *recognizes* this identity 
> is another matter, and a potentially very complicated one, requiring 
> a detailed analysis of what it takes to perform an act of recognition 
> and many other arcane and complex issues in psychology: but this is 
> all COMPLETELY IRRELEVANT to ontological classification.

OK, how about we revise the docu-string for 'Occurrent' as follows:

(documentation Occurrent "An entity that has temporal parts and, thus, does
not have a stable identity
during any interval of time.")

> 
> >(documentation OrganicObject "A CorpuscularObject such as a 
> tree or flower
> >that has parts that are not completely separable, even 
> though there are
> >discontinuities.")
> 
> Being organic is simply a matter of how well-connected your parts are 
> to one another? It has nothing to do with carbon-based chemistry, or 
> an elan vital, or nothing like that at all?

I agree that this sounds a bit wierd.  Perhaps John Sowa can clarify the
intent here.

> 
> >(documentation Physical "An entity that has a location in 
> space-time.")
> 
> A technical question: do spaces and times themselves have a location 
> in spacetime? (My own preference is to say not, but Cyc takes a 
> different view. But either way, we need a clear answer to avoid 
> rapidly descending into muddled axioms.)

OK, let's just pick one option or the other and go with it.  I don't think
any engineering applications will be hampered by our decision to assign or
not assign spaces and times themselves a location in spacetime.  

> 
> >(documentation Procedure "Any time- or sequence-dependent 
> specification of
> >actions and events: including: computer programs, 
> finite-state machines,
> >Petri nets, robot commands, cooking recipes, musical scores, 
> conference
> >schedules, driving directions, and the scripts of actions 
> and dialog in
> >plays and movies. Although a script is intended to represent 
> a dynamic
> >process, it may have static parts.  In a movie film, for example, the
> >sequence of images is a script that determines the motion, 
> but each frame is
> >a schema of a static image.")
> 
> See comments below on 'schema' and 'script'. Also this list is very 
> confusing, since some of the things in it are linguistic in nature 
> (computer programs), others are mathematical abstractions (Petri 
> nets) and others are physical (musical scores and movie film). In 
> what sense is a movie film a 'specification' ?

OK, how about we revise the docu-string as follows?

(documentation Procedure "A sequence-dependent specification of actions and
events.  Some examples are computer programs, finite-state machines, cooking
recipes, musical scores, conference schedules, driving directions, and the
scripts of actions and dialog in plays and movies.")

> 
> >(documentation Process "A Physical Occurrent during the interval of
> >interest. Depending on the time scale and level of detail, 
> the same actual
> >entity may be viewed as a stable object or a dynamic 
> process. Even an entity
> >as stable as a diamond could be considered a process when 
> viewed over a long
> >time period or at the atomic level of vibrating particles.")
> 
> Quite. So there is no useful categorical distinction to be 
> made, as I suggest.
> (BTW, what is the 'interval of interest', exactly?)

I think this problem is resolved by making 'Process' an instance of
'OccurrentType'.  In that case, something can be seen as either an object or
a process, but the kind 'Process' is an 'OccurrentType', while 'Object'
would be a 'ContinuantType'.

> 
> >(documentation Proposition "A subclass of Assembly. In 
> logic, the assertion
> >of a proposition is a claim that the abstraction corresponds 
> to some aspect
> >or configuration of the entity or entities involved. As an 
> example, the
> >statement cat(Yojo) expresses a proposition that Cat 
> characterizes the
> >entity named Yojo.")
> 
> This doesnt seem to distinguish the sentence from the proposition 
> expressed by that sentence. Exactly what a proposition is, is a 
> matter of controversy, but in at least one view, it isnt anything at 
> all like a sentence. BTW, what exactly is the word "Cat" supposed to 
> indicate in that last sentence?

Well, we could replace the concept of Proposition (which probably is of much
more interest to philosophers than engineers) with that of Sentence.  We
could then make the latter an immediate subclass of 'ContentBearingObject'.
Or perhaps we could make 'Sign' an immediate subclass of
'ContentBearingObject' and 'Sentence' an immediate subclass of 'Sign' and
'Arrangement'.

> 
> >(documentation Relation "An entity in a relationship to some 
> other entity.")
> 
> ? The relation is an entity in a relationship to some other entity? 
> So if (R a b), R is not the relation, but a and b are? This is just 
> nonsense.

OK, how about the following instead?

(documentation Relation "A set of ordered n-tuples.")

> 
> >(documentation Schema "An Abstract form that has the structure of a
> >Continuant and, thus, does not specify time or timelike 
> relationships.
> >Examples include geometric forms, the syntactic structures 
> of sentences in
> >some language, or the encodings of pictures in a multimedia system.")
> >
> >(documentation Script "An Abstract form that has the structure of an
> >Occurrent and, thus, represents time sequences.
> 
> Both of these (and 'kineticForm') are muddled ideas. They confuse the 
> structure of the form with the structure of what the form describes. 

OK, I agree with you here.  What if we use the following docu-strings
instead?

(documentation SchemaObject "An object that embodies information that has
the structure of a Continuant and, thus, does not specify time or timelike
relationships.  An example would be a box that embodies the geometric form
of a cube.")

(documentation Script "An object that embodies information that has the
structure of an Occurrent and, thus, represents time sequences.")

> First, as noted earlier, abstract forms simply do not (cannot) have 
> spatiotemporal properties such as being continuants or occurrents; 
> but even if we were to somehow allow them to have such aspects, there 
> is no connection between the structure of the form and the structure 
> of what the form describes.  We are all intimately familiar with 
> various notations which give spatial renderings of temporal 
> relationships, and many of us have seen temporal representations of 
> spatial relationships (a slow pan in a movie, for example). More 
> acutely, there are many, many abstractions throughout mathematics 
> which are entirely neutral as to the spatial or temporal aspects of 
> their interpretation. The widespread Allen algebra of timeintervals 
> for example is in fact a theory of intervals, and can be applied to 
> spatial intervals just as well as to temporal ones.
> 
> > Examples include computer
> >programs, a recipe for baking a cake, a sheet of music to be 
> played on a
> >piano, or a differential equation that governs the evolution 
> of a physical
> >process. A movie can be described by several different kinds 
> of scripts: the
> >first is a specification of the actions and dialog to be acted out by
> >humans; but the sequence of frames in a reel of film is also 
> a script that
> >determines a process carried out by a projector that 
> generates flickering
> >images on a screen.")
> 
> A movie film, it seems, is many things at once. It is a continuant 
> which is also a procedure which is also a script.
> 
> >(documentation Sign "A Sign is considered as representing 
> something to some
> >agent.")
> 
> Just a quibble, but I think that it might be more use to characterise 
> a sign as something intended by one agent to represent something for 
> another agent (or agents). People often find meaning in things that 
> arent usually categorised as signs (except in a metaphorical sense), 
> cf. many folk sayings about smoke and fire.

OK, this sounds like a good refinement.  Why don't we rephrase the
docu-string as follows?

(documentation Sign "A Sign is something that is intended by one agent to
represent something to another agent or agents.  Note that this definition
excludes natural phenomena and their interpretation, e.g. a cloud formation
that resembles a physical object.") 
> 
> >(documentation Situation "A Relational entity considered as 
> an Occurrent. A
> >situation relates the participants in some process, whose 
> stages may involve
> >different participants at different times.")
> >
> >(documentation Structure "A Relational entity considered as 
> a Continuant. A
> >structure relates multiple objects whose connections constitute the
> >structure.")
> 
> Both the above are clumsy because of the awkwardness of the 
> occurrent/continuant contrast. Rainstorms have a dynamic structure, 
> for example, and cells (in biology) are structures which consist of 
> interlocking processes. The very idea of 'dynamic equilibrium' 
> couldnt be considered in this overly simplistic ontological 
> classification.
> 
> >(documentation VariableObject "A ContinuousObject that 
> nonetheless varies
> >in, e.g., temperature, chemical constitution, density, etc.  
> An example
> >would be a large body of water such as the ocean.")
> 
> Consider a homogenous object which has the same chemical consituency, 
> temperature, etc., throughout; like a cube of pure gold. It is as 
> unVariable as one could possibly achieve, let us suppose. 
> Nevertheless, it does vary in some ways. Parts of it, for example, 
> are further north than other parts, and again, some parts of it are 
> higher in the gravity field than other parts. So its parts can be 
> distinguished from each other. Now, is it therefore a VariableObject? 
> I suspect you will want to say not, but on what basis? You cannot 
> claim that *every* property of all its parts are shared by all its 
> other parts. What I suspect you want to say is that all its parts 
> have the same *intrinsic* properties, maybe, or maybe the same 
> *physical* properties. Or maybe you could say that the object is made 
> of the same *stuff* throughout. But in any case, you do need to say 
> what it is you actually do mean.

I agree.  Why don't we rephrase the docu-string as follows?

(documentation VariableObject "A ContinuousObject whose physical properties
may nonetheless vary.  That is, the temperature, chemical constitution,
density, etc. may change from one part to another.  An example of a
VariableObject would be a large body of water such as the ocean.")

> 
> >3.  The third proposed change is to eliminate the axioms of 
> the "Theory of
> >Holes".  As I see it, these axioms are very interesting, but 
> they are also
> >controversial and probably more relevant in a philosophical 
> context than an
> >engineering one like the SUO.
> 
> Well, engineering is largely concerned with holes, as a matter of 
> fact (see any history of machine tools: all the great manufacturing

> with the centerless precision grinder. ) So we will almost certainly

> to be a lot more use to engineering than all that stuff about scripts 
> and situations and signs.

OK, I've put the theory of holes back into the merged ontology.
