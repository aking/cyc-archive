Pat, may I yet again suggest we can avoid a lot of problems if we are clear
and consistent about whether we use any particular term to refer to the
real world or to a representation.

In particular I use the word relation only to refer to a representation of
the real world, and in conformity with Ted Codd's definition.  As such, I
see no problem in it only being a table of surrogates.  Conversely I use
the word relationship only to refer to the thing in the real world made up
of arbitrary entites.

In my experience, every relationship in the real world of whatever
arbitrary entities can be adequately represented using one or more
relations with suitable surrogates in its columns.

Does this help?

Incidently, while I agree that the word relation is well defined in
symbolic logic and relational databases, I am not aware of the same being
true of the word relationship.

>Graham and Pat,
>
>To avoid endless problems caused by mismatched definitions,
>I suggest that we adopt the following equivalences between
>terms:
>
> 1. The terms "relation" and "relationship" in ordinary
>    English have multiple meanings.  But the use in both
>    symbolic logic and in relational databases is firmly
>    established.  I recommend that we restrict all uses
>    of those two words to the meanings in logic and RDBMS.

I'm happy with that. That was my point about relations being
identical to their extensions (as far as logic is concerned.)

> 2. In RDB, a relation is defined by a table.  The number
>    of columns in the table is critical:  a binary or dyadic
>    relation has two columns, a ternary or triadic relation
>    has three columns, etc.  Each row of the table is
>    called a tuple or a relationship.

In (old) KIF, one could also have mixed-arity 'tables', so I wouldnt
say that the number was *critical* exactly. But I'm just quibbling.
More seriously (and this gets back to the old disagreement between us
which arose again recently). I'm a bit unhappy about identifying a
relation with a 'table', since "table" suggests something that could
be stored in a computer, ie a datastructure of some kind. But as long
as we allow 'tables' that can be made up af arbitrary entities (and
not just 'surrogates') then I agree, this is a useful metaphor for
thinking about extensional relations.

>......
>
>The words "property" and "attribute" have been used in many
>confusing ways.  I suggest that we use them only as synonyms
>for monadic (or unary) predicate.

I agree. This is the only sense of them that I intend to use.
