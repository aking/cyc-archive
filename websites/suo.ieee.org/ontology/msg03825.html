JD = Jean-Luc Delatre

Just by way of trying to catch up,
here is a piece of JD's commentary
that I think I can answer right now:

JA: When things get too confusing I need to draw a picture.
    Peirce is implicitly working within a relational context
    that will eventually come to be called a "sign relation".
    This is a 3-adic relation whose role-places or role-players
    are conventionally appellated as the "object", the "sign",
    and the "interpretant sign" (or "interpretant" for short).
    In extension, then, a sign relation L would be a subset
    of a cartesian product, L c !O!x!S!x!I!.

JD: From what Peirce said:

| Thus every increase in the number of equivalents of any term increases either
| its extension or comprehension and 'conversely'.  It may be said that there
| are no equivalent terms in logic, since the only difference between such
| terms would be merely external and grammatical, while in logic terms
| which have the same meaning are identical.  I fully admit that.
| Indeed, the process of getting an equivalent for a term is
| an identification of two terms previously diverse.  It is,
| in fact, the process of nutrition of terms by which they
| get all their life and vigor and by which they put forth
| an energy almost creative -- since it has the effect of
| reducing the chaos of ignorance to the cosmos of science.
| Each of these equivalents is the explication of what there is
| wrapt up in the primary -- they are the surrogates, the interpreters
| of the original term.  They are new bodies, animated by that same soul.
| I call them the 'interpretants' of the term.  And the quantity of these
| 'interpretants', I term the 'information' or 'implication' of the term.
|
| CSP, CE 1, pages 464-465.

And:

| A disjunctive term, then, -- one which aggregates the extension
| of several symbols, -- may always be replaced by a simple term.

And:

| Such a term, formed by the sum of the comprehensions
| of several terms, is called a conjunctive term.

And:

| Most combinations of words are also symbols.
| A proposition, an argument, even a whole book
| may be, and should be, a single symbol.

JD: I conclude that what Peirce calls a "term" is not only
    a single word but also disjunctions and conjunctions
    of such words with the provision of substituting
    "simple terms" for disjunctions which happen to
    be "true symbols".

Peirce is using the word "term" the way that it used
to be used right up until the onset of the Dark Ages,
to wit, 1901 or so.  Thus, "term" suggests "terminus",
that is, a sign or a "mark" that "aphorizes", "bounds",
"defines", "delimits", or "draws a boundary around the
region that it rules".  In this sense, the word anchors
its place in the series:  term, premiss, argument(ation).

The critical point to note here is that Peirce is defining
the word "symbol" to mean a very special genus among signs.

| The third and last kind of representations are 'symbols' or general
| representations.  They connote attributes and so connote them as to
| determine what they denote.  To this class belong all 'words' and
| all 'conceptions'.  Most combinations of words are also symbols.
| A proposition, an argument, even a whole book may be, and
| should be, a single symbol.
|
| CSP, CE 1, pages 467-468.

Symbols "connote attributes and so connote them as to determine what they denote".
To fully comprehend this definition of a symbol, then, once would also have to
look up Peirce's definition of "determination", a crucial idea for his whole
point of view.  I append some links to an extended discussion of this.
The definition of determination will be found in there somewhere.

So what Peirce is saying with regard to the sublanguage of symbols
is that it is not closed under "junctions", that is to say, under
the various and sundry sorts of logical connectives that he is
contemplating in this context of discussion.

JD: Therefore I figure that the "signs" and "interpretant signs"
    are in fact one and the same domain and that they include
    conjunctions and disjunctions.  So the cartesian product
    is !O!x!T!x!T! where T = !S! u !conj! u !disj!.

Yes, in many situations that we consider, the sign domain !S! and the
interpretant domain !I! happen to be the same as sets, so !S! = !I!.
In these cases, the sign relations L that we discuss may be considered
to have the form L c !O!x!S!x!S!.  Very often in these cases we will be
concerned with more that just the "static" structures of sign relations.
We will indeed be interested in using sign relations as formal models
of "sign processes", or "semioses" (the plural of "semiosis", I guess).
In these more "dynamic" settings, of which the semiotic process that
all the world calls "computation" is one very salient instance, one
tends to write the sign triple <o, s, i> as <o, s, s'> and to think
a lot about the "sign transitions" of the form s ~> s' as they wend
their merry way though CPU time.

In any case, I have taken to calling the union !S! |_| !I!
by the name of the "syntactic domain", and when !S! = !I!
then everything folds into one:  !S! = !S! |_| !I! = !I!.

And yes again, all of the cases that really interest us
have infinite syntactic domains, defined themselves as
formal languages L c !A!*, for some alphabet !A!, of
which languages the various and sundry term calculi,
closed under assorted connective operations, would
make moderately interesting transitional objects.

I have to break for some sustenance ...
