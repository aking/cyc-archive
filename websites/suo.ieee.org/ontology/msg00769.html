At 03-02-01 01:40, pat hayes wrote:

Matthew,

        You claim that modal operators may not be appropriate for
representing alternative courses of action.  Could you give us an example of
such a situation that cannot be adequately represented with the first-order
predicate calculus and the modal operator of possibility?

-Ian

Since Matthew has not replied, allow me. It is almost too easy to do this, since in fact there is almost nothing that *can* be said about alternative courses of action using the modal operator of possibility. First, one would need a temporal modality, since 'possible' might refer to the past or the future. But leave that aside, as it is a minor point.  The real, er, showstopper is that the modality amounts to an existential quantifier over alternative worlds (not courses of action, another quibble) , but provides no way to say anything about what is true in one possible world as opposed to another. For example, suppose one wants to decide whether to pull or push a door to open it, by imagining the consequences of each action. Both actions are *possible*, of course, and it is *possible* that the door will open in the future. But what we want to do is reason along the lines: if the door is pushed (and other relevant conditions apply) then it would move thus (I omit the gory details of frame reasoning and ramifications), and moreover if it were pulled (and if other conditions obtained, etc.) then it would move so. But of course we know it can't move both thus and so at the same time, so we need to say that under one circumstance it moves in one way and in the other it moves differently. These 'circumstances' are not *times*, notice: both the circumstances have the same clock time (just after I do my thing to the door); they are something like alternative courses of events, or alternative histories, or maybe alternative action sequences (in the situation calculus they would be alternative situtations accessible from the present by different action functions. ) This can't be done using logical modalities. In fact, *no* action planning can be done that way, as far as I know.

Pat Hayes


### I don't think the matter can be brushed off quite so easily. Two connected issues. First issue: theory. (1) Any useful, (domain-)ontology will have to serve disparate and initially incompatible applications in that domain as these will become developed independently by a -large- number of people over time, adding new possible knowledge to the domain model as one goes along. One way to accommodate this could be to start off (or permanently see?) an ontology (or at least what I call its ontobase, its set of possible ground atomic facts) as denoting a non-disjoint collection of possible worlds. There are a number of adequate semantic formalisms to handle this rigorously, e.g. Kripke, to a lesser degree Montague. In this way an application may be validated/authorized/... if it maps to the ontobase, under certain constraints.
### Second issue: practice. (2) The problem focus now becomes the effective and efficient organization of this ontobase and its (few) truly generic constraints, and of course I realize that the complexity burden now moves to said application environments. I conjecture however that this latter issue should and will more pragmatically -and usefully- be treated in the application domain context (e.g. by agreement, in glossaries, in packaged program libraries, ...). For this same reason it is my opinion that a uniform KIF-like model while conceptually beautiful, rigorous and most enlightening at the detailed level of knowledge, is doomed to die of terminal reductionism as it collapses under the conceptual complexity of even the most prosaic domains. (NB. I once claimed this during a talk at Stanford and survived). People in practice design systems by agreeing on a fact base, not by proving it. Waiting for the efforts of this august guild of ontologizers to come up with the Grand Integral Ztandard Model Ontology may prove to be, ah, less effective. Not that I'd mind continuing to contribute! The best science is blind :-)
