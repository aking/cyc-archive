> Matthew wrote:
> > [Menzel wrote:]
> > > Matthew wrote:
> > > 
> > > >  (<=> (gives John Mary Book1)
> > > >       (and (gives x)
> > > >            (giver x John)
> > > >            (receiver x Mary)
> > > >            (given x book1)
> > > >       )
> > > >  )
> > > 
> > > This can't be right, as the free variable "x" occurs on the right
> > > side of the <=> but not on the left; it just sits there like a
> > > pronoun with no associated context to give it meaning.   
> > 
> > MW: x is intended here to be a constant. It is relevant to 
> just this one 
> > act of giving. Though I would agree it is a bad choice of name.
> 
> Then I'm even more confused.  Is your intention to introduce 
> a distinct
> variable C for every three names N1, N2, and N3 in your language and
> then provide an axiom of the form
> 
> (<=> (gives N1 N2 N3)
>      (and (giving C)
>           (giver C N1)
>           (receiver C N2)
>           (given C N3)
> 
> for all such C, N1, N2, and N3?  That seems very awkward indeed.  What
> does such a C denote if the thing denoted by N1 does not give 
> the thing
> denoted by N2 the thing denoted by N3?  A nonexistent event?  A merely
> possible event?  I really don't think this is what you want to do.  I
> think what you want is to guarantee a giving event *whenever* three
> things stand in the gives relation.  That has to be stated generally
> with a quantifier as I suggest below, not with constants, which force
> you to find something for them to denote.
> 
> > > Also, you are using "gives" ambiguously here. If I recall 
> another of
> > > your recent posts correctly, you are using "(gives x)" to mean
> > > something like "x is a giving", i.e., an act or event of giving.  
> > 
> > MW: You are quite right. The two gives have different meanings. In
> > a later version I have used gives1 and gives2 (or some such) to
> > distinguish them.
> 
> I'll stick with "giving" to apply to events.
> 
> > > If so, I think what you're after is the following (I change to the
> > > more mnemonic (and KIF'ish) variable "?e"):
> > > 
> > > (<=> (gives John Mary book1)
> > >      (exists (?e) 
> > >              (and (giving ?e)
> > >                   (giver ?e John)
> > >                   (receiver ?e Mary)
> > >                   (given ?e book1))))
> > > 
> > MW: Well no, as I said in my response to Fred it is necessary to
> > ensure that there is exactly one e for this case, which is probably
> > best denoted by a constant. 
> 
> Except that using a constant doesn't do a thing to guarantee 
> uniqueness
> (at least, not without adding an ungainly bunch of "unique 
> names" axioms
> -- unique naming might be a convenient constraint to implement in a
> database perhaps, but it is unlovely, to say the least, in a formal
> ontology).  Nothing you've said would rule out there being another
> constant denoting an event distint from your original in which John is
> giver, Mary receiver, and book1 the given. If you want 
> uniqueness, then
> you do that by means of quantififiers as well, supplementing the above
> as follows:
> 
> (<=> (gives John Mary book1)
>      (exists (?e) 
>              (and (giving ?e)
>                   (giver ?e John)
>                   (receiver ?e Mary)
>                   (given ?e book1)
>                   (forall (?e')
>                           (=> (and (giving ?e')
>                                    (giver ?e' John)
>                                    (receiver ?e' Mary)
>                                    (given ?e' book1))
>                               (= ?e ?e'))))))
>                      
> If it now turns out that (gives John Mary book1), then you 
> are warranted
> by the above in *introducing* a new name, which will denote the unique
> event that is guaranteed by the above axiom.  But should John 
> *not* give
> book1 to Mary, then, again by the above, there is no corresponding
> event in which John is giver, etc (and no embarrassing constant around
> seeking a nonexistent denotation).  Of course, I think what 
> you *really* 
> want is the purely generalized form of this axiom, of which the above 
> is just an instance:
> 
> (forall (?x ?y ?z)
>         (<=> (gives ?x ?y ?z)
>              (exists (?e) 
>                      (and (giving ?e)
>                           (giver ?e ?x)
>                           (receiver ?e ?y)
>                           (given ?e ?z)
>                           (forall (?e')
>                                   (=> (and (giving ?e')
>                                            (giver ?e' ?x)
>                                            (receiver ?e' ?y)
>                                            (given ?e' ?z))
>                                       (= ?e ?e')))))))
> 
> > > What all this means for the triadicity debate I have no idea, as I
> > > have no idea what triadicity in the Mystical Peircean Sense is all
> > > about.  I would understand the above axiom as nothing more than a
> > > way of providing a (perhaps) more robust analysis of the giving
> > > relation by enlarging one's ontology to include events.
> > 
> > MW: You make a good point here ...
> 
> Then I'll quit while I'm ahead!  :-)
