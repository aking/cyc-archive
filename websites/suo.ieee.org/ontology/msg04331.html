SS: I don't feel very confident about Peirce's theory of identity.
    A computer search of Collected Papers has left me somewhat confused.
    Have you (or any lister) a good idea of Peirce's philosophy of identity?

JA: My guess would be that Peirce's criterion
    for the concept of "identity" to have a
    meaning would be the same as that for
    any other concept to have a meaning
    to wit, the pragmatic maxim.

JR: One of James' versions of the pragmatic maxim is useful in practice,
    I think:  "A difference that makes no difference is no difference at all".
    No criterion required;  just lack of reason to make a distinction.

JA: One of themes that keeps arising here is the idea that we always begin with
    what various folks calls interpretive, local, regional, or relative concepts,
    and only with time and not a little luck are able to construct slightly more
    invariant concepts out of the frame-&-path-dependent or perspectival notions.

JA: One of the implications of this is that 2-adic relations like identity, implication,
    membership, and subsethood, are never fundamental primitives in Peircean logic, but
    constructed as limits or projections from 3-adic relations.  So we always start from
    3-adic relations like "A means B to C", or "X is Y for Z", and only in special cases
    and circumstances find ourselves able to approximate these by a 2-adic relations.

JA: On a related note, one should look to why Peirce
    found it necessary to consider "teridentity" as
    constituting the irreducible notion of identity.
