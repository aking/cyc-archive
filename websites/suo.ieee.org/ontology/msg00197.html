Dear John Awbrey,

let me germanspeak:
Man kann auch zu Tode kommen,
wenn man zu schnell liest.
Bleibe uns erhalten!
Do you really think, that I asked for an answer yes or no?
What does Wordsworth tell to Simple Simon, the ontologist?
Tentatively I propose a relation here, alas, not a binary one:

nrOfFamilyMembers

1: argument: the family object
2. argument: the number of family members
3. argument: the interpretant.
4. argument: date

For simplicity let me assume, that theLittleGirl and Bureaucracy
share at least the name space. And let God
(me the watching and judging engineer for now)
be a virtual fifth agument saving all the many 5. Kolonnen
in the relational DB,
also sharing this name space.
Fortunately, the whole relation can be a virtual one,
in the same way as the popular hasAge slot.

Then we get:
nrOfFamilyMembers(TheLittleGirlsFamily, 5, Bureaucracy, 1.4.1798)
nrOfFamilyMembers(TheLittleGirlsFamily, 7, TheLittelGirl, 1.4.1798)
What does God do with this discrepancy? No problem?
Let me assume now, that TheLittleGirl becomes an orphan,
then, if the girl has not changed her mind (how do we model that?), we get
nrOfFamilyMembers(TheLittleGirlsFamily, 7, TheLittelGirl, 31.5.1809),
but
nrOfFamilyMembers(TheLittleGirlsFamily, 1, Bureaucracy, 31.5.1809)?
Matthew would propose states here for the entities and skip the date argument.
What is the "nature" of the time slice TheLittleGirlsFamily[31.5.1809]
in the  interpretation of the Bureaucacy, an impossible ?,
a post mortem state? How do we axiomatize this?
We must supply help for the axiom that a family must
have more than one member (in a prae mortem state).
Oh, good old times, when there were only employees, salaries and managers
to model, and forgive me for my simplistic modeling.
You expect bards singing where folk is chatting, are you?
Then let us listen to
http://www.bartleby.com/145/ww798.html
I like it, especially in this season.
Dietrich
