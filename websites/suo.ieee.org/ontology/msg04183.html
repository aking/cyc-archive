I continue with the causeway work, which is more a matter
of looking for continuities, however slim, than stressing
distinctions, however stark.  It's another kind o'caisson
for this doughboy.  Now, there's a whole raft of troubles
ahead that I've been through so many times before but not
yet troubled you about, and so it does not bother me that
we appear to be moving so agonizingly slow.  Just to cite
a few of the issues that have yet to appear on your radar,
there is the question of potentially crucial continuities
among probability and statistics, information, and logic.

We can begin to make this connection by trying to follow
a single slender thread through the three tangled realms.
This would be the construct that we may severally regard
as a function on a sample space, f : X -> Y, for various
odd reasons called a "random variable", or a proposition.

When f : X -> Y is a random variable, roughly, a "statistic",
then X is the sample space and Y tends to be the real line R,
an interval [0, 1] c R, or else the boolean space B = {0, 1}.

When f : X -> Y is a proposition, or an "indicator function",
then X is the universe of discourse and Y is a boolean space.

The feature of this construct that is preserved in the transition
from quantitative applications to qualitative applications is the
way that the value of f, whether it be in R or whether it be in B,
is really just a way of "indicating" antecedent elements within X.
It is this feature of the functions f : X -> Y that permits us to
ford the space between the spaces, affording transitional objects.

When the codomain is the real line R or the unit interval [0, 1],
elements of the domain X are indicated with continuously varying
amplitudes, intensities, probabilities, or "strengths".

When the codonain is the boolean space B, then the indication is
all or nothing.

Power has been off and on all day, and I hear thunder again ...

Jon Awbrey
