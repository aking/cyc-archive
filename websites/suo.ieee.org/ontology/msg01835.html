At 24-03-01 13:01, Chris Menzel wrote:
> > where it not being definable was my principal objection.  Why have
> > both?
>
>No reason other than that nth-domain is fairly entrenched, so it was
>important to show that it could be expressed.  I think the following
>should fall out as a theorem that relates the two relations (WARNING!!
>KIF AHEAD!! ;-) :
>
>(forall (?rel ?n @args (= (arity ?rel) ?n))
>         (<=> (signature ?rel @args)
>              (forall (?m (posint ?m) (=< ?m ?n))
>                      (nth-domain ?rel ?m ([@args] ?m)))))

### Chris,

### milli-niggle (= 1 microsquiggle = 1 nanotrifle): this theorem would in 
my opinion have appeared slightly more intuitive notation-wise if you 
interchanged the variable names ?n and ?m

### of course I understand it is just a direct consequence of the notation 
you used in the definition of nth-domain in the main text, which I assume 
resulted from a case of somebody having acute LISParrhea  :-)   --it is an 
example of unnecessary generalization inspired by ease-of-programming 
considerations rather than an ontological concept; the final "special case 
theorem" expresses things much clearer and probably is the only form in 
which nth-domain will be invoked, i.e. general enough for a SUO. BTW the 
documentation of n-th domain has a typo i<n instead of i<=n. Also, the 
final NOTE of that particular documentation is so imprecisely formulated it 
confuses rather than clarifies --

### allow me to suggest you devise a suitable numbering (e.g. decimal?) for 
the axioms and theorems if we are going to discuss some.

### a propos, you guys must be used to this but all those question marks in 
variable names give me eyebrow cramps  :-)

--Robert Meersman
