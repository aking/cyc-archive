> > >We have a rather broad view of
> > >what a state is, which interestingly includes activities. So
> > a state for us
> > >is any piece of space time where the time dimension is
> > non-zero (though it
> > >may be vanishingly small). The useful ones are those for which some
> > >predicate is true, e.g. being a person, being at 20C, being
> > a husband, being
> > >a football match etc. Some states are a temporal part of
> > others. So being a
> > >husband is a temporal part of being a person. Being a
> > quarterback in a
> > >particular footbal match is a temporal part of the football
> > match AND a
> > >temporal part of a person.
> > >
> > >MW: It really is possible to represent accurately quite
> > complex ideas with
> > >some very simple basic concepts - but this is what 4D is all about.
> > >
> > >MW: Oh the changes (timeless events) are the temporal
> > boundaries of states.
> >
> > Purely temporal, or could they slope? eg think of a moving sprayer,
> > and the spatiotemporal locus of where the water stops being under
> > high pressure.
>
>MW: We have the concept of a stream which slopes. I think this would cover
>this case, but I'm not sure what sense of moving you mean here, a rotary
>sprayer, or a moving truck. I guess in either case a stream is part of it.

I was thinking of a hosepipe with a sprayer at the end. Water passing 
through that point is undergoing a state change (pressure, velocity, 
etc.) which can itself be the subject of detailed analysis.  If 
someone is waving the hose around in order to spray a garden, say, 
then the locus of that water-state-change is moving in space, so it's 
not a purely temporal boundary (?)


> > >
> > >MW: Quite. I would be inclined to say "A class of tuples",
> > but in KIF the
> > >relation stands as a predicate, and I have never quite got
> > to the bottom of
> > >whether predicate and class are synonyms (for which I find as yet no
> > >contrary evidence).
> >
> > Strictly speaking a predicate (extension) on X is a set (= class) of
> > 1-tuples of members of X, rather than a set of members of X. To
> > identify these is a common abuse of notation.
>
>MW: I have seen different definitions of one tuples, one that said that a 1
>tuple was the object it contained, the other that it was the singleton set
>of the object it contained.
>
>Do you have a view on that?

The latter is correct, but one often slurs the distinction as long as 
it doesn't cause confusion. Since it clearly has caused confusion on 
this list, I will try to be more careful here in future.

If someone insists that (not as a mere careless convention, but in 
fact) a 1-tuple or a singleton is the the object it contains, then 
denounce them as a closet mereologist and tell them to stop using 
set-theory talk.
