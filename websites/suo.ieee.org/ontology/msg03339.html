Let us now pause to equip ourselves with the means to untangle
a number of wrangles, terminological and interminological, and
then move on.

We have taken up the following string of topics:

Logic  =?<  Semiotic  =?<  General Semiotic  =?<  Pragmatic Theory Of Signs.

Peirce will announce that Logic is just another name for Semiotics,
but then go on to theorize about signs in vastly more general ways.
One way to accommodate this perspective, to express his definition
but leave enough room for the more general subjects, is as follows:

Logic  =  Semiotic  <  General Semiotic  =?<  Pragmatic Theory Of Signs.

But it seems inefficient to use two separate terms for the single subject
of logic, and again, it eventually becomes inconvenient to use a compound
of two words to discuss what strikes one as a simple subject of semiotics,
and so I will tend to exercise the following option:

Logic  <  Semiotic  =  General Semiotic  =?<  Pragmatic Theory Of Signs.

At any rate, I think that the appropriate sensitivity to context is
enough to resolve the residual ambiguities and differences in usage.

So let us return to the train of thought already in progress ...

I would never have the nerve of Peirce, to flash out and capture logic
and its object in one sweeping brush stroke across the canvass, and so
I will venture only so far as to sketch a detail of the larger picture,
enough for the sake of illustration, but not without some interest and
utility of its own, namely, propositional calculus.

I can remember stopping to ask myself, some time ago:

1.  What is it that I expect a calculus to help
    me do in the case of a propositional domain?

2.  What is it that I want to be able to do in
    regard to a given language of propositions?

The answer that came to mind was something like this:
Given a propositional expression, I would like to be
capable of quickly figuring out just about where it
is in the lattice or partial order of propositions.
This means finding the logical neigborhood of the
expression in question, in the following senses:

a.  To recognize the logical equivalents of an expression.
b.  To know what expressions are above it in the lattice.
c.  To know what expressions are below it in the lattice.
d.  To recognize simpler expressions that approximate it.

And so if you ask me, "What is the object of ZOL?",
where "zeroth order logic" is roughly the same as
Peirce's Alpha Graphs, propositional calculus, or
sentential logic, then I would start out thinking
in terms of these objectives, and probably sum up
by saying that the object of this logical language
is a family of logical lattices, or partial orders.

Given the general recognition of this object, it affords
a natural point of reference with regard to which we may
evaluate the facility of each proposed language about it.

But not without a fresh cup of coffee ...

Jon Awbrey
