| Gaze no more in the bitter glass
| The demons, with their subtle guile,
| Lift up before us when they pass,
| Or only gaze a little while;
| For there a fatal image grows
| That the stormy night receives,
| Roots half hidden under snows,
| Broken boughs and blackened leaves.
| For all things turn to barrenness
| In the dim glass the demons hold,
| The glass of outer weariness,
| Made when God slept in times of old.
| There, through the broken branches, go
| The ravens of unresting thought;
| Flying, crying, to and fro,
| Cruel claw and and hungry throat,
| Or else they stand and sniff the wind,
| And shake their ragged wings;  alas!
| Thy tender eyes grow all unkind:
| Gaze no more in the bitter glass.
|
| William Butler Yeats, 'The Two Trees'

The glasses raised by others have shown me reflections
of myself that I must reflect on further -- someday we
may have the chance to discuss the practical necessity
of poetic discipline for certain types of temperament --
I know that some will suspect me of chaining together
a fourfold contradiction in roots, but I am forced to
let it pass until the normative science of aesthetics
is better understood, and another day we will delight
ourselves with paradoxes incumbent on good advice and
the uses of adversa, but I am obliged to let it slide
till the normative science of ethics, notwithstanding
its hour come round at last, is better practiced than
preached.  In all the cosmos that leaves me aught but
the normative science of logic, watching every nuance,
like a cloud of Dionysus, vanish from beneath my gaze
in the glaring sun of Apollo, and so to that I return,
yet again, once more.

Another Time,

Jon Awbrey
