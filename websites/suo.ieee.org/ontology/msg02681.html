Josiane Caron-Pargue wrote,
        
Hi Jean-Marc,

        Thank you for your mail. I agree with this. I know that material
qualities are not necessarily signs, I am always looking after differencies
between material qualities.
        The problem is that I have not yet understand how to work with
Peirce. I would like very much to do it.
The problem at the cognitive level is that to suppose cognitive processes
without material observables which can be observed by anyone else is
dangerous. I think I am a sort of reductionist as anyone else.

        But at the same time I understand Peirce and I think it would be
nice if I understand how to work with.
I am sure it would help me to formulate my observations. 
       Yours
       Josiane

>Josiane,
><blockquote>MS 381 -On the nature of Signs .</blockquote>
>
><blockquote>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; A sign is an object
>which stands for another to some mind. I propose to describe the characters
>of a sign. In the first place like any other thing it must have qualities
>which belong to it whether it be regarded as a sign or not thus a printed
>word is black, has a certain number of letters and those letters have certain
>shapes. Such characters of a sign I call its material quality. In the next
>place a sign must have some real connection with the thing it signifies
>so that when the object is present or is so as the sign signifies it to
>be the sign shall so signify it and otherwise not. [...] <b>In the first
>place it is necessary for a sign to be a sign that it should be regarded
>as a sign for it is only a sign to that mind which so considers and if
>it is not a sign to any mind it is not a sign at all.</b> It must be known
>to the mind first in its material qualities but also in its pure demonstrative
>application. <b>That mind must conceive it to be connected with its object
>so that it is possible to reason from the sign to the thing</b>.</blockquote>
