Doug, John, & All,

A couple of points:

Taking the view from a historical perspective -- The Horror! The Horror! --
what we now call "programming languages" can be seen to have developed
in the following way:  A program ("a writ before") is a certain order
of "effective description", for instance, the likes of all of these:
an algorithm, a certification, a civil order, an epitome, a formula,
an imperial decree, a judicial injunction, a papal order, a pithy maxim,
a poem ("the words that do"), a prescription, a protocol ("glued up front"),
a recapitulation, a receipt, a recipe, a specification, a summons, a warrant
for the arrest of a named or a specified person (with all of the "halting"
problems that ensue upon its putative execution), a writ of habeas corpus,
or a writ of mandamus, in effect, a simple sort of injunctive or instructive
description that a chemist, a cleric, a clerk, a computer, a cook, or a cop --
job descriptions that were all of them originally filled by human, all too
human executors and interpreters -- can pick out their own ways to follow.

From this long view, it is something of an incidental side-effect that
a particular "genre or motif of very effective descriptions" (GOMOVED's),
as were writ by "formal or logical or mathematical executors" (FOLOME's)
in the Mid-Twentieth Century, constituted such maximally simple-minded
maxims that even the likes of a machine could be made to follow them
more or less faithfully.

More or less -- Ay, there's the rub, and the friction thereof!
