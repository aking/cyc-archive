At 10:16 PM 4/6/2001 -0700, pat hayes wrote:
>>Graham,

Just because we haven't yet found success is no reason to think it 
impossible.  What lesson would you have me learn, and how should that 
lesson follow from the SUO experience?

>>I believe that the solution is not improved document management or better 
>>tools but rather for folks to be specific and concrete in developing a 
>>formal document expressing their preferred version of an upper ontology.

>I think it is simply a reflection of a fact of life: people do not agree

>to utilize it, rather than try to 'solve' the 'problem'?

Not at all.  People disagree when there is reason to do so, and find ways 
of agreeing when there is reason to do so.  In the KIF working group we 
have a compelling need to agree on a common language and semantics for the 
language.  We might each have our preferences, but we're willing to give up 
certain preferences here and there in order to reach consensus.

We don't all need to agree on every aspect of the SUO.  We merely need to 
have a product we can accept as a result of a lot of give and take.

>No, we would simply have a larger and even more confused stew. The 
>axiomatizations would be incompatiable with each other and would be based

>You have thanked me for my contribution on temporal axioms, but I wish you 
>would not, since I regard the temporal axioms in Niles' document as a

>temporal CATALOG, setting out the ALTERNATIVES and noting the ways they

We may disagree on English terms but I'm not clear on which axioms you 
disagree with.  In fact, although you've said often that you consider the 
current merged ontology "incoherent" or, as above, that there are sections 
that are "incompatible" with one another, I don't believe you've given a 
precise characterization of what you mean by those words.  Do you mean that 
the current ontology is logically inconsistent?  If so, let me issue a 
friendly challenge: show a proof using the axioms in the merged ontology 
which leads to a contradiction.  Let me issue an even stronger challenge 
since if there are in fact any contradictions I suspect we can fix them: 
show two proofs leading to contractions for which any fixes would generate 
more contradictions.

>>This could also include domain-specific ontologies as the exercise of 
>>linking such ontologies to the merged ontology will elucidate the general 
>>areas that need to be developed.
>>  How about a practical semiotics of books, recordings and documents;
>
>Would it take a book to be a semiotic text with a physical encoding, or a 
>physical object (a continuant or a 4-d lifetime?) to which contentful 
>relations can be applied, or a kind of 'sum' of a physical entity plus a 
>nonphysical 'text', or a collection (what kind of collection?) of surfaces 
>bearing information-encoding marks, or...? (Please don't say 'all of 
>these') Some people think of books as things with a history, like old 
>furniture.

These are exactly the sort of questions the author of an ontology of 
semiotics should ask, and answer.  That they have not been answered in this 
discussion group should not suggest that they can't be answered 
satisfactorily.  Since you've asked, let me attempt an answer.  I'll call 
on Ian to correct any erroneous connections that I may make with the merged 
ontology (given below as a term with an initial capital).

A book is a physical object with a lifetime - subclass of VariableObject
A book is related to the "conceptual work" which is the information content 
of the text in the book.
A conceptual work is a ContentBearingObject (it may more specifically be a 
Sign).
A conceptual work has temporal extent, at least one author, and no physical 
extent.
A particular coding of the conceptual work is also a Sign.
A book contains a particular coding of the conceptual work.
