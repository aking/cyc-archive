>Occurents and continuants:
>
>I'm certainly new at this, but I was becoming comfortable with the idea
>that occurents depend necessarily on contintinuents  (my life depends on
>me),
>but that the continuents are not parts.  (I am not part of my life).
>Mary's smile
>depends on Mary, but isn't part of Mary.   The stabbing of Ceasar is a
>stabbing
>(occurent) that depends on Ceasar, Brutus, and a certain knife, (all
>continuants),
>but these aren't  _parts_ of the specific stabbing.     This seems quite at
>odds
>with much of the continuant-occurent discussion I see.

It shouldnt be, I think you have all that dead right. Those things 
are participants in the stabbing, but not PARTS of it (except in a 
rather extended sense which ought to have scare-quotes round it.)

>I've been away most of January and February till now, so I've certainly not
>read
>everything in this thread, but the idea of an occurant being a _part_ of a
>continuent
>bothers me.

Yes, it should, you are right. An occurrent cannot be PART of a 
continuant (though the reverse is possible, some claim.) A brief 
stage of an occurrent might be coextensive with a part of a 
continuant at a time, of course (eg consider the stage in the 
lifetime of my liver happening *right now*), but it wouldnt BE that 
part.

>I'm not sure if this makes me a non-extensionist...

Well, if you are willing to countenance the cont/occ. distinction 
then you are a spatiotemporal non-extensionist already (modulo the 
point that Nicola makes about
this not requiring a nonextensional *language*.)
