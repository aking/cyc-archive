David, I have that hand-waving feeling again,
so let me go back and try to address a couple
of things:  the reasons why the cactus language
is really clearer, more efficient, and simpler
for human & machine alike, as far as prop calc
goes, anyway, and then some aspects of formal
representation in fol-de-rol and its ilk that
have nothing to with my own way of desiring
to re-configure it.

DW = David Whitten
JA = Jon Awbrey

DW: I am trying to figure out how the cactus language
    that you (Jon) are proposing is going to provide
    what normal First Order Predicate Logic doesn't.

DW: If I want to say "all cats have a tail" something like:

DW: Forall c
       such that c is an instance of Cat
    There exists a t
       such that t is an instance of Tail
    There exists h
       such that h is a particular tuple/relational-instance of 'has'
    There exists no more than one h
       whereby h expresses the connection between c and t

It's always hard to tell about artificial examples,
but the first question that you have to decide is
what level of information you really care about.
That is a question of application or purpose,
and no matter what anybody tries to sell you,
there is just no "one-size-fits-all" formalism
that fits every purpose that might be conceived.

Do you really care about vertebrate anatomy?
Or is this merely a "tail-bearing" feature
that could be expressed as a proposition?

I used to know folks who worked in the research arm of a medical school --
was the anatomy wing under the research arm?  yes, it was! -- and they
specialized in a field called "numerical morphology".  I know a little
about how they represented their datasets, and they would regard your
statement about cat's tails to be an act of gross anatomical vaguity.
The minimal adequate data set for them would be something that could
generate a function from species and breeds of animals to the mean
lengths of their tails, if any.  Do you really care about that?

The point of this tale is:  Why use a bigger gun
than you need for the beast that's in your sights?

Perhaps "Cat => Ourophorous" is all you really need here?

Okay, beside the point, maybe, since we both know that there are cases
where you can't really avoid quantificational and relational notions,
but the point remains, even then, that it's better to tease these
factors apart in one's "ontology utility software" (OUS), and to
have a sense of what subtools you need for the task at hand.

And a lot of the really tough inference work that gets done these days
leans heavy on a technique of "quantifier elimination" to do the job.

Something to think about.

DW: Now this is a rough way of expressing this.
    How would the cactus language make this expression

    1)  clearer
    2)  more compact
    3)  less ambiguous
    4)  more powerful

    or any other qualifier you would want to examine?

DW: Right now, the cactus language looks like a lot of syntax
    to memorize with no discernable advantage in expressing
    common facts about the world to a computer.

"Talking to Humans" -- Do you get the Canadian TV show "22 Minutes"? --
