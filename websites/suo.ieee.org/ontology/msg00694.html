Hi Jon

There is indeed a pattern, and it indicates a well-known and rather 
elementary point about use and mention, viz. that quoting a string 
gives an expression which, when used, mentions the string itself; 
that is, in other words, that quotation creates a name for a thing 
out of that very thing; provided of course that the thing in question 
is the kind of thing that can be rendered linguistically, ie a string 
of symbols. This doesnt help us much however with the interesting 
questions, which tend to arise before one even gets 'up' (in the 
sense in which metalanguages are 'higher' than object languages) to 
the use of quotation.  To give an example, your second line above is 
highly problematic:

  "x"        is a sign that denotes x         (in the real world).

Really? And what is "x (in the real world)" exactly? No. Variables, 
unlike names, do not denote anything in the real (or any other) 
world. At best they assert, implicitly, an existence; but they do not 
denote. They play a syntactic role of binding a quantification to a 
relation position, and their model-theoretic semantics requires some 
delicate handling for just this reason (it was one of the very few 
things that even Alonzo Church once got wrong).

You yourself, two lines later, use the very same variable *as a variable*:

  "R"        is a sign that denotes {<x, y> : <x, y> in R}

What you mean, I take it, is that "R" denotes the set of all pairs 
<x,y> which are "in R". Let me say that again: the set of ALL .... 
hear that quantification? Can you guess which variables get bound by 
that quantifier? But if "x" is a sign that denotes some actual thing 
in the real world, how did it just manage to un-denote that thing and 
denote everything else? (And it achieved this magical trick without 
even moving on the page! )

Confusing variables with names is very like confusing existence with 
a predicate (or even an identity relation), another well-known 
philosophical bear trap waiting to destroy the lives of careless 
wanderers in these woods.  I think you would do well to extend your 
reading from Pierce (God rest his noble American soul) to include a 
few writers on these topics from a more recent time. You could start 
with Tarski, who waxed lyrical on the theme of not confusing a 
variable with a name, and then move on to ponder, with Quine, how 
many possible people are standing in an empty doorway.

Best wishes

Pat Hayes
