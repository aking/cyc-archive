Dear Graham,

Thank you for the query.  I regret that I am late in replying, but I am
able to give this project only limited time under a non-academic
("material interest") rubric. 

I see from your note that I have not been as clear as I would have hoped,
perhaps because the phrase 'reference object' is jargon. It does not stand
for a contentless SUO, however.  To the contrary, it is a way of
facilitating a rich content by sidestepping inevitable areas of
philosophical and methodological difference.  To ask that the final
product be seen as a reference object is to assure that the content be
developed with self-contextualization and self-limitation-- placing its
own design choices in a broader context of methods and specifications.
This is a stronger requirement than internal documentation within the
standard or annexes to it.  The requirement of self-limitation means a
recognition that whatever SUO is developed will be, as Fred Chase has
kindly put it for me in his list of "show-stoppers", neither more nor less
than an "example". If the reference object/example is developed in this
spirit of self-limitation and specification it can be quite useful, and
people can be helpful in developing it without having to commit themselves
to the particular choices made.  On the other hand, if they are asked to
subscribe to design choices of one artifact as if they were the only
reasonable ones, progress would be difficult on the open forum model that
has been chosen;  instead the protagonists of the preferred method would
have to move explicitly to a consortium of like-minded collaborators, or
to try to achieve a similar result by a self-discrediting process of
parrying highly sophisticated methodological and philosophical
reservations with suggestions that the critics be "more constructive" and 
confine themselves to marginal notes on the document at hand. 

To my mind, the weight of experience ('best/existing practice') in the
ontology area argues strongly for a reference object/example approach, and
it is no accident that by my reading nearly all on the NO vote list
and quite a few YES and ABSTAIN voters have tried to suggest that issues
of method be addressed independently of the proposed editing effort on the
proposed "base"  document. 

Frederick Chase has also provided off-line an example of 'Common Criteria'
in securitization protocols that suggest how components of an ontology and
their dependencies can be explicitly pre-engineered to permit users to
rearrange and disable whole sections to serve varying purposes.  

Of course, the difficulty with a reference object approach is that it
requires, concurrent with work on the object/example, a survey of methods
and artifacts that does not now exist, so that it is an "academic" task
that the standard of material interest will not produce without direct
funding well beyond that which sustains the current effort.

Again,  my thanks for your query.


Best,

Lee


PS You asked also, more generally, in a separate email about Cyc's upper
level. My understanding is that the axioms and microtheories that work
around the inevitable inconsistencies are proprietary and that the bare
upper level is of limited use without them.  Theproblem of proprietary
enhancements may also affect other artifacts produced under a commercial
discipline, and I do not believe that the bare IEEE rules on copyright and
patent disclosure adequately anticipate *all* the problems of an upper
level ontology reference object. Note also that the Cyc ontology is honed
for inference not search-- I vaguely (mis?)remember a retrieval rate of
2-5% during HPKB-- so that the issue of "Practical Purpose" raised by
Chris Partridge would have a real bearing on whether a user would wish to
use Cyc or some other upper level.

Lee
