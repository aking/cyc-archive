Hi David.  You wrote:
> In the ongoing dicussion between Pat and Ian, I have decided to
> attempt to slice a tiny part of their issues and see if we can start a
> separate thread on the issue of axioms for granularity.  
> ...
> Now it seems to me that this change from heap-ness to non-heapness
> is very similar to the change from countable sets to finite sets.

I really don't think so.  The heap paradox (in my view) depends on the
vagueness of the predicate "heap".  Heaps change gradually to non-heaps,
and there is no fact of the matter about when the change takes place;
there is a grey area where the meaning of "heap" is not precise enough
either obviously to apply or obviously to fail to apply to a given
plurality of things.

There is no corresponding vagueness in the notions of the finite and the
countably infinite.  The distinction is completely precise.  (Well, I
suppose there is the odd sort of in-between notion of Dedekind
finiteness that arises when you don't assume the axiom of choice, but
even that is sharply defined, if unintuitive.)  

> According to my understanding, it is impossible to say that
> a set is finite using only First order predicate logic.

Wellll, yes and no.  You can in fact give a pretty robust definition of
finiteness in first-order set theory, and it picks out exactly the
finite sets in any so-called *intended* model of set theory.  The
problem, if it is a problem, is that you can't rule out unintended
interpretations that fool your set theory into thinking that certain
infinite sets satisfy the definition of finiteness.  This is an inherent
limitation of first-order languages.  The same is true of the concept of
number -- there is no way to axiomatize the concept of number so as to
rule out nonstandard models, models that contain things other than
the non-negative integers in the extension of "number".  But that
doesn't keep us from finding our theories of number very useful.  The
same holds of "finite".  We know what our intended models look like (or
so I would argue) even if our first-order languages are too expressively
weak to pick them out uniquely.  Hence, those languages remain useful.

> So approaching this from that perspective would be ill-advised.

Yes, but for the reason above, not for the reason you suggest.
 
> The change-over from heap-ness to non-heap-ness seems to be 
> related to 'size'. When the size of the heap gets within a
> a few orders of magnitude of the size of the granules, 
> the change-over occurs.

As long as you aren't suggesting there is some definite point at which
that change-over occurs.

> So what seems to be necessary for the axioms
> is a general idea of a heap of something,
> a general idea of the size of the heap of something,
> a general idea of the granule of something,
> a general idea of the size of the granule of something,
> a general idea of order of magnitude between sizes,
> and the idea of adding a granule to a heap, or removing one.
> 
> So what are the problems with this process of axiomizing?

All the problems associated with vagueness!  And probably more besides.
