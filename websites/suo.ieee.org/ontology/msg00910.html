Nicola, & All,

There is a classical distinction between the use
of the term "particular" and the use of any such
terms as "atomic", "individual", "instance", etc.
I am sure that you are fully versed in this, and
have but transiently forgotten or glossed over it,
but since this distinction was of great import to
Leibniz and was later revived to some purpose by
our homespun logician C.S. Peirce, and because I,
for one, among the still living, have great need
of observing it, I am anxious to take some pains,
mine and yours both, no doubt, if you will just
be tolerant of it, to point it out yet again.

Given that this particular "splitting of hairs"
has arisen before in our conversations locally,
the easiest thing for me to do is just to link
to an individual instance of a former occasion:

http://grouper.ieee.org/groups/suo/email/msg02362.html

Now, I know that this may seem like too old a reference
to be of relevance to our modern contexts of discussion,
but I have found, quite surprisingly to myself at first,
that it exhibits an almost prophetic bearing in respect
to all of those very sorts of questions that we have of
late arisen to salute under the vexillum of information
theory, for instance, our increasing acknowledgement of
the significance that our own channel capacity may just
not be as nigh unto infinity as we were once accustomed
to believe, and what this means about our practical aim
to speak of individuals, but sensibly, when all that we
can determine in practice is a particular approximation
to them, and so on, and so forth, as this is no novelty,
I am sure that everybody hereabouts can supply the rest.

Until Then,

Jon Awbrey
