>PH> But theres a deeper misunderstanding here. When you say 
>'computer language'
>do you mean 'programming language' (which is only even called a 'language'

>GH>	Whether it's one or the other makes very little conceptual
>difference. The concept is that each element has a precisely defined
>interpretation. Note that I did NOT say "a precisely defined
>implementation".

No, I hear the difference, but I still think you are wrong. A formal 
ontology language DOESNT provide a precise, unambiguous meaning for 
all its terms. (In fact, one can often prove quite rigorously that a 
truly unambiguous meaning is impossible to achieve using any of a 
wide class of formal logics, using some variation of Godel's theorem. 
)

There is a notable contrast here between logics and programming 
languages, and it comes out clearly in their formal semantic 
theories. Programming language semantics typically relies on the 
recursion theorem to guarantee the existence of *unique* minimal 
fixedpoints of recursive constructions. Ontology languages are 
usually not even able to express finiteness unambiguously, and the 
second recursion theorem doesnt apply to assertional logics, in 
general. This is the price they pay for having a semantics which 
claims to be able to talk about anything, not just the things that 
can be finitely constructed by mechanaical opoerations inside 
computing engines.

>Because KIF and CE only specify the logical framework within which the

>GH> 	Yes, you've picked me up validly here. I should have said: ".....

It depends what you mean by 'unambiguous'. I think maybe we will be 
able to achieve workable communication, just as human beings do all 
the time; which really means something like, we havnt found anything 
to disagree about yet. But that kind of 'unambiguous' communication 
always has to be open to further negotiation in order to resolve 
misunderstandings which might only surface later.  If you really mean 
full semantic unambiguity, which would be in model-theoretic terms 
there being a unique model, then one can prove that this is almost 
always unobtainable. Just having us both use Peano arithmetic is 
enough to guarantee that this couldnt be possible.

>GH> 	I am NOT saying any language will be able to achieve perfect

Yes, and these changes are often described as 'scientific

foundations and starting over, a long and painful process usually 
taking more than one human generation.

>GH> 	However, I am suggesting that a basic structure can be developed to
