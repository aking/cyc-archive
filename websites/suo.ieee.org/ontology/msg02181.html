>1. My understanding is that the proposal is to have an import `statement'
>(import url formula)
>
>2. To allow references within the formula to other ontologies O#ref

The # notation is not used to refer to other ontologies. It is purely 
a lexical device to avoid accidental clashes of namespaces, where the 
same symbol is acccidentally used in two different ontologies to 
refer to the different entities.
(Since posting the original suggestion, I have been persuaded that 
this #-prefixing device should be optional rather than compulsory. 
However I havent worked out a detailed proposal for a syntax which 
allows it to be optional yet.)

>Some comments:
>
>1. Programming languages have dealt with this problem with varying 
>degrees of success. There is actually not much that is specific to 
>ontologies per se here

There are some issues special to ontologies, since the logical 
semantics treats identifiers somewhat differently. But I agree with 
your overall point.

>2. An expression such as O#ref is quite hard to give a semantics for.

No, it is easy. As far as the logical language is concerened, # has 
no semantics. A string containing # is just another identifier and it 
denotes in the usual way. The 'semantics' is all at the purely 
lexical level.

>Consider the reasonable version: fun(Arg)#ref. Yes, this will come 
>up in practice.

If it does, it will be rejected as ill-formed by a KIF parser. It 
consists of five lexical items (in order: fun, (, Arg, ), #ref) and 
does not parse to a well-formed KIF expression. What is it supposed 
to mean?

>Does the O part have an independent interpretation? (op.ed. It 
>should if you want to make life easier for yourself)

No, it does not. The string preceding the #  is simply used as a 
prefixing device to ensure lexical separation of two vocabularies. 
(See earlier comment on making this nonobligatory.)

>3. A simple story looks like this:
>
>A micro theory (sic Cyc) is a tuple of predicates and other items.
>An item may be named: foo:item
>
>(Yes, this does smack of higher order; but at least its explicit)
>
>Exp#foo refers to the item named foo in the tuple of named items in 
>Exp. More generally, you could allow foo to be an expression: foo is 
>evaluated in the context of Exp (much like a letrec)

You could, but that would be a major extension and would take us into 
much deeper waters, involving 'contexts'.

>import(url) refers to the contents of the expression located in the url.

My 'imports' doesnt refer to anything. It is a form, not an 
expression. It simply stands for a set of sentences.

>So, a full import of an ontology looks like:
>
>import("htttp:.....")#formula
>
>where the interpretation of this is analogous to the LET
>
>LET x1:pred1
>       x2:pred2
>      ...
>     IN formula
>
>which should be familiar.

Actually it is not (and I don't quite see the analogy). Is this a 
function call? If so, I think this is not at all similar to what I 
was proposing. That would require the 'imported' ontology to retain 
its identity as a subtheory, so that 'evaluation' could be performed 
relative to that subtheory. That is not what I had in mind: I meant 
'import' to be simply a notationally handy way of, in effect, copying 
a bunch of sentences into ones ontology without actually writing 
them. Once included, their status as a member of the imported set 
plays no further role in the inference processes of the importing 
ontology.
