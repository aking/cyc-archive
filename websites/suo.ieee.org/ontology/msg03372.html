Howard,

This object was chosen precisely on account of its familiarity to most folks
of a technical persuasion.  This means that you will already have a sense that
there is an abstract object of some sort -- or maybe just that it makes some kind
of sense to speak "as if" there existed such an object -- whose points are denoted
or indicated by the propositional expressions, in whatever syntax, and what's more,
a mathematical object whose global structure is reflected in the order relations
among these syntactic expressions.  This kind of setting is fairly paradigmatic
of a whole lot of mathematical talk about the relations among (1) formal objects,
(2) models of real objects, processes, or situations, and (3) the structure that
is manifested in syntax and formal systems.  So it is helpful to look at a very
simple case where all of these aspects are present, but not yet so overwhelmimg
in its complexity that we cannot study exactly how they relate to each other.
A big part of my quest at present is to find ways of rationalizing or making
sense of the talk of several diverse communities of discourse, in particular,
computer scientists, engineers, logicians, mathematicians, philosophers, and
so on.  This has proven to be inescapable -- if you knew me, you would know
that I tried to avoid it -- in my more focal task to understand the relation
between dynamic and symbolic approaches to intelligent systems.  As might be
expected, most of the "fun", to invoke the euphemism, occurs in the zones of
interaction, where nomans have landed for long.  Now I realize that some of
the label languages may appear a bit byzantine, but these were worked out
over a period of twenty years, derived and extended from basic material
in Peirce's logical graphs, and they have been tailored to the task of
achieving both human conceptual and machine computational efficency.
Finally, it is also a "not entirely trivial" (NET) application area
for illustrating the power tools of the pragmatic theory of signs.

Jon Awbrey
