Note 109

¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

Now that a critically missive tray of my archetype galley
is in a well-temperatured, hot lead condition and on-line
for ready use, let me jump back a pace and pick up a few
bits of clues, loose threads, odds and ends, and sundry
other notions that I have been obliged by the straits
of time and circumstance to let fall hereabouts on
the shop floor.

To Wit:

1.  My thematical puzzlement, expressed in this wise:

| Thesis.  Mathematics and its applications in physics
| and the other special sciences are serving moderately
| successfully to describe the world as we know it today,
| while logic lags behind in a largely ineffectual state.
|
| Inquiry.  Why is this?
|
| 001.  http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg07248.html

2.  The reprise of Quine on a theme of Leibniz,
    which I break up into an ocatave of notes
    for the sake of a future re-composition.

| [a]  Where number is irrelevant, regimented
|      mathematical technique has hitherto
|      tended to be lacking.
|
| [b]  Thus it is that the progress of natural science
|      has depended so largely upon the discernment of
|      measurable quantity of one sort or another.
|
| [c]  Measurement consists in correlating our subject matter with the
|      series of real numbers;  and such correlations are desirable
|      because, once they are set up, all the well-worked theory
|      of numerical mathematics lies ready at hand as a tool
|      for our further reasoning.
|
| [d]  But no science can rest entirely on measurement,
|      and many scientific investigations are quite out
|      of reach of that device.
|
| [e]  To the scientist longing for non-quantitative
|      techniques, then, mathematical logic brings hope.
|
| [f]  It provides explicit techniques for manipulating
|      the most basic ingredients of discourse.
|
| [g]  Its yield for science may be expected to consist
|      also in a contribution of rigor and clarity --
|      a sharpening of the concepts of science.
|
| [h]  Such sharpening of concepts should serve both
|      to disclose hitherto hidden consequences of
|      given scientific hypotheses, and to obviate
|      subtle errors which may stand in the way
|      of scientific progress.
|
| Quine, 'Mathematical Logic', pages 7-8.
|
| Willard Van Orman Quine,
|'Mathematical Logic', Revised Edition,
| Harvard University Press, Cambridge, MA,
| 1940, 1951, 1981.
|
| 002.  http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg07256.html

3.  And this spectral series that haunts the place
    where I left off to play my play within a play,
    otherwise known as my dissertation:

| I'm going to make another run for the border
| between the quantitative and the qualitative
| styles of description.  It seems like a good
| idea to articulate the trip with a number of
| well-spaced "stagecoach stops" along the way,
| so let's try stations of the following types,
| treating them as instances of (L, X, X -> A):
|
| 1.  (L, R^k, R^k -> R),  R = real numbers
|
| 2.  (L, R^k, R^k -> N),  N = natural numbers
|
| 3.  (L, R^k, R^k -> U),  U = unit interval = [0, 1]
|
| 4.  (L, R^k, R^k -> B),  B = {0, 1}
|
| 5.  (L, B^k, B^k -> B),  B = {0, 1}
|
| Of course, the languages L that we find convenient
| for reckoning with the functions f : X -> A are
| likely to be different from case to case.
|
| Just by way of affording concrete realizations for the
| series of cases, imagine that we are passing through:
|
| 1.  A typical quantitative arrangement.
| 2.  A set of frequency distributions on a sample space.
| 3.  A set of probability densities on a sample space.
| 4.  A set of propositions about a sample space.
| 5.  The boolean functions on k boolean variables.
|
| A funny thing happens on the way from the quantitative to the qualitative picture.
| The values in the codomain A, as A passes from R to B, turn from being indicative
| of values that are valued for their own sake toward becoming values that approach
| the state of "pure indications", that is, values that possess informational value
| and nothing else.  And what exactly do these pure indications so purely indicate?
| To answer to that, I need to introduce the concept of a "functional fibre".  And
| while I'm addressing that concept, I might as well take care of a related bit of
| unfinished business that I have been putting off dealing with in the proper way.
|
| 043.  http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg07398.html

With this last note we had come to the very verge of sounding a line
of continuity between logic and mathematics and back again, but then
we lacked the where-with-all to vault the gap that gaped betwen them.
But now, I venture, we have the requisite tackle to make the attempt.

Think of a probability density p : X -> !U!
as compared with a proposition q : X -> %B%.

In this simile, X is either the "sample space" or
the "universe of discourse", as the case may be,
!U! is the "closed unit interval" [0, 1] c !R!,
where !R! is the real line, and %B% is again
the boolean space {%0%, %1%} = {false, true}.

The aspect of analogy to which I wish to attract your attention
is that the functional values in both cases are really intended
as indices of elements in the functional domain, that is to say,
as indicators, amplitudes of signal strength, attentional marks,
informational intensities, or more or less graded signs of them.

This the key to the gate on the bridge that I want to try to turn next time.

Jon Awbrey
