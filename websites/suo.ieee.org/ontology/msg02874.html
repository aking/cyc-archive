Bob,

Yes, that is the main theme of Chapter 6, the Knowledge Soup
chapter of the KR book:

> I'm delighted to hear you express this view.  It opens the way to
> dialogue with the humanities and social sciences that are concerned
> with the motivation of different choices of meaning. Each language
> game produces a more or less coherent perspective and more or less
> desired results. But they all must in the end be revealed as more or
> less colored by the motivations surrounding their origin and
> development.  The concepts of nuclear physics may not be deeply
> colored by the origin of some concepts in the A-bomb projects. But
> until nuclear physicists are prepared to eat atoms instead of
> sandwiches, one perspective on the world cannot be hegemonistic.
> Likewise, in NLP, I can ambiguate as fast as any computer can
> disambiguate.   Well... let me rephrase that.  Regardless of how
> accurate the rules encoded in an NLP processor are, they can be
> broken.

I wouldn't say that they are broken, but that they are specialized
to a specific application, which may or may not be the one that
was appropriate or intended.  So far, the only successful systems
for NL understanding have been in very specialized areas, such as
weather forecasts (e.g., the METEO system in Canada, which translates
automatically between English and French, but only for the game of
talking about the weather).

In any case, the question of determining which theory or language
game was intended is the main issue to be resolved in language
understanding.  Once you know what game is being played, everything
else falls into place.  Only then is it possible to formalize the
the appropriate patterns of relations.

John Sowa
