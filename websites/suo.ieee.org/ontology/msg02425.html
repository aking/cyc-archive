Robert, & All,

By way of converting this from a most likely adversarial and autopsical
proceeding into a potentially more productive and prospective discussion,
let us take it as an opportunity to discuss the obligatory and the optional
features that a near-ideal ontology language ought to afford its typical users.
I will take your list as an initial contribution toward that end.

First, let me suggest that we try to tease apart two different issues.
There are questions of the ideals, of "Wannabe-FOL" or "Wannabe-KIF",
that are very difficult to address, to critique, to pin down, or to
examine in any sort of concrete and reflective way.  Then there are
questions of so-and-so's implementation of the FOL or the KIF ideal
which can be hashed over very concretely, but when it comes to that,
what actual language and what real dialect would 'scape a whipping
in terms of how it fails to live up to its ideals, if not its very
own specification?  These are the poles between which the essential
electromotive tension of this inquiry are caught.

I am always hopeful of any chance to let bygones be bygones,
but I need to be honest in my criticisms.  When I first came
on board here, I was led to believe that the established order
would be open to feedback and to new directions -- but things
got said early on that disappointed me severely in this dream.
I get no indications that the situation has changed -- I wish
you better luck than I have had.

I will return to specifics, later on, maybe tomorrow.

Jon Awbrey
