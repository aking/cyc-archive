>On 17.05.2001 4:11 Uhr, "West, Matthew MR SSI-GREA-UK"
><Matthew.R.West@is.shell.com> wrote:
>
> >
> > Dear Chris and Adam,
> >
> > Of course some of these considerations are only relevant if you
> > have an ontology that allows class membership to vary over time
> > (e.g. not 4D).
> >
> > To me one of the attractions of 4D is the way that it simplifies
> > some of these questions.
>
>I'm not sure what the connection is between four-dimensionalism and classes.
>Are you talking about dealing with the problem of property change over time
>(thus with identity)?
>
>4D is not entirely unproblematic.  First off the main reason for adopting it
>is to preserve the utility of indescernibility of identicals in explaining
>identity across a change of parts over time.

That was not my main reason for adopting it. In fact I never even 
thought about identity of indiscernables. I adopted it because it 
seemed to be the only framework that made it possible to say what 
needed to be said.

>Doing that you get merelogical essentialism,

That is not forced on you by a 4d ontology. A single 3-d mereological 
sum may be a slice of any number of distinct 4-d histories. (If you 
mean to apply mereology to the 4-d histories themselves, that is one 
way to go, but it is only an option.)

>which is not a universally
>accepted thesis, especially since it runs counter to our modal intuitions
>that continuants might have parts other than what they have.

(1) There are no continuants in the 4-d ontology; and (2) the 4-d 
framework can be adapted to handle other possible worlds if you want 
to allow for other might-have-been possibilities, so it does not run 
counter to any modal intuitions, as far as I know; assuming of course 
that you have modal intuitions (I don't, myself.)

>Alternative endurantist views (4D is often called perdurantism and 3D + Time
>endurantism) are those defended by Peter Simons, among others.  On this
>view, the identity of a continuant is not bound up with the extension of its
>parts in 4D, but with a hierarchy of continuants superimposed over the parts
>and connected by a relation of constitution.

Yes, Simon's notion of a continuant is incompatible with the 4-d 
ontology, though it is possible to provide a 'neutral' view that can 
be translated into either of them.

>This is also the view
>advocated by Guarino in his work.  One disadvantage is a proliferation of
>individuals (I am different than the merelogical sum of my parts at any
>given time so I must be something else).

That is a different issue, orthogonal to the 4-d/endurantist 
distinction, that arises from Nicola's fondness for strictly applied 
criteria of identity.

>Of course there are several perdurantist responses to all these endurantist
>ways out of the problem.
>
>Personally, I find Simons' and Guarino's account compelling but the jury is
>definitely still out.

Personally, I find Simon's account close to incoherent. But the jury 
should stay out (or be dismissed). Niether view is correct or 
incorrect: they both have their uses. We need a way for them to 
coexist.
