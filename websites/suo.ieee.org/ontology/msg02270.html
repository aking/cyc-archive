Dear Chris and Pat,

> 
> On Fri, 20 Apr 2001, pat hayes wrote:
> > >
> > >I suppose there is a sort of general, metalinguistic 
> schema here that
> > >exhibits the pattern that any such relation will fit, 
> something like
> > >(ignoring the uniqueness clause for brevity):
> > >
> > >(forall (?X1 ... ?Xn)
> > >        (<=> (REL ?X1 ... ?Xn)
> > >             (exists (?e)
> > >                     (and (EVENT-TYPE ?e)
> > >                          (ROLE-1 ?e ?X1)
> > >                          ...
> > >                          (ROLE-n ?e ?Xn)))))
> > >
> > >But this is pretty useless, as the event types and corresponding
> > >roles for relations still have to be analysed out 
> piecemeal, relation
> > >by relation.  There's no general principle or algorithm.
> >
> > I protest: this IS a general algorithm, if properly stated. The key
> > is not to predicate 'event-type' of the ?e in question, but to
> > connect it to the original predication of REL, by mapping 
> REL from an
> > n-ary to a unary relation REL': ...
> 
> Pat, you misunderstand, and please do not place me in the 
> middle of all
> this triadicity nonsense.  I was only interested, first, in helping
> Matthew get clear about the logical form of what he was trying to
> assert.  

MW: And its appreciated.

> Second, I was interested in the general idea behind Matthew's
> attempt -- as a piece of *ontology* -- to flesh out certain 
> relations in
> terms of the occurrence of certain events.  Thus, with regard 
> to what I
> was doing -- as opposed to what you *thought* I was doing -- 
> the key is
> *exactly* the predication of "EVENT-TYPE" of the ?e in question.  The
> interesting question for me here was simply when, if ever, this is a
> fruitful thing to do, ontologically.  That is, when, if ever, is it
> fruitful to enlarge one's ontology to encompass events to 
> give a fuller
> ontological analysis of a given relation?  *That* is what has to be
> decided "piecemeal", that is, by the careful analysis of a class of
> relations.  

MW: And this is just the right reason for looking at this stuff. For
what it is worth, our conclusions have been that as in this case there
is always a benefit in challenging the constraints that are implied
by representing an activity by a relation. After doing this often
enough, and always finding some constraints that were innapropriate,
we concluded that it was just simpler to always represent the event
as a constant rather than a relation in the first place, for
consistency.
