Graham,

I fully understand the magnitude of the forces behind the
MS monopoly:

>While I agree with the thrust of your sentiments, and in any
>case vastly preferred the logicality of WordPerfect, surely the driving
>factor is that Microsoft has by far the bulk of the market, and many of us

>are required to use the software corporately provided and mandated by our IT

>people.

That is why I never to accept any .doc document without
registering a protest.  Acquiesence simply reinforces
a very unfortunate status quo.

>Where I do, and have previously, worked, use of
>non-corporate software is prohibited without special case acceptance of a
>business justification, and will be wiped from one's computer overnight if

>attempted. 

That is why I recommend HTML.  It is universally available.
Although the current browsers do not fully support the few
available features (such as Cascading Style Sheets v. 2)
which can help overcome some of its limitations, the world
(including the Redmond monopoly) is moving toward XML as
the replacement for proprietary formats.

>The fact is they have the market sewn up for the time being.
>I suggest this won't be overcome till serious analysis and response to the

>matter occurs at either international level, such as WTO or similar, or in

>the main economic groups, such as NAFTA, USA, EU, etc. 

There is something that everyone can do:

 1. Whenever possible, use HTML or plan text instead of .doc
    formats.

 2. Whenever you receive a document in .doc format, request
    an HTML or plain text form instead.

 3. Make anyone in a position of power fully aware of the fact
    that Microsoft is committed to making current .doc files
    obsolete -- that is the stated MS policy for .NET, which
    they are beginning to support with the beta version of
    Windows XP and its follow-on for 2002.

 4. Whenever an opportunity arises, recommend HTML or XML as
    the preferred format for documents, spread sheets, etc.

>My guess this won't happen for at least a half decade, no
>matter how irritated little people like us may be irritated by it.

Whenever you get irritated, do your best to ensure that someone
in a higher position of power feels the irritation (of course,
it is best to focus their irritation on the source, rather than
on you).

John Sowa
