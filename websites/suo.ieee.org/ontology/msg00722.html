Petty point:  It would probably be a bit more apt to attribute
this form of "arboreal thinking" to Plato.  My personal favorite
example can be found distributed throughout his 'Sophist' dialogue --
co-incidents?  I do not think so!  -- where one discovers, over time,
the burlesque bit that is batted back and forth and carried on between
the Stranger and Theaetetus expounding as it goes a roughly binary tree
of depth roughly 10.  But still, now that I am reminded of it, even here
the terminal leaves of the tree, at least, the ones that it is the whole
object of the exercise to ripen into fruition, if even to the point of
their overripe rot becoming thoroughly manifestered in the roasting
that the Sophist is being thrown -- these branches all retroserrate
back upon the person in question who is being celeberated in this
style.  So, I take it all back, Plato was far more sophisticated
than we commonly give him his "due-fors"!

But actually, that was not what I opened
this window to say, so let me move on.

> If you have a lattice this is not so.  Interestingly
> the shift from Aristotelian to Newtonian physics, the
> structure of the theory became more lattice-like, less
> tree-like.  And also more general and more 'traction'.
> (if anyone is interested this is a central theme of
> my book Business Objects).
>
> It seems to me that one of the overriding reasons
> for producing an ontology is to have something general
> with traction.  My book describes a method for taking more
> specific data models and constructing more general models with
> more (not less) traction.  In fact, one of the surprising aspects
> of my experience using this is that generality naturally results
> in more rather than less traction.
> 
> Regards,
> Chris

Chris, for those of us who cannot seem to catch up with our email
long enough to get away to a bookstore -- would you be willing to
e-laborate a little bit more on your notions of "sophistication"
and "traction"?
