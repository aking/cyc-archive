> MW:  Well "in the real world" it would be more than two columns,
> even if that was the conceptual model you started with.  Lets just
> run through what you might have on a table representing a relation.
> 
> ThingID
> 
> A surrogate for the relation instance, so that it can be
> referred to in other relations.  This is a practical point.
> In KIF you can just repeat teh whole relation whenever you
> want to refer to it.  In a relational database you can't put
> a whole relation in a column, so you have to put a reference
> to it there.

You are now at 1-adic relations.

I believe I mentioned that keys and indices count as columns.
In general, a k-place relation plus 1 key is a (k+1)-place relation.

> Referenced1
> 
> The first element in the relation.

You are now at 2-adic relations.

> Referenced2
> 
> The second element in the relation.

You are now at 3-adic relations.

> Relation
> 
> If you had noticed that all your relations were binary,
> after a bit you might notice that you had maybe 100 tables
> all with the same structure.  You would probably spot that
> for adding one column with the relation in it you could
> reduce that to 1 table.

You are now at 4-adic relations.

> RecordID
> 
> The record is an object as well as what it refers to, and in some
> circumstances it may be an object of interest. Providing a record ID
> gives a clean way to do this. Don't expect to see this except from
> people who take signs seriously.

You are now at 5-adic relations.

> Record Creation Date
> 
> The date the record was created. I am assuming here that the relation
> instance is timeless, but the record of it certainly isn't. As an
> alternative, you might keep records of system actions, in which case
> this could be dealt with elsewhere.

You are now at 6-adic relations.

> Record Logical Deletion Date
> 
> We would not generally physcally delete records,
> but logically delete them.  This means you can
> get back to a previous database state.

You are now at 7-adic relations.

> So what's that?  7 columns. Same principle though.

> MW: Well it's funny you should mention that ...
> 
> We didn't set any such rules, but we did end up with a data model
> with just about that structure, and when we took it to these folks
> (some 8 or 9 years ago now) they just looked at us exactly as you
> described and said that was impossible/impractical.  But as the good
> theoreticians we are, we asked them for the proof (evidence even) for
> their claims.  They replied that there was no proof or evidence for
> their claims, but that it was "obvious".  We suggested that a little
> experiment would settle the matter, and probably to humour us our
> colleagues agreed.  They even gave us help in doing the experiments.
> 
> Of course with the first experiment there were some little
> compromises at the edges, but do you know what, it actually
> did work.  Performance wasn't sparkling, but we could get
> things done.  We could do some neat stuff that having such
> a flexible structure made possible that "traditional" designs
> couldn't touch.  And it turned out things were mostly breaking
> down around the compromises we made.
> 
> So there were a number of other experiments, each pushing back
> on more and more assumptions, until we did end up with something
> looking pretty much like what I have suggested above, in what we
> called a "reduced table design" because there were fewer tables
> than entity types in the data model.  In fact one of our most
> successful experiments had just one table.
> 
> This gave us the basis of a database design where the design
> was independent of what you wanted to hold data about, i.e. in
> principle it could hold any data about anything, without changing
> the database design.  There are couple of areas where that flexibility
> can be extremely useful.  One of those is reference data, such as asset
> data where I have been working for some years now.  The other area is
> Management Information, a direction some of my colleagues took these
> ideas.  So $30m later, whilst I have been developing standards for
> engineering data, they have been developing a Management Information
> System called Kalido (think kaleidoscope).  This uses a meta data
> management approach to creating and managing MIS data warehouses.
>
> http://www.kalido.com/ for more information.
>
> Shell has implemented Kalido in more than 100 locations worldwide,
> and it provides the backbone of our management information.
> 
> Today the software is a V6, Kalido Ltd is established as
> a separate company (currently wholely owned by Shell) and
> Kalido is a product you can buy -- if you have deep enough
> pockets.  The product architect -- Chris Angus, is on this
> list and contributes occasionally.
> 
> So I guess it isn't all talk and theory.
