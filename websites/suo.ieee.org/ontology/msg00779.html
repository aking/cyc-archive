Jon,

I have changed the subject of this email thread to note that it is about a
specific subtopic, the role of semiotics in SUO, while my original email
thread was about more general topics, i.e. the need for requirements
specifications, requirements traceability, etc. in SUO.

To summarize your message, there seem to be at least the following
alternative perspectives:

1) semiotic concepts have no place in SUO, since ontologies are supposed to
be about things that exist, while semiotics is about signs for things that
exist.

2) signs are things, and so semiotic concepts could be included in an
ontology, as "yet another subject matter" of the ontology.

3) a theory of signs is fundamental to logic and to the language for the
ontology. So the language, and perhaps the ontology also, needs to be
developed based on a theory of signs.

The first two perspectives may easily be translated to a requirements
question: Should signs (and related concepts) be included in or excluded
from the ontology?

Can the third perspective be phrased in terms of concise, well-defined
requirements for either the ontology or its language? If so, what are these
proposed requirements? This is a question which everyone can address as they
choose, of course...

One answer to this question might be that KIF is already adequately based on
a theory of signs, since there is a mapping between KIF and Conceptual
Graphs, which are based largely on Peirce's existential graphs. It would be
interesting to hear some specific thoughts pro or con this point of view.

Cheers,

Phil Jackson
