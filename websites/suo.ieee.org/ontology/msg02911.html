John F. Sowa wrote:
> 
> Jon,
> 
> I agree with you that triadicity is very important.  But incessant
> repetition of that claim is counterproductive.  It is just causing
> people to set up filters to send your email notes directly to trash.

My purpose here is not to prove a claim about triadic irreducibility.

"Been there.  Done that.  Got the T-shirt 'n' everything."  ~~~ Bart Simpson

My purpose here is develop the application of some formal techniques
that arise out of the pragmatic theory of sign relations and to use
them toward one specific end, that of constructing a web framework
in which intercommunicability and interoperability might actually
be feasible and not just something that people prattle on about,
the way that they have always done about AI, until one of four
things up & happen:  (1) they realize that "gee, this is hard",
and drop it, (2) they get tired of their own repetitiveness,
(3) they discredit themselves with the utter futility of
their attempts, (4) the mainstream does what it usually
does, and drowns them all in their sleep, with the able
assistance of not few off-mainstream contributaries.
It's not like we have not seen it happen before.

> Recommendation:
> 
>  1. Organize your notes on this topic into an article
>     and contribute it as a paper on the SUO web server.
>     Then send one note to all and sundry about that paper.

Look, I am working the applications of these ideas in dialogue fashion.
It may be in dialogue with somebody I was talking to last week, last year,
or 30 years ago, that I am just now working out an answer to, or somebody
in the future, but dialogue it is.  I have already spent those 30 years
writing papers and reports that nobody will ever read.  Any day now
I will chuck this whole hassle, go back and finish my dissertation,
wrap it up in black leather on some library shelf, and you can all
read about it in 120 years or so, if ever, the way that it's always
been done before.  And I will have learned what I am already beginning
to suspect, that all of thes folks running around telling us how this
brave new web is going to catalyze that process and give us new ways
of speaking and working togteher, presnet company not excluded --
well, I've heard that one before, too.

>  2. Put together a reading list for the benefit of anyone who
>     would like to explore this topic further.  Send it to your
>     favorite email lists and post it on your web site or as a
>     contributed paper to the SUO web server.

This is new stuff, John.  The computational and extensional work-ups
of the sign-relational basis have been ignored by the philosophical
side of the Peirce community, and this whole field has lain fallow
since Peirce left it.

> An example of a short (100 page) book that people have found useful:
> 
>    John K. Sheriff, _Charles Peirce's Guess at the Riddle:
>    Grounds for Human Significance_, Indiana University Press, 1994.
>    100 pp.  $20.00 cloth; $9.95 paper.  (Amazon.com, by the way,
>    offers the paperback version for $12.95.)

I have this book.  It is a very nice, very gentle, very non-technical
popularization of a well-chosen sample of Peirce ideas.  Fine & dandy.
That has very little to do with what I am trying to do here on behalf
of the SUO effort, for the sake of developing the theory of signs, or
in pursuit of its yet untapped applications to problems of computation,
communications, and intelligent systems engineering.  Aside from that,
you and I both know the history of pragmatic thinking:  the likes of
James, Dewey, Morris, and others, to various degrees and all with the
very best of intentions, afforded extremely convenient straw-targets
for the likes of Russell, Carnap, Quine, and others to set afire or
just plain ignore.

> Following is the commentary by Nathan Houser
> that appears on the IUP web site:
> 
> As Emerson describes it in his essay "Nature,"
> the riddle that the Sphinx puts to every great
> thinker concerns the relation between mind and
> matter.  In this introduction to the thought
> of Charles S. Peirce, John K. Sheriff presents
> a philosopher who speaks to this fundamental
> question of the nature of human existence.
> In clear and concise prose, Sheriff describes
> Peirce's "theory of everything," a vision of
> cosmic and human meaning that offers a positive
> alternative to popular pessimistic and relativistic
> approaches to life and meaning.  Aimed at nonspecialists,
> this book does not attempt to evaluate every concept in
> Peirce's philosophy but instead shows how Peirce's analyses
> of aesthetics, ethics, logic, and human consciousness rest
> on the foundations of his grand theory of the cosmos, mind,
> and signs.  Sheriff convincingly demonstrates that Peirce's
> answer to the riddle of the Sphinx has the potential to
> be a powerful, positive force in contemporary culture.
> 
> For this summary and others about more specialized books on Peirce,
> see http://www.iupui.edu/~peirce/web/news/books/notesx.htm
> 
> John Sowa

What a lot of the expert commentators seem to forget is that
Oedipus already answered the "Riddle Of The Sphinx" (ROTS),
and if they think that the Sphinx was dispatched by this,
then they do not know the sound of Sphinx distress from
from Sphinx laughter, since the Sphinx knew then what
Oedipus was yet to find out, that the answer is
a bigger riddle than the question.

Jon Awbrey
