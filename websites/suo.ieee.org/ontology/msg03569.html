david, i see that some confusion below is due to purely orthographic considerations.
peirce gives a "careful" (= excruciatingly meticulous) discussion at the beginning
of his paper as to what typefaces he will use for what discriminating purposes,
but the editors of CP and CE apparently disagreed about what was "Kennerly"
and what was "Madisonian", so I will just revert forward to the convention
of modern mathematical monography and use 'italics' (= single quotes) for
formal symbols in text.  here is a cleaned-up copy of the text in view:

"A Simple Desultory Philippic"

| In reference to the doctrine of individuals, two distinctions should be borne in mind.
| The logical atom, or term not capable of logical division, must be one of which every
| predicate may be universally affirmed or denied.  For, let 'A' be such a term.  Then,
| if it is neither true that all 'A' is 'X' nor that no 'A' is 'X', it must be true that
| some 'A' is 'X' and some 'A' is not 'X';  and therefore 'A' may be divided into 'A' that
| is 'X' and 'A' that is not 'X', which is contrary to its nature as a logical atom.  Such
| a term can be realized neither in thought nor in sense.  Not in sense, because our organs
| of sense are special -- the eye, for example, not immediately informing us of taste, so
| that an image on the retina is indeterminate in respect to sweetness and non-sweetness.
| When I see a thing, I do not see that it is not sweet, nor do I see that it is sweet;
| and therefore what I see is capable of logical division into the sweet and the not sweet.
| It is customary to assume that visual images are absolutely determinate in respect to color,
| but even this may be doubted.  I know of no facts which prove that there is never the least
| vagueness in the immediate sensation.   In thought, an absolutely determinate term cannot
| be realized, because, not being given by sense, such a concept would have to be formed by
| synthesis, and there would be no end to the synthesis because there is no limit to the
| number of possible predicates.  A logical atom, then, like a point in space, would
| involve for its precise determination an endless process.  We can only say, in a
| general way, that a term, however determinate, may be made more determinate still,
| but not that it can be made absolutely determinate.  Such a term as "the second
| Philip of Macedon" is still capable of logical division -- into Philip drunk and
| Philip sober, for example;  but we call it individual because that which is denoted
| by it is in only one place at one time.  It is a term not 'absolutely' indivisible,
| but indivisible as long as we neglect differences of time and the differences which
| accompany them.  Such differences we habitually disregard in the logical division of
| substances.  In the division of relations, etc., we do not, of course, disregard these
| differences, but we disregard some others.  There is nothing to prevent almost any sort
| of difference from being conventionally neglected in some discourse, and if 'I' be a term
| which in consequence of such neglect becomes indivisible in that discourse, we have in that
| discourse,
|
| ['I'] = 1.
|
| This distinction between the absolutely indivisible and that which is one in number from
| a particular point of view is shadowed forth in the two words 'individual' ('to atomon')
| and 'singular' ('to kath ekaston');  but as those who have used the word 'individual'
| have not been aware that absolute individuality is merely ideal, it has come to be
| used in a more general sense.  (CP 3.93, CE 2, 389-390).
|
| Charles Sanders Peirce,
|"Description of a Notation for the Logic of Relatives,
| Resulting from an Amplification of the Conceptions of Boole's Calculus of Logic",
|'Memoirs of the American Academy', Volume 9, pages 317-378, 26 January 1870,
|'Collected Papers' (CP 3.45-149), 'Chronological Edition' (CE 2, 359-429).

[ Note on the square bracket notation that was used above:
|
| Peirce explains this notation at CP 3.65, cf: CE 2, 366.
|
| | I propose to denote the number of a logical term by
| | enclosing the term in square brackets, thus, ['t'].
|
| The "number" of an absolute term, as in the case of 'I',
| is defined as the number of individuals that it denotes.

DW: ok first off, if A is a term, it is a unit of syntax.
    then when you talk of 'all A is X' or you talk of 'some A is not X'
    I think you are mixing up syntax and semantics.

see orthographic excuses above.

DW: because A as a term can refer to individuals in the world.
    (Are these called particulars?) and it is those individuals
    that are X or not X.  the 'all' and 'some' quantifiers must
    semantically be referring to those individuals.

i will just say how i currently understand the issues here, and
leave it to another time to decide whether I am orthodox or not.

what things are, and the wise in what we wot them to be,
are often wrested apart, the one from the other, if not,
as is likely, or not, the latter being wrong altogether.

so there may be atomic individuals -- let's say there are --
it hardly matters, since our saying so does not give us
one whit more power to know them more particularly than
we actually do.

DW: This is in contrast to the use of quantifiers as syntax.
    In terms of syntax, I'm not sure how 'some' comes into play.
    but 'there exists' is like an infinite OR connective
    and 'for all' is like an infinite AND connective.

better to say "indefinite" than "infinite", but okay.

DW: since both AND and OR can connect truth-valued terms to make a new term,
    it is reasonable to allow 'there exists' and 'for all' to do so as well.

look, it's 1870.  quantifiers of the brand you describe are not invented yet.
in time you may come to understand why i read that statement very analogous
to saying "crack of the potency that you demand has not hit the streets yet".

peirce has a better idea here, one that makes quantifiers
look like a derivative, de-based, or trivial afterthought.
with respect to its "functionality" the progress of logic
is all downhill from here.  but i am thoroughly cognizant
of just how hard some habits of thought will be to break,
so i will have to defer the detox to a still future time.

DW: 'some' as a connective doesn't make sense to me, as there is no
    way to talk about the truth value of a term made up using 'some'.

DW: Unless you increase your truth-universe to include a bigger range of values,

    world would gain you.

you must understand that our primitive ancestors,

DW: If a term is not 'realized' == 'made real' in thought,
    then where can it be realized?  And while there is no limit
    to the number of possible predicates, the whole POINT of the
    standard upper ontology is to try to come up with a limited,
    useful set, with clear definitions, useful for this synthesis.

look, it's 1870.  peirce discovered a thing he calls "information theory"
just barely five years ago.  that puts him only about 136 years ahead of
most folks today -- in or out of the suo group.

what peirce is saying is that an "absolutely determinate term",
qua "absolutely determinate term", is not realized anywhere,
not for fallible and mortal finite information creatures
like us.  if suo thinks that it needs them to work,
then suo is sorely confused about what works.

a finitary logical system may give us "individual names" -- but --
it will not give us "names of individuals", not in any true sense.
