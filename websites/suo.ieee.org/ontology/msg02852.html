I had reached the point of discussing Item A.1.a
in the outline of this story about the 3-adic POV.
Actually, it is better to do a compare & contrast
type of exercise between A.1.a & A.1.b, as these
two options typically present themselves as the
first decision that one has to make before one
can carry on any particular investigation of
sign processes.

By way of forging an application to current discussions
on several other threads, let us consider the following
paradigm of translation data between English and French.

¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

| Olivier's Twist on the Tower of Hanoi
| 
| disk 1  =  rose   =  pink    =  tiny topmost disk
| disk 2  =  vert   =  green
| disk 3  =  jaune  =  yellow
| disk 4  =  noir   =  black   =  large bottom disk

¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

Let us focus on the color words.

If one is working in a purely monolingual context of discussion,
then one could represent the "denotative" or the "referential"
aspect of sign use in this color fragment by means of a 2-adic
table that lists the 2-tuples of the form <o, s>, where o is
the object, in this case a color, that is, a certain kind of
attribute value, feature value, intension, property, quality,
among just a few of the many terms that are commonly used,
while s is a sign, a term, or a larger expression that is
said to "denote" or to "refer to" the object o.  In my own
way of speaking, I would call the relation between objects
and signs the "semantic" component of a sign relation, but
that use is likely to be conflicted in the present context
of discussion, so I will try to specify the "denotative"
of the "referential" option each time that I mention it.

Let us first contemplate 2-adic denotation relations in which
the sign domain S is restricted to English color nomenclature.

Here is one version of an appropriate denotation relation:

¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Object       | Sign         |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| pink         | "pink"       |
| green        | "green"      |
| yellow       | "yellow"     |
| black        | "black"      |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤

Here is another version of a suitable denotation relation:

¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Object       | Sign         |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| rose         | "pink"       |
| vert         | "green"      |
| jaune        | "yellow"     |
| noir         | "black"      |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤

What gives!?

Well, what we are really doing here is having a discussion
about a fragment of English usage and I have drawn a table
by way of illustrating this discussion.  In other words,
the object of our present discourse is this restricted
fragment of English usage -- some folks would call it
a "meta-discussion", but I have seen what this does
to people who get too high on it, so let's not --
and I have used the iconic figure of a table as
a complex sign to denote a piece of this data.

There is a lot more that could be said about this,
and a big chunk of my dissertation does just that,
but all that we need to take away for the moment
is the idea that the signs that we place in the
Object column of this complex tabular meta-sign
come from our casual, informal, indefinite, or
unrestricted "meta-discussion", and thus they
can be anything that we like.  Only the signs
of signs that we place in the Sign column are
being placed under rigorous formal controls.

What eventually develops is that we will abstract
over "all posssible elections" (APE's) of filling
for the Object column, using a technique that is
a generalization of the tactic that is regularly
employed in category theory, by which constructs
can be defined in terms of "universal properties".

¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~OUTLINE~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

A.  What is this triadic relational POV, anyway?
    1.  Explain it in terms of 3-adic relation tables.
        Suppose one is given a 2-adic table of objects and signs.
        a.  Option (a).  Add a column for the interpretive agent.
        b.  Option (b).  Add a column for the interpretant sign.
    2.  Aristotle's Approximation to the full-fledged sign relation:
        a.  Assumption (a).  Commonality in the object domain.
        b.  Assumption (b).  Commonality in the interpretant domain.
        c.  Conclusion (c).  Canonically privileged frame of reference.
B.  Bearing of the sign relational POV on several design issues:
    1.  Ontological Integrity versus Ontological Modularity.
    2.  Ontological Invariance versus Ontological Relativity.
    3.  Interoperability among ontological frames of reference.
    4.  Differential topics.
        a.  Change.
        b.  Development.
        c.  Diversity.
        d.  Evolution.
        e.  Inquiry.
        f.  Learning.
    5.  Critical Reflection.
    6.  Dynamics of Inquiry.
        a.  Informational dynamics.
        b.  Psychological dynamics.
        c.  Sociocultural dynamics.
