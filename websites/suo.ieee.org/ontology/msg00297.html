I will respond to Q3, and leave Peirce to the experts.

>
>1. What are the papers that discuss the relationship between Tarski's
>semantics [attached PDF] and Peirce's semiotics
>[http://www.clas.ufl.edu/users/jzeman/peirces_theory_of_signs.htm]?
>2. More specifically, can we relate (identify in some sense) model-theoretic
>interpretations with sign relations:
>    interpretation corresponds to interpretant
>    constant, function, predicate symbols -- c, f, p -- correspond to signs
>    interpretation images -- I[c], I[f], I[p] -- correspond to objects
>3. In particular, what is the nature of the interpretation image I[c]? Can
>this be a "real world object", notwithstanding what John says below, in the
>World part of the diagram
>[http://www.bestweb.net/~sowa/ontology/mthworld.gif], or does it have to be
>a "surrogate" existing in some formal semantic domain? For example, in the
>sentence "John Sowa is less than ten feet tall", can I not define an
>interpretation that maps the string "John Sowa" to the actual individual
>within the domain of actual individual people, or do I need to interpret it
>as some surrogate such as the email address sowa@bestweb.net?

John and I argued about this for years, but the answer seems to me to 
be quite clear: yes, it can be a real world object. The Tarskian 
model theory requires that the universe of discourse be a nonempty 
set. That is (quite literally) ALL it requires: any set will do. In 
particular, it can be a set *of anything*. It might contain cows or 
molecules or gas clouds or human histories or electrons or flowers or 
notions of beauty or anything you want it to contain. Of course, it 
could also contain 'surrogates' such as symbols or integers, as well, 
and there is a kind of sense in which John's substantive point is 
well taken: there is nothing in the model theory which can possibly 
*guarantee* that the universe of an interpretation shall contain 
(say) cows rather than cow-surrogates. But there is nothing that 
*forbids* it, either.

Now, this doesnt make it impossible to discuss what John calls the 
'real issues'; but I concede, it doesnt go very far towards solving 
those issues either. It is simply agnostic about them.
