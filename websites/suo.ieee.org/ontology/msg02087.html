Tim,

I agree that LaTeX is certainly better than any of the
available word processors.  It is also one of the output
formats generated by DocBook.  The advantage of XML (which
the basic DocBook format) is that it can be automatically
translated to many other forms (of which LaTeX is the version
used for printing).

>Again, your history is interesting and makes valid points.  I am just
>surprised that you did not mention Latex.

My major complaint about LaTeX is that Leslie Lamport didn't do
his homework.  GML already existed, and instead of inventing
his own personal language, he could have written a translator
from GML to TeX.  If he wanted to give himself a plug, he could
have dropped the G for Goldfarb and called his version LaML.

The best part of LaTeX is that it is approximately a subset
of SGML.

The worst part of LaTeX is that it is different from SGML
for no good reason.

>... However, I will
>restate my previous thought:  the functionality that we appear to be
>agonising over is collaborative working.  Does anyone have a vision and a
>specific set of tools to deliver such working for all participants to use?

>I am encouraged that we have some discussion on the process, as I believe
>that we should have an appropriate process as the foundation of the vision

>for our working methods.

I agree that this is extremely important.  HTML made one
important step toward collaboration, originally for the benefit
of physicists, but it expanded rapidly to fill a vacuum.  The
next step is to get tools that can help organize and maintain
the enormous volume of contributions we are getting from SUO.
