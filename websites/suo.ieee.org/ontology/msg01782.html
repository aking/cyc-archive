Matthew,

Yes, for classical FOL the distinction doesn't make
a difference:

>So the reason I have not been able to distinguish inference and entailment

>is that in FOL there is none. Correct?

However, the distinction is very important when you are
proposing new proof procedures or versions of logic that
differ in some way from classical FOL.  The rules of inference
must be verified by using semantic tests based on truth
conditions to check soundness and completeness.

Boole did so for propositional logic, and Peirce and Tarski did
so for predicate logic.  Frege, however, protested that truth
was too vague a concept to be used as a foundation for logic,
and Russell followed his lead.

I believe that it is a very serious mistake for so many
logicians (such as Michael Dummet) to spend a lifetime in
studying Frege while completely ignoring Peirce.  Frege was
certainly a brilliant logician, but so was Peirce.  And in
a great many ways, Peirce had a deeper and broader foundation
for logic and semiotics that is more appropriate for philosophy,
linguistics, and artificial intelligence.

John Sowa
