Here is a sample data set, represented in propositional logic.
This data comes from a "real live" and major funded research study
on parent-child interactions.  I was allowed to use a small portion
of this data to test out the qualitative data analysis software that
I wrote as one part of my Master's work in Quantitative Psychology.
I have been meaning to trot this out ever since Howard raised the
issue of "logic and data entry" a while back, but I think that it
now acquires a measure of newfound relevance in connection with
the issues of "empirical content in logic" that John and I have
been going round about lately under this heading.  To save me
the effort of writing a new seque for this piece, here is how
I introduced this way of looking at "sequential interactions"
to the Conceptual Graphs List a few months back:

¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~ARCHIVE~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

When I was passing through the field of psychology,
I eventually came to write a program, "Theme One"
by name, that was designed to integrate a number
of prototypical aspects and selected functions
of inductive and deductive reasoning by basing
them on a "common denominator", as I called it,
that is to say, the LUB in an implicit lattice
of abstract graph-theoretic data structures --
but a surprising side-effect of this work was
that I learned a whole lot about the reasons
why "empircists" and "rationalists" have so
much trouble understanding each other, and
even when, or especially when, it is the
divergent sides of the very same thinker
that one discovers to be at issue here.

The deductive functions of my program are implemented
via a logical interpretation of the above-mentioned
abstract data structure that I developed from the
Alpha Layer of Peirce's Existential Graphs.

The inductive functions are built around a core utility
that consists of a "two-level formal language learner",
and which I applied at the time to problems in protocol
analysis and sequential data sets, while working within
a qualitative research paradigm.  Here is an example of
the sort of dataset that I used it to explore and model,
presenting what follows in three parts:

1.  The codebook that was used to code the videotaped sessions.

2.  A raw coded data set of a single family interaction session.

3.  A transcription of the raw data set into a logical summary,
    consisting of a single proposition in the following form:

    (( j1_f# j2_c# j3_q# j4_v#  k1_f3 k2_c1 k3_q2 k4_v3
    )( j1_f3 j2_c1 j3_q2 j4_v3  k1_f1 k2_c1 k3_q2 k4_v3
    )( j1_f1 j2_c1 j3_q2 j4_v3  k1_f3 k2_c4 k3_q2 k4_v3
    )( ...
    )( j1_f1 j2_c1 j3_q2 j4_v3  k1_f3 k2_c1 k3_q2 k4_v3
    ))

This is a disjunction of conjunctive expressions, interpreted to describe
a set of ordered pairs, where each pair codes the state transition of the
parent-child dyad by telling what was happening at the first event of the
transition followed by what was happening at the second event of the pair.
The "j?_*" and "k?_*" codes describes the 1st and 2nd events, respectively.

Accordingly the above fragment says something like this:

| EITHER  f#_start  c##_start             v#_start           AND THEN
|         f3_mother c12_talk              v3_neutral_affect
|     OR  f3_mother c12_talk              v3_neutral_affect  AND THEN
|         f1_child  c12_talk              v3_neutral_affect
|     OR  f1_child  c12_talk              v3_neutral_affect  AND THEN
|         f3_mother c42_command_ambiguous v3_neutral_affect
|     OR  ...       ...                   ...                AND THEN
| ...
|     OR  f1_child  c12_talk              v3_neutral_affect  AND THEN
|         f3_mother c12_talk              v3_neutral_affect
| END OR.
