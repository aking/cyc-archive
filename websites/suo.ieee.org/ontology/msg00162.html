Re:  "Signs, Information, Logic, Inquiry"

SILIgists,

Permit me to permute the terms of our title into what strikes me
as an incre-mentally more logical order, not to mention affording
us a more euphonious -- Lot 49, again!? -- acronym.  At my age,
and in this season, and with the quantity of snow piled around
our door today, I do not wish to be reminded of the ills that
our im/material flesh is heir to, not right now, at any rate.

I also want to clear away a few other matters of the purely-driven
flakey variety of terminological nuisance before I try to return
to the main thoroughfare of this SILIgistic discourse.

Notational Mutata:

1.  I just now realized that you probably cannot be assured
    of seeing the boldness in the eye of this beholder when
    I look upon the "B" of B = {0, 1} fame, as distinguished,
    by me, from the "B" of {A, B} that I see more plainly as
    a set of variable names.  So a "change of variables" is
    the first order of business -- let me use the set {U, V},
    or even {u, v} as the conventional variables of choice in
    the examples to follow.  This leaves {X, Y} to their more
    generic duties, as will suit the pickwickier mathematicians
    among us.

2.  Being snowed-in today, I think that I will entertain myself,
    if not many others, with drawing out all sixteen pictures
    of the propositions on two variables, corresponding to
    the boolean functions of the form f : BxB = B^2 -> B,
    and for which "function space", or set of functions,
    one commonly employs the notation "(B^2 -> B)",
    isomorphically speaking, to speak of anything
    isomorphic to that.

You may still recall that I am enterprying here
to call attention to a connection between Logic,
as we may know it, and "Information Theory" (IT),
as we came to know IT shortly after the Revolution,
however much we may appear to have regressed since
then in our more customary comprehensions of IT.

To this I promise to return, as soon as we have
a ready supply of facilitating images refreshed
within the resources of our shared imaginations.

Post-Lunch, I Think,

Jon Awbrey
