OK, I think I see what you're after now.  You want something like the
traditional philosophical distinction between substances and properties -
substances would have an independent existence, while properties would have
to "inhere", as they say, in a substance of some sort.  You claim that this
distinction will make the overall ontology simpler, because it will allow us
to avoid stating this distinction at two different spots in the ontology.  I
guess my response is that, if the distinction between substance and property
can be stated clearly and in a manner which can be inherited by both
Abstract and Physical, then we should do what you're suggesting.  I am
somewhat dubious, but I would be very interested in seeing the
taxonomy/axioms you develop on this basis.

It seems to me that there are two notions of set.  One is an abstract object
which has no location in space-time, which has elements that can be
instances of either Abstract or Physical, and which has an identity that is
determined exclusively by its elements (if elements are added to or
subtracted from a set, the set is no longer the same set).  Some examples of
this sort of set are the set of real numbers, the set of registered voters
in the U.S., etc.  On the other notion of set, which is referred to as
Collection in the Merged Ontology, a set is comprised exclusively of
instances of Physical, has a location in space-time, and the identity of the
set can remain constant even when some members are added or subtracted.
Some examples of this sort of set are football teams and flocks of geese.

The central concept in the "units of measure" part of the Merged Ontology is
MeasureFn, a binary function that assigns a specific quantity to a real
number and a unit of measure.  I'm not sure how a quantifier would be
relevant here.

I understand that you want to have two different concepts, one for the
substance/object and one for the attribute, but I guess the natural question
is:  How much does this distinction buy you?  Does it simplify the
construction of lower levels of the ontology or does it just build into the
ontology a distinction that goes back to Aristotle and may just be a product
of the fact that some natural languages distinguish nouns from adjectives?
Once again, though, I would be very interested in seeing what sort of
ontology results from taking your substance/attribute distinction as the
primary one.

I agree that the notion of purpose is an important one, but I guess I resist
reifying it the way you have suggested.  In the Merged Ontology, we have two
relations 'hasPurpose' and 'hasPurposeForAgent', but rather than make
"Purposes" a special class of objects these relations simply require that
the purposive filler of these relations be a KIF-Formula.  To my way of
thinking, this is the most economical way to proceed, because it means one
less class in the ontology and it allows any proposition to be a purpose,
which reflects the way things are, I think.

I agree that a microbe might be be exhibiting agency when it moves toward a

This is a tough area.  I guess I would argue that we should incorporate a
very generic notion of agency, e.g. acts autonomously, into the ontology and
then rely on other classes in the ontology to provide the sort of
distinctions in agency that you allude to.

if some members of the government step down.
