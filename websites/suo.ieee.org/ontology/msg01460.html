things, and, in fact, there are several examples of classes of classes in
the merged ontology.  'ContinuantType' and 'OccurrentType' are classes of
classes of, respectively, object-like and process-like things.

> Ian, I don't believe what I wrote, below, follows from what you write,

Actually, the 'nth-domain' assertions regarding 'subclass-of' do imply that
every subclass is a class.  According to the rewrite rule in the "Structural
Ontology" section of the merged ontology, '(nth-domain subclass-of 1 Class)'
is equivalent to the following:

(forall (?S ?X)
   (=>
      (subclass-of ?S ?X)
      (instance-of ?S Class)))

This says that every subclass is itself a class, and it implies the
formulation that you give at the end of your message.

> 
> Is there any reason we can't add the below as a theorem or 
> axiom of the
> Merged Ontology?
> 
> (And also add (=> (instance-of Entity Class)) as a theorem or axiom?)

This actually is already a theorem of the merged ontology.  One of the
top-level assertions in the merged ontology is the following:

(subclass-of Physical Entity)

Since the rewrite rule for 'nth-domain' and '(nth-domain subclass-of 2
Class)' imply the following,

(forall (?S ?X)
   (=>
      (subclass-of ?S ?X)
      (instance-of ?X Class)))

we derive (instance-of Entity Class).
