Here is a sample of a sign relation that abides, most often implicitly
but now and then explicitly, in a very rudimentary logical application,
namely, in the RefLog ("reflective extension of propositional logic")
syntax that I use as a linkpin for all my computational logic work.
To keep a tight rein on the complexity of matters in the currently
inchoate setting, let us take up as the syntactic domain, S = I,
the formal language RefLog(x, y), which limits expressions to
using at most two propositional variable names, "x" and "y".

And this is just for starters ...

Now I hope you understand that I understand that no sensible programmer
would actually write out such a table for the sake of any computational
purpose under heaven and over this domain -- indeed, nobody would write
it out at all unless forced to do so in pursuit of some expository goal.
But the table exemplifies the basic "data of competence" (DOC) that any
due parser or processor of the RefLog language would have to be capable
of performing, just so, if it were to find itself duly invoked to do so.

It is true that the computation of a recursive function, at the ne plus
ultra "bounding extremity" (BE) of its domain of definition, does often
reduce to an irregular verbatim of one darn table look-up after another,
and I even know of one very sophisticated, and yet somehow very simple*,
model of computation that does, in a way, reduce the whole of computing
to just such resorts, but I know that you know that I know that this is
not the way that it would usually be done.

The tedium of this example has made me forget,
I hope temporarily, what it was I was meaning
to say by way of it, so I will stop for a bit
to try if I can recall my initial sense of it.

Wish me luck,

Jon Awbrey
