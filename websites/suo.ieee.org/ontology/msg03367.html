JA  = Jon Awbrey
JeC = Jerry Chandler
JoC = John Collier

JeC: When you respond to my questions about the nature
     of certain functions in your languages, you state:

JeC, quoting JA:

    | These questions are just not apt.

JeC: Do you mean that my questions about your "inquiry into inquiry" are not apt?

JoC: I suspect that there is some confusion resulting
     from different uses of the word 'language'.

JoC: Jerry asked the quite sensible question about bijections
     from one S to another S.

JoC: Jon's answer depended on whether the Ss were considered,
     or the Ss and their denotations.  This suggests that the
     Ss are not languages at all, but are inscription systems
     comprised of sets of ordered n-tuplets of inscriptions,
     together with certain rules.  These are not languages
     in the usual informal sense.

That is why folks call them "formal languages".

JoC: I would add, not any more than 'inconsistent set',
     as Jon uses it corresponds to 'inconsistent set'
     in informal usage.

I do not know where you party, but I just do not find
the phrase "inconsistent set" used all that much in
informal usage.

Do you really mean to say that sine and cosine
functions are a part of your "informal language"?

JoC: Jon is using very special contexts, and has, as yet made no attempt
     to generalize to anything that I, for one, find very interesting.
     He gave the impression at one time that he had a broader application
     in mind, but that has faded among the huge amount of technical material
     he has posted.

No application without techne.

JoC: I fear that we are being presented with a colossal pun.

You have just discovered my "theory of the universe" (TOTU).

Seriously folks, I think that I have been pretty careful to use
the technical nomen "formal language", with all of its epithets
intact, just as it is used by a fairly broad community of logic,
mathematics, and computer science natives.  I would not want to
say exactly that everybody understands the distinction between
"formal language" and "natural language", but everyone I know
understands that there oughta be a distinction between the two.
Chomsky writes about "E-Language" and "I-Language", and so on,
but there is just no way you can write software applications
in any half-way complex domain without using this techne.

Jon Awbrey
