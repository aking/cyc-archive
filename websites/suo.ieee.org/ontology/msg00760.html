>
>;; Let REL be any n-place predicate, and let M be the numeral for the
>;; positive integer m, where m is less than or equal to n.  Then we
>;; let
>;;
>;;   (nth-domain REL M CLASS)
>;;
>;; serve as an abbreviation for
>;;
>;;   (forall (VAR_1 ... VAR_n)
>;;           (=> (REL VAR_1 ... VAR_n)
>;;               (instance-of VAR_m CLASS)))
>

I should have noticed this sooner, but this doesnt really capture the 
intended meaning of 'nth-domain', at least I don't think it does. I 
assume that (nth-domain REL M CLASS) is supposed to mean that the Mth 
argument position of REL is restricted to be in CLASS, right? A kind 
of sort restriction? If so, this doesnt do that job, since if one 
violates the sort restriction one simply generates a contradiction, 
rather than a syntax error.

There is also a more subtle point, by the way, that this 'definition' 
isn't a definition, since it uses a metasyntactic convention (the 
'triple dots') which isn't part of the language. One could define it 
in KIF 3.0 (but not in ANSI-KIF) by using sequence variables and 
recursion, but it still wouldn't be first-order definable.
