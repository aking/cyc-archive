Bill,

As I said before, I completely agree with you, and so would
Wittgenstein (with, of course, his usual quibbles:

>I agree that tying semantics to a community of users >inhibits communication
between communities.  However, the solution >that I prefer to this particular
problem differs, I think, from most of >those on this list.  I think it is ultimately
both unrealistic and >impractical to purse a single universal ontology that
everyone on earth >can agree to and use.  Won't happen.  I have seen far too
>many global data models and corporate data dictionaries  - all of >them in
fact! - turn into expensive boondoggles, leading me believe >that it is not
possible to come up with this global model with single >universal, accepted,
and understood semantics.

Of course.  There will never be one single global ontology.
That goal is impossible to achieve.  Cyc tried to do that,
and they failed.  What they have now is a collection of
microtheories (which are similar to what Wittgenstein would
call language games).

>I would rather see more work put into bridging the >community ontologies with
semantic mapping language that acts (or can >act) as a translator between the
language.  My motto these days >when it comes to ontologies and data models
is "Have It Your >Way".

I agree with your last sentence, but I don't know what you
mean by a "semantic mapping language".  It is impossible to
map a sentence from one ontology into a meaningful sentence
in an incompatible ontology.

John Sowa
