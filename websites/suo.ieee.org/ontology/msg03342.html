JA: The reader is thinking, "Hey, wait a minute!"
    The writer is writing,  "I heard that!"

HP: Actually, I was thinking the physical "force relation" is generally non-reflexive,
    symmetric, and intransitive.  Lattices are reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive.
    How does the concept "Abstractive Hypostation" solve this problem?

I do not understand where this "force relation" might come into it, as I was not really
prepared to get physical yet, but just by way of a general explanation, I sometimes use
phrases like "abstractive hypostasis" (AH) and variants thereof as alternatives to the
more standard "hypostatic abstraction" (HA) because I sense that in many discussions
people get misled by placing too much emphasis on the abstract part and not enough
stress on the hypostatic (understood substantive) part of the phrase.  In this
application the whole lattice is being treated as an abstract mathematical
object, like a number, a space, an algebra, or an object in a category.
This is really just a rationalization of the way mathematicians talk.

Jon Awbrey
