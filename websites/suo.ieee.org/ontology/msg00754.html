>Matthew,
>
>	You claim that modal operators may not be appropriate for
>representing alternative courses of action.  Could you give us an example of
>such a situation that cannot be adequately represented with the first-order
>predicate calculus and the modal operator of possibility?
>
>-Ian

Since Matthew has not replied, allow me. It is almost too easy to do 
this, since in fact there is almost nothing that *can* be said about 
alternative courses of action using the modal operator of 
possibility. First, one would need a temporal modality, since 
'possible' might refer to the past or the future. But leave that 
aside, as it is a minor point.  The real, er, showstopper is that the 
modality amounts to an existential quantifier over alternative worlds 
(not courses of action, another quibble) , but provides no way to say 
anything about what is true in one possible world as opposed to 
another. For example, suppose one wants to decide whether to pull or 
push a door to open it, by imagining the consequences of each action. 
Both actions are *possible*, of course, and it is *possible* that the 
door will open in the future. But what we want to do is reason along 
the lines: if the door is pushed (and other relevant conditions 
apply) then it would move thus (I omit the gory details of frame 
reasoning and ramifications), and moreover if it were pulled (and if 
other conditions obtained, etc.) then it would move so. But of course 
we know it can't move both thus and so at the same time, so we need 
to say that under one circumstance it moves in one way and in the 
other it moves differently. These 'circumstances' are not *times*, 
notice: both the circumstances have the same clock time (just after I 
do my thing to the door); they are something like alternative courses 
of events, or alternative histories, or maybe alternative action 
sequences (in the situation calculus they would be alternative 
situtations accessible from the present by different action 
functions. ) This can't be done using logical modalities. In fact, 
*no* action planning can be done that way, as far as I know.
