Hi Jon,

By way of reference, a fuller excerpt from the Peirce manuscript from which you take your quotation:

§6. RELATIVES OF SECOND INTENTION

 488. The general method of graphical representation of propositions has now been given in all its essential elements, except, of course, that we have not, as yet, studied any truths concerning special relatives; for to do so would seem, at first, to be "extralogical." Logic in this stage of its development may be called paradisaical logic, because it represents the state of Man's cognition before the Fall. For although, with this apparatus, it is easy to write propositions necessarily true, it is absolutely impossible to write any which is necessarily false, or, in any way which that stage of logic affords, to find out that anything is false. The mind has not as yet eaten of the fruit of the Tree of Knowledge of Truth and Falsity. Probably it will not be doubted that every child in its mental development necessarily passes through a stage in which he has some ideas, but yet has never recognised that an idea may be erroneous; and a stage that every child necessarily passes through must have been formerly passed through by the race in its adult development. It may be doubted whether many of the lower animals have any clear and steady conception of falsehood; for their instincts work so unerringly that there is little to force it upon their attention. Yet plainly without a knowledge of falsehood no development of discursive reason can take place.
Peirce: CP 3.489 Cross-Ref:††
 489. This paradisaical logic appears in the study of nonrelative formal logic. But there no possible avenue appears by which the knowledge of falsehood could be brought into this Garden of Eden except by the arbitrary and inexplicable introduction of the Serpent in the guise of a proposition necessarily false. The logic of relatives, affords such an avenue, and that, the very avenue by which in actual development, this stage of logic supervenes. It is the avenue of experience and logical reflexion.
Peirce: CP 3.490 Cross-Ref:††
 490. By logical reflexion, I mean the observation of thoughts in their expressions. Aquinas remarked that this sort of reflexion is requisite to furnish us with those ideas which, from lack of contrast, ordinary external experience fails to bring into prominence. He called such ideas second intentions. It is by means of relatives of second intention that the general method of logical representation is to find completion.

I see that in this context your excerpt from the above, viz., 

490. By logical reflexion, I mean the observation of thoughts in their expressions. Aquinas remarked that this sort of reflexion is 
requisite to furnish us with those ideas which, from lack of contrast, ordinary external experience fails to bring into prominence. He 
called such ideas second intentions. It is by means of relatives of second intention that the general method of logical representation is 
to find completion.
refers to the observational logic of "experience and reflection," or, in other words, to logical critic, as opposed to the "paradisaical logic" which forms part of the "nonrelative formal logic" discussed earlier in the manuscript. Critic= fiinally, we might know "true" from "false." Anyhow, the only possibility of ever approaching that is by close observation.

Thus Peirce gives the definition: 

By logical reflexion, I mean the observation of thoughts in their expressions.


Which is to hold that critical logic is an observational science, so in its essence diagrammatic. Here one sees both the similiarity of logic to mathematics, and the difference. For while logic "observes," it doesn't observe "pure possibilities"--like mathematics--but thought in all its richness and complexity, tied to a real world. So we, in our essential humaness, participate in thought in its movement; or, alternatively, are in this thought of a world of experience--in fine, a real world.

Moreover, it is apparently possible for us to discern the true from the false in some matters of great importance (science and its subsequent technologies fairly proves that this is the case in at least that domain). The concept of the final logical interpretant further suggests that we then ought reasonably to modify our conduct in the direction of the truth that we have become assured of.

[Perhaps we will one day create the conditions for such a modification at the socio-cultural level--and not just the pull-yourself-up-by-your-boot-straps--level of the extraordinary individual.]

Next Peirce comments: 

Aquinas remarked that this sort of reflexion is requisite to furnish us with
  | those ideas which, from lack of contrast, ordinary external experience fails
  | to bring into prominence.
In short, the mighty impact of secondness, of the sensual, of the world--is so overpowering that without the "distancing" that reflection, that thirdness, affords we would never know that there even is "a world;" and  consequently we'd be as innocent as the plants and animals. {I myself would have then have liked to be a pampered tom cat or some meadow heather in a temperate clime.}

But since we have indeed "lost our innocence" (and "lost our way" as far as I'm concerned), then we of sound mind ought work together to try to make some sense out of our world. It doesn't even take an eco-alarmist to realize that if we don't do this soon (in the next 50-100 years?) there won't be a world to be responsible to. (But that's another issue.) Meanwhile, we ought to at least attempt to be true the Peircean summum bonum of science, namely, the "reasonable in itself."

Refering again to Aquinas: 

He called such ideas 'second intentions'.


Our second intentions turn out, it seems to me, to be exactly related to that human "second nature" which famously makes us homo sapiens--we are fated to be reflective, to look at things not only "subjectively" but also "objectively," that is,  to think diagrammatically according to Peirce's model (or we wouldn't even be able to frame these concepts, to have art and science, philosophy, politics, technology, mass culture, the "good and the bad and the ugly," etc.)

Finally: 

It is
  | by means of 'relatives of second intention' that the general method of
  | logical representation is to find completion.
Therefore, critical logic seems to be this clear and adequate observation of diagrams relating to this--our--reality (not just those observations referring to "mere"  possibilities--which is the great work of mathematics). Critical logic concerns itself finally with truth and falsity in the real world and all its representations (our representations)--that is, when it is ultimately taken up by metaphysics, when that very real science (metaphysics--the science of the real in all its manifestations) becomes convinced--by methodeutic and through rhetoric--of the validity of the matters upon which Critic has passed judgment formally. (This is not expressed well--Lord, I hate my convoluted style! I too tend to think better in diagrams than in words.)

So now I see more fully the eminent and imminent need in logic for such things as conceptual graphs and (here's a leap) the Semantic Web. Here, finally,  critical logic (as second) and methodeutic/rhetoric (as third) might meet to inform us of that before which we stand and need to act together in our own best interest. May we each have the humility finally to press our shoulders to that communal task, apart from the furtherance of our egoic needs

(However, sometimes I wonder just how long it will take before we reach deep consensus on things even "methodological" let alone those issues of "vital importance.")

You, and John Sowa, and Mary Keeler, and Joseph Ransdell, and Lucia Santaella and--of course--Tim BL, and  others might yet help bring about the transformation that we all desire, and even more, that we need! No doubt the work of Charles Peirce will remain central to that endeavor (as it is, for example, to conceptual graphs as regards both their form and possible content).

Thank you all for your excellent work.

We are in a new era. So we ought try to colloborate as fully as we can--in the spirit of that idealistic-realistic-pragmatic Peircean "community of researchers"--to achieve. . .what? . . . scientific quasi-consensus? Now I'm at a loss. It's seems clear enough that the scientific summum bonum might be designated "the reasonable in itself." But what is our summum bonum, taking into consideration not just that one, but all three of the Universes of experience?

Best regards, Jon. You continue to be an inspiration.

Gary
