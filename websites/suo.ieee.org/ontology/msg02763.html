> Why do you have a 3-way split of Physical, Relation, & Abstract?

> Another question:  Why do you have an object/process distinction
> under Physical and no parallel distinction under Abstract?
> The parallel distinction that I put under abstract is the
> Schema/Procedure distinction.  Those are the abstract descriptions
> of objects and processes respectively.

The reason that I don't have anything like an object/process distinction
under Abstract is that, as I see it, this distinction is, however you cash
it out exactly, predicated on temporal attributes - an object, unlike a
process, persists through time, in some sense.  Since instances of Abstract,
by definition, do not have space-time locations, I didn't see how the
Schema/Procedure distinction could be retained.  This was based on a
criticism that Pat Hayes advanced a couple of months back, but I'd be very
interested in hearing your response to it.

> 
> Third question:  Why do you have set, quantity, proposition,
> and attribute at the same level?  There is a great deal of
> analysis that has to be done to distinguish them.  And most of
> that analysis belongs to the general field of "formal semiotic",
> which also includes logic as a subset.
> 
> I would suggest a two-way split of Physical & Abstract, with
> physical relationships under physical and abstract relation
> types under Abstract.

I'm not sure what you mean by a physical relationship.  Even if a
relationship mediates physical objects, I guess I would be more inclined to
lump it with Abstract than with Physical.  How about this compromise?  We
could move the whole Relation subtree and position it under the class Set.
This would make Relation a lower level distinction, and it would make
Relation inherit ultimately from Abstract.
