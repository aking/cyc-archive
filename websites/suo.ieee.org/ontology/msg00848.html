> > I have a similar problem understanding the following categories. In
> > any sense of 'relation' I understand, it doesnt make sense to say
> > that a relation is either a continuant or an occurent. Relations
> > don't exist through time, they hold between entities. So what do
> > these categories even mean?
>
>MW: Well I agree that is how relations should be used, but take the
>relation:
>(married Matthew Lydia)
>This does not hold for all time.

Well, in my logic, if you say it like that it DOES hold for all time, 
so you said the wrong thing. People write things like this because it 
reads like the English sentence "Matthew and Linda are married", 
forgetting that English sentences are always spoken with an implicit 
'now' (and often an implicit 'here' or 'this way' as well), and since 
our logical languages are not indexical, we need to somehow make all 
this implicit stuff explicit.

>Of course the problem is that people are
>modelling the wrong thing, but what some people do (and I have done - not
>now recommended) is to add some temporal information to the relation, so
>something like:
>
>(married Matthew Lydia 3-Jul-1976 present) (yes 25 years coming up)
>
>So what is this? You could call it a temporal relationship. There are
>(believe me) worse things that people do.
>
>(Of course it really represents the state of Matthew and the state of Lydia
>from 3-Jul-1976 to the present, and a timeless relation between them, but
>that is several steps away from what is represented here, which seems to be
>a relationship that is also a spatio-temporal extent.)

Yes, I agree that people do all these things. People also lay 
landmines, but it shouldnt be encouraged.
I think the 'nonrecommended' temporal-parameter has its uses, but 
should be thought of as an abbreviation for the 4-d temporal-slices 
version, which I agree is the most general formulation.

>MW: Well I have used pretty much just this example to explain the difference
>between a collection (sense of fusion) and an assembly relation between a
>whole and its parts. I'm not sure if this is Ian's sense here. He seems to
>be talking about the wholes, rather than the relations but the distinction
>is much the same. The key is that with a collection (fusion) the whole is
>precisely the sum of the parts, with an assembly the whole is something more
>than the sum of the parts. It is quite interesting just how many sorts of
>whole/part there are (and which ones really are distinct and useful).

Interesting observation, which ought to be relevant to Guarinian 
ideas about individuation criteria. Got any proto-classification of 
kinds of part/whole relationships? (The sociologist Irving Goffman 
had some lovely ones, eg a 'with', which is a group of people all 
keeping conversational contact with one another inside a large crowd, 
like a group of friends at a football match. They neednt be in 
physical contact and might drift apart for a while, but soon get back 
within speaking distance.)

I see what you mean, but I was meaning a different point. Lets agree 
that at some scale  a piece of rock, say, is continuous in the sense 
that a machine with many inner moving parts is not continuous. BUt 
now think of almost any reasonably complicated piece of machinery - a 
conveyor belt carrying coke to a furnace, say - and ask is *it* 
continuous or not? And the answer has to be, parts of it are and 
parts of it aren't.
