Jon,

>Peirce referred to a tuple as an "elementary relation" --
>well, roughly, since he used this, strictly speaking,
>for the corresponding intensional concept, but let's
>not go there, not just yet -- and I find this useful
>in constantly serving to remind us that a tuple is
>an element of a relation's set-theoretic extension.

Yes, this is another example of a possible terminology.
But the terms "intension" and "extension" themselves create
more words to remember (and more words to confuse with others,
such as "intention").  There is a lot about SQL that I don't
particularly like, but the terminology of tables with rows
and columns does make things a lot simpler for many people
to visualize.

>Incidentally, in his early works he used k-dimensional matrices
>to represent k-adic relations, where the k-tuples where written
>in the following form, e.g., for 3-tuples:

Yes, but this brings in the term "k-dimensional", which can
create more confusion when people start talking about
representing a 4-dimensional space-time vs. 3-dimensional
space plus 1-dimensional time.

And the term "matrix" tends to strike more fear in the hearts
of nonmathematicians than the more homey word "table".

Bottom line:  It's important to keep the historical connections
straight, but it is also important to define a simple working
vocabulary that everbody can use with a minimum of confusion.

John
