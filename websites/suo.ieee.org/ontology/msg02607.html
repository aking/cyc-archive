>Matthew,
>  Could you explain further why you believe the temporal ontology 
>employed is relevant here?  It seems to me that these alternatives 
>are relevant regardless of whether you employ a 4D framework or not.
>  For example, in a 4D ontology I would think one could have the 
>classes Equine and SinglyHornedAnimal, and it would merely affect 
>the instances that would customarily be created such as 
>MrEdDuring1950, SeaWorldsNarwhalOnThursday.  One would still care to 
>state that the classes are disjoint so that one could or could not 
>have SallysUnicornDuring2000.

I agree. I think the discussion went this way because Chris used an 
example involving change to justify the intensional reading, thereby 
confusing issues of possibility with issues of temporality. 
("...right now, as things stand,... ", etc.) There was, I am sure, a 
rhetorical reason for this: it seems much more compelling that some 
new things might come along, as it were, and need to be discussed, 
than that we should be forced to weaken our axioms just on the 
grounds that something is *possible*, even if it is never going to 
happen. But this rhetorical device seems to have backfired.

I take it that your modal reservations, Adam, are based on genuine 
worries about possible unicorns, rather than the fact that new 
entities might, as it were, sneak up on the reasoner as time goes by?

Pat
