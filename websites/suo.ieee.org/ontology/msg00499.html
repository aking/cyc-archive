Jon,

I like your explanation:

> "Matthew"  is a sign that denotes Matthew   (in the real world).
> "x"        is a sign that denotes x         (in the real world).
> "<x, y>"   is a sign that denotes <x, y>    (in the real world).
> "R"        is a sign that denotes {<x, y> : <x, y> in R} (ITRW).
>'"Matthew"' is a sign that denotes "Matthew" (in the real world).
>'"x"'       is a sign that denotes "x"       (in the real world).
>'"<x, y>"'  is a sign that denotes "<x, y>"  (in the real world).
>'"R"'       is a sign that denotes '"R"'     (in the real world).
>
>...
>
>There's a pattern here.

My only addition is a suggestion that we use KIF as the base
language of signs for the purpose of the SUO, and we define the
terms in other languages, such as English, conceptual graphs,
etc., by their translation to KIF.

Again, I repeat my earlier caveat:  This proposal does not solve 
any philosophical problems, it doesn't answer any linguistic
questions, and it makes no claim that KIF is the best language
for everything that one might want to use a language for.  But
it allows the work on the SUO to proceed.

John Sowa
