>The pope from 1400 to 1700 would be a single physical object?

No, a single PhysicalObject. That is, this is a broader concept than 
the normal physical object concept, but it is useful precisely 
because it shares a significant number of properties with that 
ordinary notion: uninterrupted physical existence, instantaneous 
identity with a physical object at all times, a coherent 
spatiotemporal boundary, and so on. Physical objects in the usual 
sense are a distinctive subclass with one extra condition of complete 
spatiotemporal continuity at all points during their lifetime, while 
the general class of things is allowed spatial discontinuity at a 
finite (?) number of timepoints.

> Maybe
>philosophers like to speak this way, but I think it flies in the face of
>common usage and common sense.

To hell with common usage. We are, as I keep emphasising, not doing 
linguistic analysis here. As for common sense, I think in fact that 
this notion is widely used in our everyday thinking and is one that 
all adults are quite competent with. The idea of the continuity of 
the presidency, for example, is quite a normal idea. At the moment 
Kennedy died, LBJ became the President. Apparently even he wasnt 
aware of it at the moment, for it was not because *he* did anything, 
but because it is of the nature *of the presidency* that it be 
temporally continuous, just like a 'continuant'.

Look. You, like many people who use this awful term, seem to imbue it 
with almost a mystical importance; a kind of basic organizing 
principle for ontology design: classes should be Natural Kinds. Ive 
heard this so many times Im getting sick of it, since it never 
actually seems to be cashed out in anything but the vaguest terms; so 
vague that I suspect it actually has no real meaning, like Organic 
Food. If it is so centrally important, let's see something (in 
English or logic, but *something* nontrivial) about what it is 
supposed to actually mean. ("As a culture" , indeed! I refrain from 
further scorn, but this reaches a new low.)
