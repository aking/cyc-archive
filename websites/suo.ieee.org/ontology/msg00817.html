Pat,

	I agree that none of the elements of your list of concepts can be
related to the taxonomic part of the current version of the merged ontology.
I agree with you, but, with the exception of 'information-bearing thing',
these gaps don't worry me.  There are three ways in which a concept can be
related to an ontology, either by being subsumed by another concept, by
having selectional restrictions for the concept stated in terms of other
concepts of the ontology (this case is relevant only for predicates and
functions), and by composing the concept out of other concepts in the
ontology by means of logical operators, set-forming operators, etc.  It
seems to me that all of your examples (again with the exception of
'information-bearing thing') could be cashed out in terms of concepts which
are directly subsumable by the ontology and logical operators.  Let's take
"the shape of Marilyn Monroe's left ankle" as an example.  We have three key
concepts here, viz. shape, Marilyn Monroe, and left ankle (note that we
might want to decompose this concept into a directional notion and a body
part, but this is a subtlety, I think).  We can put 'shape' under the
high-level concept of 'Schema', 'Marilyn Monoroe' goes under 'Person', and
'left ankle' is under something like 'BodyPart', which would be subsumed by
'Organic'.  Aside from the three key concepts in the example, there are two
implicit relations, viz. something like the embody relationship between an
object and an abstract thing and the mereological notion of 'part-of'.  As
you know, we already have the latter, and the former would be easy enough to
define, in terms of selectional restrictions, by employing the concepts of
'Object' and 'Schema'.  All of this seems relatively straightforward to me,
and I think the same sort of strategy could be applied to every other
example except 'information-bearing thing'.  I am worried about this latter
concept, though, and I'll try to find a place for something like it in the
merged ontology.

-Ian
