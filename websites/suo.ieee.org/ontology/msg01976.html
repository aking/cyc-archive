Dear John, Jon, Folks--

My apologies for taking a small bit out of context in order to raise a
point and ask a question.  Most of what you all are discussing is beyond
me but I find the small part I think I can follow fascinating.  

But first to briefly state where I'm coming from:  Seems to me  that  (1)
logic is partly an attempt to clarify the nature (semantics and syntax)
of propositions,  (2) our proposition and thus our logic reflect our
basic assumptions about the nature of that supposed reality which we are
representing by our propositions,  (3) for the most part we assume that
reality to be one in which things (variables) are moving about in
space-time, (4) we call the movement of variables events, (5) we call the
impact or consequence of one event upon another event or variable the
'meaning" of that event with respect to the other variable, and (6) by
"impact" or consequence we mean the ways in which the future
developmental possibilities of the impacted event or variable are
limited.  So to recap a main point  -- we basically assume reality to be
comprised of variables moving about in space time.  Syntax defines the
permissible types of moves (given our notion of what constitutes a
variable) and semantics refers to the consequences of these moves.  
I hope all this was not too muddled.  I present it both as a set up for
the following but also for review on it's own merit.  

My point-question is this:  Are not rules of inference in effect
definitions of  variables.  IOWs a variable is that construct whose
meaning is preserved when subjected to the following  ROIs or
transformations.  So in a sense (I'm suggesting-asking) do not ROIs also
address the meaning of one component of  our presumed reality  --namely
the nature of variables?  And might not a logic (Russell's for example)
run into difficulty when it turns out that the presumed nature of a
variable (class or whatever) turns out to be contradictory under certain
ROIs.  Maybe self contradictory  variables DO exist  --just not on the
macro level we are accustomed to working with.  Hopefully I have not
completely lost my way or simply started off on the wrong foot to begin
with.  

Thanks for any comments-suggestions you have the time or inclination to
offer. 

Jim Piat
