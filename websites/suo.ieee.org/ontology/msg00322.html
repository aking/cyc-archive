No, I hear the difference, but I still think you are wrong. A formal
ontology language DOESNT provide a precise, unambiguous meaning for all its
terms. (In fact, one can often prove quite rigorously that a truly
unambiguous meaning is impossible to achieve using any of a wide class of
formal logics, using some variation of Godel's theorem. 

GH2>	I don't know Godel, but I would suspect that such a proof would
likely refer to a particular type of ambiguity with quite specific logical
boundaries, not applying to all; circumstances.. 

There is a notable contrast here between logics and programming languages,
and it comes out clearly in their formal semantic theories. Programming
language semantics typically relies on the recursion theorem to guarantee
the existence of *unique* minimal fixedpoints of recursive constructions.
Ontology languages are usually not even able to express finiteness
unambiguously, and the second recursion theorem doesnt apply to assertional
logics, in general. This is the price they pay for having a semantics which
claims to be able to talk about anything, not just the things that can be
finitely constructed by mechanical operations inside computing engines.

It depends what you mean by 'unambiguous'. I think maybe we will be able to
achieve workable communication, just as human beings do all the time; which
really means something like, we havnt found anything to disagree about yet.
But that kind of 'unambiguous' communication always has to be open to
further negotiation in order to resolve misunderstandings which might only
surface later.  If you really mean full semantic unambiguity, which would be
in model-theoretic terms there being a unique model, then one can prove that
this is almost always unobtainable. Just having us both use Peano arithmetic
is enough to guarantee that this couldnt be possible. 

GH2>	We agree here too. It's another point I've addressed in this forum
before. Basically, I don't believe that sort of unambiguity can ever be
possible, because we can never know everything about the Universe. At best
our knowledge could be asymptotic with omniscience, tho' I doubt even that.
As I see it, knowledge (and for that matter any logic, "KIF" or any other
type of language) has always been a "bottom-up" science. To me it started
from the earliest instances of biological intelligence (on Earth or possibly
some other astronomical location), and continues as knowledge and technology
advances further and further (until and unless Earth derived intelligence is
wiped out). I feel any belief in the possibility of developing a perfect and
universal logic "top-down" will be impossible as no-one has ever been up to
the "top" (ie. infinity) to survey the totality of what is involved.

Yes, and these changes are often described as 'scientific revolutions', for
a good reason. They involve destroying the old foundations and starting
over, a long and painful process usually taking more than one human
generation. 

GH2>	Agreed. In computer languages and applications, they are promulgated

are slightly overstating things here. I suggest a lot of the original
foundations are, in fact, retained, and only the incompatible parts are

GH2>	Actually, I have been giving some thought to this in the past few
