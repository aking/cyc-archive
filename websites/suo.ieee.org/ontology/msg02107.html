AP2>	We may disagree on English terms but I'm not clear on which axioms
you disagree with.  In fact, although you've said often that you consider

GH>	My reaction is that maybe any "incompatible" areas can be segregated

AP>>	This could also include domain-specific ontologies as the exercise

AP>>	How about a practical semiotics of books, recordings and documents;

PH>	Would it take a book to be a semiotic text with a physical encoding,
or a physical object (a continuant or a 4-d lifetime?) to which contentful
relations can be applied, or a kind of 'sum' of a physical entity plus a
nonphysical 'text', or a collection (what kind of collection?) of surfaces
bearing information-encoding marks, or...? (Please don't say 'all of these')
Some people think of books as things with a history, like old furniture.

GH>	We cope with this in English. I suggest that if we develop a
sufficiently comprehensive structure. Basically you are talking about a set
of differing but related concepts with the same label. Surely a sound and
unambiguous structure can inflect ("old linguistic term") the symbol
differently for each concept. So long as one is prepared to put a fair
amount of effort into developing the inflection system, and apply it
rigorously, it will work. In fact, there has been some discussion about
inflection systems earlier on (tho' no-one may have thought of it that way),
and we can perhaps extend such a system to cover these additional purposes. 

GH>	I would suggest one of the BIG problems with English in recent times
is a tendency to abandon inflections, and rely on context to interpret
English. Even some SUO participants seem to take a delight in omitting
apostrophes, etc. Sometimes I feel cynical, and interpret this as an attempt
to maximise the ambiguous potentials of the language, and then be able to
argue that one can't have an unambiguous controlled version of it. 

AP2>	These are exactly the sort of questions the author of an ontology of
semiotics should ask, and answer.  That they have not been answered in this
discussion group should not suggest that they can't be answered
satisfactorily.  Since you've asked, let me attempt an answer.  I'll call on
Ian to correct any erroneous connections that I may make with the merged
ontology (given below as a term with an initial capital).

AP2>	A book is a physical object with a lifetime - subclass of
VariableObject
AP2>	A book is related to the "conceptual work" which is the information
content of the text in the book.
AP2>	A conceptual work is a ContentBearingObject (it may more
specifically be a Sign).
AP2>	A conceptual work has temporal extent, at least one author, and no
physical extent.
AP2>	A particular coding of the conceptual work is also a Sign.
AP2>	A book contains a particular coding of the conceptual work.

GH>	The 2nd version of "book" could be inflected to "bookcc", as an
indicative example, where the suffix "-cc". indicates the conceptual
content. I realise this inflection may not turn out to be sufficiently
robust for our requirements, but I hope you can appreciate the approach I am
suggesting. Naturally such an inflection system requires rules about
hierarchies for combinations, etc., so they take a bit of thought to do
well.
