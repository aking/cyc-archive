Yes, our attentions do tend to wander a bit ...

For your orientation, this thread initially began under the title
of "Why Triadicity Matters", in which I sought to explain to the
SUO group what I thought was the relevance of 3-adic relations,
and especially sign relations, toward their ontological goals,
and later I tried to resolve its focus even more in the guise
of "Why Triadicity Matters To Interobjectivity", which nonce
word I synthesized to unify the goals of intercommunication
and interoperation that all concur are official parts of
our Scope & Purpose here.  I made use of the traduction
aspects of the problem you and others were discussing,
simply because these words were "in the air" at the
time -- it seems that we are always making use of
the other's words to mention objects of our own --
then the current title was devised by Doug in
his efforts to relate what I was relating to
his model of a communication channel of the
familiar information-theoretic sort, which
latter task still embroils us in problems.
I append below the links of this catenary
history, this chain mail armamentarium,
this lanuginously e-snarled inquiry.

Let me try to explain why I am interested in this game,
to use that deceptively simple word "game" in the way
of all familiar dissemblances that we language gamers
perform so competently if not always so critically or
reflectively.  This actual game of the Tower of Hanoi,
though I believe that I first learned of it under the
name of the Temple of Benares, was one of my favorites
in a long ago time and a faraway galaxy, and I learned
it so well that I would entertain friends and strangers
with going through the moves in perfect order, one step
per heartbeat, with eyes blindfolded.  But people being
fickle as people will be, were not entertained for long,
if they ever were, and, after a while, no longer was I.
But still there is something about this game that tugs
at my attention, and so I must try to account for it.

Perhaps it is this.  I still maintain a general interest in all aspects
of the variety of activity that we have come to call "problem-solving".
Indeed, now that I think of it, I place this species of activity that
is problem-solving under the more generic heading of "inquiry", and
I consider it to be one of the two chief moieties of the main genus.

Now, since I regard the long term game of building
a "Testable Ontological Hierarchy" (TOH') as one
that falls under the generic heading of inquiry,
that is, I see it as a "mediate end result" (MER)
of an ongoing process, and neither as an absolute
beginning nor as an absolute completeness, even
when it appears to be "done" for the time being,
this means that the specific study of problems
and the methods of solving is very relevant to
the game of ontology building, and, of course,
the study of well-chosen concrete examples is
a natural part of any generally grounded study.

I hope that this serves to e-lucidate the overall "propositional altitude"
from which I view this particular game within the setting of a larger one.
