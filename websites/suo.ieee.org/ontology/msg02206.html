Matthew wrote:

>  (<=> (gives John Mary Book1)
>       (and (gives x)
>            (giver x John)
>            (receiver x Mary)
>            (given x book1)
>       )
>  )

This can't be right, as the free variable "x" occurs on the right side of
the <=> but not on the left; it just sits there like a pronoun with no
associated context to give it meaning.   Also, you are using "gives"
ambiguously here. If I recall another of your recent posts correctly, you
are using "(gives x)" to mean something like "x is a giving", i.e., an act
or event of giving.  If so, I think what you're after is the following (I
change to the more mnemonic (and KIF'ish) variable "?e"):

(<=> (gives John Mary book1)
     (exists (?e) 
             (and (giving ?e)
                  (giver ?e John)
                  (receiver ?e Mary)
                  (given ?e book1))))

I.e., John gives book1 to Mary iff there is a giving ?e in which John is
the giver, Mary the receiver, and book1 the thing given.

Fred wrote:
> Dear Matthew, 
> I wonder if the following isn't yet closer to what you have in mind.
> 
>    (<=> (gives3 ?d ?r &g)
>         (and (gives2 ?x)
>              (giver ?x ?d)
>              (receiver ?x ?r)
>              (given ?x ?g)
>          )
>     )     

Same problem with the free "?x" here.  Also, why would gives3 take three
arguments while gives2 takes only one?  And where is gives2 axiomatized?
As for this being closer to what Matthew had in mind, I believe h was just
giving an instance of what you intend with your more general axiom, 
though I'd express it as follows:

(forall (?x ?y ?z)
        (<=> (gives ?x ?y ?z)
             (exists (?e)
                     (and (giving ?e)
                          (giver ?e ?x)
                          (receiver ?e ?y)
                          (given ?e ?z)))))

What all this means for the triadicity debate I have no idea, as I have no
idea what triadicity in the Mystical Peircean Sense is all about.  I would
understand the above axiom as nothing more than a way of providing a
(perhaps) more robust analysis of the giving relation by enlarging one's
ontology to include events.
