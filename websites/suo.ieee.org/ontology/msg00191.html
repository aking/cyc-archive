   This is an important issue, and I will provide my
current thoughts on it.
   This phenomenon is not one I would represent as being
some form of intermittent "concrete_existence".  I would reserve 
"concrete_existence" for individual objects.  A specification of the
parts for some artifact, or protoypical natural object, is a definition
of some class of objects, which a particular object in the real world 
may or may not conform to.  To be consistent, I think such definitions
need to refer to prototypes or "complete" objects.  The individual 
parts may be classified as "vital", "essential" "typical", "optional",
"disjoint-alternate", or "accessory" -- for example.  The assertions of 
the "presence" or "absence" of some designed part of an artifact at 
some particular time and place, whether it is connected as intended in 
the design, and whether it is functional, are time-dependent assertions 
about specific instances of objects that don't change the definition of 
the complete artifact.   A particular artifact that can serve the role of
a part of a plane may or may not be designated as "belonging"
to some particular plane (e.g. while being repaired), which is 
not the same as saying that it is "in-place" and "functional".  I would
view these latter three assertions as time-dependent slots (or axioms) 
on the specific instance of a part itself.
   Assuming that some instance of an artifact such as a plane is 
sufficiently intact to be termed a plane, even though incomplete, it 
should have time-dependent attributes such as "complete", or 
"functional".  If "incomplete" there will be some list of parts that 
are "missing".  Whether an artifact can be pulled apart and 
cease its "existence" and then be re-assembled and said
to exist again under the same instance-name is a convention that
depends on "identity criteria", which Guarino has labored much
to clarify.  If there is some single vital part (fuselage?)
which by convention defines whether some object exists or not, 
regardless of the presence of other parts, then intermittent 
existence should not occur; the "incomplete" artifact would exist
as soon as the vital part came into existence, and cease to exist 
when that vital part ceased to exist (and recursively for the
"vital part").  Alternatively, existence could be said to begin only 
when some artifact has achieved functionality at some time, and 
subsequent existence might be defined as being existence of the 
vital part.
    This may not seem to conform to intuitive or linguistic
usage, but linguistic usage is fairly broad.  I've heard people 
say, e.g., that "John's car is scattered all over his driveway.  He's 
getting the heads planed."  The existence of John's car is not
questioned in such a case, but its physical state of division certainly
doesn't conform to a definition of a prototypical connected and
functional object.  I'd be interested to learn what you think
is the best way to represent all the possible permutations of
parts, including multiple alternative replacements, even sticking
only to artifacts.
    I think that to cover all bases, an "intermittent existence"
should be allowed, if it doesn't conflict with other desirable
properties of existence.  But I have no idea what conflicts may 
occur; it depends on how people may want to use the concept
of "concrete_existence".

> In the SC4 community we managed to develop incompatible models, 
> even when starting from a common core. I see little chance that 
> ontologies will be mergeable with anything other than 
> a lot of hard work by humans without
> that having been part of their design intent. 
    I don't disagree.  That argues for maximizing the size of 
the extension "Reference Shared Ontology".  Your experience in
developing different ontologies using a common basis should be
quite relevant to what the SUO is doing, and if there is some 
good synopsis of the experience you mentioned, it could be 
very instructive for us.   I wouldn't be surprised to discover
that over half of the terms intended to represent the 
same concept are defined differently by different ontologists,
even with an SUO.  Without an SUO, it would be closer to 99%.
    If an SUO and a Reference Shared Ontology existed, and I wanted
my ontology to communicate with others, I would bend way over 
backwards to use those ontologies, and contribute to the RSO any
new definitions I created myself.  If that was a common 
practice, the RSO could eventually handle a lot of different
specific domains.  We are nowhere near the point where that 
can be discussed in concrete terms, but since the goal
of interoperability is central to the SUO, it is worthwhile
recognizing that the more detail that is standardized, the greater
will be the interoperability, and true interoperability will 
require a rather large shared ontology, and that won't likely 
happen for a long time unless major funding is found.  Meanwhile 
building an SUO is a good starting point to explore the problems.
