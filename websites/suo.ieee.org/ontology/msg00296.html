>Pat,
>	.	Thanks for this further elaboration, with which I agree.
>
>	.	The problem to me is that it seems what you have described
>can be applied to objects as well as relations.

Er.. it IS applied to objects. Histories can be histories of objects 
(among other things), and every object is thought of as being a 
history.

>	.	Basically it seems to me you are discussing histories, or
>memories of time-histories.

I'm talking about histories, not memories of them. Memories would 
introduce a whole lot of news stuff about mental states, 
representations, etc. .

>I am not seeing at this stage why such an
>approach does not apply equally to both objects and relations.

It does apply to objects, but then the point is that relations 
holding between histories do not themselves need to be historical: 
they can (must?) be considered to be timeless. For example, I am now 
heavier than I was five years ago. In the 4-d histories ontology, 
this would be stated as a 'heavier' relation between me-now (a slice 
of my history) and me-5-years-ago (a different slice of my history). 
This relation has no need of any further time parameter, however, and 
in fact it wouldnt really make sense to give it one. This 'heavier' 
fact doesnt have a time at which it is true: it is just plain true, 
like the fact that 2+2=4, or that 1987 was before 1988.

>	.	I can easily think about my great-grandparents, none of whom
>I ever met, and their doings. The same applies to my grandparents, all now
>deceased, but some of whom I did met. Of course the same applies to
>"inanimate" objects such as cars, etc.

Er...yes, you can. I dont quite see what this has to do with what we 
are talking about, however.
