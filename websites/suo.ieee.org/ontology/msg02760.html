> Introduction
> 
> You will be aware that Jim has been asking several of us if we
> will propose specific pieces of work to be a focus of activity
> for the SUO group, and I am one of the people he has approached.
> 
> The idea is that we might adopt a number of work items which
> members of the SUO may choose to contribute to. This implies
> formally accepting them as work items within the SUO work group.
> 
> Since we don't have much practise of doing things formally here
> I am going to start by proposing the process I think we should
> adopt. It is a straight copy of what we do in ISO TC184/SC4, and
> is probably based on Roberts' Rules.
> 
> Below is a resolution that is what you are formally asked to 
> vote on. Before it is an introduction that provides background
> material to the resolution. The introduction does not form part
> of the resolution. To proceed the resolution requires a second.
> If/when a second is found, then the resolution is open for 
> discussion. Comments may be made about the appropriateness of
> the resolution and changes may be suggested. The proposer has 
> the absolute right to accept or reject any proposed changes
> (it is their resolution). If there have been no negative
> comments, then the chair may seek to pass the resolution by
> acclamation (silence is consent). Otherwise you move to a vote.
> A simple majority of those voting for over those voting against
> (abstentions don't count) is sufficient to pass the resolution.
> 
> Since we are an e-mail community the principle issue is timing.
> Especially since members may not be "present" at all times.
> 
> I suggest that a minimum of 1 week is allowed for discussion,
> after which the proposer may call for a vote at any time at 
> their discretion. I suggest that 1 week is allowed for votes
> to be made by voting members, and that non-votes (silence) is
> taken as abstention. Votes may either be to the chair or the
> list as individuals prefer.
> 
> I know some people here are suspicious of any degree of 
> formality in what we do, so I think it is worth noting that 
> a resolution represents the position of the group as a whole. 
> The group has no power over its members and no decision of the 
> group can require any action of any individual. Resolutions
> usually offer approval, or encouragement for certain actions
> that are proposed, approval of deliverables for certain 
> purposes, or agreeing to follow certain procedures in how
> we conduct our business. (If what I have done here goes
> beyond elucidating what Roberts' Rules says anyway, then
> the subject of this note could well be the basis of a 
> resolution in this last category.)
