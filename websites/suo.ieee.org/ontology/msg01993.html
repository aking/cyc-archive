¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~NOITATICOTUA~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

In various notes regarding the "Story of A and B",
I introduced the following ideas and observations:

For each interpreter J in {A, B}, the projection of L(J) on its SI space is
an "equivalence relation" (ER), that is, a symmetric, reflexive, transitive
2-adic relation on SxI = SxS.  Whenever any sign relation L is so nice as to
satisfy this condition, I call the ER a "semiotic equivalence relation" (SER).
As with any ER, there is an associated family of "equivalence classes" (EC's)
that are said to form an "equivalence partition" (EP) of the set in question,
all of which in this context I will naturally acronymize as SEC's and SEP's.

In each of the sign relations L(J) for J in {A, B} it happens that the
associated SEP "reconstitutes" or "reconstructs" within the so-called
"syntactic domain" S |_| I, in this case just S = I, the structure of
the object domain O = {A, B}.  This is normally a very nice property.
But do we really want to suggest that the common object of the signs
in a SEC can be theoretically eliminated in favor of that very SEC?
I do not think that A and B would think so!  At any rate, the SEC's
that represent the objects A and B are not the same for L(A) & L(B).
