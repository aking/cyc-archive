"John F. Sowa" wrote:

> The example about the horses pulling the Roman chariots, which set the
> standards for modern railroads is repeated again and again in every
> area of technology.
>
> Some computer examples:
>
>  1. Our email handlers tend to be optimized for 80-character line
>     lengths (or sometimes 72) because that's the size of the old
>     character-oriented CRTs.  That size is based on the number of
>     of characters in a punched card (80, which was often reduced
>     to 72 to allow an 8-digit sequence number at the end in case
>     somebody dropped the deck and had to resort it).
>
>     The number 80 was determined by the number of holes that
>     could be accurately punched in the card by the machines of
>     the 1930s.  And the size of the card was the same as the
>     original cards used by Herman Hollerith for the 1890 census.
>     And that size was chosen because cutting machines were designed
>     for that size because that was the size of the dollar bill in
>     1890.  (And there's probably another story behind that size.)
>
>  2. Compiler writers are frustrated by the small number of
>     registers in the Intel Pentium, which makes it difficult to
>     store intermediate results when generating optimized code.
>     The reason for the small number is backwards compatibility
>     with the Intel 486, which was compatible with the 386, and
>     the 286, and the 186, and the 8086, and the 8080, and the 8008,
>     and the 4004 -- the original 4-bit microprocessor that Intel
>     designed in the early 1970s.  And that machine had a maximum
>     addressable memory of 1K bytes (just 1024).
>
>  3. And the best computer fonts for the latest and greatest
>     displays and printers are based on the old fonts used for
>     hot-lead typesetting.  Those fonts were designed by the
>     early printers in the 15th and 16th centuries, and they
>     were doing their best to mimic the best handwriting of the
>     monks who copied the manuscripts that had been copied and
>     recopied for the previous 2000 years.
>
> And there are similar stories behind all kinds of "modern" technology.
>
> John Sowa
