I will take a personnal "centrist" position as regard to these issues :
both of you look to be at the same time right and wrong as it seems to me.
I often think that someone could undertake to give an account of the whole,
large and great system of Peirce as a trial to answer this unique and
simple question : To what extent the logical categories (where logical here
means formal) can correspond to the categories of common experience ? His
answer goes through the concepts of sign and pragmatism among others. Such
concepts are some kind of analytical tools to make the answer more and more
determinate (Thanks to Jon for is citation thread about "determination").
The program of CSP does not reside in the opposition between formalism and
experience but on the contrary in the mystery of the possibility of a
unique synthesis (how formal resolves in experience and how experience
really is formal). Neither is formal a matter of practical decision choice
as Jon says, nor experience is a matter of reality as Jean-Marc seems to
tell us.
This program is a trial. That is to say that the final response is on
inquiry. From this fact some eminent thinkers have concluded that such a
peircean program failed (because it could not exhibit a proof for
pragmatism for example).
I dont think so.

Bernard

Bernard, I think that any formalism or any a priori theory that cannot provide with the means by which the formal concepts encountered in the theory can be said to correspond to any reality though the observation of empirical data has very little substance, and can be said to belong to the category of pure speculations, if the aim of the theory is to lead to any practical application at all. My argument is that a formal definition such as that of L75 only describes the internal relations between the three components of the triad, while it says nothing about the components themselves, and thereby the means by which empirical observation such as phaneroscopy enters the picture. So there are two possibilities, either to stay in pure formalism and claim that the nature of the correlates is of no importance ( are  they forms? objects present to the mind, divisions of matter ? ... ), but in that case, there is no possibility of controlling that the objects found a priori correspond in any way to the objects found a posteriori because there is nothing to look for ( to cognize a relation between 3 things you need to be able to cognize and identify those three things that are connected or you are experiencing thirdness without the underlying categories.) The risk then is to implicitly assume, by lack of a definition, that a sign is a material object independent of mind. The second alternative is to be more specific and define the sign not only as one of the correlates of a triadic relation, but also as an object of experience, the experience of which can be formalized by means of phaneroscopy. I prefer the second alternative.

JM
