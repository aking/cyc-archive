Re:  "Is Icon A Proposition?"

Here is a related question that has taken on a rather acute interest,
if not to say a critical concern, in a couple of other discussion groups,
in particular, the "Standard Upper Ontology" (SUO) Working Group, and also
in the "Conceptual Graphs" (GG) Forum.  I may pose it thus:

| When Peirce speaks of a "Proposition", how can we tell
| when he is speaking of a particular kind of a Sign and
| when he is speaking of a particular kind of an Object?

To outline the question more fully:  How can we tell when he is speaking of
the Sign, namely, that which we might now call a "propositional expression",
or indeed, more after his own heart, a "propositional diagram or graph",
in short, what some people sometimes call a "sentence", as distinguished --
since we all know well how well knows he the difference -- from that we
conceive to be the "hypostatically abstract proposition" (HAP), to wit,
the apophantically "formal or logical or mathematical object" (FOLOMO),
that we briefly call the "abstract proposition" (AP) in its own right?

Now, without having to invoke the full Hierarche of Signs,
we simply may recall that Peirce classifies what he calls
a "proposition" under the heading of a "symbol", but --
here lies the question once again -- just 'which' of the
burgeoning practical fruits of a conceivably abbreviated
delivery of that maxim-ally pregnant term "proposition"
does this Progenitor of Semeiosis bear in mind for us,
his "maieutically attendant interrupreters" (MAI's),
to deliver, if faithful, or else to render a new
reconstrual, if otherwise wiser tuit, by virtue
of his most Pentaculate Conception here!?

Thanks For Thinking About This,

Jon Awbrey
