> >Dear Graham,
> >
> >I would say that there are 3 basic types of thing:
> >
> >1. things that have an extension (exist) in space time (I 
> would call these
> >individuals - but this is a broader use than some would give 
> this word).
> >
> >2. Things that have members (rather than parts), such as
> >classes/sets/collections. These are timeless.
> >
> >3. Ordered collections - Tuples, classifications of them are known as
> >relations. These are timeless, and indeed one way of 
> defining tuples derives
> >them from sets.
> 
> Let me add a few more. First, there are many abstracta (which have no 
> spatiotemporal location) other than collections or ordered 
> collections. In fact, just about any mathematical structure can be 
> considered among these things, and there may be more kinds of 
> mathematical structure than there are kinds of physical object 
> (groups, lattices, topological spaces, modular structures, sheafs, 
> algebras of various kinds, categories, you name it.)

MW: Agreed. We deal with these under class.

> Second, most people (though not all) would make a distinction between 
> the things that occupy space and/or time, on the one hand, and the 
> spaces and/or times that they occupy, on the other.  

MW: I guess we don't, but we used to. Nothing religious, it just doesn't
seem to be necessary in a 4D world. You can always construct the necessary
classes (properties) if you want to.

> Third, there are 
> things that can be located in spacetime without themselves occupying 
> any of spacetime: moments, surfaces of various kinds, point motions, 
> pathways, etc.. 

MW: We deal with these as spatio-temporal extents, in that they exist in
space-time. We aren't particularly worried about the extent vanishing
towards zero in one or more dimensions.

> Geography alone supplies a rich vocabulary for 
> talking about such things and the relationships between them. Then 
> again there are entire other categories of entities, whose locative 
> nature is harder to classify but many people want to have as real, 
> first-class entities: causes, results, manners (the 'way' something 
> is done, eg happily or with a good style), and things indicated by 
> words like 'nature' (small 'n', as in 'it is of the nature of fish to 
> swim in water') , 'tendency', 'attraction'. Others make reference to 
> 'tropes' , in order to make sense of phrases like 'the greenness of 
> the grass delighted me'; it is not enough, on this view to simply 
> refer here to the fact that the grass is green: one has to be able to 
> talk about the *way* that this is true.

MW: All this stuff would be in the classes things belonged to. The
non-trivial bit would be defining the axioms.
> 
> "Promiscuous" ontologies, by the way, admit far more kinds of 
> distinction. For example, consider walking quickly from SRI to 
> Stanford (about a mile; Jerry Hobb's example). Most of us would think 
> of this as one thing, called something like a transporation event. In 
> Jerry's ontology it is several things: at least two: a going from SRI 
> to Stanford (which is slow, since one is walking rather than driving) 
> and a walking from SRI to Stanford (which is fast, since one is 
> hurrying along). In general , how many things it is depends on how 
> many ways there are to describe it.

MW: Well, we try to distinguish between things and their descriptions, so we
would have one thing with different descriptions that applied in different
contexts.

> I think there are worlds you have missed here. For example, I dont 
> understand the sense in which an emotion can be 4-D or 3-D. I wouldnt 
> describe an emotion as an abject at all (though it may be an entity.)
> 
> I also don't think that it is right to say that a relation has 4-d 
> attributes. One perceived advantage of the 4-d way of thinking is 
> that relations have *no* spatiotemporal attributes.
> 
> And finally, its not right to say that an attribute of something is 
> 3-d and therefore timeless. It is timeless because it makes to 
> refernece to time: it does not hold relative to a time, or at one 
> time but not at another. But that doesnt make it 3-D. I dont know 
> whatquite in what sense an attribute can be said to even have a 
> dimension.
