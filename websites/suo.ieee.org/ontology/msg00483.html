John,

Re:

> To avoid endless problems caused by mismatched definitions,
> I suggest that we adopt the following equivalences between
> terms:
> 
>  1. The terms "relation" and "relationship" in ordinary
>     English have multiple meanings.  But the use in both
>     symbolic logic and in relational databases is firmly
>     established.  I recommend that we restrict all uses
>     of those two words to the meanings in logic and RDBMS.
> 
>  2. In RDB, a relation is defined by a table.  The number
>     of columns in the table is critical:  a binary or dyadic
>     relation has two columns, a ternary or triadic relation
>     has three columns, etc.  Each row of the table is
>     called a tuple or a relationship.

Peirce referred to a tuple as an "elementary relation" --
well, roughly, since he used this, strictly speaking,
for the corresponding intensional concept, but let's
not go there, not just yet -- and I find this useful
in constantly serving to remind us that a tuple is
an element of a relation's set-theoretic extension.

Just A Thought,

Jon Awbrey

P.S.

Incidentally, in his early works he used k-dimensional matrices
to represent k-adic relations, where the k-tuples where written
in the following form, e.g., for 3-tuples:

a1:b1:c1, a1:b1:c2, a1:b1:c3, ...,
a1:b2:c1, a1:b2:c2, a1:b2:c3, ...,
a1:b3:c1, a1:b3:c2, a1:b3:c3, ...,
...,
a2:b1:c1, a2:b1:c2, a2:b1:c3, ...,
a2:b2:c1, a2:b2:c2, a2:b2:c3, ...,
a2:b3:c1, a2:b3:c2, a2:b3:c3, ...,
...,

The set of all possible k-tuples, our cartesian product,
but called "umbrals" after Cayley (?) or Sylvester (?),
formed a familiar sort of algebraic basis, with the
relation then represented as a matrix with boolean
coefficients attached to these basis elements --
and the rest is history.

J.A.
