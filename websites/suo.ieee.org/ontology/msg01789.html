John, Matthew, & All,

Though it does not make a difference in the content of the "theory",
that is, the ultimate collection of statements that are held to be true,
no matter whether one wishes to view them as "theorems" (= proved statements),
or else as "truths" (= statements established in some other way), it does make
a big difference in how you actually go about finding them, whether you use:

1.  Model-Theoretic (MT) methods, like (I will claim) "checking your data",
    also called "experience", "experiment", "inspection", "intuition", and
    many other names, or (more standardly) MT methods like truth tables.

2.  Proof-Theoretic (PT) methods, like starting from a set of axioms and
    trying to chain rules together until you arrive at a suitable theorem.

It may be either controversial or trivial or both to say it this way --
I always have trouble telling which of the three it will be this time --
but there is an analogy, I would say a near identity, between MT and
empirical methods in science.  The similarly between a data base and
a truth table is telling here.  Moreover, I gathered from my earliest
readings of both Frege and Peirce that everyone back then pretty much
understood this -- I am overdue for revisiting the Frege side of these
sources, but I have been puzzled for a long time now why people even
read Frege this way, and where exactly did MT go off track as far as
being in touch with reality, as an experiential pragmatic objective?

I have commented on this before, but I think that one of the reasons
why 19th Century thinkers never worried about (in)completeness is that,
from a standpoint that has any inkling of the platonic realities, it is
just plain silly to think that a finite formal system of any reasonably
mathematical scope (thus, not FOL) could even dream of being complete,
and I know that Peirce's tenet about math being an experimental science
leads to this very same conclusion (not a provable conclusion, of course).

Jon Awbrey
