Pat,

I was trying to focus on another aspect of the propositional level right now,
like all of the nice things that one could begin to think about if the syntax
and the its processor really supported the "functional interpretation" (FI)
in a uniform way, like being able to define derivatives and differentials
and so on.

That other little side issue takes about 20 pages in my Diss to set-up
carefully, with more fonts than I feel comfortable dragging out of the
galley right now, as I have known some people here complain about that.

The reason for doing all of this is vaguely intensional, in the sense
that you want to treat the propositions as abstract objects that just
happen to have a "functional" and a "geometric" interpretation in the
appropriate sort of coordination with each other, but you do not want
to have them tied down to a particular "universe of discourse" X, and
you would also like to say that a sentence is a syntactic sign-entity
that denotes the abstract proposition as its object, given all of the
necessary information about the context of interpretation, and on top
of all that, you would like to be able to make sense of the idea that
the proposition f "indicates" its fibers (f^(-1))(v) for v in B={0,1},
in negative and positive ways, respectively.  So there are two levels
of denotation, indication, reference, signification, or whatever here,
and that will require a "higher order" (HO) sign relation to properly
formalize.

To keep these abstract propositions "floating on air",
as it were, calls for a categorical construction that
I call a "stretch", which is like a cartesian product
and a pullback rolled into one, except that the fixed
and variable parameters are switched.  The basic idea,
so far as the tale can be told in English, is like so:

¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~DISSERTATION~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

1.3.10.6  Stretching Principles

There is a principle, of constant use in this work, that
needs to be made explicit.  In order to give it a name,
I refer to this idea as the "stretching principle".
Expressed in different ways, it says that:

1.  Any relation of values extends to a relation of what is valued.

2.  Any statement about values says something about the things that
    are given these values.

3.  Any association among a range of values establishes an association
    among the domains of things that these values are the values of.

4.  Any connection between two values can be stretched to create a connection,
    of analogous form, between the objects, persons, qualities, or relationships
    that are valued in these connections.

5.  For every operation on values, there is a corresponding operation on the
    actions, conducts, functions, procedures, or processes that lead to these
    values, as well as there being analogous operations on the objects that
    instigate all of these various proceedings.

Nothing about the application of the stretching principle guarantees that
the analogues it generates will be as useful as the material it works on.
It is another question entirely whether the links that are forged in this
fashion are equal in their strength and apposite in their bearing to the
tried and true utilities of the original ties, but in principle they are
always there.
