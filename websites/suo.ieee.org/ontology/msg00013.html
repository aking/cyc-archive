> The "activities" part of the definition of an individual 
> seems very odd to
> me. Do you really not recognize an individual chair or an 
> individual tree
> or an individual pebble?

MW: Yes of course. Personally I see things that endur as physical objects,
and interactions between things that result in change as activities. But you
can argue that a pebble is a process, in which there are atoms and electrons
bouncing around, with bits falling or wearing off from time to time.
> 
> I am corcerned currently with problems in the "wholeness" sense.
> Geographic 'things' such as a mountain, say a particular 
> mountain such as
> Mont Blanc or Mount Everest. Would you say that "Mont Blanc" is not an
> individual because it lacks a sense of wholeness, no 
> unambiguous boundary
> to seperate it horizontally from "not-Mont-Blanc" right next door?

MW: I don't know if you have read Achille's paper on this topic, but I
recommend it if you have not.

MW: My own conclusions having considered that paper is that there are a
number of objects with quite well defined boundaries which different people
may have in mind (when pressed) when they talk about a particular mountain.
Each of these is a perfectly valid "individual". In the case of those
objects that have a common peak, it is likely that a common name has been
given to all of these objects that are possible delineations of the
mountain. The issue of vagueness comes from the same name being applied to a
number of objects with substantial overlap, perhaps without sufficient
context, in terms of whose delineation of the mountain, the name is meant to
refer to.
