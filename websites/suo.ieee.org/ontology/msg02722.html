Matthew,
   I for one would welcome any proposal for a Reference Ontology whether it 
is an extension of, or completely different from the Merged Ontology that 
Ian and I have proposed.  I believe that once the group focuses on a 
particular formal ontology (or several) that we will be doing relevant work 
and that it will be much easier to see and resolve the real issues.

Adam

>My experience is obviously a bit different. Firstly the work we
>have done in Shell and EPISTLE over the last 10 years has been
>largely middle out, and we have made a major change to the top
>at least once on our journey when we moved to a 4D approach.
>However, if you start from the middle, you do get to some (one
>or more) top. Having got there our experience is that this
>can be very useful in almost every situation as at a minimum a
>basis for analysis. I.e. trying to place the concepts of the
>requirements at hand into the upper ontology (data model in our
>case) and just asking the questions you need to do that has
>been valuable both in improving the quality and the compatibility
>of the systems delivered.
>
>Our experience is even that using a not very good upper level
>model, or only using it partly is better than using none at
>all.
>
>However, that is a bit different from having a "standard" ontology
>with the intention that everyone uses it. I think we might do
>better to develop one or more Reference ontologies of different
>flavours with a mapping between. But I have said this before
>and may propose it formally in a short while.

> > on!
> > !
> >  a demand-pull basis, add upperish-level concepts as they are
> > required.
> >
> > On the other hand, I am not so confident as Pat that the
> > whole effort is a waste, and doomed to fail. Having a SINGLE
> > upper ontology, may well fail, but having components to
> > choose from may well be useful. I still have a sense that the
> > way to proceed is as follows for a given domain, at whatever level.
> > 1. Identify important concepts.
> > 2. identify different ways to model them
> > 3. Split the differences into two categories:
> > a. Those that are superficial, and are mere choices with
> > minimal consequence, all may have the same models, for example.
> > b. Those that are IMPORTANT - such as those identified by Pat
> > in his survey of time representations.
> >
> > 4. For the non-important differences, choose one that seems
> > to work,even if it is arbitrary. Anyone who does not like it,
> > can choose to use their own, and map to/from it.
> > 5. For the important ones, develop independent and presumably
> > incompatible theories which one can choose from.
> >
> > None of this is necessarily new, having been said by various
> > folk in varous ways.
