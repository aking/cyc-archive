> Adam, Graham, Matthew, Pat, ...
> 
> Just to recall a question that arose again recently,
> and just to emphasize my "automatic answer" to it,
> one that I tried to advance in an earlier reply
> to Matthew, since the issue here seems to echo
> in its own special way that more generic case,
> here is a paradigm that I think you all know:
> 
> | If doing logic is hard, then we ought to write software to help.
> | If learning logic is hard, then we ought to write software to help.
> | If teaching logic is hard, then we ought to write software to help.
> 
> Now, I think that there is a rather obvious connection among 
> these tasks,
> since finding better ways to "do logic" might just contribute 
> to the job
> of finding better ways to learn and to teach logic.
> 
> Of course the term "logic" in this paradigm is really a "dummy" --
> in the nicest and the most technical sense of that description! --
> and so we might as well sign it as an "X", for which just about
> any other term -- within reason! -- might equally well discover
> itself to be substituted.  For instance, to extend the paradigm:
> 
> | If analyzing the raw data of sensuous experience and
> | reconciling the rampant diversity of symbolic usage
> | in order to arrive at a common ontology is hard --
> | though I cannot for the life of me imagine
> | why anybody here would think that! --
> | then we ought to build software
> | to help with that.
> 
> I think that it is very likely that we can no longer
> carry out any significant portion of this task, that
> would not be utterly redundant when measured against
> our own intellectutal histories, without developing
> the requisite software-supported conceptual frames,
> prior to beginning work on the actual ontologies,
> and hopefully, soon.  A language that is merely
> designed as a representational receptacle for
> the diurnal ('day-to-day') outputs of human
> laborers will never be capacious enough
> to relieve the strain of this task.
> And That Is My Analysis!
> 
> An Die Freude,
