ONT Let's forget about truth.

Seth Russell wrote:
> > From: "Jon Awbrey" <jawbrey@oakland.edu>
> 
> > without even trying to give a complete answer to that,
> > i would be willing to venture, as i've always ventured,
> > that one object of logic is "to denote objects", in the
> > many senses of the word "object", in the objective world.

This is incoherent.  It is an object of our use of names, definite
descriptions and the like to denote objects, but the practice of
denoting has nothing to do with logic.  Denotation per se, of course,
plays a *role* in logic -- predicate logic, anyway -- insofar as
denotations must be assigned to singular terms in a semantics for a
logical language.  But it makes no sense to say that the act of denoting
itself (what else does "to denote objects" mean?) is an object of logic,
as if logic somehow grounds, or aids in, the practice.

> And I am willing to venture that this denotation of objects is ~not~
> the goal of logic but a mere diversion, and is mostly a fantasy of pie
> in the sky.  Rather the goal of logic is to get agents to agree.

Well, that's a claim that at least has some history to it among the
pragmatists.  Though I'd have thought that guns were a far more
effective tool.  But the view is confused.  It is nice if people agree
because of the validity of the relevant arguments, but it is hardly the
*goal* of logic to get people to agree.  If it were, then logic would be
a subdiscipline of psychology; logical investigation would consist in
identifying those uses of language that, as a matter of empirical fact,
most effectively lead to agreement.  But logic is not an empirical
science, it is normative.  The goal of logic is to identify and
systematize principles of *correct* reasoning -- notably, inferential
principles that preserve truth -- just as ethics identifies and (when
possible) systematizes principles of correct action.  Logic *might* be
useful in getting people to agree -- though I know of a lot of sound
arguments that seem to be falling on deaf ears these days -- but it is
useful only if people at the outset are willing and able to follow its
dicates.

> Tis better to be in sync, than to be true.

Mullah Omar couldn't have put it better.

-chris
