I agree with the following statement by Pat Cassidy:

PC> If the SUO is not sufficiently general to be useful as
> the most fundamental set of concepts for NLU as well as other
> applications, then it will be very much less useful than it can
> be.  Natural Language Understanding is one of the potential
> applications mentioned in the original statement of purpose.
> I did not notice that it has been deleted from the list of goals
> for the SUO.  I believe that a mechanism for including context
> in definitions is essential for NLU, and thus for the SUO.

And my endorsement goes far beyond issues of developing NLP systems.
Every technical concept used in any field of science, business, or
engineering is also used in ordinary language, and exactly the same
issues about vagueness and polysemy occur in technical fields.

As examples, just consider all the meanings of "operating system"
or "programming language".  If you want something smaller, just
consider all the varying definitions of "assignment statement" in
different programming languages.  Even when those definitions are
specialized and formalized for a single version of a single language,
the next release of the next version of the language (or even a
different compiler for the "same language") is likely to violate them.

But I disagree with the following statement:

PC> I'm not at all sure that discussion of the "heap" paradox
> per se is useful to advancement of the SUO...

As an example, I'd like to cite the following example about the
distinction between a language, a dialect, and an idiolect.  It raises
the same kinds of issues as the paradox of the heap:  How many changes
in words, grammar rules, or pronuciation are sufficient to make one
language "different" from another?

Answer:  It all depends on who has the bigger army.

Linguists who deal with natural languages recognize that there is
a vague boundary between languages, dialects, and idiolects (dialects
that are spoken by a single individual).  Strictly speaking, no two
individuals ever speak exactly the same dialect, and even a single
person will change idiolect as he or she learns new words, habits of
speaking, etc.

On a larger scale, there are languages such as Swedish, Danish,
and Norwegian, which are mutually intelligible.  Then there are
dialects of Chinese, which is called a single language, even though
many of the dialects are not mutually intelligible to speakers who
have not spent some time learning each other's dialect.  In countries
such as the fragments of Yugoslavia, the distinctions between languages
and various dialects of each depends more on religion than on choice
of words, grammar, or pronunciation.

For reasons such as these, linguists have a half-serious, half-jocular
definition of the difference:  "A language is a dialect with an army."
In other words, where you draw the line is at least as much a political
decision as a scientific decision.
