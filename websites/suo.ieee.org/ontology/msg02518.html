>Folks,
>  Here's a practical question on the merged ontology.  We currently 
>have the axiom
>
>(<=>
>  (disjoint ?C1 ?C2)
>  (not
>    (thereExists (?I)
>      (and
>        (instance-of ?I ?C1)
>        (instance-of ?I ?C2)))))
>
>; two classes are disjoint if no individual exists which is a 
>instance of both classes and vice versa

That seems to be mathematically correct simply by definition of 'disjoint'.

>(see 
><http://ontology.teknowledge.com:8080/rsigma/SKB.jsp?req=SC&name=disjo 
>int&caseSensitive=on&skb=Merge>)
>
>This looks problematic to me

Why?

>and Ian and I have discussed changing it from bi-implication to 
>simple implication.  One could make a case for the above version if 
>classes are extension.  The counter argument (which could be 
>labelled an argument for intentional classes) would say that Equine 
>and SinglyHornedAnimal are not disjoint just because no one has 
>reified Unicorn in the knowledge base.

Adam, could you please define what you mean by 'reify in the 
knowledge base'? (Does this refer to some aspect of KIF proof theory?)

I think you are confusing disjointness with nonidentity. If you 
weaken the axiom in the way you suggest, it would be impossible to 
conclude that two classes were disjoint, on the grounds that 
something MIGHT show up which was in their intersection.

If you want to be able to talk about what might be true, then use a 
modal logic explicitly, and keep that part of the ontology sharply 
distinct from the parts that do not involve modal notions. 
Incorporating modality into the structural ontology would make the 
SUO worthless for all industrial and engineering applications.
