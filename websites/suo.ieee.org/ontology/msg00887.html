OK, if I understand you here, you are not objecting to the
OccurrentType/ContinuantType distinction; you're objecting to the
disjointness condition on these classes.  Furthermore, you're proposing that
we define axioms on these two classes regarding the inheritance of
properties.  Specifically, we would have axioms like the following:

(=>
     (and
     	    (instance-of ?X ContinuantType)
          (instance-of ?Y ?X) 
          (spatial-part-of ?Z ?Y))
     (instance-of ?Z ?X))

(=>
     (and
     	    (instance-of ?X OccurrentType)
          (instance-of ?Y ?X) 
          (temporal-part-of ?Z ?Y))
     (instance-of ?Z ?X))

Am I understanding you right?  If so, the first axiom seems plausible to me,
but the second does not.  For example, 'Walking' (understood as the class of
all walking events) would presumably be an instance of OccurrentType.
Furthermore, a particular walking event of mine might cover the distance
between Union Square and the Embarcadero (I live in San Francisco).
However, no part of this particular walking event will cover this entire
distance, and, thus, no proper part of the event will have one property
which is possessed by the event as a whole.  This seems like an obvious
counterexample, so maybe I'm missing something in your proposal.

Well, I'm not just concerned with "engineering cultures".  We need to be
attentive to any culture that is or could be modelled by engineers in
developing information systems that permit members of the culture to perform
certain tasks more efficiently/effectively.  For example, the subject
classification scheme used by an information retrieval system (e.g. a
bibliographic database) had better reflect the world as the culture sees it,
as their attitudes present it, or however you want to put it.  If the system
restricts itself to a set of scientific categories, then it's not going to
be of much value to society at large.  As for the particular example of case
roles, we do, I think, in our day-to-day interactions with the world think
and speak of things as agents, patients, recipients, etc.  Accordingly, an
information extraction system that does not employ this same stock of
categories is not going to be very useful when it comes to extracting
information from, e.g., newswire text.

I think we're going to have a tough time writing the axioms in any case, but
let's not get hung up on the epistemology/metaphysics bifurcation.
Philosophers don't even agree among themselves about how to divide
epistemology from metaphysics, since there are reams of debate about how the
inner and outer worlds interface.  For extreme relativists, it's all
epistemology; for behaviorists it's all metaphysics, on some construal of
metaphysics.  Once again, I think the goal here should be to devise a set of
concepts and axioms that allows us to say anything we would ever want to say
in a formal context, whether these concepts and axioms are cultural
constructs or reflective of the underlying fabric of the universe.
