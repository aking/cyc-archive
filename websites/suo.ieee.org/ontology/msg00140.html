Chris P., Chris M., et al.,

I consider the C & V book as an interesting study in topology
and its applications to the analysis and representation of
three-dimensional forms in space.  As a mathematical theory,
there is no need for a distinction of im/materiality.

When that theory is applied to some particular chunk of the
world, the mathematical surface is mapped to some region
of discontinuity in the "stuff" that makes up the world.

For a doughnut, the region of discontinuity is a thin
three-dimensional covering which has a preponderance of
air molecules on one side and fatty-dough molecules on the
other side.  When you dip it into coffee, the air molecules
are replaced by a slurry of fatty-dough-hot-water-sugar-coffee
molecules.

If you look closely, there is no immateriality, or for that
matter, materiality.  It's just a change in the density of
particles per unit volume.  And whether you consider that
change to be discontinuous depends on how coarse your
microscope adjustment happens to be.

When you get up close and personal to a doughnut, it becomes
hard to determine which molecules came from the doughnut,
which came from the hole, and which came from you.

John Sowa
