> > >
> > >MW: But I haven't seen (or at least not understood) anything yet that
> > >required more than the membership of something in some set to "do the
> > >business", plus some relation occurences (logic speak) and some
> > >axioms/rules.
> >
> > Well, Hobbs would argue that if you can something about x, then you
> > have to allow that x exists. Sounds reasonable? OK, consider:
> > 1. Joe went to the store.
> > 2. Joe went to the store quickly.
> > 3. Joe went to the store by driving the car slowly.
> > Hobbs says that 2 and 3 are consistent since even slow driving is a
> > fast way to get to the store. But he also says that being quick and
> > being slow aren't consistent with each other. So, he says, we are
> > forced into saying that there is something here which is slow - the
> > driving - and something ELSE which isn't slow - the going. So what
> > seems like one event (and occupies the same spatiotemporal envelope)
> > is in fact at least two 'things'. In fact it can be arbitrarily many
> > 'things' depending on how much you feel like saying about it. Notice,
> > this isn't a way of putting the same thing into a number of different
> > sets; it forces many different things to exist in order to be the
> > things that these various ways of talking can be *about*.
>
>MW: This sounds to me like needing to understand the context. You were
>saying elsewhere that with a linguistic approach you need to be able to
>juggle the way people can. In effect, the "object in context" becomes what
>you are describing, not the object simpliciter. To get away from that you
>need to make the context explicit, so that descriptions can be come absolute
>(quick and slow are relative words anyway). They can then be given a
>linguistic interpretation in context, once the context is established.

Yes, I entirely agree. In fact there is a way to transcribe all of 
Hobbs' axioms and schemas (he has all this stuff worked out in 
enormous detail) into a slightly odd version of FOL (one that ought 
to appeal to Jon Awbry) in which every open sentence can be seen as a 
relation name (so you can write things like
(R & Q)(a,b) to mean R(a,b) & Q(a,b) ), and all the 'things' in 
Hobbs' ontology become ways of talking about the things you or I 
would think were really there. (The cute part of this mapping is that 
one gets *exactly* the same conclusions, somewhat undercutting the 
ontological claims being made. Sorry, couldn't help a bit of public 
gloating there.)

So Im not urging this stuff on you, just to emphasise the point, I'm 
only telling you about it.
