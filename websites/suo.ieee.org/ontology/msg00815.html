> OK, on what basis do you decide what the appropriate place for it is

Well, there are really two different cases here.  One relates to the
subclassing or classification of a concept within the ontology, and the
other relates to the assignment of selectional restrictions to a predicate
within the ontology.  As for the first case, I looked for the most specific
subsumer of the given class.  For example, in merging the ITBM-CNR ontology
for natural kinds (essentially a taxonomy of biological organisms), I had to
connect its highest-level notion, viz. 'Organism', with something in Sowa's
ontology.  It seemed to me that every instance of Organism (in the ITBM-CNR
ontology) was also Organic (according to Sowa's construal).  Furthermore,
there didn't seem to be a more specific class than Organic to which every
Organism belongs.  As for selectional restrictions on predicates, I tended
to follow a fairly conservative policy here.  That is, I tried to make these
constaints as restrictive as possible.  My reasoning was that a predicate
whose application is too narrow is less likely, in general, to elicit
controversy than a predicate whose application is too broad.  Furthermore,
it is easier, especially within the context of a real knowledge-based
system, to relax selectional restrictions than to make them more
restrictive.  Accordingly, the current version of the merged ontology
requires that both arguments of the 'part-of' predicate be instances of
'CorpuscularObject', which is a subclass of 'Object', which, in turn, is a
subclass of 'Continuant'.

> Well, this is where I start to worry, since this is an area where we 
> have found very complex and tricky relationships between alternative

> translation between them becomes very complicated to state. Its not 
> impossible, let me emphasise, just *difficult* to get right; the 
> axioms of one have to be rewritten in a completely different form to

OK, point noted.  But it seems to me that these hypothetical
incommensurabilities can be addressed on a case-by-case basis as they are
realized and noted.  I can't think of some sort of reasonable policy that
would prevent these sorts of incompatibilities from creeping in, as long as
the strategy is one of merging/aligning the various extant high-level
conceptual schemas.  I know you have objections to this general strategy,
but I don't think you have proposed an alternative.  Note too that an
alternative is not something like:  "Let's try to get the 'foundations'
right before we go any further".  Western philosophy has been working on the
foundations for 2,500 years now, and we're still anxiously awaiting the
moment when two philosophers agree on any epistemological or metaphysical
point.  We don't have time to wait around for another 2,500 years, and we
need to constantly bear in mind (I would argue) that the goal is to produce
an engineering artifact, not to get straight about the nature of the
universe.
