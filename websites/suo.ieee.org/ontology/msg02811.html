> If the observer is concerned about whether the team wins or loses a game,

> roles on the team, etc.

The point that I am on is more along the lines of:  The concept of a team
(in the sense of a sports team) has to do intrinsically with the intent of
sporting competition.  This is composed of a whole set of interlocking
issues of intent, such as entertainment, physical fitness, sublimation of
aggression, etc.  The intent of a professional sports team has significant
overlap with that of an organized youth team, with in turn overlaps
significantly with a team chosen ad hoc to run a three-legged race at a
family picnic.  Yet each of these types of teams has significant
differentiation on the dimensions of intent, as well.  There's a rich vein
of ontology to be found here, and it's more interesting to me to think that
this effort will mine it to some level, rather than reducing it to a dry
agent/not-agent distinction.
