Pat wrote:
> [Jon wrote:]
> >"All sets are classes" is exactly what
> >most "logic and maths people" (LAMP's)
> >whom I have ever known would say.
> 
> Sorry, we are getting our communication wires crossed by a pun on 
> "class". Mathematicians use the word in a very particular way, which 
> in its full form is written "proper class", to refer to things that 
> anyone but a mathematican (and even some of them) would call a set, 
> except they are so big that if they are accepted into the 
> set-theoretic universe they would give rise to paradoxes.  

Yes, this is often true, though in (some presentations of) set theories
like VNBG in which you can talk about and quantify over proper classes,
"class" encompasses both sets and proper classes.  Proper classes are
simply classes that are not members of other classes.

> In the wider word, however, the term "class" is used in a sense which
> is closely linked to "classification", to refer to (the extension of)
> a named category in some kind of classification heirarchy. Such
> classes all have sets as their extension (or, maybe, ARE sets), but
> not every set would normally be dignified with the title of
> 'class(ification)'

Well, depending on your underlying class theory, the top class, for
example, might have a proper class as its extension.

> >I will assume here that "pure sets" are what non-modern
> >non-ontologists call "classes", and try to read on with that in mind.
> >Let me know ...
> 
> Not proper classes. Put aside the mathematical usage of "set/class"
> terminology for now. I was using the admittedly odd term "'pure' set"
> (perhaps "set simpliciter" would have been better) just to mean 'set';

Probably would have.  Set theorists usually use "pure set" to refer to
sets whose transitive closures contain only sets (and hence which are
"built up" ultimately from the empty set alone).  "Impure sets", then,
are those that contain at least one nonset, or "urelement", in their
transitive closure.

> Do not read "class" as meaning "proper class" in the mathematical
> sense, but rather as "classification". I don't have an exact
> definition of a "class" in this sense, and I doubt if there is one;
> nevertheless the term is in widespread usage. It seems to correspond
> roughly to "natural kind", which also has no usabel definition.
> Examples of classes might be: Human, Animal, Abstract,... but sets
> such as the one that contains even integers, the Eiffel tower and my
> aunties would not normally be called "classes".

C = {x : EvenInt(x)} U {EiffelTower} U {x : AuntieOf(x,pat)}?  Surely
"class" is closed under finite unions, Pat!  :-)
