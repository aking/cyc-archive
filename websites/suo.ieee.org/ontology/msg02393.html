Hi Jon,

I am developing a representation called the Information Flow Framework (IFF)
for ontologies and their models using the new-KIF. More specifically, my
chief goal is to formalize the *truth classification* and the *truth concept
lattice*.

The truth concept lattice is generated from the truth classification of a
first-order language L -- whose instances are L-structures, whose types are
L-sentences, and whose classification relation is satisfaction. I would
guess that the truth concept lattice is a precise mathematical version of
John Sowa's lattice of theories [http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg03804.html].
In place of John's theories are the formal concepts of this lattice. A
formal concept in this lattice has an intent which is a closed theory (set
of sentences) and an extent which is the collection of all models for that
theory. The theory (intent) of the join (sup) of two concepts is the closure
of the intersection of the theories (conceptual intents), and the theory
(intent) of the meet (inf) of two concepts is the theory of the common
models. See the following links: [http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg01758.html],
[http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg02950.html], and
[http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg03839.html].

To represent this truth classification I must represent the truth formal
objects (L-structures or models), the truth formal attributes (L-sentences
or expressions) and the truth incidence relation (satisfaction). I have done
this theoretically, but have only partially completed the encoding into the
new KIF. Take for example, the representation of a first order structure or
model. Conceptuall, this is a 2-dimensional mathematical structure that uses
the hypergraph notion along one dimension and the classification notion
along the other dimension. I have written new-KIF ontologies for hypergraphs
and classifications, and it should be easy to put these together for the
new-KIF ontology for models.

Now all of this representation is at the metalevel (or structural level) of
the SUO. And in particular, this metalevel factors very nicely into two
levels with distinct objectives, which correspond to the set-theoretic
notions of sets and classes, respectively. The object level ontologies would
not be directly coded in the new KIF, but would use the language specified
in the lower metalevel consisting of the notions and terminology of models,
typed expressions, classifications, hypergraphs, etc. However, in my
metalevel encoding efforts I have noticed the following property that speaks
to the foundations of all this.

PROPERTY: The new-KIF axiomatization for spans (and hypergraphs) is strictly
category-theoretic -- in the axiomatization of spans all axioms are
expressed in terms of category-theoretic (in my approach, foundational)
notions, such as the composition and identity of large (class to class)
functions or the pullback of small sets, and no axioms use explicit logical
KIF connectives or quantification! This seems to be a hallmark of the
ontologies in the lower-level structure (the lower metalevel) -- the
ontologies for true categories (not quasi-categories), whose object and
morphism collections are classes (not conglomerates). In addition, no axioms
in the Span Ontology use explicit KIF terms from the basic KIF ontology.

This property, and my attempt to realize it for the lower metalevel
Classification Ontology, prompts the following question: should this
metalevel representation have a set-theoretic foundation or a
category-theoretic foundation? As you would expect, these are contentious
issues. For instance, look at the following exchanges on the category list
[http://www.risc.uni-linz.ac.at/research/category/risc/catlist/goedel-cat].
In particular, my problem with the universal set and functions is probably
the same mentioned by Colin McLarty in that exchange: "Anyway, the problem
here is that functions are hard to work with in set theory with a universal
set." So perhaps I should heed his advice and consider following the
foundational approach described in Benabou's theory of fibrations and
definability [Benabou J. (1985). "Fibered categories and the foundations].

Robert E. Kent
