DM:  This brings up exactly one of my points of confusion about Peirce.
     You have used the phrase "discussion and thought" a couple of times,
     in a way that seems to want to conflate them.  In an earlier stab at
     understanding sign vs. interpretant, I stated that I thought sign was
     about utterance and interpretant (sign) was about bringing the sign,
     and the object of discussion together in the mind of the interpreter.
     Notice that I didn't say "human mind" just then, nor did I on that
     other occasion, though more than one reader interpreted me to have
     done so.  Before I could correct that impression, a cascade of
     signs washed over the bow.

But this is very typical of the brand of misunderstanding that arises
in a particular sort of discussion and has very little to do with any
peculiar difficulties that might be imagined to affect the subject or
the theory of sign relations.  So let us try to address it in generic
terms.  The problem comes up whenever we have to discuss a subject by
way of examples, based on whatever scattering or smattering of points
we can toss in the ring of discussion to illustrate its circumference,
rather than fully discursively, by painting the ring itself in bright
glowing colors of linguisitic contingencies.  I guess that a familiar
case is provided by a teacher who picks up a piece of chalk and draws
the figure of a triangle on the slate before our eyes, speaking words
to the effect:  "Consider an arbitrary triangle."  Now we know better
than to think that the teacher intends us to contemplate this special
arrangement of chalkdust on the board, nor even a particular abstract
object that has the same exact shape, but that the dust and the image
are intended only to remind us of an abstract object which is insofar
as we know it a triangle and nothing more.

Likewise, in discussing the pure form of a sign relation,
the sort of thing that lies some distance from our human,
all too human imagination, I am 'faute de mieux' obliged
to begin with materiel that is more akin to our concrete,
all too concrete senses and sensibilities.  Consequently,
given our familiarity, intimate if not always reflective
or critical, that we have with the conduction of our own
streams of consciousness and the local motion of our own
trains of thought, our own thinking processes afford the
study of signs in general with an ample and ready supply
of fuel.  But these ample examples never outrun or leave
behind their purely exemplary features, and however well
they do indeed inform the subject, they only inscribe it
from the inner side, and never define its outer boundary.

DM:  I understand about reserved words, and I'm careful to use them carefully

In the pragmatic theory of signs, the words "object", "sign", "interpretant"
are defined as three roles that things may take up in a 3-adic sign relation.
In extensional terms, they are just the column headings on a specified type
of 3-column data table, one that satisfies the specs of a certain definition.
The guy who set this theory on its merry way did have a certain respect for
ancient traditions, and so the term "object", derived from the typical Latin
translation for the Greek word "pragma", with all of its echoes of "praxis",
was not your average newfangled whim.  (By the way, "newfangled" is as old
as Chaucer, at least).

DM:  Please note that I did not use "pragmatic", for the same reason.
     I like to use that word as a close synonym to practical, which
     does not seem to be the philosophical definition.

It's an old saw of pragmatic thinkers

communication, concept formation, learning, reasoning, and
ontology building -- yes, we build them, they do not come
for free.

Oh, okay, I am not saying that this factor, context of utterance,
is not a factor in communication and interpretation.  I am only

situation at a younger age, when I was so much sharper than
I am now, and what I eventually discovered, like so many
of my best ideas, to be already written down by Peirce.
And that is just that 3-adic sign relation itself is
the main repository of all the effects of context,
that context is just one of the many factors that
can be conceived to affect what the interpretant,
of a sign with respect to an object, actually is.
In sum, the sign relation itself affords us with
a minimal adequate representation of the data that
we need for any investigation of contextual effects.

I start out from the blocks of one who is annoyed by doubts and
irritated by uncertainties, and who endeavors to clear these up.
The subsequent proceeding of clarification is, by dint of a long
tradition, conveniently summed up under the heading of "inquiry".
The relation of this enterprise to the business of constructing
ontologies is a matter that I have addressed on many occasions,
and no doubt will again, but the phrase that most frequently
comes to mind is this one from John Dewey's 'How We Think':

| In any event, it is desirable that the teacher
| should rid himself of the notion that "thinking"
| is a single, unalterable faculty;  that he should
| recognize that it is a term denoting the various ways
| in which things acquire significance.
|
| http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg05116.html

This idea, that "thinking" -- for which I read "inquiry", in accord with
the context of the utterance and of its applications, both here and there --
is "a term denoting the various ways in which things acquire significance",
is a key to the evolving relationships among things as we come to call them
and the process by which we arrive at all of our concepts and terms, which,
as I keep trying to remind people, are not invented out of whim and do not
fall from the sky in some Adamaic/Mosaic manner, but need to be shaped to
fit the situation of their instigation, thus to find or to make a solution
to some very old problem or some very new question.  Without understanding
the process by which concepts are actually conceived we can only continue
in a host of false conceits.
