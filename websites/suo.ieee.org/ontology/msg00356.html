Pat,

Just one last observation:

>I agree, this isnt a debate about datastructures. Herbrand's theorem 
>can be read as showing that any first-order semantics can be 
>'imitated' by one constructed entirely from textual surrogates, in 
>the sense that a sentence is satisfiable in one sense iff it is 
>satisfiable in the other.

Yes, but...  You can interpret Herbrand's theorem in two ways:

 1. Surrogates are unnecessary because you can do everything
    without them (in the same sense that you can build list
    structures from sequences of "objects" or sequences of
    "pointers" to objects).

 2. Surrogates are valuable because they give you more
    syntactic options without introducing any metaphysical
    baggage (i.e., they don't blunt Ockham's razor).  In
    programming languages, pointers can often be used to
    implement complex data structures in simpler ways; e.g.,
    the compilers or interpreters for every object-oriented
    language are "actually" implemented with pointers under
    the covers.

So both points of view can be useful for different reasons,
and I would like to preserve the option of switching from
one to the other when convenient.
