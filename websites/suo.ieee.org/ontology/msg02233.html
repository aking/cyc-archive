Chris Menzel wrote:
> 
> On Fri, 20 Apr 2001, Frederick N. Chase wrote:
> > Chris Menzel wrote:
> > >
> > <snip>
> > > ..............................  Of course, I think what you *really*
> > > want is the purely generalized form of this axiom, of which the above
> > > is just an instance:
> > >
> > > (forall (?x ?y ?z)
> > >         (<=> (gives ?x ?y ?z)
> > >              (exists (?e)
> > >                      (and (giving ?e)
> > >                           (giver ?e ?x)
> > >                           (receiver ?e ?y)
> > >                           (given ?e ?z)
> > >                           (forall (?e')
> > >                                   (=> (and (giving ?e')
> > >                                            (giver ?e' ?x)
> > >                                            (receiver ?e' ?y)
> > >                                            (given ?e' ?z))
> > >                                       (= ?e ?e')))))))
> >
> > Now I think we're getting there.
> > But even more is needed after the "<=>" to assure that:
> > 1) The same giving does not have more than one giver.
> > 2) The same giving does not have more than one given
> > 3) The same giving does not have more than one receiver.
> 
> Well, sure, add all the details you want.  The point was to illustrate the

These are not details. They are part of the meaning of (gives ?x ?y ?z).

> correct approach to capturing the idea that standing in the giving
> relation is equivalent to playing a certain role in a certain unique
> event.
> 
> That said, can't more than one person give more than one thing to more
> than one person in a giving event?

Not if the type of giving event is (gives ?x ?y ?z).

> 
> > (I'm not quite up to writing what I want in KIF, with certainty.
> > Perhaps someone will step in and add the gory detail.)
> 
> Setting aside the question about multiple givers, givens, and receivers
> (not to mention temporality -- can't there be multiple givings involving
> the same three individuals?):
> 
> (forall (?e ?x ?y)
>         (=> (giving ?e)
>             (=> (or (and (giver ?e ?x)
>                          (giver ?e ?y))
>                     (and (receiver ?e ?x)
>                          (receiver ?e ?y))
>                     (and (given ?e ?x)
>                          (given ?e ?y)))
>                 (= ?x ?y))))
> 
> > So,
> >
> > 1) The <=> axiom above is invalid
> 
> I think you just mean that it does not adequately capture the intuitive
> understanding of the "gives" relation.  In logic, "validity" and its
> cognates are usually used to indicate certain properties of arguments.
> 
> > and probably could be used to prove a contradiction.
> 
> Certainly it could not, at least not by itself, as it is easily shown to
> be consistent.
> 
> > 2) An augmented <=> axiom like the one above but disallowing two
> > receivers, etc., might not lead to contradictions and a generalization
> > of it might be OK in the SUO.
> 
> If the augmented axiom doesn't lead to contradictions, surely the
> unaugmented one doesn't, as it is logically weaker.  You seem to be
> confusing failure to assert P with asserting that P is false.  From the
> fact that the unaugmented axiom does not say that there is exactly one
> giver (receiver, given) in a giving event, it does not follow that it is
> asserting that there there is *not* exactly one giver (receiver, given).
> 
> > 3) I certainly would not personally want to call the change from what
> > precedes the <=> to what follows the <=> a "reduction".
> 
> Neither would I.  Do I suggest otherwise?
