> I went back and read John's note again in the light of your remarks.
> The business of identifying assumptions and distinctions is a very
> important step for me.  I often find myself describing it under the
> three rubrics of "articulation", "reflection", and "relativization".
> By this I mean the kind of reflection on our autonomous experience
> and automatic conduct that allows us to articulate and express in
> explicit signs the sorts of beliefs which we just a moment before
> were taking for granted, perhaps never before even conscious of
> them, and thus to re-express formerly absolute beliefs as being
> contingent or relative beliefs.  This reminds me of what we do
> in logic when we shift from "A |- B" to "|- A => B", that is,
> if you consider the statements before the turnstile as being
> analogous in a way to unexamined or taken for granted ideas.
> 
> At the moment when I start to reflect, I usually find that
> I have been engaged in some stream of unthinking conduct,
> and what it is that ripples this stream and causes me to
> reflect is almost always one of two types of alteration,
> change, difference, dissatisfaction, disturbance, jolt,
> shift, shock, or strife that occurs within my experience.
> Notice that all of these things have in common the aspect
> or the idea of a "difference", and it is only here that we
> pass from a sense of all-pervading confluence in experience
> to a sense of boundaries, definitions, domains, and limits.
> 
> But I said that I could see these experiences of difference
> falling mainly into a couple of types.  There is what I call
> a "surprising phenomenon", which involves a difference between
> what one expects and what one observes.  There is what I call
> a "problematic situation", which involves a difference between
> what one intends and what one observes.  Each of these serves
> as a spur to a different sort of action.
> 
> A surprising phenomenon leads to a search for the sort
> of theoretical statement that will serve as an explanation
> for the phenomenon, thereby decreasing the disparity between
> the actual and the expected events and reducing the affective
> sense of surprise that accompanies the phenomenon.
> 
> A problematic situation leads to a search for the sort
> of practical policy that will serve a plan of action
> for resolving the problem, by bringing the actual
> and the desired states back into accord.
> 
> These are the themes that come to mind when I think
> of the nature, the occasion, and the springs of the
> kind of reflection that is needed to fund a critical
> examination of the automatic assumptions, the basic
> beliefs, the concepts, and the distinctions in our
> experience that constitute what we commonly call
> a "domain".

> > A. Analyzing a subject domain.

Okay, when you say "subject domain" I am probably going to be
thinking "phenomenon" or "problem" in the field of experience.
And when you say "analysis" I am probably going to be reminded
that our first address, analysis, approach, approximation, and
articulation of a phenomenon or a problem is likely to do very
little toward clearing up the muddle in which we find ourselves,
but merely to transpose it into the key, the register, the scale,
and the time signature of a discrete verbal notation.

I have a picture like this:

   P1
   ^
   |    P2
   |    ^
   .    |    P3
   S1   |    ^
        .    |
        S2   |
             .
             S3

Here, the Sj are Subjects and the Pj are Predicates,
as in some rudimentary syllogistic scheme, only there
are lots more of them and lots more criss-crossing lines
of implication that run between them, in technical terms,
thoroughly "muddling up the scene".

These are just the facts, man, but their observation
in such a raw condition hardly does much to help the
interloper get down to the brassier taxa of clarity.

What next?  Well, consider the following generic element of structure:

---------------------------------------------------------------------

          Z                     
          •
          |\
          | \
          |  \
          |   \
          |    \  Rule
          |     \
          |      \
          | A   > \
          |  \ /   \
    Fact  | <-¤-D   • Y
          |  / \   /
          | I   > /
          |      /
          |     /
          |    /  Case
          |   /
          |  /
          | /
          |/
          •
          X

Figure 1.  Basic Structure & Terminology

---------------------------------------------------------------------

Legend 1.

Deduction takes a Case, the minor premiss of the form X => Y,
matches it with a Rule, the major premiss of the form Y => Z,
then adverts to a Fact, the bound outcome of the form X => Z.

Induction takes a Case of the form X => Y,
matches it with a Fact of the form X => Z,
then adverts to a Rule of the form Y => Z.

Abduction takes a Fact of the form X => Z,
matches it with a Rule of the form Y => Z,
then adverts to a Case of the form X => Y.

Even more succinctly:

           Abduction  Deduction  Induction

Premiss:     Fact       Rule       Case
Premiss:     Rule       Case       Fact
Outcome:     Case       Fact       Rule

Further discussion of this Figure and Legend can be found at:

http://ltsc.ieee.org/logs/suo/msg00676.html

---------------------------------------------------------------------

The gist of all of this syllogistic in-formation lies in the lesson that,
before one can even think about doing any order of demonstrative reasoning,
in other words, deduction, one must first have primed the pump with a modicum
of middle terms that will serve to fund the reasoning process in the interval
encompassed by that all too apophantom gape that lies beteen the predicates
and the subjects that are given to us by the "data of the senses" (DOTS).

The business of looking at a mess of P's and S's as given by the DOTS,
and trying to find the right source of M's that can serve as a medium
to ameliorate the muddle of the situation, all of that is what comes
to mind under the heading of "analysis" for me.

Once you do come to have a rich enough channel, source, or vein of middle terms,
that are fit to mediate the right explanations for a whole panorama of phenomena,
that are fit to negotiate the right plans of action for a whole panoply of problems,
then the business of analysis becomes an almost mundane affair, a routine matter of
dealing with the facts as they occur by way of the proto-type of non-demonstrative
reasoning that is called "abductive" and by way of the post-type of non-demonstrative
reasoning that is called "inductive" by the garden variety of pragmatic nomenclators.

To make this long story as short as I can, the type of analysis of facts, like S=>P,
of the sort that serves to fund all future Ab-/De-/In-Ductions, shapes up like this:

          P
          •
          |\
          | \
          |  \
          |   \
          |    \  Rule
          |     \
          |      \
          |       \
          |        \
    Fact  | ----->  • M
          |        /
          |       /
          |      /
          |     /
          |    /  Case
          |   /
          |  /
          | /
          |/
          •
          S

That's as far as I can get for now.
