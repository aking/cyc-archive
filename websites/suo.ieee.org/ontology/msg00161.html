Dietrich,

>Do the dead members belong to it? [a community]

Definitely, as long as their sayings are being read,
quoted, and repeated within a particular community.
In my community, Aristotle and Peirce are more alive
than most 20th century philosophers.

>Aren't you playing here the physicist's language game
>with a kind of hidden untertone ("if you look closely")
>that it is the superior one?

I consider the physicist's language game to be one very
important game, but certainly not the only one, and for much
of our daily lives, it is not even the most relevant one.

But when you get down to the fundamental issues of ontology
as opposed to epistemology, the physicists are closer to the
fundamentals (what exists) than most "commonsense" theories.

Bottom line:  The SUO must be able to accommodate all language
games used in everyday life and in all the specialties and
subspecialties of every field of science, engineering, business,
and the arts.  It must also be able to relate them to one
another, to the phenomenology of human perception, and to the
established laws of the more specialized sciences.

That might sound like a tall task, and indeed it is.  But as
various discussions on SUO list have indicated, nothing less
can meet the requirements.

John Sowa
