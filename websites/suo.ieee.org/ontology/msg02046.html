I have decided to recast the Case Analysis Principle
as the "Case Analysis-Synthesis Theorem" (CAST) thus
to keep constantly before our minds the circumstance
that the logical equation it expresses works equally
well in either direction, and even if it often seems
that we have many fewer occasions for synthesis than
we do for analysis, the very nature of the case lets
us in on the fact that there must of necessity be an
equal number of relevant opportunities of both types.
This phenomenon, the apparent disparity between each
pan of what is evidently an equilibrial balance, can
be quite puzzling at first, but my guess is now that
this particular illusion is due solely to the rather
un-precipitate distraction that arises from elements
dissolved in solution being much less obvious before
the mind's eye than the very same elements when they
are precipitated out in a too, too solid fleshy form.

We are now at the threshold of a model-theoretic way
to go about satisfying ourselves of the truth or the
falsity or everything else of contingency in between
of any propositional expression that comes before us.
But at this point, I think that the best way to move
forward is to pause from this headlong formal flight,
and to take up a sampling of concrete, discrete, and
felicitously facile examples, demonstrating how this
CAST might be directed to exercise its powers and to
augment ours on a set set with representative agonia.

What better way to begin, then,
than with the Splendid Theorem,
so dubbed by Leibniz, who will
safely be estimated as knowing
splendor whenever he sights it,
devolved on to us by John Sowa,
who also knows.

You have seen one of way of proving this theorem,
and the record of it is recorded again hereafter,
but let us use the CAST that we have more lately,
if just a bit roughly, established, to see still
another way of satisfying ourselves of this fact:
that its truth, itself, is always ever satisfied.

What we have arrived at is the arboreal equivalent of
a "disjunctive normal form" (DNF) for the proposition
with which we started.  Remembering that a blank node
is the graphical equivalent of a logical value "true",
and taking the abbreviation "iwc" for "in which case",
we can read this brand of DNF in the following manner:

| either [not p] iwc [true]
| orelse [p]     iwc
|        either [not q] iwc [true]
|        orelse [q]     iwc
|               either [not r] iwc [true]
|               orelse [r]     iwc
|                      either [not s] iwc [true]
|                      orelse [s}     iwc [true]

And that is as much to state that the proposition
being submitted for analysis is true in each case.
Ergo we are justly entitled to title it "Theorem".

---------------------------------------------------
 Evaluation Rule
---------------------------------------------------
|
| The formal rule of evaluation for a k-operator is:
|
| ( x1, x2, ..., xk )  =  [blank]
|
| iff
|
| Just one of the arguments x1, x2, ..., xk  =  ()
|
---------------------------------------------------
 Interpretation Rule
---------------------------------------------------
|
| A "lobe operator" of the form "(x<1>, ..., x<k>)"
| enjoys two commonly employed interpretations for
| propositional logic, that is, ways of reading it
| as an assertion about, or a constraint upon, the
| logical values of the ordered arguments, that is,
| the mentioned variables x<j> for j = 1 through k.
|
| Existential Interpretation:
|
|     "Just one of the k arguments is false."
|
| Entitative  Interpretation:
|
|     "Not just one of the k arguments is true."
|
¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤

Let Q be a proposition with an unspecified, but context-suitable
number of variables, say, none, or x, or x<1>, ..., x<k>, as the
case may be.  (See what I mean?  I should've said "sentence Q".)

Let the "replacement expression" of the form "Q[o/x]" denote
the proposition that results from Q by replacing every token
of the variable x with a blank, which is to say, erasing "x".

Let the "replacement expression" of the form "Q[|/x]" denote
the proposition that results from Q by replacing every token
of the variable x with a stick stemming from the site of "x".

In the case of a proposition Q, that is to say,
an expression of it, not having a token of the
specified variable x, let it be convened that
that Q[o/x] = Q = Q[|/x].

Informal Justification:

To see this, if x is true, then it makes no difference
on either side of the equation, but if x is false, then
both sides are false.  There can be only one, remember.

NB.  The argument that I just gave for this form of distributive law
depended on the truth-functional interpretation of the lobe operator,
so this marks a patch that will need to be fixed up at a later point.

One theorem of frequent use is what I like to call
the "Weed and Seed" Theorem.  The proof is just an
inductive exercise, so I will let it go till later,
but it says that a label can be freely distributed
or freely erased (retracted or withdrawn) anywhere
in a subtree whose root is labeled with that label.
The second theorem on the list to be shown here is
actually the base case of this Weed & Seed theorem.
I talk about it under the LOF name of "Generation".
