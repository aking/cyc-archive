Jon,

>So I will have to stick to the Zeroth Order of Alpha Numb Logic for a while
--
>I honestly believe that if we can't make it there we can't make it anywhere.


Perhaps.  But propositional logic may be so simplified that
some of the most crucial distinctions, such as fixing the
reference to particular entities in the world, may not be
apparent.

In Polya's little book, _How to Solve It_, one suggestion is
to look at a simplified special case.  But another suggestion
is to look at a more general case.  Each approach highlights
different aspects of a problem, and they complement each other.

>...Does anybody seriously think that we are anywhere near the point of
>being able to webicize tangles of receiptful meaning such as these [Shakespeare
et al.]?

There is a difference between getting a computer to "understand"
a Shakespearean play and getting it to distinguish a use of
the word "Hamlet" as a reference to a character or to a printed
edition of a play.  There has been a lot of good work on
distinguishing metonymy such as this, and in a large number
of useful cases, it can be done.  Computers are much better
than people in detecting ambiguities, even when they cannot
resolve them -- primarily because people are so good at
recognizing and interpreting metaphors and metonyms that they
don't even notice that an ambiguity exists.

But if an unresolvable ambiguity does occur, a computer
system can just put up a menu and ask "Which do you mean?"
