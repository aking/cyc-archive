John's suggestion is a good one.    However, whether
"relation" and "relationship" are to be synonymous seems to
be left open.    It seems to me that we should read them
synonymously (a conclusion based both on John's discussion and
my prior vunderstanding).  As WordWeb says:

"('relationship' is often used where 'relation' would serve (as in "the
relationship between inflation and unemployment")) preferred usage
of 'relationship' is for human relations or states of relatedness."

 It seems that in logic, math, and database talk we often use
"relationship" where "relation" would serve just to make our prose less
boring.

Another issue that was raised in the prior correspondence
is the question of "what is a  predicate (or relation)" versus what is
an entity or thing.   There was some discussion of reification earlier
(that I didn't read because...).      I don't know a simple way to
explain this --- as a mathematician (or at least a former mathematician)
I don't think I need a 'real' explanation, just some rules to operate
by.   Nonetheless, when converting natural language statements into
FOL, there can be a lot of confusion, and more than one way to do it.
The fact that many predicates and events have names that are
related in natural language makes  explaining the difference more
difficult.     For example,  (at least in one possible ontology )   "wet"
(as in "the street is wet from the rain") is a predicate, while
"the wetting of the street" describes a "wetting" event whose
"patient" is "the street", That is, "wetting" is  a thing, while "wet" is
a predicate.   (Yes, it was raining when I drove to work this morning.)

I recently read a paper on deontic logic that made significant use of
mapping back and forth between the domain of quantification (the wetting
of the street) and the available predicates (the wetness of the street),
but,
unfortunately, I can't find the reference right now.

Graham and Pat,

To avoid endless problems caused by mismatched definitions,
I suggest that we adopt the following equivalences between
terms:

 1. The terms "relation" and "relationship" in ordinary
    English have multiple meanings.  But the use in both
    symbolic logic and in relational databases is firmly
    established.  I recommend that we restrict all uses
    of those two words to the meanings in logic and RDBMS.

 2. In RDB, a relation is defined by a table.  The number
    of columns in the table is critical:  a binary or dyadic
    relation has two columns, a ternary or triadic relation
    has three columns, etc.  Each row of the table is
    called a tuple or a relationship.

 3. In ordinary language, a relationship (row of a table)
    normally relates two or more entities.  But for generality,
    it is convenient to allow tables with just one column.
    To avoid saying that a one-column table defines a relation,
    some people prefer to call them properties or attributes.

 4. In logic, the terms predicate and relation are normally
    used as synonyms.  For those people who feel uneasy about
    calling a one-column table a relation, one could say that
    it defines a monadic or unary predicate.  Then the term
    predicate can be used to represent tables with 1 or more
    columns.

 5. The terms property and attribute tend to get very confusing
    and people keep arguing about whether attributes are
    entities.  To avoid such arguments, it is best to use a
    single word such as predicate, with an additional adjective
    to specify the number of arguments:  monadic, dyadic,
    triadic, or n-adic, if you count in Greek; or unary,
    binary, ternary, or n-ary, if you count in Latin.

Many people have different preferences in their use of
terminology, and I don't want to limit anybody's stylistic
preferences.  But for ease of communication, I would recommend
that we use the word "predicate" with a qualifier to specify
the number of arguments as the base term.  To visualize the
values of a predicate, you can think of it as a table in an
RDBMS (but with the option of having infinitely many rows, in
the case of predicates defined over the integers or even the
real numbers).

The words "property" and "attribute" have been used in many
confusing ways.  I suggest that we use them only as synonyms
for monadic (or unary) predicate.
