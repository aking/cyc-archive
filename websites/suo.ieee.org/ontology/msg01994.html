John F. Sowa wrote:
> 
> Jon,
> 
> > So I will have to stick to the Zeroth Order of Alpha Numb Logic
> > for a while -- I honestly believe that if we can't make it there
> > we can't make it anywhere.
> 
> Perhaps.  But propositional logic may be so simplified that
> some of the most crucial distinctions, such as fixing the
> reference to particular entities in the world, may not be
> apparent.

And also certain illusions, like the idea that we can
automatically refine a "particular" to an "individual",
do not there so easily become so insidiously entrenched.

> In Polya's little book, _How to Solve It_, one suggestion is
> to look at a simplified special case.  But another suggestion
> is to look at a more general case.  Each approach highlights
> different aspects of a problem, and they complement each other.

B is more general than R.  It is encumbered by fewer axioms.

> > ... Does anybody seriously think that we are anywhere near
> > the point of being able to webicize tangles of receiptful
> > meaning such as these [Shakespeare et al.]?
> 
> There is a difference between getting a computer
> to "understand" a Shakespearean play and getting
> it to distinguish a use of the word "Hamlet" as
> a reference to a character or to a printed edition
> of a play.  There has been a lot of good work on
> distinguishing metonymy such as this, and in
> a large number of useful cases, it can be done.
> Computers are much better than people in detecting
> ambiguities, even when they cannot resolve them --
> primarily because people are so good at recognizing
> and interpreting metaphors and metonyms that they
> don't even notice that an ambiguity exists.

This is a little bit like saying that folks with weak
immune systems are better at detecting health threats,
or that canaries are better at detecting toxic gases.

> But if an unresolvable ambiguity does occur, a computer
> system can just put up a menu and ask "Which do you mean?"

You mean like this one:

> > Questionnaire:  "Which do you mean?"
> >
> > 1.  I regard the meaning of a sign or expression to be:
> >
> >     a.  its denotation  (= object, referent, bedeutung).
> >     b.  its connotation (= interpretant, sense, sinn).
> >     c.  either of the above.
> >     d.  none of the above.
> >     e.  other (go to essay section).
> >
> > 2.  I regard a proposition, in use, not mention, to be:
> >
> >     a.  a sign (= expression, sentence), either token or type,
> >                not excluding the interpretants of other signs.
> >     b.  an object, either abstract or concrete, token or type,
> >                just for example, the denotation of a sentence.
> >     c.  either of the above.
> >     d.  none of the above.
> >     e.  other (go to essay section).

I am afraid that the problem is rather with getting any human being
to pick just one entre from such menu -- so far in the SUO group,
I have never seen it happen, nor have I been able yet to get
a straight answer from anybody here, and doesn't it always
seem to go that the people who affect being fussy about
ambiguities are the pickiest eaters in this regard,
stubbornly maintaining their own excesses and
vacillations while hawking their low-fat
diets to others?

Jon Awbrey
