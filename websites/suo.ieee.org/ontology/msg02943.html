> and:
> 
> | CP 4.332  [Subjectal Abstraction = Hypostatic Abstraction]
> |
> | ... The logical term 'subjectal abstraction' here requires a word
> | of explanation;  for there are few treatises on logic which notice
> | subjectal abstraction under any name, except so far as to confuse
> | it with precisive abstraction which is an entirely different logical
> | function.  When we say that the Columbia library building is 'large',
> | this remark is a result of precisive abstraction by which the man who
> | makes the remark leaves out of account all the other features of his
> | image of the building, and takes [to represent the size] the word
> | "large" which is entirely unlike that image -- and when I say the
> | word is unlike the image, I mean that the general signification
> | of the word is utterly disparate from the image, which involves
> | no predicates at all.  Such is 'precisive abstraction'.  But now
> | if this man goes on to remark that the largeness of the building is
> | very impressive, he converts the applicability of that predicate from
> | being a way of thinking about the building to being itself a subject
> | of thought, and that operation is 'subjectal abstraction'.
> |
> | Charles Sanders Peirce, 'Collected Papers', CP 4.332
>
> The RDF specification talks about reification in the following terms:
> 
> | Statements are made about resources.
> | A model of a statement is the resource
> | we need in order to be able to make new
> | statements (higher-order statements)
> | about the modeled statement.
> |
> | For example, let us consider the sentence
> | |
> | | Ora Lassila is the creator of the resource http://www.w3.org/Home/Lassila.
> | |
> | RDF would regard this sentence as a fact.  If, instead, we write the sentence
> | |
> | | Ralph Swick says that Ora Lassila is the creator of the resource
> | | http://www.w3.org/Home/Lassila.
> | |
> | we have said nothing about the resource http://www.w3.org/Home/Lassila;
> | instead, we have expressed a fact about a statement Ralph has made.
> | In order to express this fact to RDF, we have to model the original
> | statement as a resource with four properties.  This process is formally
> | called reification in the Knowledge Representation community.  A model
> | of a statement is called a reified statement.
> 
> This seems to me to fit in with CSP's notion of 'subjectal abstraction',
> as indeed does the usage in the case of XTM (Topic Maps).

There are so many things that arise here that I do not know how to begin.
I guess a lot depends on the aims and the attitudes of those who would
create a new language, so maybe I will start by trying to assize that.
Are they trying to solve a problem that is ineffable in other terms?
Are they trying to prove that they are creative?  I would need to
get a sense of that first.

> In the case of XTM the use of the term 'reification' had
> been abandoned because of its perceived general misuse.
> It came back into use at the Paris meeting (as indicated
> by Steve Pepper) because it appeared to more accurately
> reflect what part of the conceptual model needed to say.

Paris does that to people.

> In a topic map topics exist, but the subjects that they
> represent are themselves abstract (although they may have
> physical manifestations).  We can form associations between
> topics, we may also want to refer to some associations between
> topics as if they were topics.  At a data modelling level this
> can lead one to simply treat 'association between topics' as
> a subclass of 'topic'.  But this leads to problems, in particular
> it is not necessarily appropriate to have an association inherit
> from topic because, in a general case, properties of a topic are
> not all applicable in the case of an association.  What it really
> comes down to is that the association can rank alongside other
> subjects and can be converted to (or reified into) a topic.
> The topic and the association are thus distinct and one is
> not a subclass of the other.
> 
> To me the process of reification is that of converting
> a statement (which is an abstraction) into a thing or
> entity in its own right in order that it may become
> the subject of some other statement.  It is often
> the equivalent of the device of placing a statement
> in single quotes in order to distinguish between that
> part of a sentence that is in the metalanguage and that
> which is in the object language.
> 
> Do you think that I am 'barking up the wrong tree'?
