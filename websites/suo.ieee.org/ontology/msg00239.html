Bill,

Yes, people have been working on methods for mapping
databases from one to another for over 30 years.
And as a result of all that work, whenever two companies
merge, their combined database is always the disjoint
union of their separate databases.

Many banks in particular have merged, and even though they
all use terms that are superficially similar (loans, checking
accounts, savings accounts, mortgages, etc.), not a single
pair of merged banks have ever been able to merge their
databases.  They have all adopted two solutions:

 1. Continue to run the software from both of the old banks
    in parallel.

 2. Cancel old accounts from one of the banks and open new
    accounts using the software from the other bank.

>The definition of mapping specifications between data >models as been part
of the ISO 10303 (STEP) standard for over 10 years >now.  Within my company,
we've been building tools to leverage >these mappings for model-driven data
conversion/translation for several >years.  And personally I have been researching
what this mapping - >which we call Semantic (data) Mapping - is all about. 
>("Semantic Mapping" entails "Data Mapping", i.e., >the conversion of one data
representation into another, not unlike the >way XSLT behaves.) 

Yes, such techniques are very useful in many cases.  They work
best when the systems that use them have been designed with
those techniques in mind.  They are extremely difficult to
use when applied to merging two legacy systems.

>I believe it IS possible to map a sentence from one >ontology to another. 
It merely takes the assertion of some human >to say "these bits of this ontology
mean=the=same=thing=as >that collection of things from this other ontology". 
It is >true that it will only yield an approximate semantic equivalence (like
>translation of natural languages), but multiple "executions" >of the map will
surface the ambiguities and, thus, the ontologies and >the map can be "tuned"
so the translation becomes more >correct.  Just like natural language behavior,
a certain amount of >level-setting is needed before communication works smoothly.


I don't disagree that it is possible.  And I agree that a
certain amount of "level-setting" is needed.  I don't believe
that we disagree on the principles, and I think that we would
agree that the "level-setting" can amount to an extremely
large amount of work, which for large legacy systems might
be difficult, if not impossible to carry through without
scrapping one or both of the earlier systems.

John
