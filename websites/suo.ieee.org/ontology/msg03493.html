Jim --

I think the point is that there are more and less formal attempts to convey
the same message.  Some people will love one and hate the other, and vice
versa.  Our discussion over the last year has demonstrated the dynamic
tension that Jon mentions.  One way is that various general issues of
ontology are raised and Adam (and others), say "Maybe, but write it up in
KIF".  Graham (and others) advocate getting it right in English, and
formalizing it later.  I may be misrepresenting everybody's position here,
but I think this is the basic point.

graham,

maybe it's just the perspective of time -- monday morning quarterbacking?
--
but i do not get the sense that i ever got across what I was trying to
say on this score.  actually, this issue comes up in a general context
under many different names:  informal contexts versus formal arenas,
everyday ontologies versus un-common-sense ontologies, freaks/neats,
popular writing versus technical writing.  it's a big obstacle to
communication in peirce studies because peirce wrote stuff that
was very technical and stuff that was published in popular and
humanities journals, and you just cant do both without creating --
what was the word i just heard? -- barrels of "heterogeneity"
headed for the falls of sheer inconsistency.  i actually
went back and re-read some of albert einstein's popular
works to see if he did a better job than peirce, and,
no, you cant really expect to pilot the enterprise
with the level of detail you find there.

moral of the story:  there's an "essential tension" here, to borrow an old
coin,
you cant eliminate either pole of it, but only string more lines across the
gap.

jon awbrey
