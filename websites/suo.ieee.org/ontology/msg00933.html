>I am returning to your last reply again,
>to see if I can get any further with it.

>Pat Hayes wrote:
>
> > You say you are interested in integrating
> > functional and declarative aspects of languages,
> > which is also a central interest of mine.  Even here,
> > KIF is worthy of a little careful study (although this goes
> > way beyond what is needed for the SUO enterprise, I believe).
>
>I am planning to discuss this in more detail on the history channel,
>but I have yet to see what I am looking for in this regard with any
>of the more standard ways of laying out FOL, or any offspring of it --
>though I do have this dim memory that I once thought ML and Miranda
>might provide a promising base of operations on which to build what
>I want here, I never got down to looking any further into it -- but
>what 'this' is is the agility and the flexibility of interpretation
>that permits one to maintain a "functional interpretation" (FI) for
>all logical expressions.  In short, any logical expression e has an
>interpretation as a name of a function, almost as if e = "f", where
>f : X -> B, for a suitable domain X and for B = {0, 1}, as is usual.
>Here, I say "almost as if" because the range of interpretations for
>an expression e is actually somewhat broader than a relation to any
>proper name like "f" would be able to encompass.

OK, that sounds interesting. Can you elucidate in what sense it has 
to be 'broader'? I can see it would be if the expression contained 
quantifiers or bound variables, but in the propositional calculus 
what more does any expression do than define a truthfunction on its 
atoms, ie your 'f' where X is the a finite subset of the 
propositional variables?

If you have some intensional notion in mind, can it be expressed, 
however indequately, as a modality?
