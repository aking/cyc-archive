My eyebrows rose when I read this, as it seems to illustrate rather 
clearly the pointlessness of the Piercian insistence on 'triadicity', 
rather than its utility. As this probably wasnt your intention, I am 
puzzled as to quite what your point was.

Let me elaborate by offering a simpler analysis of the Aand B 
situation, more along Tarskian lines. There are two things (A,B), 
four symbols ("A", "B", "i" ,"u") and two interpretations (RA, RB). 
The key relation is that of interpretation of a symbol. Each 
interpretation f maps each symbol s to a unique thing t, so we will 
write f(s)=t to indicate that . The complete situation can then be 
summed up as follows:
RA("A")=RB("A")=RA("i")=RB("u")=A
RA("B")=RB("B")=RA("u")=RB("i")=B
This could be displayed as a pair of tables, but they would have four 
entries rather than eight.
(If one wanted to include A as a parameter of RA and B of RB, then 
one would have triples, of course, but this would combine the tables 
into one table with 8 entries. A similar move in your scheme would 
produce 16 lines.)

There is no need for the 'interpretant' elements ("I") of your 
triples, since they clearly play no role in the other relationships: 
your triple-tables just have the same information copied out twice 
for each value of I.  It seems to be just a kind of notational 
appendage. As it plays no substantial role in anything else that I 
have ever been able to detect, I suggest we simply stop talking about 
it, whatever it is, and restrict ourselves to the normal 
subject-matter of semantics, which is relationship between signs and 
what they signify. This has served logic well for the last 60-odd 
years and suffices for every ontology effort so far attempted, and I 
see no reason to turn the clock back now and re-invent a wheel with 
too many sides.
