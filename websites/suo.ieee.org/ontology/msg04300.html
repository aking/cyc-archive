Note 7

o~~~~~~~~~o~~~~~~~~~o~~~~~~~~~o~~~~~~~~~o~~~~~~~~~o

| Character is revealed by action.         ~~ Aristotle
| Action is discrete.                      ~~ Planck
----------------------------------------------------------
| The better part of valor is discretion.  ~~ Shakespeare

One of the sources of misunderstanding that affects our interpretation
of what Peirce is saying about the process of fixing belief, indeed,
one of the torques that prevents our grasping what Peirce is saying
more generally about signs and inquiry, appears to be the peculiar
distortion that has arisen in our thinking by dint of the lately
experienced detours of the linguistic turn.

Peirce's semiotics is about signs, but it is not about signs only --
there are objects in the mix of the sign relation, objects that
involve us in the hypothesis of their objective reality.

As an object example, the simple insight that Pierce tries to express by
saying that what we believe and what we believe true are the same things.
He does not say that what we believe and what is true are the same thing,
for that would deny our fallibility.  The first statement merely admits
the redundancy of the adjective "true" in the frame of mind "belief".
This is not a positive extension of beliefs into truths but a limitative
statement, on a par with Peirce's recognition of our other incapacities.
It is our incapicity to sort our beliefs into those that we believe and
those that we "really and truly" believe.  This has nothing to do with
the possibility of sorting statements along some spectrum of credibility,
likelihood, or probability, which we do all the time, of course, because
in the moment when we have to act we either do or we don't -- and that is
the razor's edge that forces the all or none character of belief in action.

If you view these statments within the context of the pertinent sign relation,
you realize that the issue of belief, which make our conduct the interpretant
of the sign accepted, and the issue of truth, which makes the sign correspond
to the object, are skew dyadic relations in the short term, and only converge
in the long run of inquiry, if at all.  So there is no immediate conversion
of one into the other.

Jon Awbrey
