hah!  i see you 'see' my big picture and 'raise' me a bigger one!
but i am determined to stay focused, and so i'll just pull a few
cards off the cuff, and sit this hand out til i can deal with it.

welcome to the wonderful world of the peircean microcosmos,
where you will find reflected in its sphere all the diversity
of views that you will find exploding through the macro-thereof.

it would be act of great foolhardiness to venture global opinions
based on the contents of an abstract, and at my age i am more and
more trying to avoid such acts, so let me venture only this much.

let's pick one or three issues that we can see arising from the current
interaction of peirce studies and bio.cyb.org.com.plex.scio.net studies
and see what we can do to clarify it an incremental bit.  tom gollier
will be able to tell you that the fire has burned for years and years
on the peirce list about these issues, and, from the sample that i saw,
shows no signs of either dimmerung down or bringing more lux than fiat,
so i peircenally would be happy with e-miserating one quantum at a time.

if i had to pick an issue that seems to exhibit the most misunderstanding,
and if i am still foolhardy enough to try and tackle that, it would be the
problems that arise over the nature of the sign relation in the interfarce
beyween peirce's theory of signs and the way that it is currently transmuted
in some sectors of the biosemiotic circle.  and this issue implicates at its
core the usual suspect, to wit, the problem about irreducibly 3-adic relations.

so that would be where i would start ---

if i had my druthers ...

jon awbrey

TG: Since you've veered off into probability and statistics,
    perhaps you, or someone else, can help me out.  T.A. Goudge
    in "The Concept of Evolution," [*Mind*, New Series, Volume 63,
    Issue 249 (Jan., 1954) p. 16-25.] writes:

TAG: | The statistical representation of this process [evolution]
     | is made possible because the genetical constitution of each
     | individual organism is held to consist of a set of discrete
     | units (genes) whose interaction controls all the observable
     | characteristics of the individual." [p. 18]

TG: Where does the statistical need for "a set of discrete units" controlling
    the observable, and statistically correlated, characteristics come from?
    Would statiticians in general recognize this requirement?

TG: Personally, I tend to favor such a requirement and am opposed to the
    idea of Galton and Pearson that statistics can be used to come to
    conclusions without worrying about the underlying mechanism (those
    discrete units). I don't consider it an historical accident that
    eugenicism arose from their attempts to employ statistics in just
    that way.  I also think the fact that Mendel was not associated
    with Darwin during most of Peirce's lifetime is a key element
    commonly ignored in assessing his thoughts on evolution.
    But I've never seen anything, except this one quote,
    along these lines.

TG: Anyway, the need for some concern with the underlying mechanism might
    tie into Frances concern with the dyadic nature of signs themselves,
    apart from agents giving them a triadic sense, so perhaps I'm not
    completely off the topic.

    the same think as planck's hypothesis of the quantum

    so the discreteness is kind of

JA: i can't remember if it had anything on genetics especially,
    but the best all-round treatment of these statistical and
    computational issues by someone versed in peirce's logic
    would be this:

    | Arthur W. Burks,
    |'Chance, Cause, Reason:  An Inquiry into the Nature of Scientific Evidence',
    | University of Chicago Press, Chicago, IL, 1977.

JA: while looking for this ref data, i also
    found this old note of some pertinence:

JA: The choice of a probability model is an abductive choice.
    You cannot eliminate the riskiness of this choice by any
    deductive or inductive means.  Some people call this the
    "reference class problem".  I think I recall there being
    a good discussion of it, perhaps under some other alias,
    in Burks, 'Chance, Cause, Reason'.

JA: It is analogous to the "system identification" problem,
    a good account for AI applications being given by Arbib
    in the 2 'Metaphorical Brain' volumes.

JA: You cannot avoid the circumstantial bias of looking under a priorly chosen
    reference class (lamppost) and you will only identify the unknown system,
    if at all, as falling in with those that are visible thereunder.

JA: The problem of systematizing inquiry, which involves in part the
    problem of "giving a rule to abduction", is a bit more like this:

JA: Given a motley assortment of clues, gathered under a motley assortment
    of lampposts, the clues entangling a mass of conflicting indictments
    with reguard to the identity of the system that is susceptible to
    being rounded up among their respective gangs of usual suspects,
    what lamppost should we look under next?

JA: As long as we don't fall for the line and the sinker
    of a risk-free method and a value-free science, then
    all the rest of this stuff I think is just beautiful.

JA: On a more timely topical note, we must not forget the possibility
    of what might be called a "hypo-critical request for information".
