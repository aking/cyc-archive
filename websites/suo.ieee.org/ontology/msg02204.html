> Dear Matthew, 
> I wonder if the following isn't yet closer to what you have in mind.
> 
>    (<=> (gives3 ?d ?r &g)
>         (and (gives2 ?x)
>              (giver ?x ?d)
>              (receiver ?x ?r)
>              (given ?x ?g)
>          )
>     )     

MW: I already went further than I should at this stage. I have some
difficulty in understanding some of the nuances of what you have written
here. For example, for the "method" I am trying to describe there would 
be exactly one x for each tuple that is a member of gives3. I am not
sure whether what you have written makes that so.
>              
> Here 
> ?d suggests any "donor" (not just the donor John"), 
> ?r suggests "recipient", and
> ?g suggests "gift", and
> ?x suggests an act of giving.
> 
> 
> (I assume that the "<=>" above is exemplary of
> what the [irreducible] triadicity disagreement is about.)

MW: Yes.
> 
> 
> From the above axiom, 
> I think I can infer both equivalences below.
> 
> 
> Relations contain tuples as follows:
> 
> gives2:
> [X0]
> [X1]
> 
> giver:
> [X0 John]
> [X1 John]
> 
> receiver:
> [X0 Mary]
> [X1 Ileana]
> 
> and given:
> [X0 Book1]
> [X1 Book1]
> 
> 
> <=>
> 
> 
> Relation (gives3) contains the tuples:
> 
> [John Mary Book1]
> [John Ileana Book1]
> 
> 
> <=>
> 
> 
> Relations contain tuples as follows:
> 
> gives2:
> [X0]
> 
> giver:
> [X0 John]
> 
> receiver:
> [X0 Mary]
> [X0 Ileana]
> 
> and given:
> [X0 Book1]
> 
> 
> 
> 
> 
> The gives3 situation clearly suggests that there were
> two acts of giving.
> 
> The one reduction to a gives2 situation 
> suggests that there were two acts of giving.
> But a different reduction to a gives2 situation 
> suggests that 
> there was one act of giving with two recipients.

MW: Yes, that is why your interpretation of my intent is incorrect.
To work, for each tuple in the initial relation there is exactly
one member of the set of objects that are essentially the reification
of the relation. I just don't know how to say that in KIF.
> 
> I would say that 
> [John Mary Book1]
> [John Ileana Book1]
> is irreducibly triadic in the sense that it necessarily will have
> more than one logically equivalent representation after being reduced.

MW: I just don't follow this.
> 
> More generally, from one diadic knowledge base and a
> triadic reducibility axiom,
> I can infer another diadic knowledge base.
> Now I have to wonder if these two (and any others I can infer)
> are logically equivalent.
> (Intuitively, they aren't.)
> 
> But from the one triadic knowledge base, I only know what I know.
> 
> 
> I propose that we not have any triadic reducibility axioms 
> such as the following in our SUO.

MW: I'm not sure there would be much use for them in the ontology
itself. Personally I use the approach as an aid to analysis to try
to find things that are implied rather than stated explicitly. Plus
it is convenient in implementation if you need considerable flexibility
in what you want to be able to represent.
> 
> 
> (<=> (g3 ?d ?r &g)
>         (and (g2 ?x)
>              (gr ?x ?d)
>              (rr ?x ?r)
>              (gn ?x ?g)
>          )
>     ) 
> 
> 
> If this is agreed, then
> maybe we can stop talking about irreducible triadicity.

MW: I doubt if we are capable of stopping talking about this
until there is a common understanding of the issue. And if we did
stop talking about it, it would be there as an unresolved issue
waiting to bite us.
> 
>    -Fred Chase
