OK, I'll respond to your three broad criticisms in turn.  First, in the
initial version of the merged ontology there definitely were cases where one
axiom contradicted another axiom or contradicted a subsumption relation
between two concepts.  Furthermore, you kindly took the time to point out
many of these cases, and they were resolved, to some degree at least, in the
revised version of the merged ontology.  I'm sure that this version contains
many other inconsistencies, but it seems to me that the way to handle these
remaining cases is to resolve them point-by-point rather than tossing out
everything that has been done thus far.  As for your criticism that the
axioms have no global coherence, I don't see that this is true.  If the
quantifiers indeed vary in scope across different blocks of axioms, this
problem can be resolved by introducing typed quantifiers or a similar
apparatus.  As for a lack of conformity to the same set of categories and
the overabundance of controversial philosophical distinctions, I know you
indicated in your earlier comments many specific points where this occurred,
but I did try to accomodate these criticisms in the latest version of the
ontology.  If there are points where additional revision is needed, please
let me know.  Finally, as regards your claim that we should get clear about
basic ontological issues before proceeding to the development of an actual
ontology, it seems to me that the 2,500 year history of philosophy is a
fairly powerful argument against this approach.  I think that speculation
about isolated points independent of a concrete fabric within which these
points have a context and an application has resulted in many centuries of
wasted effort.  I would argue that the best way of obtaining consensus is to
produce an initial document (no matter how flawed) and then to revise it
over many, many iterations of criticism and comment. 

>Moreover, it is clear 
> that the more 'pieces' are added to it, the more these problems are 
> going to get worse rather than better.
> 
> This methodology is fundamentally flawed. There is no way to achieve 
> a coherent ontology by 'merging' axioms from incommensurate 
> ontological perspectives in this simplistic way. It is rather like 
> trying to create  a large piece of software by just adjoining pieces 
> of code written by different authors in different computer languages, 
> and expecting it to magically work.

I agree that this sort of methodology would be fundamentally flawed.
However, this is not what I have tried to do.  I have tried to integrate the
axioms/subsumption relationships of various different ontologies into the
same stock of concepts (taken from a simplified version of John Sowa's
upper-level ontology).  If there are still cases of incommensurate
concepts/axioms in the latest version of the ontology, please let me know
what they are.

>

I agree.  One can interpret the call for the vote as the question:  Is it
worth going into the details?
