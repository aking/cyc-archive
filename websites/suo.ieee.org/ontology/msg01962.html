All,

A helpful framework, in my opinion, is to divide the SUO terminology and
namespaces into 3 fundamental kinds: basic, structural and content. This has
probably been said before, but I wanted to elaborate a little in order to
give a better picture of it. The borders between each of these components,
and the borders between their subcomponents, are not necessarily
cut-and-dried.

Basic:

This is the terminology in the KIF Basic Ontology. It provides a basic
ontological apparatus for the SUO. The purpose of this kind of terminology
is to be an interface between the KIF language and other ontological
terminology, in general. Because of its function, the KIF Basic Ontology
might also be called the core or bootstrap ontology. By analogy, the basic
component of the SUO is like machine or assembly language. Hopefully, this
will have only one namespace, perhaps with the namespace prefix 'KIF'. This
terminology divides into two parts: a core part and a convenience part. The
core part consists of terms such as, 'class', 'relation', 'top', 'null',
'union', 'intersection', 'complement', 'empty', 'subclass', 'difference',
'subrelation-of', 'transitive', 'symmetric', 'reflexive-on', 'functional',
'disjoint', 'unary', 'binary', 'ternary', 'posint', 'negint', 'nonnegint',
'=<', '>=', 'integer', '+', 'sequence', 'length', 'weave', 'seq', 'arity',
and 'signature'. The convenience part might consist of terms such as:
'instance-of', 'nth-domain', the 'function' class, the sequence selectors
'first', 'second', 'third', 'last', the sequence constructors 'pair',
'triple', and the sequence classes 'Pair' and 'Triple'.

Structural:

This component provides the structural aspect of the SUO. There may be
several approaches to this. Currently I am working on a building blocks
approach to ontological structure -- a rather elaborate categorical approach
that uses some insight from Barwise's theory of distributed logic called
Information Flow (and also uses ideas from Wille's theory of Formal Concept
Analysis). The structural aspect of the SUO is at the metalevel. By analogy,
the structural component of the SUO is like a high level programming
language such as Lisp, Java, ML, etc. This will have many namespaces, such
as a foundation namespace 'IFF', a set namespace 'Set', a category namespace
'CAT', a colimit namespace 'Col', a classification 'Cls' namespace, an IF
theory namespace 'Th', a concept lattice namespace 'CL', a language
namespace 'Lang', a model namespace 'Mod', a truth namespace for the
fundamental truth classification and concept lattice, and various other
supporting namespaces such as all corresponding namespaces. Some of the
terms that appear in the structural component of the SUO are:
'Conglomerate', 'Class' (note: the class in the basic component is regarded
here as a synonym for "collection"), 'subclass', 'Function', 'pullback',
'unit', 'product', 'set', 'subset', 'power', 'Category', 'object',
'morphism', 'composition', 'identity', 'monomorphism', 'epimorphism',
'isomorphism', 'sum', 'product', 'diagram', 'cone', 'colimit', 'functor',
'natural-transformation', 'adjunction', 'instance', 'type', 'incidence',
'|=', 'indistinguishable', 'coextensive', 'separated', 'extensional',
'infomorphism', 'intent', 'extent', 'invariant', 'quotient', 'sequent',
'consequence', 'Theory', 'constraint', '|-', 'subtype', 'disjoint',
'partition', 'satisfies', 'regular', 'weakening', 'cut', 'Logic', 'normal',
'sound', 'complete', 'concept', 'subconcept', 'meet', 'join', 'Lattice',
'object-concept', 'attribute-concept', 'relation', 'tuple', 'index',
'argument', 'Language', 'expression', 'quantifier', 'and', 'or, 'not',
'implies', 'arity', 'language-morphism', 'language-interpretation', 'Model',
'model-morphism', 'satisfies', 'models', 'Ontology', etc.

Content:

This component provides the content of the SUO coming from the various
domain ontologies and philosophies with their own namespaces. The content
aspect of the SUO is at the object level. By analogy, the content component
of the SUO is like the various software applications, such as word
processors, browsers, spreadsheet software, databases, etc. The content
aspect would include domain ontologies such as Casati and Varzi's ontology
of holes, John Sowa's upper ontology, Barry Smith's Formal Theory of
Boundaries/Objects, Borgo, Guarino, and Masolo's Formal Theory of Physical
Objects, the public Cyc ontology, ontologies for organizations, ontologies
for repositories, etc. Some of the terms that appearing in the content
component of the SUO might be: 'hole', 'hole-in', 'part-of', 'overlaps',
'fusion-of', 'sum-of' ... 'Independent', 'Relative', 'Mediating',
'Physical', 'Abstract', 'Continuant', 'Occurrent', ..., 'mereological-sum',
'mereological-product', 'interior-part', 'strong-connection', 'sphere',
'externally-tangent', ..., etc. Part of the purpose of the structural level
is to interrelate the various modules in this content component. There may
even be theories in this content component of the SUO that talk about
various aspects of the basic and structural components, but the separation
between the basic-structural components and the content component will
provide a separation between metalevel and object level.

As I see it,
