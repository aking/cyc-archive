JA = Jon Awbrey
JC = Jerry Chandler

JC: Dismayed.
    Simply dismayed.

JC: You enumerate 6 objects, each with 16 components.
    You name them "languages".  When asked a direct
    question about a specific logical relation on
    6 sets with identical cardinality, you reply:

    "I'm not sure about all of what you are asking."

That's because it was not clear to me yet whether you had the picture
of what I was trying to talk about, so I was trying to check that out.

The critical paragraph is here:

| The languages S_1, S_2, S_3 have 16 elements each, and so they are enumerated
| in full by the entries in the Table, but the languages S_4, S_5, S_6 all have
| a countable infinity of expressions, and so but samples of these sign domains,
| giving one of the simpler expressions for each object, are shown in the Table.

Part of the problem is that we have an informal context and a formal context.

1.  Just between you and me, in the informal context, everything that
    we talk about becomes, by that very fact, an object of discussion.

2.  In the formal context, I am using the "pragmatic theory of signs" (PTOS)
    as a conceptual framework to talk about the relationship of logical and
    mathematical objects to logical and mathematical calculi, or languages.
    In this setting one discusses any number of sets called "sign relations".
    A sign relation L_j is a subset of a cartesian product O_j x S_j x I_j,
    where O_j, S_j, I_j are its designated Object, Sign, Interpretant Domains,
    respectively.  Thus, when we are talking in this particular formal arena
    the term "object" will refer to an element of a specified object domain.
    A sign domain S_j is very often defined as a "formal language" over some
    "alphabet" A_j, hence S_j c (A_j)*.  Here, the objects are the things
    that are denoted by the signs, as given by the relevant sign relation.

JC: So, let us start from the beginning.

JC: What is a language, in your definition?  Is your usage of the term "language"
    in any way related to the more or less standard usage of the term "language"
    or "signature" in mathematical logic / model theory?

Informally, I use "language" to mean any old set of signs, expressions, and so on.
Formally, I use "formal language" in the sense of "formal language theory", e.g.:

| Denning, P.J., Dennis, J.B., Qualitz, J.E.,
|'Machines, Languages, and Computation',
| Prentice-Hall, Englewood Cliffs, NJ, 1978.

I will assume that you are using "objects" below to mean the S_j.

JC: Do any of your objects [S_j] have relations on them?

Yes.

JC: Do any of your objects [S_j] have functions on them?

Yes.

JC: Do any of your objects [S_j] have constants associated with them?

Yes, if you wish.

JC: Are any of your objects extensible?  (In other words,
    have you specified all components of the language?)
    If the number of components can be greater than 16,
    please give a crisp rule for extending them.

These formal languages all have formal grammars that define them.
For finite langauges the grammars are trivial.
For the countably infinite languages, some of
their grammars will take a while to set up.

JC: With regard to crispness, I think you have a good idea of what I am 
    pointing toward.  You may consider the problem to be one of distinction.
    Are you communicating a clear, meaningful distinction to your readership --
    your colleagues who are part of the same historical community within which
    you are working?

Do you mean like "well-defined"?
