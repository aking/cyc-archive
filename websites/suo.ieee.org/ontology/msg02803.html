Jean-Marc,

Let us leave the surly bonds of sumo russellers behind --
and not just because I suspect their matches are fixed --
but because their contentment with presocratic manners
of thinking will never hear a word of Plato, Aristotle,
much less of Peirce.  And I have just been enlightened
why all of my warnings about icebergs were in vain, as
that vehicle was not designed to fly the standard of a
research vessel, nor any kind of a seagoing craft, but
only the party banners of a museum ship, anchored in a
drydock in the middle of a desert in Arizona.  Clearly,
all talk of icebergs about, much less of risky inquiry,
is doomed to sound just as absurd as the piped-in surf
on their shore.

Now, the broader questions that I keep hearing in your
remarks strike my ears as questions about the relation
of experience and reason, matter and form, descriptive
and normative sciences.  And I just can't imagine what
I ever said that made you think that when I write "and"
that my secret meaning is "versus".  But it appears to
me that the whole problem turns on the interpretations
of words like "accidental", "essential", "independent",
and of that peculiar usage that I have noted before in
which a mathematical writer will often use the functor
"non" as a generalizing operator, almost as if it were
to mean "not of necessity", for instance, the way that
mathematicians apply "non-associative" or "non-linear".

This, to me, is the secret key to understanding what Peirce
may have meant by a "non-psychological conception of logic".
For if "non" is meant to generalize the domain, then it but
widens the circle of interest from human psychology to what
is more generic, and does not draw off to a separate corner.
It does not say that a descriptive science of "how we think"
does not busy itself with an important part of the material
from which the forms of logic are typically abstracted, but
only that it can enjoy but a species interest when comes to
constituting the necessary nature of this normative science.

OK, I feel like I am saying things that ought to be obvious,
so I will stop until the occasion when I discover otherwise.

Jon Awbrey
