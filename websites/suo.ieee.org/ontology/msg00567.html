===========================================
Revised Scope as at 25 Nov '00 (Jim, are these the latest?):
This standard will specify an upper ontology that will enable computers to
utilize it for applications such as data interoperability, information
search and retrieval, automated inferencing, and natural language
processing. An ontology is similar to a dictionary or glossary, but with
greater detail and structure that enables computers to process its content.
An ontology consists of a set of concepts, axioms, and relationships that
describe a domain of interest. An upper ontology is limited to concepts
that
are meta-, generic, abstract and philosophical, and therefore are general
enough to address (at a high level) a broad range of domain areas. Concepts
specific to given domains will not be included; however, this standard will
provide a structure and a set of general concepts upon which domain
ontologies (e.g. medical, financial, engineering, etc.) could be
constructed.
Purpose: (Why the standard needs to be developed and who will benefit.)
A.   AUTOMATED REASONING: The standard will be suitable for automated
logical inference to support knowledge-based reasoning applications.
B.   INTER-OPERABILITY: The standard will provide a basis for achieving
Inter-Operability among various software and database applications.
1)   Application developers can define new data elements in terms of a
common ontology, and thereby gain some degree of interoperability with
other
conformant systems.
2)   Applications based on domain-specific ontologies that are compliant
with this standard will be able to interoperate (to some degree) by virtue
of the shared common terms and definitions.
3)   The SUO will play the role of a neutral interchange format whereby
owners of existing applications will be able to map existing data elements
just once to a common ontology. This provides a degree of interoperability
with other applications whose representations conform to SUO.  This entails
the SUO being able to be mapped to more restricted forms such as XML,
database schema, or object oriented schema.
C: APPLICATION AREAS
1)   E-commerce applications from different domains that need to
interoperate at both the data and semantic levels.
2)   Educational applications in which students learn concepts and
relationships directly from, or expressed in terms of, a common ontology.
This will also enable a standard record of learning to be kept.
3)   Natural language understanding tasks in which a knowledge based
reasoning system uses the ontology to disambiguate among likely
interpretations of natural language statements.

===========================================

A domain of discourse

The first table attempts to clarify six concepts respecting distinctions
between Representation and Real World and between type, instance and
population.

     Representation Real World
Type 1. The definition of a table including its columns and meaning    2.
The concept of customer
Instance  3. A row in a table 4. A particular customer of a
particular trader
Population     5. A table of rows and columns with values    6. A
population of customers of one or more traders



A table of terms for referring to the domain of discourse.  Each term in
the
table below, which is congruent with the one above, should be interpreted
as
referring to the content of the corresponding cell in the table above.


     1. ? 2. Relationship [type]
     3. Tuple  4. Relationship [instance]
     5. Relation    6. ?



I believe this use of Tuple and Relation is consistent with Ted Codd, John
Sowa, and John Velman.  I believe the use of relationship is consistent
with
natural language.

I would welcome suggestions for terms to replace the question marks, though
I cannot think of any strong candidates in spite of many years of
experience
of discussion of this issue.

The problem is the risk of confusion, when one of the terms is used to
refer
to either of the concepts with question marks.  In my experience this can
usually be avoided by rephrasing the statement to use other  terms so the
distinctions are respected.

A relation of arity n, n>0,  is symbolized as predicate with n arguments.
The extension of a relation of arity 1 is the set of things in the domain
of
quantification for which the predicate is true.  The  extension of a
relation of arity n>1 is the subset of the n-fold Cartesian product of the
domain of quantification for which the predicate is true.
A relationship is an instance of a relation.    E.g.,  married(X,Y)
describes a relation. married(Bill, Hillary) describes a relationship.

Matthew, Mike, et al.,
That is a very serious and very difficult question:
>I am happy to work with these ... but what do we
>call what it is in the real world that they refer to?

As soon as you ask that question, you open Pandora's box of metaphysical
problems.  Nominalists, conceptualists, and realists can talk together
quite
happily as long as they use ordinary language for everyday life.  They can
also work together quite happily as long as they use mathematics and other
formalized languages, such as KIF, SQL, FORTRAN, C, Java, etc.
But as soon as somebody asks the question "But what do they refer to in the
real world?" all hell breaks loose.
You immediately get into a heated debate about what is real?  what is
reference?  what is the world?  what is meaning?
Suggestion:
1.   These philosophical issues, despite their perennial interest, have
never been solved to everyone's (anyone's?) satisfaction.  Of one thing we
can be absolutely certain is that they will not be solved on this mailing
list.
2.   Therefore, I suggest that as a working hypothesis for the purpose of
the SUO list, we adopt an assumption of "naive realism" that says that the
world exists, and most of the words we use in ordinary language refer to
something, but let's not worry too much about what it is.
3.   For the purpose of the formalization of the SUO, we adopt the
position that KIF is the official language to be used for everything that
can be officially expressed by the SUO.  If KIF can't express it, we don't
talk about it.

I admit that this suggestion is technically known as a "cop out" that
doesn't explain what reality really is.  It is exactly the same cop out
that
Wittgenstein adopted as the final line of his Tractatus:  "Everything that
can be said can be said clearly, and whereof we cannot speak, thereof we
must be silent."
For the SUO, I propose that we modify that line by adding some extra
prepositional phrases:  "Everything that can be said [in the SUO] can be
said clearly [in KIF], and whereof we cannot speak [in KIF], thereof we
must
be silent.
This is not an answer to the question of what do relations really refer to.
It is a pure 100% cop out.  But it is the only kind of cop out that will
allow us to get some work done.
John Sowa
