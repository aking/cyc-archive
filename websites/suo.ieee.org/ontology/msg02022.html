MW: "Aristotle designed his original syllogisms to represent facts about the
current state of the world, but he later extended ... [this] to talk about
the way the world ... may, can, should, would, or could be." I quote from
John Sowa's excellent "Knowledge Representation". If you do not have a copy,
you should certainly get one. I know of know other book that has so much of
what you need to know for this stuff in one place. Also it is quite readable
for people like you and me.

Modal logic provides some additional operators like a diamond and a square
to express some of this stuff. With a Possible Worlds approach you don't
need this. This is at least partly because a 4D approach overcomes
effortlessly the biggest problem with Aristotle's original approach - that
it dealt with a current state of affairs.

> MW: Hm. I'm probably not the best person to do this, but here goes.
> Constant
> A label that always refers to the same thing whenever it

> Variable
> A label that may range over different things.  e.g. in

> 
> GH2>	Can variables also cover non-continuous ranges, such as all
> integers, all prime numbers, or, more significantly, the 
> names of all local
> government regions in a country, and so on. 

MW: A fairly common "trick" you will see in the KIF most people here
write is something like:

(forall ?x (=> (car ?x) (has-wheels ?x))

Which can be read:

For all x, if x is a car, then x has wheels.

In theory, a variable always ranges over the whole universe, but
by using implication (if ...then...) you can effectively limit 
its range to any set/class you choose.

MW: Well lets try:

What happens is that it takes each person mentioned in this e-mail
(i.e. you me and John) and substitutes each of in turn for x in
the expression ....
