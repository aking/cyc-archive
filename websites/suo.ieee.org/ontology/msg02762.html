Regarding the minimal attributes of a physical object:

   
Bernard Vatant wrote:
> 

> << In the merged ontology, "physical" is defined (in the gloss) as an entity
> that has location in space-time >>
> 
> Space and time are not the only (physical) measurable, and other attributes
> (mass, energy, temperature, wavelength) . . .
  First, note that "physical" subsumes processes, not just physical 
objects.  The following comments are relevant to Bernard Vatant's
remarks, interpreted as applying to physical objects (as I believe he
intended).
  In my own ontology I use mass as the defining characteristic of a
"physical
object", but location in space-time is also legitimate, provided that
one
uses "location" in the broad sense, meaning any one or more of a point, 
region, or, if one is concerned with subatomic distances or interference 
pattterns, statistical distribution through space (wave function).
   "energy" is equivalent to mass, unless one wants to use "rest mass",
which would, however, eliminate electromagnetic waves as physical -- 
I prefer "relativistic mass".

> . . . are other measurable necessary to
> define physical entities, . . . 
> . . . and I wonder
> why these other attributes are not considered at the same level, along with
> space and time, under a generic "measurable attribute".

   No, temperature and wavelength are relevant only for certain types of
physical objects; ordinary temperature is a statistical property of
large numbers of particles.  If you are thinking of the DeBroglie 
wavelength, that is derivable from the mass.  Extended non-standard 
definitions of those quantities might be attributed to many objects, 
but that should not be forced on users.  That can be done at a lower 
level.  The most general "physical object" can not have all measurable 
attributes, only those common to all "physical objects".

> and many entities have no specific space-time
> location (not to mention that quantum mechanics has somehow seriously shaken
> the notion of space-time location as making any sense at all),
   I think it's best to keep the definitions in the SUO within the
realms of
common sense to the extent that they can be while still providing 
consistency with modern physics.  If you really think that "location" 
makes no sense at all, it would seem rather difficult or impossible to 
create any ontology that is useful for practical purposes.

> 
> So it seems that Measurable is more generic than space and time.
   "measurable" is an attribute of certain properties that matter may 
or may not possess, and you would need a careful definition to make that 
notion precise as a defining characteristic of material objects.  Is 
some object many trillions of light-years away in an inflationary 
universe, and moving away at near light-speed "measurable"?  by whom?
   If mass alone, or mass plus location are inadequate as generic
defining characteristics of a "physical object", it would be helpful 
if someone could find a counterexample either
(1) of a physical object that doesn't have those characteristics,
 or (2) a non-physical object that does.
