Hello all

<< In the merged ontology, "physical" is defined (in the gloss) as an entity
that has location in space-time >>

Space and time are not the only (physical) measurable, and other attributes
(mass, energy, temperature, wavelength) are other measurable necessary to
define physical entities, and many entities have no specific space-time
location (not to mention that quantum mechanics has somehow seriously shaken
the notion of space-time location as making any sense at all), and I wonder
why these other attributes are not considered at the same level, along with
space and time, under a generic "measurable attribute". Keeping Space-Time
at an upper level than other measurable looks to me like a 18th century
ontology :o)

The measurable attributes of "sunlight" for instance are not space-time, but
e.g. "spectrum" and "intensity".
A "fluid state" measurable attributes are e.g. density distribution,
pression, temperature, Reynolds number, viscosity ...

So it seems that Measurable is more generic than space and time.
Does Quantity live under Abstract or Physical? I'm quite agnostic about that
... but my scientific background makes me feel Quantity organized as below
(non-exhaustive hierachy).

      Quantity

               > Discrete (countable)

                    >> Set Cardinality
                    >> Price
                    >> ...

               > Continuous (measurable)

                   >> Space
                   >> Time
                   >> Temperature
                   >> Pression
                   >> Mass
                   >> Density
                    ....

Every Quantitative Measurable Continuous attribute for any "physical object"
is generally defined by a Value Interval [Inf, Sup]
which is in fact a raw simplification of a probability distribution - and in
the case of sunlight, for example, one has to use this distribution to
define the spectrum.
We know there is nothing like an exact measure, but we often pretend
otherwise anyway to be simple, and keep using the notion of "Point" (an
interval where Inf = Sup)

The astronomer's 0.02 Euros
