Very good questions -- just when I have to run off to take care of some
family business out of town, so this will be purely random associations
and maybe I can get back to it later tonight.

For "presocratic" ways of thinking, used in such a pejorative way that
it is probably unfair to the innocents named and their namesakes alike --
but it was a pique experience, and honest in its transient way -- I had
in mind what I often call the "Adamaic" view, that by naming all things
we are somehow graced with dominion over them.  So I mean the brand of
"essential" thinking that acts as if it believed that we could make up
the essences at will, instead of having to discover them through the
long hard labor of inquiry.  Any day now, on a recurring basis, one
expects such thinkers to pronounce that the whole of outer and inner
nature can be comprehended in just four elements and just four humors,
which I would think is pretty funny, if the practical consequences were
not so dire.

All of this especially relevant to Peirce because of the contrasts
between relative and non-relative (absolute, essential) thinking.

A fairer picture of the "sophist", based on scholarship instead of rumor,
delving into what the average sophist was really about, can be found in:

G.B. Kerferd, 'The Sophistic Movement', Cambridge University Press, 1981.
