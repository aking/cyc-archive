This is a topic on which I have had many long debates (with John 
Sowa, among others), but I honestly do not think that we need to go 
beyond Tarski, most of the time. That is, the usual, boring, familiar 
mathematical model theory, which has been the 'standard' semantics 
for logic now for over half a century and is astonishingly 
well-understood, and is used throughout mathematics and formal logic 
(and is the basis on which the metatheory of logic such as 
completeness theorems, compactness theorems, etc., all rest), will do 
just fine to be the basis for how to connect our representations to 
the real world. This is not to say that it will solve all the 
philosophical problems or even all the technical problems, but only 
that it will serve as a perfectly functional theory as far as it 
goes, and moreover that for most of what we have to do that will be 
quite far enough. (The exceptions being perhaps where we need to 
consider an explicit ontology of signs and relate or connect that 
theory to our own logical language, if we ever get that far; and 
perhaps, though I am more sanguine than many of my colleagues about 
these problems, if we need to get deeply into questions of meaning in 
natural language. I personally will be happy to go to my grave 
without ever getting deeply involved in either of these topics, but 
others may have a different agenda, of course.)

Sure. But what about if you havn't identified a particular star, and 
moreover couldn't in principle? You might still want to say something 
about the relation between stars and galaxies - for example, you 
might want to say that every visible galaxy contains millions of 
stars which are invisible (with the telescopes currently available). 
That is a perfectly reasonable thing to be able to assert - it 
happens to be true, and indeed is an important fact in any 
understanding of astrophysics or astronomy. But it cannot be 
represented as a fact about tables of surrogates.

The general point is that the sets of n-tuples which we want to allow 
in our semantics will usually have to be a larger collection than the 
sets of tables of surrogates which we could possibly store in a 
database. We need to be able to say things about realities which we 
could not possibly tabulate. Ontologies are more than just data 
models for databases. They have to be able to talk about the world, 
even those parts of it which can't be tabulated.

No, I won't, because I profoundly disagree with your philosophical 
assumptions here. Allow me to explain.

There is a widespread assumption about the 'meaning' of mathematics 
which might be called 'naive platonism': the idea that mathematical 
language is about a world of abstract mathematical 'things' which are 
essentially different from the things in the real, physical world. On 
this view, whenever we use 'formal' or mathematical concepts, we must 
be referring to this abstract world, so therefore cannot be referring 
to anything in the real world. To apply such mathematical talk to the 
real world therefore, on this view, requires yet another interpretive 
step to relate this abstract platonic world to the concerete, 
physical, world.

Now, I think this view is just plain wrong. When I use mathematical 
language to talk about the world (as for example when I say that a 
piece of wood is ten inches long), I am actually talking about the 
things in the world, not about abstractions that need to be somehow 
related to the world by yet another theory of representations. As I 
said in my comments (about a week ago) on Niles' revised upper 
ontology, when a shepherd counts his sheep he isnt saying anything 
about an abstract world of numbers: he is counting *sheep*.  When I 
talk about sets of things, I mean exactly that: sets of *things*. 
Things like sheep, for example. And this works even in more complex 
areas of mathematics. Algebra texts often say things like 'an example 
of the Klein group is provided by the rotations and flips of a 
rectangular card'. This is couched in typical mathematician's 
naive-platonist language ("the Klein group") , but what it really 
means is that those (physical) rotations and flips of a (physical) 
card provide a model - a valid interpretation - of the Klein group 
axioms. Similarly, when I say that a relation is a set of n-tuples, I 
do NOT mean to imply that a relation is some kind of ethereal 
abstraction completely removed from the real, physical, world. It may 
be - one can have relations on abstract things - but also might not 
be, since one can also have relations - in exactly the same sense - 
on concrete things. For example, consider the relation of owning. 
Among the pairs in that set are the one containing me and a certain 
Ford pickup truck (the grey one in the car park outside this 
building.) I really do mean me, the physical person, and my physical 
truck. Not datastructures or surrogates or entries in a table or 
'mathematical objects', but  two real, physical things. (In case you 
are going to ask: but is the *pair* a physical thing? I don't know, 
and I don't care. I'm not making any ontological claims about the 
pair, only about the things it pairs.)

So, to sum up, I will not use a different term for an 'abstract' 
relation and a real, in-yer-actual-world relation(ship), because I 
don't think these are fundamentally distinct. They are both sets of 
n-tuples; the only difference is the physical character of the things 
in those n-tuples. In brief: 'mathematical objects' may well contain 
'things like stars, galaxies, and other observable phenomena'.

(And, I might add, that while we can argue about this until the cows 
come home, this is the view that is embedded in the basic model 
theory of virtually every logical language ever studied, so if SUO is 
going to use any kind of extensional logic, then it had better either 
adopt this view or get busy rewriting a large part of 20th century 
formal logic. I would recommend the former, if only to get a head 
start on the competition.)

I can see that if you don't accept this view, then you will have a 
hard time following what I say. I'm not sure what to suggest to 
alleviate this problem, but one strategy might be for you to take 
everything I say with a grain of salt. I think I'm talking about the 
real world, but you think (maybe) that I'm suffering from a delusion, 
and that I'm really only talking about a kind of Platonic shadow of 
the *real* real world.  If that is more or less right, then interpret 
my position as that of being quite happy to be only talking about 
shadows.
