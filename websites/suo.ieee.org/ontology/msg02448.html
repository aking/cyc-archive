[Beginning of "my two cents" worth ...]

I'd say that the problem, from my perspective, is that the wording of Jon's postings (including the original posting you reference above) are unnecessarily complex.  Even though I am a native English speaker, I have a much much harder time reading and understanding Jon's postings than anyone else's postings (see postings recently from Adam, Jim, John, Matthew, Nicolas, Phil, etc.).  In my opinion, Jon seems to care more about getting the characters to line up in some pattern (if your E-mail displays in, say, Courier font then you know what I'm talking about) than getting the message across.

Recommendation: Higher priority should be given to being clear and concise ... it helps for native and non-native English speakers.

In my experience in E-mail and netnews distribution over the past 20+ years, the technology doesn't matter as much as the willingness of the communities to be "self-policing".  Even better would be self-discipline ... self-policing wouldn't be necessary.  Occassional face-to-face meetings help, too: they build they bonds for self-discipline, self-policing, and common goals ... we have discussed this, I know face-to-face meetings are difficult.  Regardless of the technology, "bad" participants can drag down the joint efforts, e.g., meetings, E-mail, lists, papers.

Recommendation: Determine the policy before choosing a distribution technology.  Simply put, if you haven't determined how you will handle those who violate the rules, then the technology doesn't really matter.  I think some people believe that those "bad" participants will simply have their own topics and any good technology should be able to select the "good" from the "bad".  It isn't that simple.  I've subscribed to "slashdot" for about 1.5 years, but there is *tons* of garbage in there ... it is very difficult to keep up with "slashdot".  Back to the policy issue: it would be terrible if this list became a "moderated" list (whether it is E-mail, netnews, hypermail, slashcode, etc.), but that is what it might become if we (collectively) don't have self-discipline.

[End of "my two cents" worth]
