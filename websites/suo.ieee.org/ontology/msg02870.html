Phil,

One of my main reasons for objecting to the terms "regular" and
"irregular" is that almost all concepts are irregular, and the
regular ones are highly artificial.

> Regarding terminology, the terms "regular" and "irregular" are somewhat
> neutral and descriptive,

I would say that they are misleading, since all "normal" concepts,
i.e., the ones that we use 99.9% of the time, would be classified
as "irregular".  

> while "natural" and "technical" may be confusing
> since, as you point out, scientists try to develop technical definitions /
> descriptions for natural concepts like "space", "time", etc. However, I
> would not object to using the terms "natural" and "technical" in this way.

> Another terminology that is used in the literature is, of course,
> "classical" vs. "family resemblance" category.

Again, classical has the connotation of "normal", which is also
misleading since no natural concepts can be defined classically".
The basic point that Wittgenstein was making is that all natural
concepts are learned by "family resemblance", and they only have
a single, unambiguous meaning within the scope of a single
language game.

In my proposed method of formalizing W's language games, I consider
the infinite lattice of all possible theories.  Each theory in the
lattice corresponds to a different language game.  Within any one
theory, each term (predicate name, concept type, or whatever) has
a single meaning.  But there are infinitely many possible theories.
Therefore, each term may have an infinite number of meanings.

Therefore, I would define a "normal" or "natural" concept as one
with an open-ended, possibly infinite number of "meanings" (i.e.,
one meaning for each theory or language game in which it appears).
A technical concept is one that is restricted to a single theory,
in which it has a single "meaning".

For example, the word "dog" would correspond to the concept type
Dog, which would have meanings Dog.1, Dog.2, Dog.3, ...., for every
theory in the lattice in which the concept tyep Dog or the predicate
name dog appears.

The same thing would be true of the words "space", "time", "number",
"operating system", or any other term used in science:  it would have
a potentially infinite number of meanings that would vary according
to the theory happens to appear in.

The only kinds of concepts that might be called "classical" are
the ones that are indexed by some particular theory, such as
time.890697, which is that definition of time that is used in
theory 890697.

John Sowa
