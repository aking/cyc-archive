James L Piat wrote:
> 
JR: I think, in any case, it is clear from his
    early ambitions that are expressed frequently
    in Volume 1 of the new chronological edition that
    the import of the phrase "all thought is in signs"
    is to locate thought in the observable signs themselves --
    such as, say, the signs on the screen right now (if you are
    reading this from the screen) -- as a relational property thereof,
    rather than thinking of signs as taking on sign-value because a mind
    somehow infuses them with meaning from its own "inner" resources.

> Dear Joe, John, Folks --
> 
> I'm reminded of the old issue of whether a tree falling in the forest
> makes a sound if no one hears it.  I conceive of thought as requiring
> movement of actual signs (whether they be publicly manipulated or done
> privately in ones own brain), but it does not seem to me that the force
> that animates this movement or the actual objects moved is what constitutes
> thought.  Rather, what constitutes thought is the representation and forecasting
> of events.  I understand the expression "all thought is in signs" as meaning that
> signs are tools of thought (with thought itself being a tool for forecasting the
> outcome of events).
>
> Or to return to my opening sentence, thought is like a tree falling in the forest.
> Sound  and fury is what some particular individual might make of it;  but, though
> the dogs bark, the caravan of thought moves on.  Lastly, I'm reminded of something
> I heard on NPR today.  Seems some musician and wit has observed that the British
> are not particularly fond of music but they love the noise it makes.  As usual
> I've probably mixed apples and bobbing for them, but, hey, what are friends for?
> And as for my point -- Well, again, that's something I was hoping you all might
> tell me.

Your question betrays
a certain assumption,
the assumption that
a tree cannot hear.

But I have heard ...
a tree to scream ...
as a blade, or a blaze,
or a blight comes near.

no advantage to a scream,
to adapt, appease, avert,
if a creature's compeers
have no sense of hearing.

> Oh what the hell.  I'm trying to say three things.
>
> One, that thought is the forecasting of events
>      though the manipulation of signs.
>
> Two, that thought lies in the act of representation
>      and forecasting not in the energy that animates
>      these processes nor in some mysterious process
>      by which signs are materialized.
>
> Three, that signs (even when employed covertly as in one's private thoughts)
>        are necessarily public artifacts.  Public because the consequence of
>        events, which thought is designed to forecast, is itself public.
>        Not only are the events public but any attempt to symbolized
>        them in a communicable form must ultimately be rooted in
>        shared public experience if the communication is to be
>        successful.
>
> Do these three points seem correct to you?
