>MW: My simple approach to this would be to have subtypes of wisdom that
>represented the different ways in which you could be wise. You would be wise
>if you were a member of any of them, but you could also be wise in many ways
>by being a member of several. Am I missing something?

Not really, I suspect. That would be my strategy also. However there 
is an active school of thought that would claim that this would be 
missing something important, and that rather than this being an 
extensional kind of subset relation, there is something inherent in 
every single predication of any relation to the things it relates, 
involving a 'trope', ie the 'way' it relates them, and that these 
'tropes' are inherently intensional in nature and a vital part of 
what 'relation' means. I merely pass this on to you in the spirit of 
fair reporting, and make no further claims. I don't really understand 
it, but many distinguished SUO-ers, including Nicola Guarino, Chris 
Menzel, Fritz Lehman and others have urged that these ideas be taken 
seriously. If you give 'trope' to Google http://www.google.com/  you 
will find much more on this topic.

> > And there are many such proposals in the literature,
> > including Jerry Hobb's 'promiscuous ontology' which Ive mentioned
> > before in these discussions, the fairly standard 'event' ontological
> > style based on case grammar, where a mere predication is rendered as
> > an existence claim plus a collection of binary case relations (agent,
> > object, patient, time, place, ....), the Sanskrit variation, and the
> > various ideas of 'thirdness' arising from Pierce's philosophy. All of
> > these can be seen as ways of extending the ontology - introducing new
> > kinds of entities in addition to the things that yer average naive
> > realist might think of - in order to allow finer ways of
> > distinguishing ways that things can be true which are more subtle
> > than the starkly simple crudity of being in a set.
>
>MW: But I haven't seen (or at least not understood) anything yet that
>required more than the membership of something in some set to "do the
>business", plus some relation occurences (logic speak) and some
>axioms/rules.

Well, Hobbs would argue that if you can something about x, then you 
have to allow that x exists. Sounds reasonable? OK, consider:
1. Joe went to the store.
2. Joe went to the store quickly.
3. Joe went to the store by driving the car slowly.
Hobbs says that 2 and 3 are consistent since even slow driving is a 
fast way to get to the store. But he also says that being quick and 
being slow aren't consistent with each other. So, he says, we are 
forced into saying that there is something here which is slow - the 
driving - and something ELSE which isn't slow - the going. So what 
seems like one event (and occupies the same spatiotemporal envelope) 
is in fact at least two 'things'. In fact it can be arbitrarily many 
'things' depending on how much you feel like saying about it. Notice, 
this isn't a way of putting the same thing into a number of different 
sets; it forces many different things to exist in order to be the 
things that these various ways of talking can be *about*.
