> > John,
> >
> > Thanks very much for your analysis of Peirce CP 5:569 relative to
> > meaning-preserving translation. It seems that another relevant
> > quote along the same lines is:
> >
> > "A sign stands for something to the idea which it produces, or
> > modifies. Or, it is a vehicle conveying into the mind something
> > from without. That for which it stands is called its object; that
> > which it conveys, its meaning; and the idea to which it gives
> > rise, its interpretant. The object of representation can be
> > nothing but a representation of which the first representation is
> > the interpretant. But an endless series of representations, each
> > representing the one behind it, may be conceived to have an
> > absolute object at its limit. The meaning of a representation can
> > be nothing but a representation. In fact, it is nothing but the
> > representation itself conceived as stripped of irrelevant
> > clothing. But this clothing never can be completely stripped off;
> > it is only changed for something more diaphanous. So there is an
> > infinite regression here. Finally, the interpretant is nothing
> > but another representation to which the torch of truth is handed
> > along; and as representation, it has its interpretant again. Lo,
> > another infinite series." CP 1:339
> >
> > If I understand this approach correctly, then I do not doubt its
> > validity, yet I am bothered by the definition of "truth" in terms
> > of translations of a proposition between a potentially infinite
> > set of different ways the proposition could be stated in
> > different languages.
> >
> > I think that when we perceive a statement is true, our perception
> > of truth must be finite. At most it would seem that we could use
> > some finite form of meta-reasoning, or perhaps a finite form of
> > induction, to deduce that a proposition must be true in each of
> > an infinite set of all possible circumstances relevant to that
> > proposition... This would seem a promising approach toward
> > finitizing the concept of truth in such cases...
> >
> > Two other quotes, which I like better, from Peirce are:
> >
> > "Deduction is that mode of reasoning which examines the state of
> > things asserted in the premisses, forms a diagram of that state
> > of things, perceives in the parts of that diagram relations not
> > explicitly mentioned in the premisses, satisfies itself by mental
> > experiments upon the diagram that these relations would always
> > subsist, or at least would do so in a  certain proportion of
> > cases, and concludes their necessary, or probable, truth." - CP 1:66
> >
> > "Induction is that mode of reasoning which adopts a conclusion as
> > approximate, because it results from a method of inference which
> > must generally lead to the truth in the long run. For example, a
> > ship enters port laden with coffee. I go aboard and sample the
> > coffee. Perhaps I do not examine over a hundred beans, but they
> > have been taken from the middle, top, and bottom of bags in every
> > part of the hold. I conclude by induction that the whole cargo
> > has approximately the same value per bean as the hundred beans of
> > my sample. All that induction can do is to ascertain the value of
> > a ratio." - CP 1:67
> >
> > What I like about these quotes are the explicit references to
> > "observation" as a component of determining that a proposition
> is true...
> >
> > Quite often we are concerned with the truth of propositions not
> > in all possible worlds, but only in certain contexts, and limited
> > to certain times and places. Thus when I say "it is raining", I
> > am asserting a proposition to be true in a very limited context,
> > time and place. The truth of my proposition depends on whether I
> > and others who are observing the same context, at the same time
> > and place, would agree that the statement correctly describes
> > what is happening.
> >
> > Granted, an unlimited number of different people could have
> > different interpretants (internal conceptual structures) all
> > representing that it is raining. Yet I do not need to reason
> > about all the infinity of different possible conceptual
> > structures they may have. Rather, I need to somehow know that my
> > own perceptions tell me it is raining, and that I describe these
> > perceptions by the sign / phrase "it is raining", and I need to
> > know that most often whenever I say "it is raining", other people
> > agree with me, and that most often whenever other people say "it
> > is raining", I agree with them -- which seems to be a form of
> > finite induction... Though how we all learn to say "it is
> > raining" in response to the same kinds of situations is another,
> > "Gavagai" problem...
> >
> > Generally, it seems to me that a predicate is a sign representing
> > a kind of property, relationship, event, situation, etc., that
> > may be asserted to exist, and which may involve other referents.
> >
> > A predication, or proposition (in general, I would use the terms
> > synonymously) would then be a "complex sign" involving the use of
> > a predicate along with signs representing one or more referents,
> > to refer to the existence of a property, relationship, event,
> > situation, etc. involving one or more referents.
> >
> > Whether a predication is true or false then, in general, would
> > depend upon the observation of an agent, to verify whether the
> > state of affairs claimed by the proposition actually exists. A
> > proposition is considered to be true iff one observes that the
> > referent (property, relationship, etc.) that it refers to
> > actually exists, within a certain context, space and time where
> > the proposition is being considered.
> >
> > It would seem then that the "meaning" of a proposition is its
> > referent, i.e. it is whatever property, relationship, event,
> > situation, etc. that the proposition refers to. A proposition is
> > meaningful iff its meaning exists, i.e. iff the proposition is
> > true according to the observation of some agent.
> >
> > This approach seems close to that of a Russellian proposition, as
> > described by Robert C. Moore in "Logic and Representation", yet
> > it also seems possible to define in terms of Peircean semiotics...
> >
> > Anyway, these are just some preliminary thoughts, and I would
> > very much appreciate your comments, and those of others, about them...
> >

> > > For my definition of meaning-preserving translation (MPT),
> > > I defined a proposition as an equivalence class of sentences.
> > > Each sentence, as uttered, is a sign instance.  Each sentence
> > > as a syntactic construct is a sign type.  My propositions are
> > > a class of sign types in one or more languages.  That class
> > > represents the supertype of all the sign types that have the
> > > same interpretants in all those languages.  It too is a sign
> > > type.

> > > >Thus, a
> > > >portrait with the name of the original below it is a

> > > >that if anybody looks at it, he can form a reasonably correct

> > > because there would be no indication of its object.  The
> > > addition of the name plus the convention that a name below
> > > a portrait indicates the name of the person portrayed adds
> > > the necessary connection between the form (picture) and the
> > > object (person).  That is why I require condition #3 of
> > > vocabulary preserving in my definition of MPT.  Without
> > > that condition, one could claim that the following three
> > > sentences "express the same proposition":

> > >  1. "Every cat is a cat."

> > >  2. "Every dog is a dog."
> > >
> > >  3. "Every unicorn is a unicorn."

> > > All three of these sentences have the same logical form,
> > > and all three would have the denotation true in every
> > > possible world.  But their content words have different
> > > referents (and "unicorn" has no referent in our world).
> > >

> > > >receiving an interpretation, that is, by virtue of its

> > > >sign of the same object. This is as true of mental judgments
