Adam, 
	.	Please don't get me wrong, I have been strongly supportive
of all the development work to date, and the effort that has gone into it.
This should be plainly evident from my support for the merged ontology and
ALL contributions to it or elsewhere. Your e-mail seems to indicate you
don't appreciate that FACT, and even seems to infer the opposite. 

	.	Basically my concern is that there is also an enormous and
valuable amount of discourse in the existing database the explications and
resolutions of which would be very worthwhile material for the SUO
supporting documents necessary to explain assumptions and intended
perspectives underpinning the SUO. I am concerned that this material
currently looks like being discarded, along with much similar discussion
still ahead of us if we don't develop and implement ways of capturing it or
its essence. 

	.	Meanwhile, Matthew West is beginning to embark on an
exercise that will necessarily amount to the replication of much of the
insights and background developed in that material. 

	.	Furthermore, much of the discussion has been unco-ordinated,
excessive and, because many of us are too busy to take it all in, repetitive
as people cover the same ground in parallel with each other. It is the
resulting wasted effort that I am trying to address, because of the energy
it is sapping. 

	.	I would appreciate it if you could make suggestions relating
to this area, in addition to your suggestions below, which go without
saying. 




	.	But, now that you have put out your plea below, I feel I
should address another side of that area also. In terms of concrete
proposals for SUO content, I expressed the desire some months ago about
contributing generic physiological material I have developed, covering most
vertebrate land animals, and relating to the rest plus vertebrate sea
animals. 

	.	I figure content of such a generic character would be
relevant to such an enormous proportion of all biological and related
sciences and practices that it would warrant inclusion in any very general
ontology of sufficient breadth. I also figure that it is not so different to
some of the topological/geometrical material already in the merged ontology.


	.	Unfortunately I didn't even get any replies indicating
whether or how such general subject matter should be added. I have held off
from proposing it for that reason. 




	.	Incidentally, I note you mention David Whitten's
contributions. When I look at the Merged Ontology, I can see no
acknowledgment of his work, and have therefore concluded it is not included.


	.	Is anyone able to advise what happened to David's work?
Surely, since he was the first person to actually begin a precursor to the
SUO, there should be some recognition of his work.

Graham,
   I too am dismayed by the volume of discussion and the sense that although
individual contributors have benefited from the discussion, and a rather
small group is making progress on an ontology, that we're not working
together and making collective progress on a standard.  I believe that the
solution is not improved document management or better tools but rather for
folks to be specific and concrete in developing a formal document expressing
their preferred version of an upper ontology.
   If you believe something specific is lacking in the Merged ontology that
Ian has been working on, suggest a new axiom, or set of terms.  If some
folks would prefer EXIST or some other basis for the standard, then offer
concrete extensions to it using the EBNF syntax defined for it.  Formalisms
without explanation or discussion may make it difficult for some folks to
communicate but discussion without formalisms will lead us simply to more
talk.
   I would like to applaud those folks who have been very concrete (and
forgive me for those who I have forgotten): Helena Sofia Pinto for
contributing an ontology of units and measures, Nicola Guarino and his
colleagues for their work on mereology, Pat Hayes for his detailed and
specific help on temporal axioms, Chris Menzel, David Whitten and Robert
Kent for their work on the "structural ontology".  We should also thank John
Sowa, Achille Varzi, Roberto Casati, James Allen, Barry Smith, Stuart
Russell, Peter Norvig and all the unattributed contributors to the
Ontolingua repository.
   If everyone on this list would create just some small ontology of even a
dozen terms supported by axioms that has not yet been covered in the merged
ontology, we could make tremendous progress.   This could also include
domain-specific ontologies as the exercise of linking such ontologies to the
merged ontology will elucidate the general areas that need to be developed.
   How about a practical semiotics of books, recordings and documents; how
about some basic graph theory with application to roads or computer
networks; how about an ontology of human obligations;  an ontology of speech
acts;  an ontology of textures and coverings;  an ontology of materials;  an
ontology transfers of possession.  The number and richness of available
areas is astonishing.  For academics and industrial researchers there are a
wealth of possible papers to write on these topics.  For applied computer
scientists and systems analysts, formalize the information model of your
current project and I guarantee that you that you'll produce a better model.
   Let me reiterate Leibniz' call to "Let us calculate!"

Adam

>         .       Let me set out some initial perspectives on this
suggestion
>a bit further. We are talking about a methodology for managing e-mail
>discussion used for developing a complex standard in a highly sophisticated
>area.
>*       Each topic gets a title, and a document defining the topic, that
>aims at providing a position that resolves the issue. If consensus isn't
>reached, then several perspectives are provided in the document, if
>necessary one per participant.
>*       Whenever anyone diverts from the subject at hand, their
contribution
>gets reallocated to another topic, either existing, or a new one.
>*       Breadth of a topic is governed by the quantity of text required to
>resolve any particular perspective
>*       The moderator:
>*       .       needs to be able to differentiate clearly between the
>varying perspectives by accurately and precisely identifying where each
>contribution fits,
>*       .       piece together and co-ordinate the contributions of
>different participants, so as to minimise the number of topics / documents.
>         This takes a particular skill set not everyone has, an ability to
>delineate between, and co-ordinate concepts.
>*       Each contributor:
>*       .       has a say about how their contribution is added to the
>document, if necessary by providing a new perspective. and
>*       ,       is responsible for the maintenance of their own
perspectives
>(thereby limiting the number of parallel perspectives each contributor adds
>to any topic). This would be by using portions of other perspectives so as
>to flesh out the parts they don't have any problems with.
>*       Each perspective in each document is not discarded until the
project
>is complete, lest any seemingly irrelevant issue turns out to be needed
>later. Where agreement is reached, perspectives can be merged. Each
document
>then matures into a number of competing bases for a portion of the SUO
>standard, or its supporting documents.
>*       Discussions about why anything should be a particular way amount to
>additional topics, and this is likely to assist others later, who can be
>referred to the relevant topic to answer their query. This means that many
>topics should be quite small and succinct.
>
>         .       This may sound like a lot of work, and it is. But, how
much
>work has happened even on-line to date???? How much value will be gained

lot
>of effort was expended by a group of people
