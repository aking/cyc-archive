Pat, Aldo, et al.,

The word "intended" was an unfortunate interpolation in Aldo's
statement.  Intentions are most definitely not included in the
definition, since they are something to be defined rather than
anything that can be used to define something else.

AG>'Sign' is primarily a relation between an item from some vocabulary
>>of a language (natural, formal, or artificial), and an intended
>>meaning.

PH>Wow.  No wonder I havent been able to follow what Peircians have been saying.

>
>That is what I would call a semantic relationship, but a very special 
>one (because of the 'intended'). In fact, it is a notion which I 
>would argue is not only not central to the current business of 
>ontology, but is actively dangerous and should be studiously avoided.

I agree that any definition that included "intend" or
"intention" as a defining term would be useless for the
purpose of the SUO or anything else that must be computable.

Aldo is correct in saying that a sign is defined by a relation,
but Peirce explicitly called it the representation relation,
and it relates a sign s, an interpretant i, and an object o.

The interpretant is what has also been called the intension,
and the object is what has been called the extension.

The question of what the speaker intended can only be inferred,
often by very indirect means that involve the entire context.
It cannot be assumed in the definition of sign.

John Sowa
