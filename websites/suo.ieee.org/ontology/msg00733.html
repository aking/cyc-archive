> Two points:

> 1. It relies on at a high level concepts that are not yet

I'm not sure exactly what you mean by "high level concepts", but formulas
with the 'nth-domain' predicate don't involve anything fancy.  'nth-domain'
is a ternary predicate that takes as its arguments a predicate, a number
corresponding to an argument position of that predicate, and a class.
Consider for example the following typical formula taken from the merged
ontology:  (nth-domain exists-at 2 TimePoint).    

> 
> 2. It is actually no more efficient than writing it out properly, so
> actually there is no special benefit.

Actually, it is much more efficient (in terms of number of characters) to
abbreviate assertions with the 'nth-domain' predicate.  Consider the
definition of 'nth-domain' given in the "Structural Ontology" section of the
merged ontology:

;; Let REL be any n-place predicate, and let M be the numeral for the
;; positive integer m, where m is less than or equal to n.  Then we
;; let
;;
;;   (nth-domain REL M CLASS)
;;
;; serve as an abbreviation for
;;
;;   (forall (VAR_1 ... VAR_n)
;;           (=> (REL VAR_1 ... VAR_n)
;;               (instance-of VAR_m CLASS)))

If we were to write out the 'nth-domain' formula given in my preceding
comment, it would look as follows:

(forall (?X ?Y)
     (=>
          (exists-at ?X ?Y)
          (instance-of ?Y TimePoint)))

This formula has about three times as many characters as the abbreviated
form.  This means that use of the 'nth-domain' predicate results in an
ontology which has far fewer lines (since every predicate defined in the
ontology should have exactly one 'nth-domain' assertion for each of its
argument positions) and which can, I think, be more easily absorbed by the
reader.
