Jim,

Thanks for given me a reply.

If you are asking me whether we should starting working on this document, my
answer is YES. If you ask me whether we should also start working on Matthew
West's document, my answer is YES. Presumably if you ask Robert Kent whether
we should start working on his document, the answer is YES (I do not have a
strong view as I have not had time to consider his document - but surely he
has a right, as a participant, to get it considered). I am not at all sure
why we should have to choose one now, and which one it should be.

As I said before, it seems sensible that we have some transparency.

If you are (understandably) keen to start tying the SUO down, one way of
rescuing the process might be to:

* Ask people to agree to the following process (i.e. ask in advance),
* Agree that the 'analysis' will proceed on the basis of documents submitted
in KIF - which are merged into a single document, which is then reviewed.
* Suggest a list of documents (or better still ask for suggestions) as
inputs to the process, including Niles'
* Set a deadline for people to submit other documents as candidate inputs,
* Invite people to take on the task of merging the inputs, but make the
merging as transparent as possible, so that people can see why, and
influence if they wish, any decisions.

Then we might have a base document.

BTW I do not think this is likely to lead to a coherent merged ontology -
maybe only a sort-of aligned ontology - but at least the process will feel
as if it has been managed more fairly.

I can appreciate that you feel we need to get on - and that just exchanging
emails or producing documents at random could go on forever. However, it
seems to me sensible management practice (rather than IEEE policy and
procedures) that once you have worked out where you want to go, to then make
some kind of plan/process to get there and ask people whether this is OK -
at least if you want to keep them on side.

The way things have been happening now makes me (and Pat - as you can see
from his comments) feel there is another agenda.

I think following some procedure like the above will not actually lose time
in the long run. Any delay in getting a working paper out now will be made
up by avoiding problematic discussions later on.

Regards,
Chris

IEEE provides very few Policies and Procedures for how a working group does
its work.  But this is probably good since all groups are unique.  This is
why we had to invent our own rules for who can vote.  I assure you we will
continuously run into situations where we don't have policies and procedures
or a process for dealing with something.  We can try to prepare ourselves,
but when not prepared, we'll have to do our best to invent ourselves as we
go.  If there's anyone motivated to head a committee on Policies and
Procedures, please step forward.  But it's very important that we not get
too hung up on procedures and lose sight of our main objective of developing
a useful document and building maximum consensus.  Also, the only vote that
really matters is the final ballot, and that is closely controlled by IEEE
and key to their maintaining ANSI accreditation.
