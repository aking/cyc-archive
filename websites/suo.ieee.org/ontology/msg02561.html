>Pat,
>
>The semantics I have been urging is nothing new.  It is what Tarski
>specified in his 1935 paper:  a hierarchy of levels, each of which
>uses pure FOL, but level n+1 is allowed to talk about the syntactic
>constructs of level n (or any lower level) and the relationships
>between those constructs and the domain of discourse.
>
>For most practical purposes, a two-level version is quite sufficient.
>
>PH> Possible worlds and updates to the knowledge base have nothing
> > whatever to do with one aother, which is why modality and rigidity
> > are mutually irrelevant. To see why, make the simple observation that
> > it is as easy to alter a modal sentence as to alter a non-modal one.
>
>You observation is falsified by every SQL database in the world.
>The facts are in the DB, which any authorized user is allowed to update.
>The definitions and constraints are stored separately, and only the DB
>administrator is allowed to modify them.

Fine. As I have often said, the only way to stop axioms being altered 
is to not give someone write permissions to the file they are stored 
in. That has nothing directly to do with modality. If the DB 
adminstrator is allowed to alter the axioms, he can just as easily 
alter one that says (NEC (P A)) as he can one that merely says (P A). 
Contrariwise, if some grunt can't alter any of them, (P A) is just as 
secure as (NEC (P A)). Putting a modal operator into the axioms (or, 
seems to me, even into the axioms of a formalized metatheory) doesnt 
do a damn thing to alter filewriting privileges one way or the other. 
In your picture, what connects modalities to user authorization? What 
is the model-theoretic distinction between users and adminstrators?

>There is a major difference
>in the ease of modifying the two kinds of assertions.

But not because of their form; because of where they are stored. 
Tarski didnt say anything about read/write permissions.

> > If you provide a model theory for it, John, we will consider putting
> > it in. We took out all the definitional syntax because it had
> > (literally) no meaning whatever.
>
>Tarski provided that model theory in 1935.

No, he didn't. See last comment.

>You simply have assertions
>at the metalevel that say certain statements at the object level are
>laws (the deductive closure of the definitions and axioms) and other
>statements are facts (ordinary assertions).

Being a 'law', and being alterable by some class of people with 
certain kinds of file access, seem on the face of it to have nothing 
to do with one another. All I need to do is type 'rm *.*'  and the 
laws vanish along with everything else.

The model theory I want to see needs to actually specify what it 
means to 'alter' an axiom, what the effect is of doing that altering, 
and what it means for someone to be able to do altering or not. If 
one can say, in the formal language, something that if true will 
prevent itself being erased, then the truth conditions must somehow 
refer to erasure and prevention.

>In belief revision systems, this metalevel distinction is also called
>"entrenchment":  laws are more deeply "entrenched" than facts, and the
>implementation makes it harder to change them.
>
>There's nothing new about this.  It's been done before.  Millions of
>times a day in the commercial world.

Of course file permission levels are used in the real world. I want 
to know how to incorporate those ideas in a model theory for an 
assertional language.
