On Fri, 20 Apr 2001, pat hayes wrote:
> >
> >I suppose there is a sort of general, metalinguistic schema here that
> >exhibits the pattern that any such relation will fit, something like
> >(ignoring the uniqueness clause for brevity):
> >
> >(forall (?X1 ... ?Xn)
> >        (<=> (REL ?X1 ... ?Xn)
> >             (exists (?e)
> >                     (and (EVENT-TYPE ?e)
> >                          (ROLE-1 ?e ?X1)
> >                          ...
> >                          (ROLE-n ?e ?Xn)))))
> >
> >But this is pretty useless, as the event types and corresponding
> >roles for relations still have to be analysed out piecemeal, relation
> >by relation.  There's no general principle or algorithm.
>
> I protest: this IS a general algorithm, if properly stated. The key
> is not to predicate 'event-type' of the ?e in question, but to
> connect it to the original predication of REL, by mapping REL from an
> n-ary to a unary relation REL': ...

Pat, you misunderstand, and please do not place me in the middle of all
this triadicity nonsense.  I was only interested, first, in helping
Matthew get clear about the logical form of what he was trying to
assert.  Second, I was interested in the general idea behind Matthew's
attempt -- as a piece of *ontology* -- to flesh out certain relations in
terms of the occurrence of certain events.  Thus, with regard to what I
was doing -- as opposed to what you *thought* I was doing -- the key is
*exactly* the predication of "EVENT-TYPE" of the ?e in question.  The
interesting question for me here was simply when, if ever, this is a
fruitful thing to do, ontologically.  That is, when, if ever, is it
fruitful to enlarge one's ontology to encompass events to give a fuller
ontological analysis of a given relation?  *That* is what has to be
decided "piecemeal", that is, by the careful analysis of a class of
relations.  I was not talking about what *can* be done logically -- I
have agreed with you from the beginning of your debate with the
triadicians that the algorithm for translating from n-ary relations to
unary and binary relations is obvious and trivial -- but about whether,
when, and how a certain class of entity might serve some useful
ontological purpose.  Ok?
