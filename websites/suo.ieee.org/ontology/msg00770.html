At 02-02-01 23:36, pat hayes wrote:

Mike, et al.

But there seem to be some options about what the individual sibling
relations are in the real world (is this possible if there is a real world?)

As Fritz Lehmann has also noticed, there seem to be two camps using the same
word (tuples) to denote different things.

The logical view is that they are tuples - as Pat Hayes wrote:
Close. The relation is a *set* of triples: all the triples that the
relation holds true of. So for example if A sent Bill to B , then the
triple <A, Bill,B> is one of the triples in the "billing by post"
relation-set. Of course there are probably others in the same set.
Every time someone gets billed, that defines another triple in the
relation-set. Think of this set of triples as God's database of all
the billings that ever happened or ever will happen. (It has to be
God's since it might well be infinite.) As far as logic is concerned,
this (potentially infinite) set of triples *is* the relation in
question.

The relational database view is that they are a different kind of tuple -
where, somehow the tuple also carries content.

Wait a minute. You think that a triple of actual things (people and messages, say) doesnt carry content? How much more concrete could content get than actually being some pieces of the world? Its like pointing to the actual thing instead of describing it.

We can distinguish them by their identity conditions. If we swap the columns
in relational database we do not change the identity (or meaning) of the
tuple.

If you swap the entries in one tuple in a table (without swapping the columns) you do change the meaning, though; thats the comparison you should make. This comparison is a bait-and-switch, since when you swap those 'columns' you are also swapping the implicit labellings of the columns. You can do that to mathematical relations as well, if you allow them to include that much extra structure.

If we swap positions in a tuple e.g. <A, Bill, B> to <B, A, Bill> we
do. Similarly while in logic a tuple can belong to a number of relation
classes, it is not clear that this is so for the relational view.

So two things can only have one relation in a RDB ?

One advantage of the logic view is that relations can be treated like
properties - and there can be a simple taxonomy of relations. This is more
difficult to achieve under the relational view. However, the logic view does
lead to the seemingly odd conclusion that for every relation class and
particular relation there also exists its inverse.

No, it doesn't. This is a delicate point but an important one (since it will be central in SUO-KIF, which can quantify over relations). Saying what a relation is does not automatically say that all things like that must be relations.  If every set of tuples was a relation, then we would have a genuine higher-order logic; but we do not make this assumption (though it is, I concede, often made by mathematicians.) So it is quite possible for a relation to exist but its inverse not to, or at any rate not to be considered a relation in the universe of all relations.

Pat Hayes

### Perhaps this particular discussion should be carried on after everybody has had a chance to read the absolutely seminal paper "Towards a Logical Reconstruction of Relational Database Theory" by pur sang AI person Ray Reiter already in 1978. It is accepted by DB theorists everywhere, certainly those that dabble in semantics of data, at least as an ontological-avant-la-lettre point of departure for talking formally about (relational) database. The paper quite simply but firmly imbeds relations and all the stuff that floats around them in Database World in first-order logic or indicates where or why this is difficult.

### After this, the discussion at hand may happen to go away entirely. Relations à la Sowa, Reiter, ... are mathematical objects and as such part of the semantical domain (i.e., ontology) for the language L1 in which we talk about them (viz. as in the message above). Relations à la King are part of a different language L2, the practice of relational database as pontificated by Codd et al., and large parts of the relation-concept this language in fact may be (formally semantically) interpreted in terms of the above base ontology, but some terms and usage belong to the domain ontology for L2. The interesting issues here are merely *grin* the (interpretive) mapping where in L1 and the building and alignment of L2 to L1.

--Robert Meersman

Whatever you call it, there is something in the real world which
entails two things being related to each other in a particular way.
Take for exampl, being ciblings.  I'm sorry, but I can see no way round
ciblinghood being in the real world.

MK> I absolutely agree

You can choose to call it a
relation, or a relationship, or a frobnit, but it is a real world notion,
which from time to time one may wish to represent.

MK> I choose to use the word relationship to refer to the thing in the real
world, and reserve it for only that. Conversely I choose the word relation
to refer to the construct in my model which represents the things in the
real world.  I sometimes want to talk about the one and sometimes the other
and find it essential to have different words to refer to them.

MK> A complication is that the word relation, in the singular, is used by
Ted Codd (and I believe generally by mathematicians) to mean the set of
tuples, whereas the word relationship, in the singular, in natural English
can only mean either a single instance, or a type of, relationship.  Thus a
relation (singular) actually represents many relationships (instances).
