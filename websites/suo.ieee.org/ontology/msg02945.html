Chris, and other reificators.

If you are barking at the wrong tree, we are a pack of hounds down there :)
Being at the Paris reunion where reification made its come back, and having
supported it heartily, I want to second.
What is common to RDF and XTM may be limited to the reification of a
statement, either in the technical sense it has in RDF, either in the sense
that an XTM <association> represents a "natural language statement".
XTM goes further on, allowing to reify (making a topic proxy of) anything
you can speak, write, think,
dream about, be it a statement or anything else ...
But it's altogether consistent IMO, and I think there is a growing consensus
about it in the semantic web community at least.

Cheers

[Chris Angus wrote]
> In the case of XTM the use of the term 'reification' had been abandonned
> because of its perceived general misuse.  It came back into use at the
Paris
> meeting (as indicated by Steve Pepper) because it appeared to more
> accurately reflect what part of the conceptual model needed to say.  In a
> topic map topics exist, but the subjects that they represent are
themselves
> abstract (although they may have physical manifestations).  We can form
> associations between topics, we may also want to refer to some
associations
> between topics as if they were topics.  At a data modelling level this can
> lead one to simply treat 'association between topics' as a subclass of
> 'topic'.  But this leads to problems, in particular it is not necessarily
> appropriate to have an association inherit from topic because, in a
general
> case, properties of a topic are not all applicable in the case of an
> association.  What it really comes down to is that the association can
rank
> alongside other subjects and can be converted to (or reified into) a
topic.
> The topic and the association are thus distinct and one is not a subclass
of
> the other.
>
> To me the process of reification is that of converting a statement (which
is
> an abstraction) into a thing or entity in its own right in order that it
may
> become the subject of some other statement.  It is often the equivalent of
> the device of placing a statement in single quotes in order to distinguish
> between that part of a sentence that is in the metalanguage and that which
> is in the object language.
>
> Do you think that I am 'barking up the wrong tree'?
