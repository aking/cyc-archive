This has become such a general issue and such a constant source
of misunderstanding in our joint and several efforts to explore
the logic of relations that I am going to extract it for a bit
of focal attention back on the mainline of the "Exposition".

This basic point here is that there are two levels of description:

1.  The level having to do with relations,
    which are sets of tuples.

2.  The level having to do with ordered tuples,
    which can be seen as lists or as sequences,
    which are actually functions from an ordered
    set, like counting numbers, to a component set.

To describe a relation is to describe a set.
To describe a relation as a set it is necessary
to refer to either one of two sorts of things:

a.  Properties of relations, which are properties of sets.
    Here one is choosing to "change horses mid-stream"
    and to continue the res on the intensional ponens.

b.  Categories, classes, or sets of relations, which are
    categories, classes, or sets of sets.  Here one is
    choosing to "stay the course" and to keep betting
    on the horse of the initial extensional color.

To describe a relation as a set of tuples it is, of course,
necessary to describe the tuples to some degree and extent,
perhaps even to remind oneself of their structure as tuples,
according to some definitions of the cartesian product, say,
the ones that express it in terms of projections or of roles.
But I said "necessary" not "sufficient".  There is much more
to the structure of a relation, qua set, than the structure
of its tuples, taken severally, one at a time, in isolation.

Let me state this more carefully.

1.  Speaking purely of "compositive reduction",
    all k-adic relations can be "reduced to
    "compositions" of 3-adic relations, whereas
    no 3-adic relations can be "reduced to
    "compositions" of 2-adic relations.

2.  Speaking purely of "projective reduction",
    the situation is more mixed, but one must
    note that this form of reduction takes it
    for granted that one can use a well-known
    set of 3-adic relations, the connectives,
    in order to perform the reduction at all.

3.  The "change of variables" formula:
    For all x in X,  L(x) <=> [L(y) for some y in X] --
    the introduction of "y = x" is just another distraction --
    is a true thing to say about the application of a relation L
    to a tuple x in a cartesian product space X, but it contributes
    nothing non-trivial to the articulation of L as a relation, per se.
