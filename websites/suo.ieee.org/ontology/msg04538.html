Proof As Semiosis

We have been looking at several different ways
of proving a propositional equation, and along
the way we have been exemplifying the species
of sign transforming process that is known as
a "proof", to be more specific, an equational
proof of the propositional equation at issue.

Let us now draw out these semiotic features of
the business of proof and put them into relief.

Our syntactic domain S contains an infinite number of signs
or expressions, which we may choose to view in either their
text or their graphic forms, glossing over for now the many
details of their parsicular correspondence.

Here are some of the expressions that
we find salient enough to single out
and confer an epithetic nickname on:

| e_0  =  "()"
|
| e_1  =  " "
|
| e_2  =  "(p (q))(p (r))"
|
| e_3  =  "(p (q r))"
|
| e_4  =  "(p q r, (p))"
|
| e_5  =  "(( (p (q))(p (r)) , (p (q r)) ))"

Under Ex we have the following interpretations:

| e_0 expresses the logical constant "false"
|
| e_1 expresses the logical constant "true"
|
| e_2 says "not p sans q and not p sans r"
|
| e_3 says "not p sans q and r"
|
| e_4 says "p and q and r else not p"
|
| e_5 says that e_2 and e_3 say the same thing

We took up the Equation E1 that reads:

(p (q))(p (r))  =  (p (q r)).

Each of our proofs was a finite sequence of signs.
Thus, for a finite integer n, it took on the form:

s_1, s_2, ..., s_(n-1), s_n.

Proof 1 proceeded by the "straightforward approach",
starting with s_1 as e_2 and ending with s_n as e_3.

Proof 2 lit on by "burning the candle at both ends",
changing e_2 into a normal form that reduced to e_4,
changing e_3 into a normal form that reduced to e_4,
once over and once back, thus tethering e_2 and e_3
to a common stake.

Proof 3 took the path of reflection, expressing the
foreign equation between e_2 and e_3 in the natural
equation e_5, then taking s_1 as e_5 and exchanging
it by dint of value preserving steps for s_n as e_1.
