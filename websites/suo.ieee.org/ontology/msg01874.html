John, &  All,

Let me augment, clarify, and fix a couple
of mistakes in this propositional material.

For ease of future reference, I list here the exact forms of the axioms that I use,
devolving from Peirce's "Logical Graphs" via Spencer-Brown's "Laws of Form" (LOF).
In formal proofs, I will use the annotation scheme from LOF to mark each step of
the proof according to which axiom (or "initial") is being invoked to justify
the corresponding step of the syntactic transformation, on graphs or strings.

The axioms are just four in number,
divided into two types, as follows:
the "Arithmetic Initials" I1 and I2,
the "Algebraic  Initials" J1 and J2.

(I use a trivially different J1 here.)

Notice that all of the axioms in this set have the form of equations.
This means that all of the inference steps they permit are reversible.
In the proof annotation scheme below, I will use a double bar "====="
to mark this fact, but I may at times leave it to the reader to decide
in which direction the indicated axiom needs to be applied. 

---------------------------------------------------
 Axiom I1.  Condense=> / <=Distract
---------------------------------------------------

      o   o         o
       \ /          |
        @     =     @

     ( ) ( )  =    ( )

---------------------------------------------------
 Axiom I2.  Refold=> / <=Unfold
---------------------------------------------------

        o
        |
        o
        |
        @     =     @

      (( ))   =

---------------------------------------------------
 Axiom J1.  Delete=> / <=Insert
---------------------------------------------------

       xo           o
        |           |
       x@     =     @

      x(x)    =    ( )

---------------------------------------------------
 Axiom J2.  Distribute=> / <=Collect
---------------------------------------------------

      y   z      xy   xz
      o   o       o   o
       \ /         \ /
        o           o
        |           |
       x@     =     @

    x((y)(z)) = ((xy)(xz))

---------------------------------------------------

As you are undoubtedly aware, Peirce introduced these formal equations
at a level of abstraction that is one step higher than their customary
interpretations as a propositional calculus, which readings into logic
he called the "Entitative" and the "Existential" interpretations, and
so I will refer to the interpreted systems of graphs as "EnG" & "ExG",
respectively.

I will now give a proof of the "Double Negation Theorem" (DNT).
This is the proof that was given by George Spencer-Brown in his
book 'Laws of Form', and therein credited to two of his students,
John Dawes and D.A. Utting.

Here is the proof of the Double Negation Theorem,
marked as "Reflection" or "Consequence 1" in LOF.

----------------------------------------------------

----------------------------------------------------

        @     =     @

The other theorem that I use a lot is what I call
the "Weed & Seed" theorem.  The proof is a boring
inductive exercise, and so I will let you have it,
but it's just the one that we have discussed many
times before under divers & sundry names & guises,
which says that a label can be freely distributed
or freely erased (retracted or withdrawn) anywhere
in a subtree whose root is labeled with that label.

The minor mistake that I made in the proof of the
Splendid Theorem of Leibniz was that I marked the
next to the last step as proceeding by way of an
axiom, whereas it is really via an early theorem,
namely, the one that GSB calls "integration", but
that I tend to think of as "dominance/recession".
Don't worry about the names, as I will probably
think of new ones in a couple of weeks or years.
In order to prove this theorem, following along
in the course that GSB set out to do it, I will
need just one prior theorem, called "generation",
that is a simple case of the weed & seed theorem.

-----------------------------------------------------
 Generation Theorem C2.  Regenerate=> / <=Degenerate
-----------------------------------------------------

       yo         xyo
        |           |
       x@     =    x@

Just to gather everything on one page, I repeat with these
corrections the proof of the Splendid Theorem from Leibniz.

---------------------------------------------------
 Theorema Praeclarum (Leibniz).
---------------------------------------------------

    r o   o s     o rs
      |   |       |
    p o   o q     o pq
       \ /        |
        o---------o
        |
        |
        @                =                @
