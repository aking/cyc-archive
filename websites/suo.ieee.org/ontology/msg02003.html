Jon,

Peirce's triad <s, i, o> is the central semiotic relation.
But there are also other kinds of relations between signs.
Token, type, tone is one.  Genus-species is another.  We seem
to be seeing different patterns in CSP's writings.  Perhaps
all of them, perhaps some of them, or perhaps none of them
are what he had intended.

So far, it seems that you are finding some patterns, and
I am finding others.   The ones I have been highlighting
are ones that are particularly useful for solving a kind
of problem that I have been dealing with.

>Except that the way you read it,
>it means something different than
>than what it meant when it was writ.

Perhaps or perhaps not.  You haven't given any evidence
to support your claim.

>Except for the special case that I discussed above,
>where "sampling relations" amount to special cases
>of sign relations, considerations of type hierachy
>are not relevant to the relation of object to sign.
>It is, in general, a very distinct type of relation.

I agree that they are very different types.  The question
of the relation of object to sign is not the one I was
concerned about.  I was looking for a general type of sign,
which I called a proposition, whose possible interpretants
are the same as the interpretants of its subtypes, as expressed
in different languages.

>John, what you are engaging in here is a particular type of reductionism,
>trying to reduce an irreducibly interpretive feature to an objective one.

No, that is not what I am doing at all.  I am looking for
a general type, which I called a proposition, whose subtypes,
namely the sentence types in different languages, all have
the same range of interpretants.

John Sowa
