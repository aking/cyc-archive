Pat, Lee, Matthew, et al.,

Some comments:

> MW: This is no longer true when you introduce a possible worlds 
> approach which allows you to consider possible futures, either 
> hypothetical or intended, as well as possible pasts, which may be 
> linked to or detatched from the present.

I agree that this is a very useful technique.  But I also agree
with Lee that the notion of "futurity" is not automatically associated
with those worlds.  Following is a comment by Quine, which applies
to all applications of possible worlds to any variant of modal logic,
including temporal logic:

WVOQ>The notion of possible world did indeed contribute to the
>semantics of modal logic, and it behooves us to recognize the
>nature of its contribution: it led to Kripke's precocious and
>significant theory of models of modal logic. Models afford
>consistency proofs; also they have heuristic value; but they do
>not constitute explication. Models, however clear they be in
>themselves, may leave us at a loss for the primary, intended
>interpretation.

In other words, you can use possible worlds to verify that your
axioms are consistent, but they won't automatically ensure that
they mean what you really wanted them to say.

PH>If so, then, it must be that this philosophical category has never
been successfully formalized. All the formal modal logics every devised
can be interpreted in a possible-world model theory, a basic insight
due to Kripke.

Kripke certainly formalized his theory, but as Q. pointed out, he only
ensured consistency of the axioms.  The accessibility relation is
an undefined primitive that does not guarantee that his "successful
formalization" captures anyone's actual intuitions (whatever they
may be). 

PH>Whatever that last sentence means, it isn't what logicians mean
>by 'modality'. Like much of Peircian lore, it smacks more of theology
>than any other science.

I am opting out of the subsequent theological polemics.  My only
comment is that I'm sure that Peirce would have disavowed most of
the so-called "Peircean lore" in the same way that he disavowed
William James' version of pragmatism:  he changed the name of his
theory to "pragmaticism"
