Well, I'm not sure this response helps to clear the air, Pat.  The fact
is that the modal character of a concept might be reflected in its
nonmodal axiomatization.  It doesn't follow from that that the
axiomatization is therefore "really a modal assertion in disguise", or
that it doesn't mean what it seems to mean; to the contrary, it means
exactly what it seems to mean.  There are no hidden modalities lurking
in the nearby bushes.

Here's the kind of situation that it seems to me important to avoid.
Maybe it will illustrate some confusion on my part that needs clearing
up.  Suppose, in the usual extensional fashion, we define a relation R
to be symmetric if and only if, for all x and y, if Rxy then Ryx.
Suppose now we have an ontology of human relationships that includes the
loving relation, and that we are applying that ontology with respect to
a given domain of persons in which, as fortune would have it, all love
is requited.  By our axiom, we infer that loving is symmetric.  Now
suppose our domain broadens to include Johann and Martha, and that
Johann loves Martha, but that his ardor is not reciprocated.  Is not our
ontology now inconsistent with this fact?  How do you propose to avoid
situations like this?  Seems to me (though I'm willing to be convinced
otherwise) the thing to do is to recognize that there's more to symmetry
(call it a modal element, or don't) than just satisfying -- as a matter
of current contingent fact -- that Rxy materially implies Ryx, and that
this is most naturally expressed, in a nonmodal language, by using a
conditional rather than a biconditional in the symmetry axiom.  Symmetry
thus becomes a property that has to be *declared* true of a relation
rather than *inferred* from its instances.
