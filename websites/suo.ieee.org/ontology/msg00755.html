>Hi Matthew,
>
>	I understand that your view is that the lack of a representation of
>change in the current version of the merged ontology is a "show-stopper"
>rather than I desideratum (as I have claimed).  However, you need to provide
>some sort of reasoning for your view.  As Adam has pointed out, we cannot
>make progress as a group unless we offer support for our respective
>positions.

Heres why:
1. While many particular applied ontologies can be done without 
considering dynamics (eg many bioinformatics Kbases do not), it will 
certainly need to be included in anything like a 'standard' for 
widespread use in engineering. (Do I need to argue this point in more 
detail?)

2. The basic distinctions in the SUO upper heirarchy (right at the 
top, in the current version, two steps below 'entity') depend on 
temporal distinctions and issues of change. Until these notions are 
clarified, therefore, the meanings of these basic distinctions will 
remain unclear.

3. Every aspect of the 'physical' part of the ontology - any of it 
concerned in any way with things that exist in time and space - will 
have to be re-written from scratch if the basic ontology of time and 
change is ever altered. One cannot take a 'static' ontology and then 
just graft a 'dynamic' ontology onto it as a new set of axioms, since 
the 'static' axioms one has already are now a theory of a *section 
through* the new universe, not a theory of that new universe. All the 
quantifiers will change their meanings when you make this 
'extension'; every predicate will need an extra argument, or every 
truth need to be modified by modalities, or every term rewritten in 
terms of spacelike sections of fourdimensional entities, or some 
other tectonic shift will have to be performed. Adding change is not 
adding axioms: it is completely rewriting (and usually re-thinking) 
every axiom in the ontology. So it is almost certainly better to 
think about all this as soon as possible and do it right the first 
time.

4. In addition, different ways of thinking about change and dynamics 
give one different perspectives on the highest parts of the 
ontological classification. For example, from the 4-d perspective, 
the distinction between object and process which is so central in the 
Sowa ontology is almost invisible, and in fact often does more harm 
than good.  So not only the axioms change when you change the changes 
(if you see what I mean); the very conceptual fabric of the ontology 
tends to alter. Another reason to at least be thinking hard about 
this stuff as soon as possible.
