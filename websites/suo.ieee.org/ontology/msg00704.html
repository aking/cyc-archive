>But, let's have a go. Does this mean, say,

Close. The relation is a *set* of triples: all the triples that the relation
holds true of. So for example if A sent Bill to B , then the triple <A,
Bill,B> is one of the triples in the "billing by post" relation-set. Of
course there are probably others in the same set. Every time someone gets
billed, that defines another triple in the relation-set. Think of this set
of triples as God's database of all the billings that ever happened or ever
will happen. (It has to be God's since it might well be infinite.) As far as
logic is concerned, this (potentially infinite) set of triples *is* the
relation in question.

GH4>	We need also to bear in mind the legal role of standards also.

>GH3>	I would prefer that we avoid esoteric language, and just say things
>in plain English. For example, the essence of a wheeled vehicle is that it
>incorporates one or more wheels on which it is capable of running to
achieve
>travel. I fully agree that we need to have an unambiguous language for
>stating that, but we shouldn't forget that this sort of simple concept is
>all we will be explaining for many instances in the SUO (cf. wheeled
>ornament, and conceptual vehicle).

If only it were that simple. Starting from simple English sentences there is
a huge terrain of analysis to cross before one can start writing axioms. The
problem is that almost anything you say in English has hidden assumptions
that need to be made explicit in an ontology. Take your example: what
exactly does 'incorporate' mean? What does it mean to say that something is
'capable' of doing something? (What if it never it fact does that, like an
auto that is put through a crash test immediately after manufacture: was
that a wheeled vehicle?)  What does it mean to say that one kind of event
makes something else 'achieve' something? What kind of claim is made by the
assertion that something is travelling? (Is this saying that a certain kind
of event exists? Or that a certain kind of state changes? Or that a certain
kind of dynamic property is true of the moving thing? All of these will look
very different when written axiomatically. Do you want them to entail each
other? What are the general rules of such entailments?) 

GH4>	These are all good points, giving indications of the nature and
extent of the challenge of our task. Writing a Java standard, to quote your
example, involves similar challenges. 

It might be worth reiterating a point of Doug Lenat's. People often think
that Cyc is so called because it is a kind of encyclopedia, but that's
wrong: Cyc is supposed to represent all the stuff that ISNT in the
encyclopedia because the writers of the encyclopedia assume that their
readers already 'know' it. That's the hard part of ontology-building. 

GH4>	I didn't realise this, so I appreciate the elaboration. This gives

doing. However, I admit I only realised that aspect of the ontologies after
about 2/3rds of the time from the start of the project to now, thanks to
some of the elaborations of Doug McDavid. 

GH4>	I never said I expected the SUO work would be easy. Much standards
