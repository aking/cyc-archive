PLG.  Reflective Note 1

o~~~~~~~~~o~~~~~~~~~o~~~~~~~~~o~~~~~~~~~o~~~~~~~~~o

| More strange than true.  I never may believe
| These antique fables, nor these fairy toys.
| Lovers and madmen have such seething brains,
| Such shaping fantasies, that apprehend
| More than cool reason ever comprehends.
| The lunatic, the lover, and the poet
| Are of imagination all compact.
| One sees more devils than vast hell can hold:
| That is the madman.  The lover, all as frantic,
| Sees Helen's beauty in a brow of Egypt.
| The poet's eye, in a fine frenzy rolling,
| Doth glance from heaven to earth, from earth to heaven,
| And as imagination bodies forth
| The forms of things unknown, the poet's pen
| Turns them to shapes, and gives to airy nothing
| A local habitation and a name.
| Such tricks hath strong imagination
| That if it would but apprehend some joy
| It comprehends some bringer of that joy;
| Or in the night, imagining some fear,
| How easy is a bush supposed a bear!
|
| Theseus, 'A Midsummer Night's Dream', 5.1.2-22

We have all heard the likes of this story before ---
a lunatic, a lover, and a poet walk into a bar ...
a logician, a physicist, and a playwright are
on a small plane when the captain announces ...

I must confess, I have become so fascinated with what these three,
Shakespeare, Quine, and Duhem, will say to each other next that I
will just pretend that I don't already know where it's going, and,
who knows? maybe I don't.

But I can't help musing at this point on a funny, peculiar twist of fates:
that I began with a tragicomic and two straight men, and now I am sensing
that the poet is telling me more and more the truth, while the scientists
are telling me more and more the fiction, a pleasant fanatasy, to be sure,
but all too beautiful to be true, while the harder task lies on the skald,
who must try to wake the noddy rest of them with waters both hot and cold,
and tell a tale that is choice, in word, in deed, but all in all too true
to be pretty.

Jon Awbrey
