Leo,

I think you are pointing to a distinction between psychology and logic.
Psychology is a descriptive science, and cognitive psychology endeavors
to observe and to describe 'how we think'.  Logic is a normative science.
It says something like:  "If you have such and such goals, the aims that
are proper to a certain mode of deliberate thought, then here is how you
ought to conduct your thinking process, if you want to succeed at it."

The relationship between these is an old wound in pragmatic thought,
being one of the issues that divided Peirce from James and Dewey,
though Dewey had time to get it better straightened out later.

Part of the problem that we have in thinking about this stuff
is that we have been handed such ramshackle conceptual tools
to try and tackle it with, mostly a lot of dichotomies and
dyadic relations when what is called for are trichotomies
and triadic relations.

In my own efforts I form the analogy with Chomsky's distinction
between competence models and performance models of any capacity,
which trace back to a theme in Aristotle that distinguishes between
the mode of 'episteme', rendered as "possession of knowledge", and
the mode of 'theorein', rendered as "exercise of knowledge".  And
yet Aristotle -- strangely enough, given what we have been taught,
though it turns out to be a generic aspect of his thinking if one
reads what he actually wrote -- anyway, Aristotle verges on using
a trichotomy here, dimly highlighting the scheme that involves
possession (competence knowledge), exercise ('how to' knowledge)
and critical reflection (descriptive and/or normative knowledge).

Jon
