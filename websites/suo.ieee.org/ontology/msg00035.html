Robert, (& John),

This will be brief and off the cuff,
as my time is scarce and my mind is
scattered at the moment, but I keep
coming back to John's WTM Triptych
on a periodic basis, in association
with Aristotle's example of analogy,
the "war between neighbor's" picture.
I have a dim awareness that there is
some sort of near-analogy between the
two schemes, but each time that I try
to put my finger on it the substance
of it just seems to evaporate away.

http://ltsc.ieee.org/logs/suo/msg01293.html
http://ltsc.ieee.org/logs/suo/msg01350.html
http://ltsc.ieee.org/logs/suo/msg01772.html

If I had to force a rough correspondence,
it would probably look a little like this:

A = Theory, at least, a theoretical predicate,
B = Basis of the current theoretical paradigm,
C = Casual (formative, informal) Context, or an
    object, phenomenon, problem, situation therein,
D = Domain of formal acquaintance, formalized knowledge.

The "analogy arrow" or the "model morphism" goes from
the formative to the formalized, for example:  C -> D.
You know this because the arrow of formalization is in
the direction of approximation, loss of information,
neglect of detail, reduction of complexity, or just
plain simplification.  The underlying idea is that
you are using what you know about easy or familiar
cases, like D, to deal with hard, new, strange,
or unknown cases, like C.

This is closely connected with the theme in my 'dissipation'
that has to do with the relation between what I describe as
(0) a "casual", "formative", or "informal" context and
(1) a "formal" or "formalized" domain.

This formative versus formalized stuff is not yet moved into the attic
at Arisbe, but there is some treatment of it in the paper that Sue and I
presented at the Amherst conference on "Re-Organizing Knowledge" (ROK),
and a version of this paper can be found there:
