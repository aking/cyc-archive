>
>I suppose there is a sort of general, metalinguistic schema here that
>exhibits the pattern that any such relation will fit, something like
>(ignoring the uniqueness clause for brevity):
>
>(forall (?X1 ... ?Xn)
>        (<=> (REL ?X1 ... ?Xn)
>             (exists (?e)
>                     (and (EVENT-TYPE ?e)
>                          (ROLE-1 ?e ?X1)
>                          ...
>                          (ROLE-n ?e ?Xn)))))
>
>But this is pretty useless, as the event types and corresponding roles for
>relations still have to be analysed out piecemeal, relation by relation.
>There's no general principle or algorithm.

I protest: this IS a general algorithm, if properly stated. The key 
is not to predicate 'event-type' of the ?e in question, but to 
connect it to the original predication of REL, by mapping REL from an 
n-ary to a unary relation REL':

>(forall (?X1 ... ?Xn)
>        (<=> (REL ?X1 ... ?Xn)
>             (exists (?e)
>                     (and (REL' ?e)
>                          (ROLE-1 ?e ?X1)
>                          ...
                           (ROLE-n ?e ?Xn)))))

The 'role-n' relations are standard and are used throughout the 
translation. This translation requires no further 'piecemeal' 
analysis, and can be used to translate any collection of n-ary 
assertions into a collection of assertions using only unary and 
binary relations. It is a truth-preserving translation in the sense 
that any interpretation of the original vocabulary can be transformed 
into an interpretation of the final one which maps truths precisely 
into truths (using the mapping described by Matthew West, ie the ?e 
is the n-tuple of entities which satisfies the REL in the original 
assertion: the mapping is compactly described by REL(x1,...,xn) --> 
REL'(<x1,...,xn>).) If you draw the resulting logical sentences as a 
semantic network, in fact, this mapping *is* CSP's reduction which 
shows the universality of 'triadicity', which appears here as the 
fact that one needs to mention ?e three times in places (not for a 
single assertion, but when you start conjoining them you do.)

All this is so obvious that I wish we could stop arguing about it. 
It's not like this is anything *new*. This translation mode has been 
familiar for over a century, was known to CSP, has been noted, 
commented on and used by philosophers, linguists, logicians, database 
technologists, AI Krep designers and ontologists. It is the basis for 
an entire collection of linguistic theories (case grammars) and an 
entire information-processing technology (semantic networks), and at 
least one influential approach to ontology design (Hobbs' 
"ontological promiscuity".) It ought to just be one weapon in our 
armory, to be used when appropriate, not the subject of religious 
debates about arcane doctrines of the holy trinity. Surely we can 
find more useful things to talk about?
