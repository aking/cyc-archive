Dear Jon and Jean-Marc,

I keep only the end of your discussion there :

I do not think that you get what formal models are for. 
They are not intent to exhaust the being or even meant 
to exhaust the description of any real thing.  At best, 
they capture something of importance about that thing, 
enough to get by for a while.  There does not have to 
be any confounded kind of gawd-aweful death-struggle 
between formal and practical.  The choice of a formal 
model is a practical decision.  Formal is beautiful 
and beautiful is practical, like I done tole you. 

Jon Awbrey
<<<<
Jean-marc :
>>>>

Yes, but if a formal model is too all-encompassing,  as a result of which
some things become sign which otherwise would not have been signs
practically, its usefulness is limited to theoretical speculations detached
from reality. 
  JM  --&nbsp;
Jean-Marc Orliaguet ( jmo@medialab.chalmers.se )

I will take a personnal "centrist" position as regard to these issues :
both of you look to be at the same time right and wrong as it seems to me.
I often think that someone could undertake to give an account of the whole,
large and great system of Peirce as a trial to answer this unique and
simple question : To what extent the logical categories (where logical here
means formal) can correspond to the categories of common experience ? His
answer goes through the concepts of sign and pragmatism among others. Such
concepts are some kind of analytical tools to make the answer more and more
determinate (Thanks to Jon for is citation thread about "determination").
The program of CSP does not reside in the opposition between formalism and
experience but on the contrary in the mystery of the possibility of a
unique synthesis (how formal resolves in experience and how experience
really is formal). Neither is formal a matter of practical decision choice
as Jon says, nor experience is a matter of reality as Jean-Marc seems to
tell us.
This program is a trial. That is to say that the final response is on
inquiry. From this fact some eminent thinkers have concluded that such a
peircean program failed (because it could not exhibit a proof for
pragmatism for example).
I dont think so.

Bernard
