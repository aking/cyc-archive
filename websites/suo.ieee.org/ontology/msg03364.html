jlrchand@erols.com wrote:
> 
> Jon:
> 
> You propose no less than 9 "languages."
> You decline to answer an elementary question with regard to these
> nine languages.   What bijections exist among these languages?

S_1 has 16 elements.
S_2 has 16 elemtnts.
There are 16! bijections between S_1 and S_2.
If you mean "bijections that preserve the objects (= denotations of signs)"
then there is only one such bijection.

> You propose:
> >
> > but the languages S_4, S_5, S_6 all have
> > a countable infinity of expressions,
> 
> Are you also proposing that S_1, S_2 and S_3
> have "a countable infinity of expressions",
> or not?

Here, read this again:

| The languages S_1, S_2, S_3 have 16 elements each, and so they are enumerated
| in full by the entries in the Table, but the languages S_4, S_5, S_6 all have
| a countable infinity of expressions, and so but samples of these sign domains,
| giving one of the simpler expressions for each object, are shown in the Table.

> You propose:
> > A sign relation L_j is a subset of a cartesian product O_j x S_j x I_j,
> > where O_j, S_j, I_j are its designated Object, Sign, Interpretant Domains,
> > respectively.
> 
> I do not understand the meaning of the term "Cartesian Product" under
> these circumstances.

The "cartesian product" of k sets X_1, ..., X_k, written X_1 x ... x X_k,
is the set of all k-tuples of the form <x_1, ..., x_k> that are formed
by choosing x_j from X_j for j = 1 to k.

> In particular, what is an element of the co-domain?
> In other words, how is the codomain formed?

Domain and codomain are defined for binary relations, for example, functions.

> Are you certain that you wish to use the term "Cartesian Product" for this relation?

I think so.

> With regard to language, you assert:
> > Formally, I use "formal language" in the sense of "formal language theory", e.g.:
> >
> > | Denning, P.J., Dennis, J.B., Qualitz, J.E.,

> > | Prentice-Hall, Englewood Cliffs, NJ, 1978.
> 
> This reference is not available to me.
> Would you state the definition of language
> that the text presents?
