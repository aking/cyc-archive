>  3. Some logicians have proposed some answers, but
>     they have glaring deficiencies.  Richard Montague,
>     for example, defined the intension of a sentence to
>     be a function from possible worlds to truth values.

> 
>     a) All theorems of mathematics and anything else that is
>        true by definition would turn out to have exactly the
>        same "intension", since they are true in all possible

> 
>     b) For the purpose of the SUO, one would hope that the
>        axioms and definitions would be true in quite a few,
>        if not all possible worlds, and Montague's definition
>        would not be able to distinguish much, if any difference
>        in "meaning" among any of them.
> 
>     c) Possible worlds are very difficult to reach by any
>        commercial transportation service, but provability

>        would take exponential time to verify.
> 
> Problem to be solved:  Consider the informal definition

> Certainly, "2+2=4" should not have the same "meaning" or "intension"

Even as "5 + 7" has a different meaning than "12", to refined enough eyes.

> > Yes, but, er, if your patent formulations did not compare
> > in some favorable way with what is ordinarily understood
> > by the words "meaning" and "proposition",

> First of all, there is no well-defined "ordinarily understood"
> definition of those terms.

There are two or three well-recognized aspects of meaning,
dimensions of address, or directions of approach that are
broadly and commonly marked out, especially within any
pragmatic theory of signs, Peircean style, namely, the
aspect of a 3-adic sign relation that has to do with
the 2-adic relation between objects and signs, and,
in contrast, the aspect of a 3-adic sign relation
that has to do with the 2-adic relation between
signs and interpretant signs.  I simply do not
understand people's hesitancy, yours especially,
to use this rather well worked-out terminology
and body of techniques for analyzing real-live
sign relations, or even just simplified models.

> The definition that I proposed makes finer distinctions than Montague's,
> it distinguishes "2+2=4" from "2+3=5", and it can be computed in linear
> or nearly linear time.  And, most importantly, it corresponds rather
> closely to informal intuitions about differences of meaning.

Again, OS (denotative) or OI (connotative) meaning?
I am not even trying to eliminate one in favor of
the other.  The former is much more concerned with
recognizing the "coincidences" of objective meaning
between "2+2" and "4", or between "2+2=4" & "2+3=5",
than it is with preserving all of the nuances of the
potential distinctions between them.  Neither account
of "meaning" is sufficient to all of our purposes, so
why not keep and continue to develop both?  However,
the later realm is much more unruly, and not likely
to be "tamed" without risk of laying waste to it.

> To be formal, it is essential to define the procedure in terms

> The four criteria for a meaning-preserving translation (MPT)
> have the following justification:
> 
>  1. Invertible:  If sentence s is mapped to another sentence
>     f(s), which is said to have the same meaning, then it should
>     also be possible to map f(s) back to another sentence that
>     has "the same meaning".
> 
>  2. Proof preserving:  The rules of inference in each language
>     under consideration must be justified by truth conditions,
>     in the sense of everybody from Aristotle to Peirce and
>     Tarski.  Therefore, the syntactic operations on proofs
>     have a direct link to corresponding semantic operations.
> 
>  3. Vocabulary preserving:  The content words of any language
>     must be linked to the universe of discourse by triadic
>     sign relations.  Therefore, preserving the vocabulary is
>     a prerequisite to preserving the referential connections.
> 
>  4. Structure preserving:  By structure, I mean the minimal logical form
>     preserved by Peirce's EGs (existence, conjunction, and negation).
>     Existence is much simpler and easier to verify pragmatically
>     than the universal quantifier, conjunction is pragmatically
>     the least controversial of all the Boolean operators, and
>     negation is essential to support at least FOL.  Peirce gave
>     many, many pragmatic discussions of the interpretation of
>     negations in his graphs, and his arguments can be assumed
>     for this purpose.
> 
> These four conditions can be verified by purely syntactic
> tests, but each of them is justified by pragmatic reasons.
> 
> Another important point:  although my condition #2 is the
> same as Montague's, condition #4 of structure preserving
> makes the proofs almost trivial:  any two statements that
> have the same structure by #4 can be tested for equivalence
> in linear or nearly linear time.
>
