John,
   Thank you for the correction.  I mixed up the 
distinctions of the CYC #$Collection and #$Group.  
I should have re-checked before mentioning it.
Mea culpa.
   I forgot that in CYC a #$Group is a subclass of
#$Individual, not of #$Collection, as in SUO.  It's one 
of the facets of CYC that seem non-intuitive, however 
logically self-consistent.
   In the SUO browser, the "Collection" entry
reads:
-------------------------------
Collection
                                                                                                      
(constant id 141)
 Arg 1:
    (subclass-of Collection Object)
    (documentation Collection "Collections have elements like Sets, but,
unlike Sets, they have a position in space-time and elements can
be added and subtracted without thereby changing the identity of the
Collection. Some examples are kits, football teams, and flocks of
sheep.")

 Arg 2:
    (subclass-of Group Collection)

 Arg 3:
    (nth-domain member 2 Collection)
    (nth-domain subCollection 1 Collection)
    (nth-domain subCollection 2 Collection)

Appearances of Collection in Rule Antecedents:

    (=> (instance-of ?X Collection) (exists (?Y) (member ?Y ?X)))

----------------------------------------

    What threw me off, in part, was the assertion that a collection in
SUO 
has a position in space-time, which should qualify it as a physical
entity,
unlike the #$Collection of CYC, as you point out.  The SUO "collection"
inherits the SUO character of "physical" via the intermediate "object"
    I am still somewhat uncertain what is the relation of the terms 
"Collection" and "Group" in the SUO merged-ontology to those same terms 
in CYC.  It seems that the CYC #$Group is intentionally similar to
the SUO "collection", and the CYC #$Colleciton is intentionally similar
to SUO "class".  The public CYC upper ontology has no class named
#$Class,
but they refer in their documentation to some collections as "classes".
Is that your understanding?
    My apologies for not getting a firm grip on these definitions before
mentioning them.

    Pat Cassidy

> Patrick Cassidy wrote (in part):
> ----------------------
>     Well, I thought that there was general agreement that,
> as in CYC, "collections" can be physical objects.
> ---------------------
> 
> In Cyc, Collections can have physical objects as members (if that's what Patrick meant), but the collection itself is an Intangible.  In Cyc, if you want a collection that is itself a physical thing located in time & space, you have to make it a Group, not a Collection.
> 
> By the way, in Cyc, they don't assume that time and space have to go together.  There is one class for TemporalThing (located in time) and another, partially overlapping class for SpatialThing (located in space).  The presumption is that there are some things with temporal attributes but no spatial attributes, and some things with spatial attributes but no temporal attributes.  I haven't seen this possibility represented in the ontologies proposed here so far.
