David,

You may want to consider generalizing the concept of a relation
to ones that do not have a definite arity (or adicity) but that do
still have a "typing relation" (generalizing the signature function?)
that relates each "elementary relation" in the relation to its class
of compatible types.

For example, you can treat a whole formal language R,
a subset of A* for some finite (countable?) alphabet A,
as though its words were written like this:

   a1:a2:a3: ... :aj: ... :an

where n is the length of the word in question,
and you can treat these as the "elementary relations" of R,
working out the appropriate algebra of relations. 

This is an off-the-cuff remark, coming out of a memory of some work
that I began more than 20 years ago, and so I am a bit hazy on how
my last and best formulation of it worked out, or if it ever did,
so I will put off saying any more until I can find my old files.

Jon

People who say "unary" (or "singulary" (Quine)), "binary", "ternary", etc.
usually say "arity", but then they run into the resulting confusion about
"binary" numerals, so some of them resort to calling these "dyadic" numerals.

People who say "monadic", "dyadic", "triadic", etc. say "adicity" on those
few occasions when they are being consistent, but mostly they defer to the
common usage.  Still, this prevents a clash with the names of numeral bases.

Here is what I remember of the conceptual "construction kit"
that I used to generate these prospective generalizations.
I was trying to treat the logic of relations in the style
of category theory, on beyond binary (dyadic) relations,
which has already been done, more or less.  Also, if you
think about the analogies between extensional relations
and formal languages, as sets of that embody informational
constraints in the form of subsets of tupled or concatenated
elements, it turns out that they have a whole lot in common.
Except, with formal languages one loses the notion of arity.

In "category theory", if one has a "category" X, then one has a collection
of "arrows" Arr(X) and a collection of "objects" Obj(X), and a couple of
maps Source, Target : Arr(X) -> Obj(X), and one writes "f : A -> B" to
indicate that Source(f) = A and Target(f) = B.  Plus some other stuff.

| J. Lambek & P.J. Scott, 'Introduction to Higher Order Categorical Logic',
| Cambridge University Press, Cambridge, UK, 1986.

So if you try to imagine what a "relational arrow" R would look like,
it would have "j^th domain maps" Dom(j) : Arr(X) -> Obj(X), and one
would write "R : A1 x ... x Ak" to indicate that Dom(j)(R) = Aj,
for j = 1 to k.

Now think about the composition of ordinary functional arrows,
and try to devise a fitting generalization for these new-fangled
relational arrows.

Well, composition is really a two-step operation, if you want to
parse it that way.  Suppose you are given f : A->B and g : B->C.
First you assemble the product of the domains, AxBxC.  Then you
put the constraint associated with f : AxB on the first two places
and the constraint associated with g : BxC on the last two places,
and then you take the projection on the first and last places.
(I often call this "Tarski's Trick", but it's probably ancient.)

So a suitable generalization of composition for relational arrows
involves you in doing stuff like this:  Write out a product domain
of interest, place the constraints of Rj on a specified subset of
places, for j in some J, then take various projections of interest.

Now, once you have taken the trouble to generalize everything this far,
then you get as an extra bonus the added utility of having all of these
same ideas work more or less the same way for formal languages, viewed
as sets of strings that satisfy collections of informational constraints.

Sorry, here I was just reverting to Peirce's 19th Century language.
An "elementary relation" is just the intensional name for a tuple,
that is, whatever it is that corresponds, for an intensional thinker,
to an "element of the relation".  All of these fine distinctions aside,
I usually just go ahead and identify elementary relations with n-tuples,
but here it saves me having to mention the "n" in a situation where all
of the fixed arities are no longer guaranteed.
