>      I would like at this point to comment on just one part 
> of this thread, and I
> am not
> sure if I am in agreement with Pat Hayes on this point, 
> specifically the
> question of whether there should be an "abstract object" 
> category at the highest
> level of the SUO.  I believe it is a serious mistake, at 
> least insofar as it
> inhibits
> expressiveness of an SUO,  to create a high-level category of 
> "abstract objects"
> or "abstract
> entities" for which the property of "abstractness" is inheritable.

MW: I agree, but that is principally because I see abstract object and class
as synonyms, so you don't need both.

>     What this does is to prohibit a user from ever 
> considering a non-physical
> concept as
> being an "abstraction" of a physical object and therefore a 
> parent or more remote
> ancestor on
> an inheritance chain.  Although the high-level binary distinction of
> abstract/concrete is
> common in general ontologies, it flies directly in the face 
> of the menage of
> abstraction, which is to
> create a more general concept by taking away some attribute 
> from a more specific
> concept.

MW: When you start of with a class, what you get from this is
subclass/superclass relations. When you start from something physical and
take away some attributes (quite literally it doesn't have them, not that
they are unknown) then you get a class, of which the original object is at
least one member.

> It also ignores the cognitive process by which abstract 
> concepts are formed, i.e.
> by generalization,
> typically by abstraction of certain common attributes from 
> some physical objects,
> properties,
> or processes.
>     It is understandable that when one thinks of mathematical 
> objects as being
> abstract, one can
> probably agree that all descendents of mathematical objects 
> will also be
> abstract.  But this
> is only one kind of object commonly thought of as "abstract". 
>  There are many
> physical
> objects and processes for which one can easily produce a 
> non-physical (and
> therefore
> "abstract") analogous concept by simply removing the 
> attributes which make the
> object
> physical, such as mass for objects or physical time as the 
> co-ordinate of change
> (or "progress")
> in a process.   Mental objects and processes are also often 
> thought of as
> abstract, but are
> typically intimately associated with physical processes.

MW: I agree, also states, and their classes "She was in an emotional state".

>      I believe that at the highest level, we need to be able 
> to create classes of
> objects and
> processes which are not themselves physical, but can be 
> considered as ancestral to
> 
> physical objects and processes.  

MW: I'm not quite sure what you are saying here. What I would want to say is
that you should be able to create classes of which physical objects were
members. Is that what you mean?

> In this view, an "abstract' 
> object is simply one
> that is
> not "physical", i.e. if it is an object, it must not have 
> mass, and if it is a
> process or
> event, it must not involve physical objects nor be located in 
> space-time.  The
> absence of
> the property "physical" is what I think of as defining an 
> "abstract" concept , but
> the
> absence of an attribute need not be an inheritable property in itself.

MW: And this works fine if you see this as a class/member relationship.

>     In this way, the existence of objects, like 
> information-bearing objects, with
> both
> physical and abstract characteristics, will not be 
> problematical, as they can
> inherit
> characteristics from both physical and non-physical lines in 
> an ontology.

MW: The way I (well we EPISTLE really) deal with this is by having the
physical object, say a piece of paper with ink on it, and a "class of
information representation" (sorry its a bit of a mouthful - just read
information, but it is a class) which is the pattern that is common to that
piece of paper and all copies of it.

>     Although the public CYC (I do not have access to other 
> parts of CYC) has the
> class of "#$CompositeTangibleAndIntangibleObject", it also 
> seems to include a
> notion
> of inheritable abstractness (the concept "#$Intangible" and 
> its descendants).  

MW: I'd be interested in some examples of instances of these things. One of
the few things the EPISTLE Core Model does not allow is for a thing to be
more than one of an individual (spatio-temporal extent), a class, or a
relationship (instance of a relation).

> I
> think
> this is what led to the creation of  the classes 
> #$PartiallyTangible and
> #$PartiallyIntangible,
> and though I have tried to determine what utility such 
> distinctions have, I have
> not found
> in the public CYC any evidence that these categories are 
> themselves useful in
> reasoning.
> (The notion of an information-bearing object is, however, 
> probably quite useful).

MW: Yes, but it is an incidental not necessary class (in 3D speak).

> I conclude
> that these classes are probably kludgey and inefficient ways 
> to avoid the problems
> created by
> not allowing non-physical concepts to be ancestors (more 
> abstract versions) of
> physical concepts.

MW: I agree, see above for my analysis.
