HP = Howard Pattee
JA = Jon Awbrey

HP: Michael Conrad's "tradeoff principle" is a good argument that programmability,
    efficiency, and evolvability (creativity) are incompatible in one type of structure
    (e.g., Conrad, "The brain-machine disanalogy" BioSystems 22, 197-213, 1989).

JA: But the motto you mention is just like any number of heuristic slogans that
    people form and use as guides.  It represents a post hoc rationalization
    for why something keeps happening a certain way, like why something you
    tried in every way you can think of just keeps failing to work out.

HP: This is neither a motto, slogan, nor heuristic principle but a careful
    argument that has been empirically verified by many efficiency comparisons
    of structurally programmed (meaning sequential or formal language-like strings
    of symbols) vs. concurrent, distributed "parallel" networks.  Tasks like pattern
    recognition, learning classifications, data correlation, and many statistical
    physical systems are not efficiently modelled by sequential programs.

1.  You and I both know that there is no such thing as
    "experimentally verified" -- there is only
    "experimentally not yet falsified".

2.  Programs are programs -- there is only this one class
    of effectively computable functions, and everything
    else that amounts to a difference of efficiency is
    just a distinction that happens within this class.

3.  This line that you draw between parallel and sequential programs
    is drawn on a plane that all realistic programs live outside of,
    that is, it does not reflect the way that any realistic program
    for symbolic computation is actually written.  There is a name
    for problems that are solvable by parallel programs, in those
    few places where the distinction can be drawn in a hard and
    fast way, and that is the "easy" problems.  All truly tough
    problems demand a stage that comes after the "divide and
    so you think you stoop to conquer" phase, no matter how
    the problem was split off to independent processors in
    the meantime, and that is a stage of synthesizing the
    partial results.  Easy problems are "linear", in the
    true sense that partial results can simply be
    "superposed".  Everything else is tougher,
    and runs up against a serial bottleneck
    at the information fusion phase.

JA: Heuristic principles are indispensable, in the mean time, anyway,
    but each has its symmetric anti-maxim, as it is in the very
    nature of approximate truths to have.  I happen to know
    a few ways around this one.

HP: I'll pay attention when you show me one way.

Been doing that.

Jon Awbrey
