Matthew wrote:
> You seem to have leapt ahead of me with what I was originally
> proposing as just an example. But you still leave us short of
> the final destination.
>
> > (forall (?x ?y ?z)
> >         (<=> (gives ?x ?y ?z)
> >              (exists (?e) 
> >                      (and (giving ?e)
> >                           (giver ?e ?x)
> >                           (receiver ?e ?y)
> >                           (given ?e ?z)
> >                           (forall (?e')
> >                                   (=> (and (giving ?e')
> >                                            (giver ?e' ?x)
> >                                            (receiver ?e' ?y)
> >                                            (given ?e' ?z))
> >                                       (= ?e ?e')))))))
> > 
> 
> MW: The next step is to move from a ternary relation to an n-ary
> relation ... Is that possible?

I'm not sure I see any logical "next step" here.  Any relation that
corresponds to an event with objects playing certain roles can be cashed
out in a manner analogous to the above.  The arity of the relation in
question is a separate issue.  There is no general axiom or procedure here
that would enable us to churn out axioms like the above just given a 
relation.  

I suppose there is a sort of general, metalinguistic schema here that
exhibits the pattern that any such relation will fit, something like
(ignoring the uniqueness clause for brevity):

(forall (?X1 ... ?Xn)
        (<=> (REL ?X1 ... ?Xn)
             (exists (?e) 
                     (and (EVENT-TYPE ?e)
                          (ROLE-1 ?e ?X1)
                          ...
                          (ROLE-n ?e ?Xn)))))

But this is pretty useless, as the event types and corresponding roles for
relations still have to be analysed out piecemeal, relation by relation.
There's no general principle or algorithm.  

Maybe you're asking for something else.
