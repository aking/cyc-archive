> ENTELECHY, (Gr. entelechia, word invented by Aristitole,
> from en telei echon, having attained the end).
> 
> Literally, attainment, realization;  opposed to power, potentiality, and nearly the
> same as energy or act (actuality).  The idea of entelechy is connected with that of
> form, the idea of power with that of matter.  Iron is potentially in its ore, which
> to be made iron must be worked.  When this is done, the iron exists in entelechy.
> The passage from power to entelechy takes place by means of change (kinesis).
> This is the imperfect energy, the perfected energy is the entelechy.  First
> entelechy is being in working order, second entelechy is being in action.
> The soul is said to be a first entelechy, that is a thing precisely like
> a man in every respect, execept that it would not feel, would be a body
> without a soul;  but a soul once infused is not lost whenever the man
> is asleep.  This is the Aristotlian sense, but Cudworth and others
> have used entelechy  and first entelechy somewhat differently.
> Cudworth calls his plastle nature or vital principle the
> first entelechy, and Leibnitz terms a monad an entelechy.
> 
> ----------------------------------------------
> 
> Signs as first entelechy would be best described by the formal definition
> of L75 (like a soul without a body, if the metaphor helps you see what I
> meant in claiming that there was something missing in it).  Signs as second
> entelechy would correspond to semiosis itself, i.e. signs in action, change;
> and when the ultimate interpretant is reached whenever that may happen that
> would be called entelechy (not "third" entelechy, but simply entelechy)?
> 
> JM

Jean-Marc,

Still just free associating ...

I have the impression that I learned this word from Goethe --
the image of the Homunculus in 'Faust' comes to mind, and
the sense in which I have always understood its etymology
is that of an "end in itself", in other words, the mark
which distinguishes an art of conduction or recreation
from all the arts of acquisition and production, but
these memories are far too dim and old to trace, so
I will leave them be as personal poetic kennings.

Jon Awbrey
