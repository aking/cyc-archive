> My note was addressed to Jim.  I try to accord people the respect
> that they accord me, modulo the mistakes that all of us make from
> time to time.  I am speaking as sincerely as I can afford to, and
> I simply have no way of being certain what some people are up to
> from their conduct, if it does not fit with models that I know,
> and so there is a lot of guessing from imperfect data that one
> has to do.  Many times you and Ian have signed off with the
> conventional request for feedback, and I simply cannot say
> it any other way than to say that these sorts of requests
> no longer strike me as sincere.  Nobody has to take all
> the advice that is offered them, of course, but I think
> that I summarize the situation fairly accurately when
> I say that even my most prosaic remarks about rather
> cut and dried logical errors, even when reinforced by
> other observers, have yet to be answered in any one
> of the several ways that I would generally expect
> from people who are genuinely concerned about
> solving rather obvious problems.  These sorts
> of communication gaps do engender mistrust.
> 
> One of the reasons that I have started to emphasize
> the obstacle-identifying, problem-solving approach to
> our mission is that the solution to a genuine problem,
> one that is concurrently faced by the whole discipline,
> is not the sort of thing that can be faked, talked around,
> or worn down into submission, the way that a shakey accord
> by way of political niceties of procedure, even the way that
> ultimate compliance with a pro forma standard, can be rigged.
> Consequently I think that facing up to genuine problems, the
> kind that it benefits everybody to solve, in the long-run
> at least, is one way to keep everybody honest, thereby to
> encourage a sense of common purpose, and to direct the
> majority of our considerable energies at something
> beyond each other.  Just my current sense of it.
> 
> Yours Truly,
