Phil,

I think that we are disagreeing about terminology.  Your last
paragraph gets to the point:

> I think they give an argument that humans need both regular and irregular
> concepts, to represent and make predictions about the real world, and that
> the regular concepts which are needed to represent the real world are not
> entirely artifical, but reflect important properties of the world. They also
> agree that regular concepts are formalized within theories.

I would say that almost all concepts are irregular in the sense
you define (which is a good reason for dropping the term, since it
has little or no useful meaning for distinguishing between different
concepts).

Within any particular theory, you take a natural concept, such
as Space, Time, OperatingSystem, etc., and give a formal
axiomatization.  What I would say is that you have taken a
concept such as Space and a theory, say #288601, and created
a formally defined predicate, which I will name space288601,
for use only within the bounds of that particular theory.

So I am using the word "concept" for what you call "irregular concept"
and "predicate" for what you call "regular concept".

John Sowa
