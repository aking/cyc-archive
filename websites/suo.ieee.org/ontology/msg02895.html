Bob,

Yes and no...

> Perhaps there is truth to the idea that a bad standard is worse than no
> standard.

There is also truth in the idea that the best is the enemy of the
good -- if we wait for a perfect solution, we never get the advantages
of picking an adequate solution.

The width of two horses has been a satisfactory solution for
thousands of years, and the 80 or 72 character line length is a
convenient upper limit for human readability.  Those are reasons
why nobody has tried to change them.

On the other hand, the limited register structure of the Intel x86
family was well recognized even in the late 1970s.  Intel engineers
had planned to replace it with a new 32-bit architecture in 1980.
But some IBM engineers from Boca Raton thought that it might be
useful for a toy that they code-named "Peanut".  But Peanut became
the PC, which became so successful that Intel and the rest of the
world were locked into a recognizably bad architecture for the
next 20+ years.

An even more egregious example is MS Windows -- which is the most
unreliable, least flexible OS offered by a major vendor.  Yet the
whole world is stuck with it, at least for the next couple of years.

John Sowa
