Bill and Robert,

I agree, and I believe that Wittgenstein would as well, but
only with a great many caveats, objections, and complex
digressions.

Bill Burkett wrote:

>The notion of "communities" is becoming
>more recognized as a source of commonly-held semantic understanding of
>a language such as an ontology.  The Geographic Information System
>(GIS) research community call them "information
>communities"  (see Goodchild, M., et al., eds. Interoperable >Geographic Information
System. . 1999, Kluwer Academic Publishers:
>Norwell MA); data people in DoD call them "communities of >interest"; even
W3C has a loosely and poorly defined notion of >"community" in Namespaces. 
There are even sociological >theories that state that the meaning of a language
is a jointly >negotiated and accepted convention among the members of a language

>community.

The term Wittgenstein used was "language game", which he
introduced in section 7 of his _Philosophical Investigations_
(translated by G. E. M. Anscombe, Blackwell Publishers, Oxford).

>I think tying the semantics of an ontology to the
>target community is the only viable and practical way for the elements
>of the SUO to "mean" anything.

Unfortunately, such a strict tying cannot support communication
between communities.  And every major enterprise or even every
large project in an enterprise involves multiple communities
that use and reuse the same words in different language games.

That use and reuse of the same words in different language
games is both a curse and a blessing.  It is the ultimate
source of the power of natural languages as tools for
negotiation *before* the details have been settled, but
it is also the ultimate source of ambiguity and confusion
when different people (or their computer systems) from
different communities try to communicate and cooperate.

I recommend Wittgenstein's _Philosophical Investigations_ as
the single best discussion of ambiguity in natural languages
(and in any formalization that attempts to represent what
was meant in some NL).

Many logicians tend to despair after reading it, but I believe
that no approach that ignores it can succeed.  I also believe
that it contains the germ of a solution.  But the task of
developing that germ to a complete solution is a topic for
another long discussion and even longer project.

John Sowa
