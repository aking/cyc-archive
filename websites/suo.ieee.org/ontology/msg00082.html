Pat,

Thanks for your careful attention to this issue.
I can see that I am going to have a long evening,
if not, then a very interesting rest of the week.

I would accuse you of somehow having gotten a hold of
and read the last chapters of my dissertation, but I
am pretty sure that nobody -- I recuse myself -- has
yet done that, not even my committee.

In that work, I eventually get around to constructing
an "integrated sign relation" -- at least, I think that
is what I eventually called it, the last time that I looked --
for this example -- and I am guessing this is something like
what you are talking about below.

However, I think if you think carefully about the mental operation
of reflecting on the discourse situation, and what kind of grasp
of the objective, the syntactic, and the interpretive setting
that it takes to garb -- I meant "grab" -- exactly the right
additional bits of context that it takes to construct this
integration, and so to render the indexical signs "I" and
"thou" unambiguous within the new sign relation and with
respect to its associated partitions, then I think that
you will see that there is something just a little bit
beyond the "realm of triviality" (ROT) to all of this
triadic stuff.

But I will need to return to this fray after I have
fortified myself with some dinner and maybe some wine,
so let me just toss out the following SOP to Cerberus:

"A wheel with but two sides is a flat tire indeed."

Cheers,

Jon Awbrey

pat hayes wrote:
> 
> Jon, in an archived message
> ( http://ltsc.ieee.org/logs/suo/msg01224.html )
> you sent the following (here abbreviated:)
>
> ¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤
>
> To specify a sign relation one has to give three domains,
> the Object, Sign, and Interpretant domains, schematized
> here as O, S, and I, respectively.
>
> .........
>
> R(A) has the following eight triples of the form <o, s, i>:
> 
>     <A, "A", "A">
>     <A, "A", "i">
>     <A, "i", "A">
>     <A, "i", "i">
>     <B, "B", "B">
>     <B, "B", "u">
>     <B, "u", "B">
>     <B, "u", "u">
> 
> R(B) has the following eight triples of the form <o, s, i>:
> 
>     <A, "A", "A">
>     <A, "A", "u">
>     <A, "u", "A">
>     <A, "u", "u">
>     <B, "B", "B">
>     <B, "B", "i">
>     <B, "i", "B">
>     <B, "i", "i">
> 
> Following a usage of C.S. Peirce, the elements of a sign relation R,
> that is, the ordered triples of the form <o, s, i> ¤ R, are known as
> its "elementary sign relations".  (This is roughly Peirce's meaning.)
> 
> ¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~¤
> 
> My eyebrows rose when I read this, as it seems to illustrate rather
> clearly the pointlessness of the Piercian insistence on 'triadicity',
> rather than its utility.  As this probably wasnt your intention,
> I am puzzled as to quite what your point was.
> 
> Let me elaborate by offering a simpler analysis of the A and B
> situation, more along Tarskian lines. There are two things (A, B),
> four symbols ("A", "B", "i" ,"u") and two interpretations (RA, RB).
>
> The key relation is that of interpretation of a symbol.
> Each interpretation f maps each symbol s to a unique thing t,
> so we will write f(s)=t to indicate that.  The complete situation
> can then be summed up as follows:
>
> RA("A")=RB("A")=RA("i")=RB("u")=A
> RA("B")=RB("B")=RA("u")=RB("i")=B
>
> This could be displayed as a pair of tables,
> but they would have four entries rather than eight.
> (If one wanted to include A as a parameter of RA and B of RB,
> then one would have triples, of course, but this would combine
> the tables into one table with 8 entries.  A similar move in
> your scheme would produce 16 lines.)
> 
> There is no need for the 'interpretant' elements ("I") of your
> triples, since they clearly play no role in the other relationships:
> your triple-tables just have the same information copied out twice for
> each value of I.  It seems to be just a kind of notational appendage.
> As it plays no substantial role in anything else that I have ever been
> able to detect, I suggest we simply stop talking about it, whatever it is,
> and restrict ourselves to the normal subject-matter of semantics, which is
> relationship between signs and what they signify.  This has served logic
> well for the last 60-odd years and suffices for every ontology effort
> so far attempted, and I see no reason to turn the clock back now
> and re-invent a wheel with too many sides.
>
