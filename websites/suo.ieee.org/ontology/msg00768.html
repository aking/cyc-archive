At 03-02-01 00:52, pat hayes wrote:
For example, consider the ontologies that have been developed for qualitative physical reasoning, in which there are entire algebras of processes and one reasons about them as fully-fledged entities. These things have spatiotemporal *shapes*; there are notions such as a rapid ramping, or a brief acceleration, or a sudden phase change. These cannot be described in terms of 3-d plus time; it doesn't provide a rich enough vocabulary of distinctions. Most industrial machinery cannot be described in terms of 3-d plus time. A mechanical clock cannot be described that way, and neither can a water pump, or even a bouncing rubber ball.


### Pat could you provide us with a pointer to one of these spatiotemporal ontologies please? I have a keen interest in these. Also, if the text would not describe it, how were they arrived at (as agreements between mathematicians, phycisists, ... or just as authors' theses, ...). and what is their "level of authority" (presuming that not everybody's perception, interpretation and representation of the physical world works in the same way nor forever).

--Robert Meersman
