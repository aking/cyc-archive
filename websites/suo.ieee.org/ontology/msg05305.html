Hugh,

This particular thread refers to Peirce's development, in 1865-66,
of a subject that he called the "Theory of Information", which he
derived from first principles in his endeavors to analyze the way
that empirical scientific inference actually succeeds in practice.
I became interested in this about fifteen years ago because I was
looking at ways to integrate qualitative and quantitative methods
in research, and Peirce's information theory presented a striking
example of information theory mined from a logical or qualitative
vein, well, more like the mother lode.

In the same mine-field of research, one tries to identify general principles
that affect the development of knowledge in any system that is intelligent
enough to carry out inquiry.  Thus one asks questions about the sorts of
architectures, algorithms, data structures, and other infrastructures
that are needed to support inquiry, questions about the dynamics of
inquiry, and the laws that envelope its trajectory through time.

Now, whether it's an individual brain, learning organization, social network,
species collective, or web of maya, it is worth at least looking for generic
principles that apply to all such "inquiry driven systems" (IDS), and that
is what I'm about here, whatever the application of the moment might be.
