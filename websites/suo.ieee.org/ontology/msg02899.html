Jon --

I do see that you've changed the subject of this thread, but I don't think
of myself as particularly audacious, in this particular discussion.  At
this late stage of my career, I feel particularly humbled that there is
apparently something so very fundamental and important that I can't get a
grasp on.

I'm not trying to solve the riddle of the Sphinx, as John Sowa has pointed
out as one of Peirce's great contributions.  I'm just hoping to facilitate
better communication among people, particularly in a commercial
environment, by using conceptual and technological tools to reduce the
levels of ambiguity that otherwise would exist without that effort.

I don't know if it helps to dwell on my thoughts, but I'll answer your
questions below, for what it's worth.  My main goal is not to defend what I
have written, but to use something that I do understand (because I've
written it) to come at something I don't understand, from the point of view
of how this is useful for the purpose I've expressed of facilitating
clearer and less ambiguous communication.

This brings up exactly one of my points of confusion about Peirce.  You
have used
the phrase "discussion and thought" a couple of times, in a way that seems
to want
to conflate them.  In an earlier stab at understanding sign vs.
interpretant, I
stated that I thought sign was about utterance and interpretant (sign) was
about
bringing the sign, and the object of discussion together in the mind of the
interpreter.  Notice that I didn't say "human mind" just then, nor did I on
that
other occasion, though more than one reader interpreted me to have done so.
Before
I could correct that impression, a cascade of signs washed over the bow.

> Now, I use the word "referent" in conformity with a whole user community

> so take your pick, and pay the consequences of doing so.

I understand about reserved words, and I'm careful to use them carefully in
reserved contexts.  Which is why, for instance, I can't bring myself to use
"object" or "interpretant" just now, since I can't seem to get them clear
enough to make practical use of them in my area of concern.  Please note
that
I did not use "pragmatic", for the same reason.  I like to use that word as
a close synonym to practical, which does not seem to be the philosophical
definition.  In any event, I am using "referent" exactly in accordance with
the definition from my humble Webster, lying close to hand: "one that is
referred to; esp : the thing that a symbol (as a word or sign) stands for"
(a definition apparently established in 1884)

I'm not sure about this despair.  Again, from a practical point of view,
I'm not
really concerned with how we think, internal dialogs, etc. in my own dialog
with
the SUO study group.  It is my opinion that we can make progress on certain
practical objectives without resolving whether the brain is actually
processing
signs.  For this purpose, my personal preference would be that we should
reserve
"sign" to mean externalized utterance, where utterance is generalized to
include
verbal, graphic, alphabetic, etc.  With a special emphasis on utterance
captured
and transmitted via computer-based networks for the purpose of amplifying
and
directing communication among human beings.

Given this last paragraph, I should also stop using the word "sign" on this
list,
because it clearly has a reserved meaning for some that doesn't match the
meaning
I tend to assign.

This is what I thought, but it has tended to get obscured (in my opinion)
in
the long debates about reduction of triads to dyads.  Maybe to say it
another
way, an issue of communication has tended to get lost in a discussion of
representation.  I don't know if this is true, it just strikes me as
possible.
It seems to me that expression of the big object is important to provide
context
for the bit stream, but then would not have to be repeated for every bit
that is
subsequently emitted.  This, of course, is an oversimplification, since in
real
communication the context continuously emerges as the bit stream(s) emerge
and
interact.

Again, I'm not aiming at the riddle of the Sphinx here, or any grand

But, I do think that when a sign is emitted is a critical aspect of
context.  Just
take a look back through the Peircean scholarship to see how often there
are
discussions of *when* he said what he said, shedding light because it was
either
before or after he said something else, in a certain stage of various
debates with
his contemporaries.  In business emission of externalized utterance (my
ugly term
for what I really can't mean as "sign"), the order and timing of emission
is very much
of the essence of meaning and communication.
