>Just because we haven't yet found success is no reason to think it 
>impossible.  What lesson would you have me learn, and how should 
>that lesson follow from the SUO experience?

That there are genuinely divergent and incompatible views on many 
ontological issues, and that consensus cannot be achieved by any 
simplistic approach of trying to merge or conflate them by simply 
inserting them into a single overarching classification. That 
reconciliations between divergent views are best achieved by looking 
for mutually acceptable mappings between them, rather than by 
choosing one over the other.  That a generally useful ontological 
classification should be based on an attempt to discover orthogonal 
categories from which a useful lattice can be constructed, rather 
than on an ad-hoc initial tree, no matter how carefully based on some 
philosophical agenda.
All of which, by the way, we concluded in 1998 in Heidelberg.

>>>I believe that the solution is not improved document management or

>>>developing a formal document expressing their preferred version of 
>>>an upper ontology.

>>I think it is simply a reflection of a fact of life: people do not

>Not at all.  People disagree when there is reason to do so,

People disagree for all kinds of reasons. I don't think the 
disagreements that have been revealed on the SUO list arise from any 
*need* to disagree. They arise from genuinely divergent, often deeply 
felt, convictions about the nature of reality, the appropriate way to 
describe it, and so on. You seem curiously unable to appreciate that 
there even are such differences.

Maybe it would help if you were to state explicitly what your basic 
assumptions are about the SUO and the methodology that you and Ian 
are adopting towards creating it. For example, you seem to assume 
that all reasonable people can be made to agree, or at least their 
axioms can be. Do you think this is correct? (If so, why?) You 
sometimes seem to think that just by translating axioms into a common 
logical notation, they will somehow come to be mutually compatible. 
You seem to think that the exact definitions of the terms in the 
concept heirarchy are not all that important, and are really only 
hooks to hang things on. Are all these impressions correct?

>and find ways of agreeing when there is reason to do so.  In the KIF 
>working group we have a compelling need to agree on a common 
>language and semantics for the language.  We might each have our 
>preferences, but we're willing to give up certain preferences here 
>and there in order to reach consensus.

Well, you introduced the example, so let me respond. First, KIF is a 
language, not an ontology, so there is lot less to disagree about. 
Even in that severely limited domain, and that relatively small 
group, we managed to discover at least three basic, irreconcileable 
differences in viewpoints, as you may recall. We have indeed managed 
to find a route through this particular minefield, but only with the 
greatest care and by some of the group agreeing to bow out of the 
discussion and leave it to a smaller subgroup to thrash out the 
details. And only 2 weeks ago, we discovered that we had been talking 
at cross purposes over such a basic notion as what constitutes a 
sorted language for the previous six months, and were obliged to 
redefine our overall goals as a result. We once spent several wseeks 
arguing over the exact from of the BNF for 'identifiers'. If the KIF 
group is a microcosm of the SUO effort, then the SUO is doomed.

>We don't all need to agree on every aspect of the SUO.  We merely 
>need to have a product we can accept as a result of a lot of give 
>and take.

The product that Ian is creating will never be acceptable to me, 
under any circumstances. I say this not in any way as a criticism of 
Ians work, but as a comment on the overall methodology. I have tried 
to make my reasons for this as vividly clear as I possibly can, ever 
since I first saw the first draft of the ontology. If you still don't 
understand them then I am at a loss as to how to communicate them to 
you.

>We may disagree on English terms but I'm not clear on which axioms 
>you disagree with.

Asking me to disagree with individual axioms is like answering 
someone who criticises Windows 98 to cite a particular line of code 
they don't like. It just misses the point. Its not that I disagree 
with any axioms in particular, but that they disagree with each other.

Look, surely it is obvious that one cannot expect, just because 
people are using a common logical langauge, that they are 
automatically agreeing with each other? On what grounds would you 
*expect* to be able to 'merge' ontologies written by different people 
about different subject-matters, with different philosophical or 
pragmatic or mathematical agendas, so that they fit together into one 
framework? This entire ambition strikes me as close to insane. Is 
your experience of the world really so limited that you have never 
come across divergent points of view? I managed to find about 20 or 
so incompatible points of view just on temporal orderings.

Let me turn the point around. Show me some nontrivial consequence of 
the axioms in the merged ontology that you think illustrates its 
utility, and that uses axioms from several different sources. Let me 
give you an example to get things started: try inferring that if 
there is a hole and that some fluid stuff is moving into the hole and 
the hole doesn't get smaller, then the stuff must be going somewhere. 
That should involve holes, time and fluids, and maybe pathways.
Or take a few combined concepts. Suppose that someone wants to 
describe a subclass of human beings, those who are related to a 
British citizen in some way that gives them the right of abode in the 
UK (a *very* sensitive issue in UK politics, by the way). What kind 
of ontological description would be used to describe such a class? 
(The legal definition was changed a number of times, so that the 
answer is highly connected with the time the request was made. Just a 
heads-up about the kind of issues that are going to come up.) Heres 
another: the notion of an insult. What *kind* of thing is an insult? 
Which hook would you suggest hanging it on? (Similarly 
slander/libel/offense/apology...)

> In fact, although you've said often that you consider the current 
>merged ontology "incoherent" or, as above, that there are sections 
>that are "incompatible" with one another, I don't believe you've 
>given a precise characterization of what you mean by those words.

I can't give you a LOGICAL definition. What I meant is that various 
parts of the ontology are written with different, and incompatible, 
assumptions in mind. The 'foralls' in one part of the ontology range 
over different universes than those in other parts, for example. I 
have already documented several of these cases, but the point is that 
with this methodology such clashes are inevitable.

I have another problem with the current merged ontology, which is 
that its basic class distinctions don't make sense to me and aren't 
properly documented,and seem to form a tree, which is wildly 
unsuitable for any complex reasoning. I notice however that you have 
a much more casual, ready-to-hand attitude to conceptual 
classification than I am comfortable with. You use "Sign", for 
example, in ways that would I suspect make a true-born P**rcian 
cringe (whatever it is, it is inherently triadic, so classifying a 
novel as an instance of Sign has got to be a mistake. Maybe one 
particular event of reading a novel might qualify?), although as I 
have no idea what it means I am not really qualified to judge. On the 
other hand, I will never, to my dying day, use an ontology that 
requires me to categorize Hamlet as an instance of Sign.

On what grounds should they be answered one way rather than another? 
All are perfectly fine answers, for some purposes. A book is one 
thing to a bookbinder, another to a librarian, another to a 
publisher, and still another to a copyright lawyer, and yet something 
else to a freight line operator. None of them are *right* and the 
others *wrong*. They are all perfectly good, but subtly incompatible, 
views.

> That they have not been answered in this discussion group should 
>not suggest that they can't be answered satisfactorily.  Since 
>you've asked, let me attempt an answer.

Im sure that an answer CAN be given. My point is that other answers 
can also be given, and the SUO cannot possibly legislate some out of 
court and others in.
