>Tarski himself did not apply his version of model theory to
>anything but mathematical constructions.

John, come now, you shouldnt say such things. Tarski developed 
axiomatic geometry, among other things, and was quite explicit that 
he was there referring to *space*.

> The title of his
>most famous paper is "The concept of truth in formalized
>languages."  In that paper, he was highly sceptical about the
>possibility of extending his approach to natural languages
>and to objects in the real world.
>
>Various people who have followed Tarski have applied his
>techniques to areas where the master feared to tred.  Some
>of them have simply "identified" the elements of Tarski's
>abstract sets

Tarski nowhere describes 'abstract sets'. He simply talks about sets. 
The implication that a 'set' must be 'abstract' is yours, not 
Tarski's (or Quine's, or Carnap's, or ....)

>with "objects" in the world without getting into
>the question of how that identification might be carried out.

Ah, yes, true. But model theory doenst deal with how such 
identifications are to be "carried out": it deals only with ways the 
language can be interpreted in a world. Each different way of 
carrying out the identification might provide a distinct 
interpretation of the language in the world. True, but irrelevant to 
the point at issue.

>
>Peirce himself applied his methods of pragmatism to the issues
>of how a correspondence between the world and the elements
>of a set might be established, investigated, criticized, etc.
>He would allow such an identification to be made, but only if
>the reason or purpose for the identification were explicitly
>considered and analyzed -- and always with the caveat that a
>different mapping between the sets and the world might be
>adopted if the purpose happened to change.

What changes here is the relationship between the language and the 
world. There is no need to hypothesise an intermediate level of 
analysis.

>Many other logicians, including Barwise and the situation
>semantics crowd, have also been critical about a direct
>mapping from set-theoretical elements to real world objects.
>They have set up various kinds of intermediate structures,
>similar to the model that I put in the middle between the
>theory and the world.  In Barwise's version, that middle
>structure is called an "abstract situation type".

One can define something analogous in Tarskian model theory also: it 
is an interpretation structure considered as an autonomous 
mathematical object (ie abstracted from the mapping form the 
linguistic signature.)
