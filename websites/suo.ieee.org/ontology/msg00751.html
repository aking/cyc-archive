>"I must confess that I'm not entirely sure what this means.  In a sense, the
>ontology has a 4D orientation, since it includes a framework for 3D objects
>and for the fourth dimension of time.  If by a 4D orientation you mean
>something more than this, it seems likely to me that this something extra
>can be superimposed on what we already have.  If you think this is not
>possible, I would be very interested in hearing your reasoning. "

The basic problem, Ian, is that what you have is like having two 
projections of a plane onto its axes. The space you want to be able 
to describe is the *product* space, not the sum, and you can't do it 
in terms of pairs of projections.  There are things in the plane that 
cannot be described solely in terms of their projections: in effect, 
all that gives you are 'bounding boxes' surrounding all your objects.

For example, consider the ontologies that have been developed for 
qualitative physical reasoning, in which there are entire algebras of 
processes and one reasons about them as fully-fledged entities. These 
things have spatiotemporal *shapes*; there are notions such as a 
rapid ramping, or a brief acceleration, or a sudden phase change. 
These cannot be described in terms of 3-d plus time; it doesn't 
provide a rich enough vocabulary of distinctions. Most industrial 
machinery cannot be described in terms of 3-d plus time. A mechanical 
clock cannot be described that way, and neither can a water pump, or 
even a bouncing rubber ball.
