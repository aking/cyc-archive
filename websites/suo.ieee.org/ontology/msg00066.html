Title: Re: SUO: Individual
At 10:25 AM +0100 30/11/2000, West, Matthew MR SSI-GREA-UK wrote:
Dear David,
Let me at least explain how I use the word "individual".

Let me say first what it is not (to me in a formal context).

It is not a class, and it is not a relation (or relationship).

It is something that has existence in space time, though this may be in some
possible world.

It is something that has a sense of wholeness about it (very difficult to
define). It is not just people.

It includes activities as well as static things.

Some examples: me, my car, my planned holiday next year.

If I compare it to instance, I use instance as a role relative to some
class. But classes can be instances of other classes, so not all things that
are instances are individuals.

My intuitions are exactly the same as Matthew's. Let me clarify a couple of things.

I believe there are at least 5 different senses for the word "individual":

1. An element of the domain of discourse: in this sense, anything can be an individual. [I believe this is the sense David Whitten has in mind when he says that "Individual" is coextensional with "Top"]

2. A "particular", i.e. something that has no instances (it can't be "predicated" of anything, except perhaps itself): in this sense, me, my hand, the left part of my hand, a specific amount of water (like the water existing in my house) are all individuals.

3. A "connected particular", i.e. a particular to which it is possible to ascribe it some "criterion of connectedness", in the sense that it ceases to exists when such unity is destroyed ("in-dividual"): in this sense, I am an individual, but an amount of water is not, since the latter does not need to be connected to exist.

4. A "whole", i.e. a particular that, besides being self-connected, has also an intrinsic "criterion of unity", which isolates it from the rest of the universe. An undetached part of my body is not an individual in this sense. A particular must be *maximally* self-connected (according to a suitable notion of connectedness) to count as a whole.

5. An atom, i.e. an entity that has no proper parts. [I believe this is the sense John Sowa referred to]

I like sense 4 best, but of course the terminology issue is not the most crucial here.

Concerning the difficult stuff Matthew mentions, the definitions Chris Welty and I have introduced in our recent papers about the notion of "Criterion of Unity" may be of help here [see the ECAI2000 paper on "Identity, Unity, and Individuality"]. Intuitively, a criterion of unity is a certain equivalence relation that, given a part of a certain entity, allows us to gather all other parts. Physical connectedness is the prototypical criterion of unity for physical objects.

As discussed in the paper, there are various kind of unity: topological, functional, social... A bikini, for instance, is an example of an object that can be counted as one according to a functional criterion of unity.

At 11:03 AM -0800 30/11/2000, Robert E. Kent wrote:
This is very close to my use of the term "Individual." Actually, I am willing to use any agreed upon term such as "Singular" or "Single", in place of Individual, if it is more acceptable.

If we take sense 4 above for "individual", it may be the case that an individual contains other individuals as parts. This is the case of mechanical assemblies, for instance: a car is an individual that has other individuals as parts (its *components*). To me, this means that is a plural individual. A single nut, on the other hand, is a singular individual (at least at a certain granularity, forgetting the molecules it is constituted of, which are themselves individuals). So the terms "individual" and "singular" are not synonymous.

Or perhaps "Separate" or "Particular" or "Member" or "Unit".

See above. "Particular" is more general than "unit". The tip of my finger is a particular, but not a unit.

I am more interested in the meaning of the Heraclitus distinction. I think a reconstituted version of this may lay at the heart of the structure of the SUO. I am suggesting that this core structure is in the form of a formal context (from Formal Concept Analysis)

  • Heraclitis = (Entity, Class, instance-of),
where Entity is the disjoint union
  • Entity = Individual + Class,

I haven't looked at your reference yet. I would reasonably happy with this proposal if we agree that "individual" in the above statement corresponds to my sense 2. So I would prefer writing

Entity = Particular + Class

or, better,

Entity = Particular + Universal

where universals can be properties or relations, and are intended as intensional entity (see the discussion about class, which does not need to start again here).

At 7:28 PM -0500 30/11/2000, David Mark wrote:
I am corcerned currently with problems in the "wholeness" sense.
Geographic 'things' such as a mountain, say a particular mountain such as
Mont Blanc or Mount Everest. Would you say that "Mont Blanc" is not an
individual because it lacks a sense of wholeness, no unambiguous boundary
to seperate it horizontally from "not-Mont-Blanc" right next door?

I would say that Mount Everest is not a whole, according to sense 4 above. To me, it is a *feature* of a much bigger whole, namely the Earth... The matter is complicated here, however, and not just because the vagueness problems studied by Achille. The point is that Mount Everest *has* some kind of wholeness, which is however different from that I have defined. I tend to say this is not an *intrinsic* wholeness, it is a wholeness that depends on something else...
