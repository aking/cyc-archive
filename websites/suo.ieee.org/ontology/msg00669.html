I vote NO, with the following comments: 

<snip>

Methodology/Reference Object:  I brought this distinction forward several
times before to no avail, and others have put it in other ways without much
traction, but let me try one last time: a crucial conceptual watershed in
this area (and one that was the agreed 'best practice' and building block of
the previous ad hoc ontology effort)  is to distinguish the underlying
methods, architecture, specifications and algorithms of the structure of
what would be an acceptable upper level from a given instantiation
determined by specific design and semantic choices.  This latter artefact is
properly seen as a 'reference object' embodying one version of an underlying
body of standards.  Several people on this list have further expanded on
this point by pointing to the possibility of growing and self-maintaining
ontological structures permitting a variety of users and uses.  These draw
on methods that go well beyond the merge-and-winnow approach, and cannot be
adeuqately covered merely by making explicit the 'syntactic' choices and
axioms of the reference object.

</snip>

<snip>

This has been a very useful and lively discussion group, and I, for one,
have learned from many of the comments. But all that is different from
producing a standard.  For a successful effort, a synergy between
institutional arrangements and software design is needed.
