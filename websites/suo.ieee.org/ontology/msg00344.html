Pat,

I agree that a crystal isn't the best example.  A much
better example would be DNA, which would have made Peirce
ecstatic, if he had known about it.

>> > (Peirce, CP 4.536, 4.550 ff).
>> >
>> > | Thought is not necessarily connected with a brain.
>> > | It appears in the work of bees, of crystals, and
>> > | throughout the purely physical world;  and one
>> > | can no more deny that it is really there, than
>> > | that the colors, the shapes, etc., of objects
>> > | are really there.  (CP 4.551).

>OK, I stand corrected. Hats off to CSP, though I have to say I think 
>he could have chosen a better example than crystals. He seems to be 
>identifying 'thought' with something like 'order', or, in more modern 
>terms, low entropy.

Peirce did, in fact, discuss entropy extensively (I don't
have the quotations handy).  But I believe that what he was
getting at in this passage was the fact that there is something
inherent in the molecular structure at a submicroscopic level,
which has a causal effect on the kinds of structures that are
produced at the macro level.

That "something", which was unknown to CSP, serves to guide
each individual molecule into its proper position in the crystal
structure.  In the same way, there is some microscopic pattern
in the bee's DNA, which causes the bees to cooperate as they
do in finding honey, building a hive, and caring for the larvae.

Although we have much more knowledge about DNA than Peirce had,
no one today can show how any particular sequence of amino acids
determines how bees cooperate in the hive, yet we can safely say
that there is a causal link between the DNA and that behavior.

Peirce would say that "thought" in the human brain is of the
same nature as the bit patterns that cause a robot to perform
some action, the DNA that causes a bee to make honey, or the
quantum-mechanical wave functions that cause molecules to form
a crystal.
