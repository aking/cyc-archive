Jon,

Peirce's "mind-less" formal definitions are only partially true because too abstract
if they are supposed to explain anything at all in practice. The formal definition
from L75 only describes the sign as one of the correlates of a triadic relation, i.e.
the sign as a being for an other. But a sign has in fact two beings: its being for an
other (the object) and its own being. It is not an isolated entity because it belongs
to a genuine triad , but nevertheless its entire being is not completely swallowed by
the triadic relation either. If the three elements (S, O, I) are of the nature of a
sign, in that they take part in a triadic relation (Peirce writes in the Lowell
Lectures that they are of the nature of "thirds" ), likewise, they have their own
being, the interpretant has its own being, the sign has its own being and the object
has its own being. So if you start from a formal definition that only refer to a
triadic relation and believe that you can explain everything from there, where each
element is reduced to its mode of being respective to the other correlates, then you
are missing half of the picture. In asking "what is a sign?", do not simply answer the
question by saying how a sign enters in relation with the other correlates, but ask
yourself also what a sign in actu really is, what is "an object of direct experience"
like? and the object of the sign:  what is a "fragment of history" like? , and finally
what is the interpretant, what is the determination of a mind? Or you have only walked
half the road.

JM
