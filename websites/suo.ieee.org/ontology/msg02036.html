Frederick,

My sense of Matthew's position is also that it is, in effect, an
intelligent reflection upon practices that have proved servicable in work
with standardizing diverse databases.  He does not claim it as an adequate
philosophy or logic, but he does see it as an adequate view for
computational purposes-- a philosophy of and for metadata, if you will. 
If he is right for the whole range of computational work, we shall
willy-nilly have to translate every other view into his to get it into a
relational database. 

The claim that strikes me as excessive is that his construct should be
taken on a par with other views of 'the world'. Views of 'the world' that
try to reduce it to things and sets either find that they must redefine a
'thing' to cover much more than spatio-temporal existents (as Pat Hayes
did in a posting on this list)  or that they must abandon the notion of
real sets for a looser notion of a collection.  One might also mention in
this context the stately retreat of Quine who began with a radical form of
thingism (or 'reism' as a heroic writer in this tradition called it) and
when he saw a need for set theory, decided to say that this was 'outside
logic', so that logic could remain free of 'abstract' entities. 

Lee
