MK>> I agree a formal SUO must not use a term to mean two different things.
Therefore if we choose to define Relationship as an SUO term, we must
choose
a particular meaning for it.  I would like this meaning to be "in the real
world".  In the Enterprise Ontology NL version, we actually wrote sentences
with the word Relationship which I interpret as statements about
relationship instances, but (by what I call sleight of hand), these
sentences by implication define the properties of the relationship type

GH>> This sounds OK to me for now. If it runs into problems later, there
may be a need to extend it.

GH>> Incidentally, did you find yourselves restricting your vocabulary in
any ways for the Enterprise Ontology NL version (ie. making it a de facto
"restricted English" version)?

MK>>> I believe we did restrict our vocabulary, and our sentence
construction quite strictly, though we did not document the restrictions
completely.  Part of the restriction was the set of what we called the
Meta-Ontology terms (e.g. Entity, Relationship, and Role), and how they
were used.  Details of this can be seen in

Mike Uschold, Martin King, Stuart Moralee and Yannis Zorgios (1998) The
Enterprise Ontology The Knowledge Engineering Review , Vol. 13, Special
Issue on Putting Ontologies to Use (eds. Mike Uschold and Austin Tate).

obtainable via
http://www.aiai.ed.ac.uk/project/enterprise/enterprise/ontology.html

See in particular the penultimate paragraph of Section 1.5 on page 8.
There was a degree of iteration of writing definitions of terms in the body
of the ontology (e.g. Customer) using the meta-ontology terms and
refinement of the meta-ontology (e.g. Role).  Sentences were honed using
heuristic methods like comparison of definitions of Customer and Vendor for
symmetry and differences.
