JLCA = Jon Awbrey
JLRC = Jerry Chandler

JLRC: Thank you for your efforts to respond.  Your response to the simple question
      of what are the bijections among your "languages" addressed only one pair
      of "languages".  Why did you not address the other pairs?

I figure in a theatre like this it is best to follow the advice of Gypsy Rose Lee.

JLRC: Nevertheless, I am beginning to understand your world view,
      a world view which is unique to you.

JLRC: The question "What is your definition of a formal language?" remains open.

I posted the basic defs on a reference thread entitled "Formal Languages":

http://suo.ieee.org/ontology/msg03366.html

JLRC: Are you so deeply into Platonic forms that you are unaware of
      the current definitions of formal languages in category theory?
      As I consider the frame of reference you project to readers, it
      appears to me that you fail to use the knowledge that has been
      created in the 100+ years since Pierce made his primary and
      seminal contributions on quantifiers.

I suspect that we are using the phrase "formal language"
to refer to a couple of discrepant notions or objects.
I took my basic graduate classes in the subject still
commonly known as "automata & formal language theory"
in the last century, it's true, but that was the 20th,
not the 19th.

JLRC: In particular, the intimate linkages between category theory
      and logic are not considered in your monotone of "wordplays".

JLRC: The past 50 years have created a remarkable edifice of logical linkages
      between mathematics and logics.  Your inquiry into inquiry could benefit
      substantially from developing a familiarity with modern works in the area.

I used to have a horribly inflamed edifice complex, but I'm much better now.
If you are interested in these subjects, I am presently presenting a series
of e-lucubrations on the ONT list.  Here is the launch of that thread:

http://suo.ieee.org/email/msg06638.html
http://suo.ieee.org/ontology/msg03373.html

JLRC: For relationships between Computer Science and Logic, see:
     'Basic Model Theory', Kees Doets, European Association for
      Logic, Language and Information, 1996.

JLRC: The following quote is from page one:

      | Setting up a system of logic starts by choosing a set of symbols. 
      | The symbols are partitioned into logical and non-logical ones.
      | The logic symbols of first order logic are classified into four groups:
      |
      | 1. countably many (individual) variables,
      | 2. the equality symbol, "=",
      | 3. symbols for logical operations, (symbols for not, and, or, implies,
      |    double implies, and quantifiers:  "for each" and "there exists".)
      |
      | The nonlogical symbols comprise:
      |
      | 5.  relation symbols,
      | 6.  function symbols,
      | 7.  constant symbols or (individual) constants.
      |
      | The logic symbols are fixed for every first order language.
      | The set of non-logical symbols is the vocabulary of the language.

Again, I had my graduate courses in logic and model theory a long time ago,
but I am comforted to see that not much has changed since that distant era.

JLRC: Now, if you are seriously interested in communicating with the community
      of logicians and others interested in logic, you will examine your NINE
      languages and inform the readers of the meanings of your symbols in
      these terms -- where your usage is the same and where you elect to
      differ from this usage.

I think my last couple of notes to HP may have explained better than I have before
why exactly I am spending so much time and attention on such a simple system as
propositional calculus (alias "sentential logic", Peirce's "Alpha", or "ZOL").
It's more as a simple example of a semiotic system than for its logic content.

JLRC: In your "table" of relations, you are not consistent in your usage
      of symbols (particularly in the naming of your columns) and hence
      I could not understand it.

I was using an ad hoc collection of nicknames,
but the content of the columns is what it is.
If there is a particular area of concern,
please let me know.

JLRC: If you study this little book, you will find that an essential distinction
      between finite logics and infinitary logics is the notion of compactness.
      If you understand this notion, then you may wish to revise your table
      or to explain to the reader your process for relating finite sets
      to infinite sets.

JLRC: A second book which I would recommend is:

      |'A Shorter Model Theory',
      | Wilfred Hodges,
      | Cambridge University Press, 1997.

JLRC: This book uses similar definitions of logic, relations and functions.
      It provides for detailed constructions of the relations between logic 
      and categories and mathematics in general.  It presupposes that the
      reader understands the distinctions between bijections, injections,
      and surjections -- basic combinatorics.  Thus, you may wish to
      review some of these topics before tackling this more advanced
      and mathematically oriented text.

JLRC: A third book I would recommend is:

      |'Philosophy of the Logics',
      | Susan Haack
      | Cambridge Univ. Press, 1978.

JLRC: You probably ought to start with this book in hopes that it might 
      help you identify the incompleteness of Platonic thought and open 
      your eyes to the role of correspondence relationships in category 
      theory and in logic and in mathematics.

JLRC: A fourth book:

      |'Sets, Logic, and Categories',
      | Peter J. Cameron,
      | Springer Verlag, 1999.

      is to be highly recommended.  It is simplification of the materials 
      presented in the first three recommended books.  It is intended as
      an undergraduate text.

JLRC: Below, I add a few comments to your response.
      Clearly, we are using very different vocabularies.

Dinnertime!

Will get to the rest tonight or tomorrow.

Jon Awbrey
