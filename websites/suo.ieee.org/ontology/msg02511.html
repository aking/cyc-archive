> Here's a practical question on the merged ontology.  We currently have 
> the axiom
> 
> (<=>
>    (disjoint ?C1 ?C2)
>    (not
>      (thereExists (?I)
>        (and
>          (instance-of ?I ?C1)
>          (instance-of ?I ?C2)))))
> 
> ; two classes are disjoint if no individual exists which is a instance of 
> ; both classes and vice versa

The meaning of "vice versa" here is unclear to me.  I guess it is a way of
expressing the biconditional, but if so, I'd suggest just changing "if" to
"if and only if" and deleting "and vice versa".

> This looks problematic to me and Ian and I have discussed changing it from 
> bi-implication to simple implication.  One could make a case for the above 
> version if classes are extension.  The counter argument (which could be 
> labelled an argument for intentional classes) would say that Equine and 
> SinglyHornedAnimal are not disjoint just because no one has reified Unicorn 
> in the knowledge base.

The issue is whether you want disjointness to have an implicit modal
connotation.  If all you ever mean by saying C and C' are disjoint is
that, right now, as things stand, they have no common instances, then the
above axiom is fine.  You just have to bear in mind that classes that are
in fact disjoint can suddenly cease to be disjoint.  On the other hand,
if, by saying C and C' are disjoint, you mean something stronger -- namely
that C and C' *can't* have common instances -- then you need to use
implication rather than bi-implication, since, obviously, from the fact
that C and C' happen to have no instances in common it doesn't follow that
they couldn't possibly have instances in common.

Note that this issue arises for most common properties of, and relations
among, classes; e.g., transitivity, reflexivity, subclass, etc.
