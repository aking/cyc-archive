>     of my discussion, to
>     the best of my present abilities, to accommodate the 
>     purposes and even the
>     reading tastes of the Working Group.

MW: I agree you have made changes (improvements) to the style
of your contributions. I have noticed in particular that your 
contributions are much less eliptical. I am gateful, it has meant
I have been able to follow a greater percentage of what you have
to say. For the record I probably manage to read half of what you
write, and get the gist of two thirds of that.

MW: OK. Well here comes the tough bit. How do I think you could
improve the effectiveness of your contribution? Note this is a
personal view. It is what I would like to see.

MW: The real problem is not what you are trying to say, but the
way you are saying it. You are using what I would describe as
an academic approach rather than a business approach. An academic
approach builds up the foundations on which an argument is built
before setting out the key conclusions of an argument. A business
approach presents the conclusions, and promises the foundations
in an appendix that is yet to be written, and will not be unless
the conclusions are challenged.

MW: The consequence of the approach you take is that the volume
of your contributions is significant, with it taking a long time 
to reach the point you are trying to make, by which time most
people have lost interest. Indeed I suspect many feel there
probably isn't any conclusion at all.

MW: If you started with some bold assertions/conclusions about
what we needed to do to achieve something useful (1 page max)
then you might expect to be challenged, but that would give you
the opportunity to elaborate on the issues someone else raised.

MW: One story burned in my memory from long ago is the one about
the manager who asked his subordinate to produce a 1 page summary
of some burning issue. The subordinate worked all night to distill
his 10 page thesis down to 1 page, but he could only get it down to
one and a half pages. He took it to his boss and apologised, but
said he really couldn't get it down to just a page. His boss took
the 2 pages from him and told him not to worry, because he knew just
how to do that, as he threw the second page in the waste bin.

MW: The point is, that if your customer will only read one page, it
doesn't matter how much more you write.

MW: OK, that is one suggestion, start by bringing the conclusions to
the list, not the working. On the other hand I would appreciate the
working of some of your threads as PDF documents as backup to the
conclusions. PDF would have the advantage of solving the presentation
issues you have too, since you would not be limited to ASCII, and I 
would then have a coherent record I could refer to more easily.

MW: I know you think no one will read something in a PDF document,
but I got some responses to my recent contribution on modularisation
that I did in this way, and if you have presented your conclusions in
a tempting way, then I think you would get more interest in what you
have to say.

MW: Well enough I guess, except perhaps to offer a little time to work
with you off list on some trials at my proposed approach should you
choose to accept the challenge.
