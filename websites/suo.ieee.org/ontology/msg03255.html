John Collier wrote (JC):
Jon Awbrey wrote (JA):

JA, quoting JVN, epitomizing DH:

    | We must regard classical mathematics
    | as a combinatorial game played with the
    | primitive symbols, and we must determine
    | in a finitary combinatorial way to which
    | combinations of primitive symbols the
    | construction methods or "proofs" lead.
    |
    | JVN, TFFOM, page 62.
    |
    | Johann Von Neumann,
    |"The Formalist Foundations of Mathematics",
    | from a "Symposium on the Foundations of Mathematics",
    | originally published in 'Erkenntnis' (1931), pp 91-121;
    | translated from German by Erna Putnam & Gerald J. Massey,
    | reprinted in Paul Benacerraf & Hilary Putnam (editors),
    |'Philosophy of Mathematics, Selected Readings', 2nd ed.,
    | Cambridge University Press, Cambridge, UK, 1983.

JC: OK.  That is a good case of meaningless formalism.
    Goedel's theorem need not apply.  This position is
    usually called constructivism, for which there is a
    special place in intellectual hell.  Now, at least,
    we have a concrete example of what your target seems
    to be.

Once again, I have nothing much against this position
but its posing against its supposed opposite position.
It's not one's enthusiasm for the game that one shows
by exclaiming "We must do this!" -- it's the put down
of all other games that claims "We must only do this".
I have no brief against ping-pong or tilde-wffs, only
when I hear "In life as in tiddlywinks ..." do I know
that I have to put on my critical reflection sunvisor.

JC: As an example of a system (as opposed to an opinion) that fits your notion,
    I was going to eventually mention Post canonical systems, developed by
    Emile Post.  There is a syntactic version of Goedel's proof for this,
    but, of course, it makes  no sense to call it an "incompleteness theorem".

So it's "Name Your Poison" is it?
"Combinator Calculus" is my brew!

Jon Awbrey
