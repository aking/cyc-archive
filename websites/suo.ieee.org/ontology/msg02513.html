Chris,

I am happy that you have now come around to my position that any
assertion that "<name> = <expression>" by definition is a kind of
modality:

   "A = B" is a statement that A and B happen to be equal
           in the current state of affairs (possible world, context,
           or permissible update to the knowledge base).

   "A =df B" is a statement that A and B are equal by definition
           not only for the current state of affairs, but for any
           permissible or accessible state.

Now that we agree on this point, I would like to have a suitable
notation in KIF, such as "def", for asserting that some statement of
equality or equivalence is by definition rather than by happenstance.

John Sowa

Chris Menzel wrote:

> The issue is whether you want disjointness to have an implicit modal
> connotation.  If all you ever mean by saying C and C' are disjoint is
> that, right now, as things stand, they have no common instances, then the
> above axiom is fine.  You just have to bear in mind that classes that are
> in fact disjoint can suddenly cease to be disjoint.  On the other hand,
> if, by saying C and C' are disjoint, you mean something stronger -- namely
> that C and C' *can't* have common instances -- then you need to use
> implication rather than bi-implication, since, obviously, from the fact
> that C and C' happen to have no instances in common it doesn't follow that
> they couldn't possibly have instances in common.
> 
> Note that this issue arises for most common properties of, and relations
> among, classes; e.g., transitivity, reflexivity, subclass, etc.

Yes, and it also arises for any definition of any kind.
