This point, concerning the plurality of meanings
that are contested within the term "independence",
and about the option that I think we are bound to
choose for our main meaning in the end, at least,
for all our work on ontology -- and old point, yes,
but still a rather sharp point with me, if not to
say a poignantly pungent point in many contretemps
between mathematicians and normal language users,
has raised its pointy head once more in my study
of the TLC in KIF document, but I thought that it
might be a good idea to break it out for e-special
appraisal, rather than to bury it, as I am wanton
often to do, in the usual suspension of thrilling
disbelief.

To be brief, for once, the point is quite simply this:

Independence is a relation, not an absence of relation.

This point is critical to the viability of all pragmatic thinking,
going back to Peirce's early papers on how we ought to acknowledge
our several "incapacities", in which he began his lifelong critique
of the Cartesian and the Kantian traditions that had already become
the main bents of mind by the time that he wrote.

http://www.door.net/arisbe/menu/library/bycsp/bycsp.htm
http://www.door.net/arisbe/menu/library/bycsp/question/qu-frame.htm
http://www.door.net/arisbe/menu/library/bycsp/conseq/cn-frame.htm
http://www.peirce.org/writings/p41.html

This point is important for many reasons, not the least of which is to
prevent us from pursuing the will o' th' wisp of a "Ding An Sich" (DAS).
Wie ist das, you ask?  Well, one of the several incapacities that Peirce
singled out was that we have no concept of the absoulutely inconceivable,
and this is just a stone's throe away from the circumstance that neither
do we have any concept of a thing that is totally disconnected from our
thinking process.  If independence is worth anything at all, it must be
a standing in relation of a different kind than total denial, disavowal,
disconnection, or exclusion from the swaddling address of our thought.

So it's a pretty important point.

I could also bring this point to bear by way of deflating, just a little,
the myth of the "ontologically hermeneutral medium" (OHM), but I think
that I will elect to resist that temptation for now.

Many Regards,
