Greetings.

Your point has many resonations. It is traditional to make a distinction
between absolute and relative terms. Thus, for instance, Quine's Methods of
Logic, Chap. 25 (P. 125 in the 3rd Edition):

"The characteristic of a relative term is that it describes things only
relatively to further things, which have afterward to be specified in turn.
Thus 'father' as in 'father of Isaac'... Words capable of behaving as
relative terms can be used also as absolute terms, through what amounts to a
tacit existential quantification in the context; thus we may say absolutely
that Abraham is a father, meaning that there is something of which Abraham
is a father... A relative term, like an absolute one, may occur
indifferently as substantive, adjective, or verb..."
