Sorry, I do not understand your question here.

Free association:  Peirce Scholar Mary Keeler recently remarked to me that
Peirce never really used the term "semantic" -- I have been meaning to check
this out as soon as I get the CD version of CP, hopefully in a couple of weeks,
if I am judged to have been nice enough this year -- but it illustrated to me
the way that one anachronistically projects one's present usage into one's
reading of the texts of the past, and not just the texts, but the events.

Much of the current confusion as to what Peirce really said about that
Triple Muse, SyntacTic-SemanTic-PragmaTic, Tic-Tic-Tic, was caused by
the secondary sources and the tertiary commentators -- Charles Morris,
Ogden & Richards, and Walker Percy come to mind especially acutely --
who, for all their skill as apologists and popularizers, did what the
ilk of such writers usually do, which is to try to assimilate complex,
difficult, and rather unfamiliar perspectives and topics to the opinions
and the points of view that constitute the currently comfortable fashions
and the presently fashionable comforts of their prospective audience.

As it happens, it was actually Morris, the Categorizer, who was, by the way,
endeavoring to make Peirce's work palatable to the Analytic VIP's of his day,
who had the Gall to divvy up the omnibus of Peirce's Theory of Signs this way.
That is the way that I first encountered it myself, and I never had much trouble
with this scheme, until I began to see, much later and much lately, how corrosive
are the Barnacles that were thus Procrusted on the Ship of Signs in the Mean Times.
So my advice, learned the hardest way, would be to recur to the pure Peirce per se.

The rest of your question-set will take more concentration, reflection,
and time than I can scrape together right at the moment, so I will try
to get back to it later in the day.  Just skimming over the remainder,
they look like really good questions, so I will be looking forward to
my revisit with a measure of pleasant anticipation.

Many Regards,

Jon Awbrey
