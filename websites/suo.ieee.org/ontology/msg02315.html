Maybe it's already been said, but I'd like to suggest that whether something
is a heap or not does not depend solely on how many elements it contains. It
depends at least equally on spatial organization of the elements, and it
also depends on statistical usage. Ignoring these factors, and restricting
our logic only to the number of elements, seems to be part of the paradox.

Suppose I have 4 large boulders, arranged roughly in a pyramid, with 3
boulders providing a base for the 4th. I might say "there's a small heap of
boulders" and people might understand me and continue to refer to it as a
small heap of boulders. However, other people might disagree and say it's
not really a heap, because it doesn't have enough boulders to satisfy their
usage of the word "heap".

However if the same 4 large boulders were separated spatially and lying
scattered over an area, people would not normally call that a heap. Likewise
if 1000 sand grains were scattered across a tabletop, people might not call
it a heap, though if the same 1000 sand grains were clustered together in a
pile, with some grains supported by others, people would call it a heap.

In the same way, if I walk outside and feel, say five raindrops on my head
in a period of say 3 seconds, I might say "It's raining". Other people might
agree with me, while some might say it's only sprinkling. At what point will
a majority of people say it's raining and a minority say it's sprinkling? At
what point will everyone say it's no longer sprinkling, and everyone say
it's raining? In the case of rain, it depends not just on the number of
raindrops, but on their spatial and temporal distributions.

Terms like "rain" or "heap" are signs that people use to describe things,
and whether or not it is accurate to describe something as "rain" or a
"heap" depends on a kind of statistical consensus reasoning -- what is the
chance that others will understand me and agree with me if I say it's
raining, or if I say "please move that heap of boulders"?

Such terms are not completely vague, because we can learn how to use them
and communicate successfully with them. But the best way to model their
meaning may need to include some formalization of the statistics of how they
are used.

I also agree that support of Natural Language Understanding should be one
goal of SUO. Whether that means that SUO itself must somehow directly model
how words are used (and/or understood) is another question -- We may be able
to provide some support of NLU without doing this, but a broad support of
NLU may require it.
