Pat,
I have more than a technical niggle with Frank Farance's response to my note. My note contains neither the language shown within the sets of quotation marks nor the sense of either quote in other language.  The point was that the SUO participants  background, experience, knowledge and objectives vary significantly. This will make creation of either a paper standard or a functioning software object more difficult and more costly.  As you suggest there may be a certain naiveté about this among some of the participants.

Wide adoption of a standard (de facto or de jure; paper or software) does not depend on how difficult it was to create, how much it cost or how many people participated.  Wide adoption depends on whether the ontology is suitable for the user's purpose, well constructed, easily available and free - completely in the public domain and completely public (i.e. everything needed to effectively use it is included in the release and placed in the public domain).

Another way of saying this is never confuse effort with results.

Bob

ps-- Who participated, for how long, under what circumstances matters greatly to the quality of an ontology.  The user community cares very much about quality, but as long as it's free, they don't care much about how difficult or costly it was to  develop.

pat hayes wrote:

>[Frank Farance, to Bob Spillers:]
>.....It is contradiction to state "wider participants will produce
>lower adoption" and then "lower participation gets wider adoption".

Well, just a technical niggle, but its not a contradiction. It's just
saying that participation and adoption will have a Galois
relationship. For example, suppose that participation in attempting
to construct the SUO produces a strong impression, among many of
those who participate, that the complexity of conforming to such an
oppressively complex and relatively useless standard isnt worth the
effort of compliance. In other words, suppose that participation in
the process itself constitutes a rather sharp education in the
philosophical complexity of ontological  practice, sharp enough to
overcome the rather naive, starry-eyed optimism which seems to
motivate many SUO adherents. Then this kind of Galois relationship
might indeed hold. (One might draw an analogy with the relationship
between gung-ho patriotismat the beginning of WW1 and the amount of
enthusiasm for continued participation in trench warfare among the
veterans.)

Pat Hayes
