> Pat, 
> I have more than a technical niggle with Frank Farance's response to my note. My note contains neither the language shown within the sets of quotation marks nor the sense of either quote in other language.  The point was that the SUO participants  background, experience, knowledge and objectives vary significantly. This will make creation of either a paper standard or a functioning software object more difficult and more costly.  As you suggest there may be a certain naiveté about this among some of the participants. 

Bob-

----

You state that CYC should be used as the default SUO <-- I presume your opinion is that SUO is acceptable.

----

You state "If one cannot create a general purpose upper level ontology that is significantly better than CYC then the effort should stop" <-- sounds reasonable in the sense of "don't reivent the wheel", but there is still work necessary to formalize the work as a standard (assuming the CYC is acceptable to the SUO participants).

Here's some possibilities:

        - If it is possible to "a general purpose upper level ontology that is significantly better than CYC", then there may be some participants who want to contibute their work.  If they want to contribute, they will let us know.

According to my note above, even it CYC were accepted, some standardization effort would still be required.

----

You state that it is desirable to have wide adoption for success <-- sounds reaonsable.

Wide adoption would mean that it would satify the needs of a variety of stakeholders.

You believe that there are significantly varying needs of stakeholders, so consensus is unlikely.

Then why do you propose CYC?

----

> Wide adoption of a standard (de facto or de jure; paper or software) does not depend on how difficult it was to create, how much it cost or how many people participated.  Wide adoption depends on whether the ontology is suitable for the user's purpose, well constructed, easily available and free - completely in the public domain and completely public (i.e. everything needed to effectively use it is included in the release and placed in the public domain). 
> 
> ...
> 
> ps-- Who participated, for how long, under what circumstances matters greatly to the quality of an ontology.  The user community cares very much about quality, but as long as it's free, they don't care much about how difficult or costly it was to  develop. 

I agree that "wide adoption of a standard does not depend on how difficult it was to create, how much it costs or how many people participated".

But later on you state "Who participated, for how long, under what circumstances matters greatly to the quality of an ontology.  The user community cares very much about quality ..." and quality affects adoption.  On one hand you state that only end result matters (only the document, not the participants), and on the other hand you state that the effort matters (e.g., the participants).

I agree that a freely available SUO will promote adoption, but I don't believe anyone is requesting anything otherwise.

> Another way of saying this is never confuse effort with results. 
> 
> Bob 

I'm just confused by your statements.  Can you clarify?
