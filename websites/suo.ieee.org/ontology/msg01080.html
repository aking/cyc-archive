Josh,

When there is a problem with interpretation,
one may find it helpful to consider several
additional features appertinent to the text:

1.  Consider the Context.
2.  Consider the Recipient, Perhaps Thy Self.
3.  Consider the Source.

Now, the context of my text entitled "Echos" --
okay, I will take 'some' rispondibility for
re-transmutating it the way that you read it --
is a discussion going on all around us about,
or rather, allegedly about, apparently about,
let us say, what is abstract, and what is not,
and what is concrete, and what is not, gliding,
if not glossing, over an unremarked assumption,
that "abstract" = "non-concrete", and inversely,
that "concrete" = "non-abstract", I am guessing --
there's always a lot of "plausible deniability"
with the lot of these "unremarked assumptions" --
and who has a space-time locus, and who may
located "elsewhere", I guess again, and so
on and so forth, and so it goes, I might
have missed a lot of the details, but,
anyway, I just had a dim sense, which
everyone will tell you I have a lot of,
that I had heard many a talk verisimilar
to this, somewhere, somewhen, once or twice
before, and so I was trying to remember where
and when, and to see if I could guess what the
upshot would be before I actually had to wait for
all the shooting up of it, and if I had taken the
trouble to continue the cascade of echoes that I
could hear reverberating throughout the emptier
barrels of my mind, I would have recountered
the extra questions of what is a particle,
and what is a wave, and all the ruckus
that ensued from forgetting to ask
some simple questions of those
subjects and those predicates,
and that was more or less the
drift or the gist or the point
of those echoes that I transmuted
to the group, for your consideration.

Many Regards,

Jon Awbrey
