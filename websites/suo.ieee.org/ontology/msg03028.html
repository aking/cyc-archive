> I only quite recently realized why Peirce decided to use the word "sign" as
> the generic term, whereas in his earliest published work he used "sign"
> rather as the name for what he later called "index".  Why the change from
> the earlier usage when the earlier one seems to be the usage more consistent
> with common sense usage?  I believe the reason is this.  
> 
> For one thing, Peirce's original motive for developing the theory of signs
> was in the interest of establishing logic as a science, which requires that
> its subject-matter by publicly observable.  Thus although his dictum that
> "all thought is in signs" comes to have a number of different meanings, the
> original sense of it was that we are to regard thought as present in
> publicly available inscriptions and sounds, like marks on a blackboard or a
> sheet or paper or in aural media, and thought as something hidden because
> private is to be regarded as the special case, of no special interest to the
> logician, anyway, until it gets publicly expressed.  (This is not to be
> confused with a behaviorist conception of thought: that wasn't what he had
> in mind.  What he had in mind was that thoughts are in the signs you see in
> front of you as relational properties thereof, grounded in one way or
> another in properties of the publicly available inscriptions: intrinsic
> (i.e. monadic, qualitative), dyadic, or triadic properties, grounding,
> respectively, what he later called iconic, indexical, and symbolic signs
> respectively.
> 
> Still, this doesn't fully account for it since he could have used some other
> term for any sort of meaningful or significant entity and reserved the word
> "sign" for indices only, which are signs of the type grounded in dyadic or
> existential relationships, which is what you have in mind in your message, I
> take it.  Now, we actually do find it fairly natural to refer to actual
> inscriptions of symbols on a page or a blackboard as being signs, and Peirce
> is certainly not the only theorist to refer to actually occurring symbols
> and icons as being "signs", but, nevertheless, there is apparently some
> infelicity in his decision to use "sign" as the generic term, even given all
> that I have said thus far, and it has always made me uneasy, too, even
> though I could never come up with an alternative terminology set that
> seemed, on balance, to be nearly as good.  
> 
> But then I noticed something the other day that had completely escaped my
> attention all along that does seem to me to fully justify it, given ordinary
> usage, namely, the fact that in treating meaningful entities as public in
> the way indicated above, he was regarding thinking as a dialogical process,
> too, and his decision to use the word "sign" as the generic term must have
> been due to his realization that the development of logic as a theory of a
> dialogical process -- which is what distinguishes his view from the usual
> way of thinking of logic -- requires regarding the publicly available sound
> or inscription as an actually occurring entity the significance of which is
> to be responded to (i.e. interpreted).  That is, by using the word "sign" as
> his technical term of art for the entity to be interpreted, he was, in
> effect, requiring of the logician that the logican recognize that the
> meaningful entity must first of all be understood by the interpreter in its
> actual occurring or existential character, regardless of whether or not it
> is its iconic or indexical or symbolic value that one is especially
> interested in.  
> 
> Even if, say, one is interested in it primarily as a symbol, there is no
> access to it as a symbol without prior understanding of its evidential value
> as something actually occurring hic et nunc, i.e. at this time and this
> place, i.e. its value as an index.  Peirce warns us of this in his
> insistence that the replica of the symbol, the actually inscribed symbol, is
> not itself the symbol proper, which is general whereas the replica is
> singular.  My guess is that he just took it for granted that anybody
> interested in the matter would understand that he was quite intentionally
> drawing upon our ordinary understanding of "sign" as a resource, forcing us
> in that way to regard thought as dialogical.  As usual, he over-rated his
> readers.    
> 
> Thus it is symptomatic of our failure to understand what his most basic
> logical move is, namely, to regard thought as essentially of the nature of a
> dialogical process, that we do find it odd that he would use the word as he
> does!  We say "Oh, great idea that thought is dialogical! Peirce has such
> good ideas!" and then go right ahead and regard logic from the point of view
> of monologue instead, and wonder idly, now and again, why we cannot seem to
> get any mileage out of the idea of thought as dialogical after all!
> 
> Our hang-up here is that we cannot get out of the box built by the
> architects of the so-called "linguistic revolution", who have dominated 20th
> Century thought in nearly every field concerned with meaning, who thought
> you can say what words mean apart from any actual context of usage, and
> regarded logic as the construction of an ideal system of some sort instead
> of as a theory of the functional aspects of a process.
