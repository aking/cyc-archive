Phil --

I thought one of Lakoff's major points was that all concepts are like what
you call irregular concepts.  He uses the framework of idealized cognitive
models (ICMs) to represent the multi-dimensionality of categories, where
individual categories have a closer or more distant fit along each of the
dimensions.

Since I'm concerned with business applicability of this stuff, I like to
use as an example the concept of "product".  For anything we might call a
product there are dimensions of offering agent, target agents, input
components, creation or assembly process, value, distribution, buy/sell
transaction, etc.  The canonical example of a product might be a consumer
packaged good, like a box of cereal.  Other examples that are more or less
distant on the various dimensions include: processed grain flour used to
make the cereal, a boxcar load of wheat destined for the flour mill, a
grain futures contract, a box used to package the cereal, cardboard
destined to become cereal boxes, an advertising campaign to help promote
the cereal brand, a set of graphics designed as part of the advertising
campaign, product liability insurance associated with the brand, etc. etc.

My position is that almost everything we deal with has this kind of
complexity.  The service provided by the ontological discipline is in
articulating key dimensions of differentiation and patterns (like Lakoff's
ICMs) whereby they apply in various combinations.  I think we really pretty
much waste our time in trying to find or create "single, logical
definitions".  My experience is that most attempts to simplify essential
complexity end up introducing additional complication based on all the
argumentation around how to apply the supposed "simplification".

One other point to make is that the concept of "contest" is related to
"game", which many people (Wittgenstein, Rosch, Lakoff, ...) argue is an
"irregular concept", i.e. not susceptible to a single, logical definition,
and needing to some extent to be described by a collection of examples,
exceptions, prototypes, etc. This ties in with the ideas of mining
ontologies, and describing ontologies via associations, ontology maps, etc.

To better represent the range of real world concepts, we may need to
develop
a framework for "heterogenous concept representation", which allows
intermixing regular and irregular concept definitions, i.e. giving a
definition / description of a (regular / irregular) concept in terms of
other concepts, each of which may be regular or irregular. Such a framework
may also need to allow use of metaphorical mechanisms to define / describe
new concepts in terms of others.
