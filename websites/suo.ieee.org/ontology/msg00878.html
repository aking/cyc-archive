> This last use of "instance" corresponds exactly to the usage for

MW: Yes you are blinded by a mathematical and logical education.

Mere mortals may have quite a different tradition of speach.

In EPISTLE for example we started out having lots of fun with people talking
about "products" or "activities" and it being completely ambiguous whether a
class or an individual was meant. We talked just as blythely about "the
analysis activity" (a class of activity) as we do about "the sports activity
on this Thursday afternoon" (an individual).

For ourselves we decided on a rule that we mostly stick to, which is that
words like activity, and for that matter relation when used alone apply to
the instance/individual level, and that at the class level we add class as a
qualifier, so activity class and relation class. It is a little unnatural at
times, but it does make things explicit.

Hence all the recent discussion. The main point of course is to be aware of
the potential for ambiguity, and since one is in mixed company not to assume
the same background or usage of language.
>

MW: Yes, a singleton. Useful for queries on databases, but not much

> 
> This 'extensional' way of thinking about relations has the merit of 
> corresponding to logical analysis and, if used carefully, to much of 
> ordinary usage.  What it fails to do, however, is provide a more 
> delicate distinction which some folk have suggested is necessary in 
> an ontology which can reflect the distinctions made in natural 
> language, which is that between the various *ways* that a relation

> Socrates' wisdom, as opposed to wisdom in general. ("How do I love 
> thee? Let me count the ways..." If 'love' is a set of pairs, there's 
> no counting to be done.) 

MW: My simple approach to this would be to have subtypes of wisdom that

by being a member of several. Am I missing something?

MW: A key assumption here of course is that relations can have subtypes (it

> But the LOGICAL 
> point is that by making these things explicit, one can manage with a

> of relationship, the ontological moral is, identify as clearly as 
> possible what it is that makes your notion of 'relation' so complex,
