On Mon, 26 Mar 2001, pat hayes wrote:
>
> > >  - A little something about rationale, approach and choices, e.g. why
> > >  did you introduce @arg,
> >
> >You mean, why did I introduce row variables?  Well, there's four
> >paragraphs on row variables there, including an explicit semantics for
> >them, but the simple answer is that they give KIF a great deal of
> >additional expressive power (noted at various points in the
> >documentation statements).
>
> Also, to be fair, Chris didnt *introduce* row variables: they are in
> the original KIF spec, and all we have done is to provide them with a
> decent semantics.

Thanks for that clarification.  I should have said "why did I use row
variables?"

> > > Why did you stop short of extensional classes?
> >
> >Because people often talk about classes in a way that does not
> >presuppose extensionality.  Seems to me to be a good methodological
> >principle not to force a relatively simple issue when you don't have
> >to.
>
> Chris is being slightly disingenuous here.

Actually, I'm not, thank you very much!

> There are actually two distinct issues. First, Chris has a
> philosophical taste for a non-extensional view of classes and
> relations.

Depends on the context.  Intensional relations are, I think, nearly
indispensable in modal contexts and some temporal contexts (but see
below).

> I disagree with him about that.

One of the few things you are wrong about! ;-)  That said, Pat, I am
*not* going to get into an argument with you on this point, at least not
until I'm forced to confront the issue!

> But there is another, technical, reason for keeping the
> relation/extension distinction in the KIF semantics, which is that it
> provides an elegant semantic mechanism for interpreting a logic with a
> 'free syntax', in which for example expressions like (R R) can be
> written and are meaningful.

Right, that's the more important point.

> Chris' view of 'existence', class-membership and time is idiosyncratic
> and I think woefully confused, and it is not forced on one by
> non-extensionality or by assuming that the future is indeterminate.

*teeth grinding*  Must...not...respond.....Must...not...respond...  :-)

> ...The issues of 4d/3d, and of representing nondeterminate
> futures or alternative possible worlds, are almost completely
> orthogonal.

Ok, I still don't believe it, but I haven't heard your arguments on this
point in detail, so I'll withhold...
