>My hunch is that description is primary and that the normative function

Following is Peirce's definition:

  "Logic proper" is "the science of what is quasi-necessarily
  true of the representamina of any scientific intelligence in
  order that they may hold good of any _object_, that is, may
  be true.  Or say, logic proper is the formal science of the
  conditions of the truth of representations."  (CP 2.229)

Elsewhere, he defined a "scientific intelligence" as any
that could learn by experience.  By that he would include
dogs, humans, and extraterrestrials.

Therefore, logic is normative because it is the way any
scientific intelligence would reason if it were trying to
be consistent with experience.  Peirce did not draw any
distinction between "formal logic" and "informal" or
"cognitive" or "human" or "natural" logic.  The only
distinction is between logical and illogical.
