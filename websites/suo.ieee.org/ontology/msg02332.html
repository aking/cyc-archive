John,

Let me see if I can make my concern here a little more clear.
The materials that go into what I am trying to say are these:

1.  The analogy that people often cite in linguistics
    where they say that a grammar is a theory of the
    language, but the people who say that are using
    the word "theory" in a different way than folks
    in logic, who use it to mean a set of sentences,
    which may just happen to be derived from axioms.
    So if I try to rephrase what is being said here,
    my first guess is something like this analogy:

    Axioms : Theory :: Grammar : Language?

    But this is only true if we have a decent axiom set
    for a content domain and when we have a full grammar
    for a language, which we may have in artificial cases,
    but never really have in the empirically natural cases.
    So the claim that any accumulated empirical corpus of
    information about being, data about what is, which is
    to my way of thinking the primal form of any ontology,
    can be supplanted by any of our currently developed
    and very partial theories about being, well, that
    is just not a sensible suggestion.  And yet this
    working assumption is explicitly set out by some
    and even more presumptively taken for granted as
    an unreflective and uncritical rationale by others.

2.  The notion that logic is just a species of semiotic,
    whose differentia is either "formal" or "normative".
    This immerses all matters of logic and theory within
    the broader context of "casual" and/or "descriptive"
    semiotic, maybe more general in some sense or other,
    but perhaps just more diffuse, an embedding within
    the raw data of sign-bearing experience, only parts
    of which are formalized enough to theorize about at
    any given time.  Now, I notice "stories about being"
    already arising within this less regimented setting,
    in the way that myth, narrative, and poetics precede
    the regime of logic proper, and so I have to treat
    the genesis of ontology as prior to theory proper.

3.  As far as context goes, then, I cannot treat it
    in the first instance primarily as a parameter
    indexing theories, though maybe something more
    like a constraint on the POV of a given agent,
    but I have to see it as a factor that affects
    the transition from sign to interpretant sign
    in the relevant sign relation, and all of this
    stage-setting prologues the entrance of logic
    and theory, as full-blown characters, at least,
    onto the scene.

Oh well, that's about as good as I can do at the present hour.

I will sleep on it.

Jon Awbrey

John F. Sowa wrote:
> 
> Jon,
> 
> I deliberately used the word "theory" rather
> than "ontology", because I wanted to make the
> statement more general.  I regard an ontology
> as a very special kind of theory.
> 
> JS>> The first step toward a theory of "context dependence" is to drop
> >>   the word "context".  Or at least restrict its meaning to something
> >>   very narrow, such as "container for a single well-defined theory",
> >>   which reduces it to the solvable point #1.
> 
> JA> This would work if ontologies really were just theories,
> >   that is to say, just sets of sentences called "theorems"
> >   because they derive from a core set of sentences called
> >   "axioms".
> 
> That is all that I intended to say.  I would prefer to
> define a "context" in this sense as just a container for
> any kind of theory.
> 
> JA> Since I read "ontology" as "a story of being",
> >   I am almost tempted to fall in line with this prevailing
> >   and widespread party line, but there are some things that
> >   worry me about it.  The person who says that an ontology
> >   is just a set of sentences that belong to a theory is not
> >   saying something analogous to the bon mot that literature
> >   is just one damn sentence after another, which is at least
> >   referring to an empirical corpus, and not a book of grammar,
> >   which is of what the generative axioms are more the analogues.
> 
> I would prefer to use the word "theory" for the more general sense.
> In fact, I would rather avoid using the word "ontology" in most of
> the SUO discussions.
> 
> > No, that person is saying that the empirical corpus, messy and
> > raw as any natural corpus can be, can be replaced by the theory,
> > as if to say that literature is redundant given the manual of style.
> > And this is simply to forget that before we can have theories we must
> > first have data, unruly, unsystematic, and untheoretic as data will be.
> > It's the scientific way, but how quickly they forget.  How easy we slide
> > into apriorities, forgetting the unpleasantries of wherefrom axioms come.
> 
> I agree that these issues must be addressed before any theory is
> promoted to the status of an ontology about some real-world subject.
> 
> John Sowa
