Bill,

As I said before, I don't like the terms "perdurantist" and
"endurantist", which were popularized by David Lewis (the same
nut who thinks that possible worlds are "really real").

The view that I recommend is Whitehead's "process philosophy",
which is four-dimensional, but it doesn't suffer from the flaws
that Simons (and others have been criticizing).  It would be a
mistake to call W's processes "perdurantist" because he most
definitely makes time and processes acting in time a very
important part of his system.  He also makes room for "objects",
which he defines as repeatedly occurring event types, characterized
by "forms of definiteness", such as the forms of a human body,
a tree, or a house.

> Alternative endurantist views (4D is often called perdurantism and 3D + Time
> endurantism) are those defended by Peter Simons, among others.  On this
> view, the identity of a continuant is not bound up with the extension of its
> parts in 4D, but with a hierarchy of continuants superimposed over the parts
> and connected by a relation of constitution.  This is also the view
> advocated by Guarino in his work.  One disadvantage is a proliferation of
> individuals (I am different than the merelogical sum of my parts at any
> given time so I must be something else).
> 
> Of course there are several perdurantist responses to all these endurantist
> ways out of the problem.

You can define similar structures in Whitehead's system by viewing
different "forms of definiteness" superimposed over the same
mereological extent.

But in any case, the "identity conditions" that Nicola advocates
are orthogonal to the perdurantist, endurantist, or Whiteheadian
views.  The ones Nicola recommends were first proposed by Peter Geach
in 1967, and they have been very severely criticized ever since.
On of Geach's "intuitions" for his identity criteria is to explain
the Christian position of "three persons in one God".  Personally,
I prefer St. Patrick's explanation using a shamrock.

John Sowa
