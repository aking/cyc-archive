W.M. Jaworski wrote (WMJ):
Jon Awbrey wrote (JA):

WMJ: 'Chunks' of inquiry are appended ;-)

JA: Aristotle verges on using a trichotomy here,
    dimly highlighting the scheme that involves
    possession (competence knowledge), exercise ('how to' knowledge),
    and critical reflection (descriptive and/or normative knowledge).

WMJ: Could you elaborate on the trichotomy
     in the context of knowledge engineering?

JA: I am going to make a fresh attempt to focus
    on the specifics of your question, and then
    I will have to gather together another load
    of background material to fill in the frame.

JA: The main idea that comes to my mind is this:
    In my way of thinking, a state of knowledge,
    information, consensus, clarity, certainty,
    belief, and like conditions of intellectual
    settlement are the end results of a process,
    which is convenient to call "inquiry", thus
    encompassing the everyday actions of living
    souls, from animals to angels and all that
    occupy the mediate domains, along with the
    rather more formalized methods of science.

JA: This means that the whole focus of my research
    and hopeful application to intelligent systems
    engineering has shifted toward an examination
    of the varieties of inquiry process that lead
    to this "knowledge", or whatever else in time
    the end products of inquiry come to be called.

JA: One other thing, I will try to avoid speaking
    overmuch of "trichotomies", since most of the
    universes of discourse that I find hereabouts
    are covered with overlapping discs of domains.
    Accordingly, it will generally be more apt to
    speak of 3-adic relations, 3-fold categories,
    degrees of freedom or qualitative dimensions
    that come in 3's.

JA: Now, when it comes to inquiry by 3's, there
    is another one of those Very Big Ideas that
    CSP selected, mutated, and evolved from the
    work of Aristotle.  This is the 3-fold duct
    of abductive, deductive, inductive thinking,
    on which it would well repay our attentions
    of in the study to expend its due attention.
    And so I will, anon.

WMJ: Agreeing and learning from your "fresh attempt",
     I have to limit my "inquiry" process.  To create
     an impression of being competent (true or false)
     in my specialty (context/knowledge engineering)
     is a full-time occupation.

WMJ: Ad TopicMaps

     (1) "The varieties of inquiry processes" performed by a community
         produced the end product called TopicMaps construct.

     (2) A variety of inquiry process performed by an author
         lead to the end product called TopicMap.

WMJ: Lets use the (coarse!) trichotomy --
     competence, 'how to', and declarative knowledge --
     to classify processes and products of (1) and (2).

Okay, maybe, but I wasted far too many years playing the viscous circuits of
declarative/procedural volleyball to want to go through that again, which is
why I left cogsci for a better refereed net.  Provisionally, though, if you
declare "declarative" to mean something like the kind of fully critical and
more or less reflective knowledge that it takes, in the ideal, to write out
an explicit axiomset for a domain, a grammar for a language, or a program
for a recursive function, then I think that I can go along with that.

WMJ: TopicMaps construct and TopicMaps are used to represent declarative
'knowledge'.
     Inquiries of the processes at (1) and (2) are examples of "Inquiry Into
Inquiry".

Yes, in particular.

WMJ: Could such inquiries be done using natural language as a main
notational tool?
     No!  Why?  ProcessMaps constructs are multi-dimensional.

WMJ: Short answer: ProcessMaps are similar to TopicMaps, that is  mapping
(associating) processes and its attributes.

Longer answer: ProcessMap consists of a Set of Sets of Process 'Atoms' and a
Map allocating Roles to Sets and Set Members.
