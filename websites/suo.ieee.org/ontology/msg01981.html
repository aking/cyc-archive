> (1) logic is partly an attempt to clarify the
>     nature (semantics and syntax) of propositions,

Jim, I view logic as a normative science.  This view has the following
array of logical implications and practical consequences.  As a science,
logic is a form of inquiry that seeks to end, like any form of inquiry,
a particular variety of uncertainty.  In the case of logic the specific
type of uncertainty in question is one as to how we ought to conduct our
reasoning in order to achieve the aim, end, goal, object, or "pragma" of
reasoning in general.  The normative quality of this science comes in with
the word "ought", but this is an "ought" that is related to a predesignated
object, and this, as you know, tends to bring down on the heads of pragmatic
thinkers the charge of crass utilitarianism, if not the moral equivalent of
warlocks.  As a science whose celebrants feel fortunate enough to claim that
it has met with the occasional if provisional success, logic claims to have
arrived, here and there, now and again, at genuine bits of knowledge as to
how we ought to conduct our thinking if we want to succeed at it.  As you
know, this view is often discovered to be controversial, even among those
who call themselves "pragmatic thinkers".

> (2) our proposition and thus our logic reflect our basic assumptions
>     about the nature of that supposed reality which we are representing
>     by our propositions,

One of the functions of language is to describe the phenomena of experience,
and all of the descriptive sciences depend on its success in this function.
Language has a logical aspect, constituted by the logical forms that are
embodied in it, and so it may seem that these logical forms have a role
in the description of reality.  I guess it depends on what a role is.
But it is also true that the normative function of logic is rather
independent, not "exclusiove of all relationship to", but just
independent, of its descriptive role.
>
> (3) for the most part we assume that reality to be one
