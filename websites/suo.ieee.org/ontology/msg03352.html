JA = Jon Awbrey
JC = Jerry Chandler

JC: You assert that objects with names S_1, S_2, ..., S_9 exist.

Just to double check, the S_j are intended to be taken as languages --
of course, they become "objects of discussion and thought" (OODAT's)
as soon as anyone start to talk or think about them, but that's just
incidental for the moment.  The primary object of the discussion is
a lattice of 16 elements U = {u_0, ..., u_15}, and each of the S_j
is being considered as a candidate sign domain in a sign relation
L_j c U x S_j x S_j.

JC: The meanings associated with S_1, S_2, ..., S6 are not questioned at this time,
    with the exception of the following question.  Would I be correct to presume
    that the column labeled "Vec" refers to a tuple and not a vector space?

I think so.  I just made these up on the spot,
and was probably thinking "Vec" for a set of
bit-vectors or coordinate tuples, regarded
more as codes than as mathematical vectors.

JC: The meanings associated with S_7, S_8, and S_9 are not crisp.
    Is it possible to make these assertions crisp?   When they
    are made crisp, will they become redundant to earlier
    members of the series?

Is it possible to make the meaning of the word "crisp" crisp?
I studied fuzzy set theory when it first hit the streets,
but do not recognize much trace of that in the usages
that I am hearing lately, especially hereabouts.

JC: Do you assert that bijections exist among any of the classes S_1, S_2, ..., S_9?
    All of the classes?  Would a graph of the bijections among the 9 classes of S
    be connected?  Or would the bijections among the nine objected be related via
    two distinctive connected graphs?

I'm not sure about all of what you are asking.  What will happen is that the signs in
each sign domain S_j will form up into various sorts of "equivalence classes" (EC's),
and then the relevant bijections between languages will be defined between these EC's.

JC: Are you asserting that the notion of a function is identical to the notion
    of a proposition?  That is, do you intend to distinguish the symbolism for
    a function from the symbolism for a proposition crispy?

The way I understand these words -- and here I have to differ from Peirce,
mostly just to preserve conformity with a very insistent COI that rules
the roost these days -- a proposition is an abstract logical object,
denoted by what we would have to call a "propositional expression"
or a "sentence".  Given the structure of a domain of propositions,
we can generally recognize any number of more concrete domains
that have the same structure, and we may then refer to these
as "interpretations", "realizations", or "representations"
of the abstract structure, though, of course, these are
extremely overloaded terms, and have to be taken only
in the context that makes clear how they are used.
The boolean functions f : B^k -> B form one such
concretion, as do the subsets (f^-1)(1) c B^k
that are commonly visualized as the marked
nodes in a k-cube or the shaded cells in
a venn diagram, and so on.

Jon Awbrey
