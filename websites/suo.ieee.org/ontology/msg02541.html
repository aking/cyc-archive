> Here's the kind of situation that it seems to me important
> to avoid.  Maybe it will illustrate some confusion on my part
> that needs clearing up.  Suppose, in the usual extensional fashion,
> we define a relation R to be symmetric if and only if, for all x and y,
> if Rxy then Ryx.  Suppose now we have an ontology of human relationships
> that includes the loving relation, and that we are applying that ontology
> with respect to a given domain of persons in which, as fortune would have
> it, all love is requited.  By our axiom, we infer that loving is symmetric.
> Now suppose our domain broadens to include Johann and Martha, and that
> Johann loves Martha, but that his ardor is not reciprocated.  Is not
> our ontology now inconsistent with this fact?  How do you propose to
> avoid situations like this?  Seems to me (though I'm willing to be
> convinced otherwise) the thing to do is to recognize that there's
> more to symmetry (call it a modal element, or don't) than just
> satisfying -- as a matter of current contingent fact -- that
> Rxy materially implies Ryx, and that this is most naturally
> expressed, in a nonmodal language, by using a conditional
> rather than a biconditional in the symmetry axiom.
> Symmetry thus becomes a property that has to be
> *declared* true of a relation rather than
> *inferred* from its instances.

Chris,

The part of the whole problematic
about extension and intension that
really interests me comes in here,
but I see it as arising from the
rub of concepts and data against
each other in the inquiry process.

A person gathering data is forced into an extensional attitude --
the givens arrive in the form of one damn tuple after another,
and the job is to conceive concepts and to formulate theories
that bring some modicum of order to the welter of confusion.

A person who has conceited the provisional theory that
all love is requited will most likely be disappointed
at some point or other, and be forced to find another --
theory, that is.

I think that there is much of practical utility
that could be talked about here, if we drop the
presumption that all of inquiry can be confined
to a deductive logical box.

I do not even want to think about
how many times I've said that one.

Jon Awbrey
