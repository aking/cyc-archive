Dear Matthew,  you are too kind in your grading!

Let me once again try to capture your POV as briefly as possible.

1. A spatio-temporal object (alias a spatio-temporal extent) is something
in the real world, and can be represented at as a whole by a constant.

2. An activity is something in the real world.  An activity is involved in
actual relationships but they are not the activity.

3. An activity is a spatio-temporal extent (you believe that this follows
from 1 & 2, but see below).

4. A relation is a representation (and therefore not in the real world, but
if you are qualified to study advanced topics you may wish to consider
representations of relations in the real world)

5. From 1 and 3 it follows that an Activity can be modelled as a whole by a
constant. The relationships of an activity can be modeled by relations
containing that constant.  The example is modelled using multiple dyadic
and triadic relations.

6. Attempting to model activities as a single n-adic relation may be
adequate for some purposes, but is inadequate for intercommunication
between systems, and by implication for the SUO.

Whether 3 follows from 1 & 2 depends on whether or not anything other than
spatio-temporal extents can be in the real world.  Elsewhere you said
"Actual relationships exist in the same way that actual classes do. They
are abstract objects that are out there that we can take an interest in and
use to organise and think about the world.".  This suggests you do
recognize things other than spatio-temporal extents in the real world.  It
follows there needs to be some justification why an activity is a
spatio-temporal extent and not anything else.

Please can you specify what other things you do recognize in the real world
at the same sort of meta-level as spatio-temporal extents, idealy with a
specific example (like Activity) of each.

My only other comment at this stage is that, while I am happy to see a
constant in some sense as a surrogate for something as a whole, I see no
way of thinking or talking about e.g. an activity as meaningful, without
seeing the actual relationships as part of it.  I see this as a matter of
interpretation and not an inconsistency.

I hope you will agree with me that the answer to the original question "Is
an activity a relation?" is, in this context, an emphatic no, because the
activity is in the real world and the relation is a representation.  The
six points above are an answer to what might have been the real question
"Can an activity be represented by a relation?".

I have trimmed some of the tail of this exchange.
