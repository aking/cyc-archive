My comment was directed to the question of what you "syntacticians"
intend by the various symbols that you introduce on the right side
of this purported definition, equivalence, or whatchamacallit this
very hour or minute.  You are a sensible person.  If you ask me to
define or to explain some problematic concept or term that I foist
on the discussion, and if my response is stated in terms of eleven
or twelve rather more problematic concepts or terms, then you will
immediately "smell a rat", as they say, and demand that I try once
again to reduce the total complicity of what I tender as definiens.
Thus, a person does not escape the charge of a circular definition
simply by placing primes on the recursed subsequent appearances of
the symbol or term to be defined, by conferring a diversionary new
alias upon it, or by changing its name to protect his innocence of
knowledge about the subject.  No, sensible people are not deceived
by callous gambits and shallow tricks such as these, now are they?

In the beginning I had been giving your party the benefit of the doubt,
assuming only that you were introducing the exact same relation on the
right as appeared on the left -- a somewhat common political stratagem --
only under a sleightly transmogrified name, like converting verb forms
to the corresponding gerunds, and other diverting derring doings, but
now I am begining to suspect that you are actually thinking that you
can get away with squirreling away a changeling, a still more mystic
predicate on the right than ever dared to show its face on the left --
a somewhat common political stratagem, too, sad to say.

> MW:  I also pick up from between your comments that in logical

>      We do not have such an operator in data modelling, so I
>      have been applying a stricter test of "is the same thing".

I do not know what context you are referring to here.
The last time that I remember using an equal sign in
quotes it was merely to underscore a straightforward
reading of "is".  When questions of equality get any
more involved than that I will typically use several
standard devices, like "square bracket" operators to
signify "the denotation of" a sign or expression, or
subscripts on the equal signs that specify the exact
sense of equivalence that is meant in the given case.
