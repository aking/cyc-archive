Notice that this example is created from the point of view of the sign
emitter, rather than the sign recipient.  I am just now realizing that this
may be a major disconnect between what you are trying to do and what I hope
to accomplish through participation in SUO.  Your work seems to be centered
on inquiry, where my concern is communication.  For the kind of issues I am
trying to address e.g. commercial transactions among humans, using computer
and telecommunications networks) I think that it is much more possible to
address the problem from the point of view of what's going on with emitters
than recipients of signs.  We're much more likely to be able to identify
them, and to actually inquire into what is going on in their minds with
respect to purposes and understandings.

Some observations about this example:

*  In a very real sense every sign is irreducible with respect to these
eight aspects (sign, referent, emitter, recipient, occasion, medium,
purpose understanding).

*  Some of the aspects may be unknown at various points in time, or
unknowable external to an individual sign emitter or recipient.

*  The medium tends to be common between emitter and recipient (sound
waves, magazine page, billboard, etc., with more variation possible in the
instance of computer networks).

*  There is complexity within each of these aspects, but most especially
with respect to purposes and understandings.

* Correspondence among understandings has a lot to do with correspondence
(or symmetry) among purposes.

*  The general purpose for efforts like SUO, in my opinion, has to do
specifically with helping to line up understandings between emitters and
recipients.  It is because of the ambiguity of signs based on implicit and
mismatched understandings that we want to devise schemes for making
dimensions of understanding explicit, so that signs will be interpreted
identically (or at least similarly) by multiple recipients, and in
correspondence with the purpose of the emitter.  For instance, the Tower of
Hanoi is not really a discussion of the spectral analysis of color, but
rather imaginary discs that are distinguished by color, such that the
dimensions of understanding will not line up properly without placing them
in the ontology of imagined molecular objects, rather than the ontology of
color terms.

Each of these sign relations has familiar type of structure.
The common object domain is O = {black, green, pink, yellow},
and "at" or "over" each object o in O one finds a "part" of
a "partition", that is to say, an equivalence class of an
equivalence relation on the correspoinding "syntax domain",
which is a convenient name for the set-theoretic union of
the sign domain S and the interpretant domain I, in these
cases having S = I.

For the monolingual interpreters Olivier and Godfrey,
each equivalence class of signs over each object is
in fact just a singletion set, containing the only
name that the agent knows for that object.

For the bilingual interpreters Josiane and Kim+Lex,
the sign relations are completely identical, since
the order of rows does not matter.  The partition
of signs that these two agents share, as a set of
sets, can be written as follows:

|    {{ "pink"   , "rose"  },
|     { "green"  , "vert"  },
|     { "yellow" , "jaune" },
|     { "black"  , "noir"  }}

A.  What is this triadic relational POV, anyway?
    1.  Explain it in terms of 3-adic relation tables.
        Suppose one is given a 2-adic table of objects and signs.
        a.  Option (a).  Add a column for the interpretive agent.
        b.  Option (b).  Add a column for the interpretant sign.
    2.  Aristotle's Approximation to the full-fledged sign relation:
        a.  Assumption (a).  Commonality in the object domain.
        b.  Assumption (b).  Commonality in the interpretant domain.
        c.  Conclusion (c).  Canonically privileged frame of reference.
B.  Bearing of the sign relational POV on several design issues:
    1.  Ontological Integrity versus Ontological Modularity.
    2.  Ontological Invariance versus Ontological Relativity.
    3.  Interoperability among ontological frames of reference.
    4.  Differential topics.
        a.  Change.
        b.  Development.
        c.  Diversity.
        d.  Evolution.
        e.  Inquiry.
        f.  Learning.
    5.  Critical Reflection.
    6.  Dynamics of Inquiry.
        a.  Informational dynamics.
        b.  Psychological dynamics.
        c.  Sociocultural dynamics.
