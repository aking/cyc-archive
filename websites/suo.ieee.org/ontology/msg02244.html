Mike,

Compare & Contrast:

¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Empirical Domain X   | A Theory That We     | A Theory That We     |
|                      | Have Of Domain X     | Wish We Had of X     |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Natural Language Y   | Book Grammar of Y    | Full Grammar of Y    |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
| Ontologic Domain Z   | Book Ontology of Z   | Full Ontology of Z   |
¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤

The notion that the ontologic domain is exhausted by a theory,
even a full theory, much less the sort of temporizing theory
that we are likely to encounter in our brief lives, is not
just optimism, but betrays a theoretical confusion that is
analogous to the common error in some circles of confusing
a language and a grammar.  If you think that the language
is contained in a grammar book, even a completed grammar,
then you may be insensitive to this mistake, as I have
seen people become habituated in similar situations.

The gensym fallacy -- finally a fallacy that aptly represents itself! --
may have been one of the more banal and common truisms for the brand
of AI that we amused ourselves with 20-30 years ago, but it exhibits
one of those ilks of totally impractical and unrealistic assumptions
about the feasibility or the probabilty of ever finding or making an
isolated system that only a "brain in a think tank" could even think
to entertain.  In short, in circuit, it asks us to fantasize that we
could ever actually be empowered to change "all" of the signs in any
realistic semiotic system, the sort of axiomatic that has been under
revision for 15 years that I know about in "embedded" and "situated"
intelligent systems research.  And, of course, I could quote you the
Peircean pedigree for this re-inquiry, but I'll spare you this time.

Yours Truly,

Jon Awbrey
