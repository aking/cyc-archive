>Pat,
>  It's possible that Doug's views have changed.  Or that he himself 
>has alternate views.  In any case, I'd suggest that any assertion 
>that "only bottom up is good" or "only top down is good" are false. 
>The most sensible approach (which does seem self-evident to me) is 
>that one does both.  Make high level distinctions as best one can a 
>priori and then test them against modelling tasks in common sense 
>reality or particular applications.  That's what my group does every 
>day.

Part of the problem of perception here may be that we really have no 
idea what your group does, or what methodology drives your 
activities, or indeed almost anything about how the merged ontology 
gets to be the way it is. All we have to go on are your and Ian's 
responses to critiques, which often seem defensive rather than 
constructive, a kind of general sense that y'all are churning away at 
something or other, and the resulting ontology, which I have to say 
does not give one a reassuring sense that it is converging to any 
kind of coherent overall shape.

>  Let me pose a pointed question in turn.  If you really believe 
>that there shouldn't be an upper ontology, or that it should only 
>contain Entity and Relation, are you not in fundamental disagreement 
>with the Scope and Purpose defined for this group?

I think that we are much more likely - overwhelmingly more likely - 
to discover a useful upper ontology by upward generalization from 
existing middle-level axiom sets (the kind that Ian is busy 
collecting and merging) than by trying to force them into a 
pre-existing heirarchy, even one with such a distinguished pedigree. 
Every single attempt to create a large-scale ontology has needed to 
undergo major revisions of its upper levels after development. Cyc 
has been through several such rebuilds. What is your basis for your 
apparent confidence that your group will escape this trap?

Let me ask you a pointed question in reply: what percentage of the 
axioms or concepts that Ian has merged would you classify as 'upper' 
rather than 'middle' ? Do you have any clear sense of how 'low' an 
upper ontology should go?

>  Let me however leave that as a rhetorical question.  I think in 
>fact we can avoid further haranguing over lofty and general goals if 
>we focus on actual substance.  Would you support an effort to create 
>simply a catalogue of theories, along the lines of your paper "A 
>catalogue of temporal theories"?  If so, I think our efforts on the 
>SUO "on the ground", as it were, will be identical.  We'll just be 
>trying to formalize models of time, spatial relations, human 
>organizations etc.  It will just happen to be the case that some 
>folks will be trying to make those theories consistent with each 
>other.

I would be much happier if there was some clearer mothodology ar 
rationale for this effort of making consistent. There seeems to be 
none, or at any rate it is being kept a close secret. For example, I 
would like to see an organised attempt made to state as precisely as 
possible the intended universes of discourse of each component theory 
beofore any attempt is made to unify them or render them mutually 
consistent. I would like to see an organised attempt to locate 
'nonstandard' models wherever that is feasible, as I tried to do in 
the time catalog (inspired by van Bentham, by the way.)

The straw man suggestion (the SHUO) was actually a serious 
methodological suggestion, by the way. that I honestly think would be 
more utility than what you are doing right now.
