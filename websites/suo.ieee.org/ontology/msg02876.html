Phil,

I never said that natural concepts are "entirely artificial",
nor did I say that there aren't important common features to all
of the meanings of a the same concept.

> Thanks for restating this point -- I am not sure I agree with it, entirely.
> I think that many important concepts are regular, and are not entirely
> artificial, but reflect important properties of the real world. I agree with
> your point however, that regular concepts are formalized within theories.

As an example, there are certain commonalities to all the games
that Wittgenstein discussed:  they are all activities played by
human beings for certain purposes.  That is sufficient to classify
the concept type Game under PurposefulActivity.  But that is not
sufficient to distinguish Game from every other kind of purposeful
activity.

That is the reason why I believe that things like WordNet are useful:
they give a rough classification of concept types, which is an
important first step toward narrowing down the language game (or
theory) that is involved.  But a formal definition of the concept
type that is sufficient for detailed reasoning can only be done
within the context of a much more detailed theory (of the kind that
Pat Hayes has been talking about).

> A discussion that I find very reasonable is given in the paper I cited by
> Pinker and Prince. To do justice to their analysis, I need to present an
> extended quotation:

Yes, I believe they give a good argument in favor of a WordNet
level of classification.  But a completely formal specification
requires a much more detailed theory.

As a matter of fact, I agree to a large extent with Ruhl's theory
of "monosemy", which says that every word (or almost every word)
in a natural language has exactly one core meaning.  The multiple
meanings given in most dictionaries is the result of the meaning
shifts caused by metaphor, meronymy, and specializations to different
language games.  Following is the reference:

   Ruhl, Charles (1989) _On Monosemy: A Study in Linguistic
   Semantics_, State University of New York Press, Albany.

But a formal definition that is sufficient for the kind of reasoning
that Pat Hayes wants to do can only be done within one of those
specialized language games.
