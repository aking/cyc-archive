> > In the subject of Real Analysis, one is concerned
> > with spaces and functions of the following types:
> >
> > ¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
> > |  Dimension    |  Space        |  Function     |
> > ¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~¤
> > |  1            |  R^1 = R      |  R^1 -> R     |

> > |  n            |  R^n          |  R^n -> R     |

> > Now, it is typical to begin with the one-dimensional case
> > and gradually to work toward the multi-dimensional setting.
> > In referring to the elements of R and mappings from R to R,
> > one naturally uses notations like "x in R",  "f : R -> R",
> > and the value of f at x is written "f(x)".  So far, so good.
> >
> > When one finally arrives at the n-dimensional case, it is
> > usual to write things like "x = <x<1>, ..., x<n>>", using
> > either the single letter or the coordinate list, depending
> > on whichever is convenient at a given moment of discussion.
> >
> > But how do you write the value of f at x?  Strictly speaking,
> > that is, according to the rulebook of syntactic substitution,
> > one ought to write either "f(x)" or "f(<x<1>, ..., x<n>>)",
> > and when you go through this ringer for the first time, some
> > professors will actually insist that you preserve this purity
> > of notational discipline, sanctioning "f(x<1>, ..., x<n>)" as
> > a vile abuse of notation.  Of course, this degree of pickiness
> > will all seem a bit silly, at first and then again a few months

> > experience teaches the experienced teacher that there is a point
> > to it.
> >
> > Moral Of The Story?  Well, one thing ought to be perfectly clear
> > by now to all sensible observers, and that is that the nature and
> > the structure of the mathematical object in question, whether it
> > is x in R^n or else f : R^n -> R, is not affected one liitle bit
> > by the syntactic expression, whatever its structure, that is used
> > to refer to it.  And if anybody thinks to make ontological capital
> > off the abuse of notation, or the poetic license, that permits us

> 1.  I agree that if you have some set of binary relations
>     that essentially define the coordinate positions of
>     components in some higher arity relation then there
>     is also the higher arity relation.

You are saying here that the definition of a k-tuple
in terms of its k coordinate projections has nothing
to do with the potential analysis, decomposition, or
whatever, of the whole k-adic relation into possibly
simpler relations, arity-wise.   Welcome to the club!

So this conception of cartesian product space, which
was not discovered by Quine fifty years ago, but has
made up a staple article of furniture in mathematics,
since, here's a hint, Descartes, has been an utterly
irrelevant red herring since the initial moment that
it first got foisted on the scene.

> That was not the issue.  Indeed, the axiom that Chris M
> has put together on my behalf specifically states this.
> The issue was whether the higher arity relation could be
> represented by some set of binary relations.  You do not
> seem to be disputing that this can be done in the manner
> I have described.

Again, you, with a little bit -- okay, a lot -- of non-help
from others, are consistently failing to distinguish between
a relation, which is, as you say, a set, and a single tuple.

The coordinate projections are relevant to the structure of
a single k-tuple.  To talk about analyzing a whole relation
in terms of other entire relations, putatively simpler ones,
one needs to bring in operations that operate on whole sets.
Examples of 2-adic set operations (so 3-adic set relations)
are things like Intersection, Union, Relational Composition.
Examples of 1-adic set operations (so 2-adic set relations)
are things like Complementation and the various Projections.
To do anything that qualifies as an "analysis" (up-chunking)
of a set, one would have to use "forms of analysis" that are
at least as adic as 2-adic set operations (3-adic relations).

> Indeed your argument now seems to be that it says the
> same thing (of course it should say the same thing).
> 
> 2.  I understood that a part of your argument was that n-adic
>     relations could ALWAYS be "decomposed" into triadic relations,
>     but not necessarily to binary relations.

Yes, that is correct.  But people are responsible only for what
they actually say.  And the way that I and my whole army uses
the terms "composition" and "decomposition" is criterial to
the claim being made.  It is only that a set of folks who
have never bothered to read or to understand the initial
statement of the issue have cluttered up the beach with
their peculiar variety of cargo-cultism.

> However, following the argument you seem to be putting forward here,
> if I have a 4-adic, or 5-adic relation, then "decomposing" it does
> not put it out of existence, it only means that it is implied rather
> than explicit in some ontology.

This one tuple at a time investigation has nothing
to do with the matter of analyzing whole relations.

> This seems to be arguing both ends against the middle to me.

No, I'm afraid you guys have been shadow-boxing in the dark,
and nobody even rang the bell.

Jon Awbrey
