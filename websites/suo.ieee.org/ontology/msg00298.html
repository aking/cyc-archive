I think what Matthew may have been talking about are cases where 
something might have an intermittent existence. This isnt plausible 
for physical things, but there are some reasonably intuitive 
examples. My favorite is the concept of a 'shed' in weaving, which is 
the space through which the shuttle passes *when the heddles are in a 
certain configuration*, each different heddle defining a diferent 
'shed'. The same piece of physical space therefore 'becomes' a 
different shed when the heddles are changed, and each shed comes into 
and goes out of existence as the fabric is woven. Another example 
might be a 'session' of a committee which meets regularly, or the 
activity of writing a book over a long period, interrupted by things 
like sleeping.

There have been attempts to figure out a calculus of 'intermittent' 
time-intervals, but I don't know of any fully successful ones. The 
relationships between arbitrary such intervals are much more complex 
than  those between connected intervals. (BTW, algebras for 
relativistic intervals have been worked out. The 13 'linear' 
relationships become, as I recall, around 19 possibilities in special 
relativity. This might seem like a purely academic exercise, but it 
has realistic applications whenever one has to consider transmission 
speeds which are comparable to the normal time measures, eg as in 
communication in networks.)
