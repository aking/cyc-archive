Dear Pat,

I think you are optimistic about how merging independent ontologies will
arise. In the SC4 community we managed to develop incompatible models, even
when starting from a common core. I see little chance that ontologies will
be mergeable with anything other than a lot of hard work by humans without
that having been part of their design intent. ISO18876 tries to set out an
architecture within which this is possible.

The port engine on a plane is an example of an object that can have periods
of non-existance. When one engine is removed for maintenance, and replaced
by another. However, your description of Pat's intent is sufficiently
unconstrained to cover that case.

No further comments below.

> [PC]:
>     Yes, this will be especially true if detailed relations or axioms
> are attached to newly-defined objects, so that there are multiple 
> links to other objects, which will increase the chances of conflict.
> There is a *possibility* of having different extensions of the SUO 
> with no conflicts.  The more likely case is for two differently-
> developed ontologies to have some conflicting definitions.  If the 
> conflicting concepts have the same names, they can be easily 
> recognized 
> and some resolution may be possible.  If the same notion is defined
> using different labels, that also can be recognized, if all
> relations are the same.  When two application ontologies use different
> labels *and* different logical terms to define the same notion, that 
> will present a really tough case to recognize.  Using an SUO is
> still light-years ahead of using no common defining vocabulary,
> for recognizing similarities and conflicts.
>     To minimize the chances of unnecessary conflicts of that 
> kind I think we
> 
> do need to have a "Reference SUO extension" which contains all of the
> *consistent*
> concepts that contributors are willing to submit and are 
> definable using the
> concepts in the SUO.  The SUO itself, I hope, will have as much as 
> possible of the basic logical and semantic elements as are 
> necessary to 
> define most or all of the specialized concepts that will be needed
> for practical applications.  
>    The SUO committee will probably have to make contentious choices
> regarding the contents of both basic SUO and any extension.  At this
> early point, I hope we can make some progress toward perhaps some 
> significant fraction of an SUO, with an embryonic "Reference extension
> containing enough defined concepts to make the SUO definitions
> clear.
>     But for the particular cases you cited above:
>  (1)  I would expect basic units of measure to be part of the 
> SUO itself.
> Many object-specific or industry-specific attributes would be more
> appropriate in an extension or a domain ontology.
>  (2) chemical processes do indeed go on in the body as well as process
> plants,
> and everywhere else in the visible universe, except extreme 
> environments 
> like neutron stars.  I would expect a "chemical_process" to 
> be part of the
> SUO, and with a reasonable definition, I wouldn't expect any 
> conflicts.  A "commercial_chemical_process" could be a subclass.
>  (3) the body as a "bag of chemicals" (or more generally a structured
> physical object as *solely* a "piece of some stuff") is 
> unrealistic and
> should not be allowed in the SUO.  Such objects have other essential
> features.
>  (4) I would expect a chemical "process plant" definition to exclude
> small-scale reactors such as laboratory vessels and animal stomachs.
> A "pilot_plant" would be an intermediate-scale case.  
> Definitions by size 
> would be fuzzy, which is why I think that for artifacts, 
> definitions by 
> intended use, with subclasses having some physical constraints,  are 
> most stable.
>   (5)  Most drugs are indeed chemical products, having a 
> specific intended
> use;
> but some are processed natural substances.  I think there is a useful 
> distinction to be made between artifactual substances and those 
> extracted from nature, and I hope that such a distinction can 
> be encoded 
> in the SUO.
