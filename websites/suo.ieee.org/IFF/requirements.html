The IFF Requirements







Figure 1: The IFF Architecture (iconic version)




This is a document on IFF requirements. As such, it involves many of the goals of the IFF development. The IFF is a descriptive category metatheory that will serve as a unified framework for the creation and maintenance of object-level ontologies. The fundamental goal in the development is to axiomatically express the IFF by following the intuitions of the working category-theorist.

The IFF architecture is a two-dimensional structure with metalevels ranging along the vertical axis and namespaces ranging along the horizontal. It is vertically partitioned into the object level at the bottom, the metashell at the top, and the vast intermediate natural part. The natural part is further divided horizontally into pure and applied aspects. The pure aspect itself is divided into core and structural components. The core component, which is encoded in the IFF Core (meta) Ontology (IFF-CORE), represents set theory. The structural component, which is encoded in the IFF Category Theory (meta) Ontology (IFF-CAT) and several others, represents category theory. The set-theoretic and category-theoretic axiomatizations constrain each other: any category is defined as a set of objects and a set of morphisms with connecting functions; whereas the collections of sets and functions (at any metalevel) form a category.

In general, the axiomatization of the IFF uses full first order expression, consisting of set, predicate (unary relation), (unary) function and (binary) relation symbols, with the usual logical connectives and quantifiers. The development of the IFF is governed overall by two design principles: conceptual warrant and categorical design. Both are limitations of the logical expression. Conceptual warrant seeks to limit the content of logical expression, by requiring us to justify any introduction of terminology (and attendent axiomatizations). These should be based upon need at lower metalevels and object level. Categorical design seeks to limit the form of logical expression to atomic expressions: declarations, equations or relational expressions.

A major goal is to require that the natural part conform to the principle of categorical design; that is, the natural part should be expressed atomically as declarations, equations and relational expressions, but mostly equations. Since functions are unary, these equations correspond to the “commuting diagrams” of category theory. The natural part is vertically divided into an infinite number of metalevels: 1 = sml, 2 = lrg, 3 = vlrg, …, n (generic), …, ∞ = ur, with level 0 being the object level.

  • The core component at the nth metalevel has a kernel namespace, which axiomatizes level n sets, predicates, (unary) functions and (binary) relations. This represents a topos (a Cartesian-closed category with subobject classifiers) denoted by Setn. As n varies, this results in the topos hierarchy 

Set1 ⊆ Set2 ⊆ Set3 ⊆ … ⊆ Setn ⊆ … ⊆ Set∞

called the IFF metastack. To satisfy categorical design, the assertion of this topos structure at any metalevel needs suitable terminology axiomatized at the next higher level in the structural component (of the pure aspect): the assertion that Set1 is a topos needs suitable terminology axiomatized in the structural component at level 2 (more generally, any large category is axiomatized at level 1, possibly in the applied aspect, but the axiomatization for the terminology of large categories occurs in the IFF-CAT meta-ontology at level 2 = lrg); the assertion that Setn is a topos needs suitable terminology axiomatized in the structural component at level n+1; and the assertion that Set∞ is a topos needs suitable terminology axiomatized in the structural component of the meta namespace (IFF-META-CORE) in the metashell (level meta = ∞+1, if you will).

  • The structural component of the nth metalevel has a basic category namespace (amongst many other structural meta-ontologies), which axiomatizes objects, morphisms and their composition. To satisfy categorical design, this assertion foundationally requires the sets, predicates, (unary) functions, (binary) relations, and finite limits at level n+1, the next higher metalevel. At the top n = ∞, the category subnamespace in the structural component of the Ur namespace in the natural part requires the sets, predicates, (unary) functions, (binary) relations, and finite limits in the meta namespace (IFF-META) in the core part of the metashell. Above the top meta = ∞+1, the category subnamespace in the structural component of the meta namespace of the metashell requires the sets, predicates, (unary) functions, (binary) relations, and finite limits in the type namespace (IFF-TYPE) in the core part of the metashell. In particular, the adjunctive style for the IFF axiomatization is initiated in the subnamespace for finite limits of type sets and type functions.

Furthermore, the vertical structuring of the IFF metastack between metalevels n and n+1 requires axiomatization for the four fundamental relations (subset, (optimal-)restriction and bridgment) at metalevel n+2. At the top ur = ∞, this means that the axiomatization for these four relations starts above the meta namespace of the IFF metashell.
