The IFF-OO Architecture

The IFF Ontology (meta) Ontology (IFF-OO) is located in the lower IFF metalevel. It contains axiomatizations for the four basic notions of first order language, first order model-theoretic structure, first order theory and first order logic. The IFF-OO represents the meta-architecture of all object-level ontologies. An object-level ontology is said to be populated when it contains not only type specification but also instance data. The IFF-OO represents unpopulated object-level ontologies with first order theories, and represents populated object-level ontologies with first order logics. The IFF-OO architecture is visualized in Figure 1 from two perspectives. As seen from both perspectives, the architecture consists of two sub-architectures, the first-order logic (FOL) architecture (in red in Figure 1) and the information flow (IF) architecture (in blue in Figure 1), connected by four embedding-entity coreflections (in green in Figure 1). The IF architecture consists of the four categories (Set, Classification, IF Theory and IF Logic) connected by five adjunctions: the free classification (model) coreflection m¢, the free theory coreflection t¢, the representation theorem adjunction w¢, the classification (model) projection reflection p¢, and the free logic coreflection l¢. As can be seen in Figure 1, the FOL architecture corresponds exactly to the IF architecture. In more detail, there are eight (4+4) categories connected through fourteen (5+5+4) adjunctions.

As witnessed by the four basic notions and the architectural congruence, the IFF-OO is a first order representation of the theory of Information Flow presented in the book Information Flow: The Logic of Distributed Systems by Jon Barwise and Jerry Seligman, and formalized in the paper The Information Flow Foundation for Conceptual Knowledge Organization. In other words, the IFF-OO integrates the metatheory of information flow as axiomatized in the IF architecture into the metatheory of first order logic as axiomatized in the FOL architecture. The FOL architecture is connected to the IF architecture through four embedding-entity coreflections, one for each basic notion (language, model, theory and logic). For any embedding-entity coreflection, the free construction embeds the information flow notion as a first order logic notion, and the underlying construction represents the entity aspect of that particular basic notion. In particular, the free model construction over classifications correlates with the free model construction over languages, which requires the axiomatic assertion that entity types (sorts) map faithfully to unary relation types (predicates), and the free theory construction over IF theories can be regarded as an embedding of taxonomic object-level ontologies as formal first-order object-level ontologies.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

There are several architectural components hidden behind the diagrams in Figure 1. First of all, closely associated with the categories Language and Model are other categorically equivalent categories. The situation is represented by the following isomorphisms (≈) and equivalences (≡):

Language ≈ Spangraph ≡ Hypergraph

Model ≈ Spangraph□ ≡ Hypergraph□,

where Spangraph□ = Classification-Spangraph is the category of classification spangraphs and

Hypergraph□ = Classification-Hypergraph is the category of classification hypergraphs. Objects and morphisms in these two categories replace sets and set functions in spangraphs and hypergraphs with classifications and infomorphisms, respectively. Figure 2 corresponds to the lower left hand parts of the two perspectives in Figure 1. As illustrated in Figure 2, three adjunctions are analogs to the free classification coreflection m¢. These three adjunctions are (1) the free model construction over languages, (2) the free classification spangraph construction over spangraphs, and (3) the free classification hypergraph construction over hypergraphs. All three correspond to the free classification construction over sets. The two dimensions of Figure 2 illustrate the IFF representation of the notion of a model-theoretic structure as a product of the notion of classification and the notion of hypergraph.

Secondly, the two categories Set© = Semipair and Set©□ = Classification-Semipair are needed as a location to formulate the correct notion of tuple for relation arguments and signatures. In particular, the notion of relation signature is represented as a natural transformation

∂ : rel è refer ◦ tuple : Language à Set© à Set

from the relation functor to the composition of the reference functor with the tuple functor, where the reference functor maps languages to set pairs (Set© objects), and the tuple functor maps set pairs to their associated tuple set.
