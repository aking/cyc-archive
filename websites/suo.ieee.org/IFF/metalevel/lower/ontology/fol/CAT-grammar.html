A Grammar for IFF FOL Languages

There are two approaches to the design of an IFF-FOL grammar, the categorical logic (CAT) approach and the assumed KIF approach (KIF). The CAT approach corresponds to the endomorphic or kernel aspect of the IFF-FOL, whereas the KIF approach corresponds to the exomorphic or shell aspect of the IFF-FOL. The CAT approach is oriented towards expression arity, where arity is an indicia (subset of variables); particularly substitution between expression arity fibers. Hence, it requires that all connectives (negations, conjunctions, disjunctions, implications and equivalences) operate on a fixed fiber. This means, for example, that all conjuncts must have the same arity. Because of this inflexibility, when variables are embedded as (elementary) terms, they must have an attached indicia that functions as the arity of the elementary term. The KIF approach is not so strongly oriented towards substitution and expression arity fibers. In fact, arity as indicia is not very explicit in traditional KIF (of course arity as natural number is explicit, but we use the term "valence" for this notion). Hence, the KIF approach is more flexible, in the sense that all connectives are unrestricted on argument arities. The arity of the result is the union of the set of argument arities. Because of this flexibility, variables can be embedded as terms using their singleton as an implicit arity.

This document describes an extension heavyweight CAT-grammar for the IFF-FOL. It is very rigorous and oriented towards categorical logic, using lisp-like functions for synthetic/constructor operations.An associated document describes a lightweight KIF-oriented grammar for the IFF-FOL. The syntax is presented in two parts. The first part called lexical syntax deals with the parsing of character streams into lexical items. The second part called grammatical syntax is the logical syntax of the IFF-FOL kernel, which assumes that lexical items have been recognized by a lexical analyzer. The grammatical syntax is divided into two parts: a subgrammar for terms and (building upon terms) a subgrammar for expressions. The main difference between the light and heavy weight grammars appears in the subgrammar for terms. The following syntax is written using Extended Backus-Naur Form (EBNF), as specified by International Standard ISO/IEC 14977. Literal characters use quotes '…', sequences of items are separated by commas …,…,…, a vertical bar | indicates alternatives, braces {…} indicate repetition, subtraction – indicates exceptions, brackets […] are used for optional items, and parentheses (…) group items together. Productions are terminated with a semicolon ;.

Lexical Syntax

Parentheses are lexical tokens that denote pairs, tuples, and subsets. They are the only delimiter (grouping device) in the IFF-FOL syntax.

open = '('
close = ')'

A character is any other ASCII non-control character, which can all be used to form lexical tokens (with some restrictions based on the first character of the lexical token). This includes all the alphanumeric characters listed here in keyboard order, where the alphabetic characters are indicated by ellipses.

nonzero-digit
 = '1' | '2' | '3' | '4' | '5' | '6' | '7' | '8' | '9'
digit = '0' | nonzero-digit ;
char = digit | '~' | '!' | '#' | '$' | '%' | '^' | '&' | '*' | '_' | '+' | '{' | '}' | '|' | ':' | '"' | '<' | '>' | '?' | '`' | '-' | '=' | '[' | ']' | '\' | ';' | ',' | '.' | '/' | 'A' | … | 'Z' | 'a' | … | 'z' ;

The natural numbers are the set of numbers {'0', '1', …}.

natural-number = '0' | ( nonzero-digit , { digit } )

A reserved-word is a lexical token that represents a logical symbol in the IFF-FOL and is used to indicate the syntactic structure of terms or expressions in the IFF-FOL languages. These must not be used as names.

variable-word
 = 'variable'
term-word
 = 'element' | 'apply' | 'singleton' | 'identity' | 'composite' | 'empty' | 'pairing'
expression-word
 =
 '=' | 'holds' | 'and' | 'or' | 'not' | 'implies' | 'iff' | 'exists' | 'forall'
reserved-word = variable-word | term-word | expression-word | 'iff:theory'   

A name is a lexical token which is not reserved.

character-sequence = ( char , { char } )
name = character-sequence – reserved-word

Non-logical symbols in the IFF-FOL include constants, variables, function symbols and relation symbols. An individual constant represents a specific individual and corresponds to a proper noun in natural languages. A variable represents a specific, but unspecified (or arbitrary) individual and corresponds to a pronoun in natural languages. A function symbol represents an operation or map, which has an input and an output. The input is provided by a list of n arguments, where n is called the valence of the function (the term arity is used for a difference, but related, notion in the IFF-FOL). A relation or predicate symbol represents a class of individuals or a relationship among individuals and corresponds to a common noun in natural languages. For each natural number n, we introduce a set of n-place relation symbols (relation symbols of valence n). Zero-place or nullary relation symbols are called "sentence symbols". They correspond to independent sentences whose internal structure does not matter. One-place or unary relation symbols correspond to linguistic items denoting properties, like "being human", "being loud", or "being a prime number". Two-place or binary relation symbols correspond to linguistic items denoting binary relations, such as "is a child of" or "is less than". Three-place or ternary relation symbols are less common directly. Examples include "x lies between y and z", etc. However, the IFF-FOL regards expressions as complex relation symbols, and the valence of expressions can be arbitrarily large. The two-place or binary equality or identity relation "=" is treated specially in the IFF-FOL – as a logical symbol. For simplicity, in some logical languages used for general discourse the only terms are constants and variables corresponding to proper nouns and pronouns. However, the logic languages used for a more mathematical discourse require the use of function symbols. These allow use of complex terms such as the constant terms "the wife of John Kerry" and "8+3", or terms containing variables, such as "((x×2)−y)" or "the brother of x". Function symbols in logical languages allow greater expressiveness, but increase formal complexity. The IFF-FOL languages include function symbols, and constants are regarded in the IFF-FOL as function symbols of valence 0.

An IFF-FOL language (lexicon, signature or vocabulary) contains variables, function and relation symbols. All three kinds of linguistic elements are names. We assume that the set of variables contains a denumerable set of canonical variables generated from the natural numbers by using the reserved word 'variable'.

canonical-variable
 =
 open , 'variable' , natural-number , close
variable = canonical-variable | name
function = name
relation = name

Core Grammatical Syntax

Terms

For both indicia and tuples, sets are used instead of sequences in the IFF-FOL axiomatics. Natural numbers and sequences have been carefully eliminated from the IFF-FOL for simplicity of the axiomatics. Implicit indicia and maps represent natural numbers and sequences used in surface syntax. An indicia-variable is a pair consisting of an indicia (the arity) and a variable. Indicia-variables represent variables as the simplest terms in the IFF-FOL. The indicia component is used to attach an arity to the variable. The semantics of indicia-variables is Cartesian product projection. In an indicia-variable, the variable is required to be a member of the indicia. Indicia-variables correspond to variable cases in the IFF-FOL axiomatics. The arity of an expression can be used to construct the arity of its included variables.

An indicia is a subset of variables. For both indicia and tuples, sets are used instead of sequences. Natural numbers and sequences have been carefully eliminated from the IFF-FOL kernel in order to recognize and axiomatize the true meaning of the logic. An additional module can be added for those who have the habit of using natural numbers and sequences.

variable-subset = { variable } ;
indicia = open , variable-subset , close ;

An elementary term is a pair consisting of a variable and an indicia (the arity of the variable). Elementary terms represent variables as the simplest terms. The elementary term is used to attach an arity to the variable. The semantics of elementary terms is Cartesian product projection. In an elementary term, the variable is required to be a member of the indicia.

elementary-term = open , 'element' , variable , indicia , close ;

Function symbols can be "applied" to tuples to form composite terms. This is an elementary form of substitution. A function symbol can be applied to tuple of terms, and a composite term formed, when the arity of the function symbol is the same as the index of the tuple.

composite-term = open , 'apply' , function , tuple , close ;

A term is either an elementary term or a composite term. Nothing else is a term. This qualification is ensured practically and theoretically. Practically it is ensured by the abstract syntax of terms; in particular, the axioms that assert the inverse-ness between the synthetic/constructor term operations and the analytic/selector term operations. Theoretically, it is ensured by the term fixpoint semantics.

term = elementary-term | composite-term ;

A tuple is a map from an indicia, its index, to the set of terms of a particular arity, its arity. Both terms and atomic expressions use the notion of a tuple of terms, which represents a vector of arguments to a function or relation, respectively. There are operations in the IFF-FOL axiomatics for describing several ways to construct tuples: either from single term, or as the identity tuple for a particular index and arity, or from a composable pair of tuples, or as the empty tuple for a particular arity, or from a tuplable pair of tuples.

Terms are tuples. An indexed-term is a pair consisting of a variable and a term. There are no constraints between the variable component and the term component. An indexed-term becomes a singleton tuple, whose index is the singleton of the variable component of the indexed-term and whose arity is the arity of the term component of the indexed-term.

singleton-tuple = open , 'singleton' , variable , term , close ;

For any indicia, there is an identity tuple with both index and arity equal to that indicia. A pair of tuples is composable when the arity of the first is equal to the index of the second. Any composable pair defines a composite tuple, whose index is the index of the first and whose arity is the arity of the second.

identity-tuple = open , 'identity' , indicia , close
composite-tuple
 =
 open , 'composite' , tuple , tuple , close

For any indicia, there is an empty tuple with empty index and with arity equal to that indicia. A pair of tuples is tuplable when they share a common arity. Any tuplable pair defines a pairing tuple, whose index is (in one-one correspondence with) the disjoint union of indices and whose arity is the common arity.

empty-tuple = open , 'empty' , indicia , close
pairing-tuple
 =
 open , 'pairing' , tuple , tuple , close

A tuple is either a singleton tuple, an identity tuple, a composite tuple, an empty tuple, or pairing tuple.

tuple = singleton-tuple | identity-tuple | composite-tuple | empty-tuple | pairing-tuple ;

Expressions

An expression or formula is a linguistic item that represents a logical proposition and corresponds to a declarative sentence in natural language. An equation or equational expression is used to assert the equality of two terms. The two terms involved must have the same arity.

equation = open , '=' , term , term , close ;

An atom or atomic-expression asserts that a relation "holds" for a tuple of terms. Hence, an atom consists of two components, a relation symbol and a tuple, where the arity of the relation symbol is equal to the index of the tuple. Relations can be embedded as atoms with the identity tuple.

atom = open , 'holds' , relation , tuple , close ;

A conjunction is a subset of expressions labeled by the conjunction symbol. The conjunction of any subset of expressions is a conjunctive expression. A disjunction is a subset of expressions labeled by the disjunction symbol. The disjunction of any subset of expressions is a disjunctive expression. In the base IFF-FOL axiomatics, conjunctions and disjunction can be applied to subsets of expressions with non-matching arities. In an extension IFF-FOL axiomatics, conjunctions and disjunction are restricted to subsets of expressions with common arity.

expression-subset = { expression } ;
conjunction-disjunction = open , ( 'and' | 'or' ) , expression-subset , close ;

A negation is an expression labeled by the negation symbol. The negation of any expression is a negative expression.

negation = open , 'not' , expression , close ;

An implication is a pair of expressions labeled by the implication symbol. The implication of any pair of expressions is an implicative expression. An equivalence is a pair of expressions labeled by the equivalence symbol. The equivalence of any pair of expressions is an equivalence expression.

expression-pair = expression , expression ;
implication-equivalence = open , ( 'implies' | 'iff' ) , expression-pair , close ;

A (existential) quantification or a (existentially) quantified expression is an expression-case labeled with the (existential) quantification symbol. A (universal) quantification or an (universally) quantified expression is an expression-case labeled with the (universal) quantification symbol. An expression-case is a pair consisting of a variable and expression. In an expression-case, the variable is required to be a member of the arity of the expression. Structurally, it would have been better to place the closing parenthesis after the expression; but we have switched these two items in order to be closer to traditional KIF.

expression-case = open , variable , close , expression ;
quantification = open , ( 'exists' | 'forall' ) , expression-case , close ;

An expression is either an equation, an atom, a conjunction, a disjunction, a negation, an implication, an equivalence, an (existential) quantification, or a (universal) quantification. Nothing else is an expression. This qualification is ensured practically and theoretically. Practically it is ensured by the abstract syntax of expressions; in particular, the axioms that assert the inverse-ness between the synthetic/constructor expression operations and the analytic/selector expression operations. Theoretically, it is ensured by the expression fixpoint semantics.

expression = equation | atom | conjunction-disjunction | negation | implication-equivalence | quantification ;

An IFF theory is a named subset of expressions.

theory = open , 'iff:theory' , name , expression-subset , close ;

Example

The same logical expression can be rendered in a variety of different syntactic external surface forms: connectives can be written as infix, postfix or prefix, groupings indicated by brackets, groups of dots or graphically by enclosing circles, etc. For example, consider the simple logical expression "all boys have been kissed by some girls" taken from the SCL standard. This can be written in the following surface forms. The IFF-FOL KIF grammar core form is almost identical to the SCL core form (they differ in their kernel forms by a quantifier exchange, since they use different quantifiers as primitive). The IFF-FOL KIF grammar kernel form is close to the existential graph form, since they both use the same primitive operations: conjunction (implicit in EGs), negation and existential quantification. The parentheses in the EG form indicate a contour of negation. Conjunctions and existential quantifications are implicit in EGs.

[Traditional logical form:]

(∀x)(Boy(x) → (∃y)(Girl(y) & Kissed(x, y)))

[Conceptual Graph interchange form:]

[@every *x] 
    [If: (Boy ?x) 
         [Then: [*y] 
             (Girl ?y) (Kissed ?x ?y) ]]

[SCL core form:]

(forall (x) 
    (implies (Boy x) 
             (exists (y) 
                 (and (Girl y) (Kissed x y)))))

[SCL kernel form:]

(forall (x) 
    (not (and (Boy x) 
              (forall (y) 
                  (not (and (Girl y) (Kissed x y)))))))

[IFF-FOL CAT grammar core form:]

(forall (x)
    (implies (holds Boy (singleton (variable 0) (element x (x))))
             (exists (y) 
                 (and (holds Girl (singleton (variable 0) (element y (y)))) 
                      (holds Kissed (pairing (singleton (variable 0) (element x (x y)))
                                             (singleton (variable 1) (element y (x y)))))))))

[IFF-FOL CAT grammar kernel form:]

(not (exists (x)
         (and (holds Boy (singleton (variable 0) (element x (x))))
              (not (exists (y) 
                       (and (holds Girl (singleton (variable 0) (element y (y)))) 
                            (holds Kissed (pairing (singleton (variable 0) (element x (x y)))
                                                   (singleton (variable 1) (element y (x y)))))))))))

[IFF-FOL KIF grammar core form:]

(forall (x)
    (implies Boy(x)
             (exists (y) 
                 (and Girl(y) Kissed(x y)))))

[IFF-FOL KIF grammar kernel form:]

(not (exists (x)
         (and Boy(x)
              (not (exists (y) 
                       (and Girl(y) Kissed(x y)))))))

[Existential Graph form:]

( Boy[x] 
    ( Girl[y] Kissed[x y] ) )

Arity is one of the notions that separate the lightweight version of the IFF-FOL grammar from the heavyweight version. The arity of any basic linguistic element (function symbol, relation symbol, term or tuple) is the indicia (variable subset) of n free variables that serve as the locations or indices for the arguments of that element, where n is its valence. We have demonstrated that arity be defined and constructed in the lightweight version of the IFF-FOL grammar. We expect it to be constructible in the heavyweight version of the IFF-FOL grammar, since that is one of the core concepts of the IFF-FOL, and the purpose of the heavyweight version is to make these concepts explicit. To demonstrate, let us analyze and construct the arity for the IFF-FOL KIF grammar kernel form. A term is either elementary or composite. The arity of an elementary term is its indicia component. The arity of a composite term is the arity of its tuple component. A tuple is a singleton tuple, an identity tuple, a composite tuple, an empty tuple or a pairing tuple. The arity of a singleton tuple is the arity of its component term. The arity of an identity tuple is its indicia. The arity of a composite tuple is the arity of its second tuple component. The arity of an empty tuple is its indicia. The arity of a pairing tuple is the common arity of its two component tuples. An expression is an equation, an atom, a conjunction, a negation or an (existential) quantification. The arity of an atom is the arity of its tuple component. The arity of a conjunction is the union of the arities of the conjunct expressions (this changes to the common arity in the pure categorical logic extension of the IFF-FOL). The arity of a negation is the arity of the expression being negated. Quantification subtracts the quantified variable from the arity of the expression being quantified.
