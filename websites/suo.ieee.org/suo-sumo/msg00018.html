> axiom.  In fact, that's precisely what we've been doing all these years
> working on SUMO.  We make lots of changes, and then propose a new version
> to the list to vote on as the new baseline.  Plenty of SUMO users,
> including folks like Philippe Martin have altered SUMO by changing class
> links and, in his case, removing all the axioms.

Both these last two statements are false. Regarding the "links", I have
certainly added generalizing and specializing classes to SUMO, and even in
between, but all the SUMO links are kept (directly or not): by filtering out
all the categories that are not from SUMO, I can regenerate the portions
of SUMO that I have integrated.
This is a work in progress: only a portion of SUMO has so far been
represented and, for this portion, currently some of the axioms have not
been represented. Much more is to come.



> He should probably be clearer that his work is simply derived from
> merging portions SUMO

I have often stated that I have just begun the integration of SUMO.
That SUMO is just one of several other ontologies, has been made clear too.



> and explain more fully how it was merged and amended, but surely there's no
> reason he should be legally prevented from doing so, now or in the
> future.  He can't, and shouldn't claim conformance.

I have not amended the parts of SUMO I have integrated and will not do so
in the future. Whether I'll integrate it all will depend on many factors
(time, usefulness for my goals, ...). The categories that I include can be
seen as "references" to their counterparts in the original SUMO. If I make
mistakes when linking them to other categories, it means that I have
misinterpreted their semantics (or the semantics of the other categories
if they are not mine), and if so, (i) other people can make the same
mistakes, (ii) the correction of such mistakes (e.g. by one of the creators
of the SUMO) will help its understanding. More generally, the links I add
help retrieval, understanding and re-use.


>  I think his effort is misguided, but he should be legally free to "build
> a better mousetrap" if he thinks he can.

"mousetrap" I do not know but in my opinion, better (in the sense of more
content, more structured, better accessible and according to complementary
lexical and structural conventions), yes indeed.


Philippe
