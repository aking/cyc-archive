Title:
Jim et al,

I  don't want to be obstructionist. I entirely approve of people downloading, using, and modifying SUMO. But not to infringe on IEEE's copyright, nor to confuse anybody that such a changed version is official. So I'll take what may be an unpopular position on this.  Having non-lawyers write or rewrite a license (or any legal document) is asking for trouble.  Potentially a lot of trouble, somewhere down the line. I've seen this happen in the software business and I have seen it happen in my involvement in a publishing business.  We can all come up with something we understand and like, but which would not hold up in court, which could lose IEEE its copyright, and have other unpleasant consequences.  Software, and especially ontology, needs to be dealt with carefully.  This project, and thus ownership of copyright, is more complicated and involves more people than the typical literary works copyright law was originally written to cover. 
 
I am admittedly not a lawyer.  However, judging from my experience, I have these comments:

Granting "use" of SUMO is legally too vague.  A license should be precise and clear as to what rights are granted. If we want to grant rights to modify SUMO, we need to say so explicitly.  Likewise, any rights to transfer the modified version to another party, for profit or otherwise.

If IEEE is to retain the copyright to SUMO, no company, organization, individual, or anyone whatever other than IEEE should put their own copyright notice on it. Somebody will make changes and assume they can do that.  The license should say that the copyright to any modifications made by anyone is automatically transferred to IEEE, and require whoever modifies SUMO to put the IEEE copyright notice on their modified version. Not their own notice, or that of whoever they think the copyright should belong to. We do not want some corporation to claim SUMO as their own. 

The issue of who gets credit for changes/improvements is legally separate from copyright.  If somebody downloads SUMO, changes it, and releases/publishes it,  we might want to specify a simple notice for them to include.  Something like "This ontology is derived by <whoever> from the IEEE SUMO version XX."  Again, the licensing agreement should specify this.

As I said above, I strongly believe the final license should be drafted by a lawyer expert in intellectual property law. We can ask them to be terse and non-Draconian about it.  I agree it would be frustrating to wait months for IEEE's lawyers to do it. I also believe the legal problems that could easily be caused by a vague or legally invalid license agreement would be far more frustrating. 

I think rewriting this license agreement would actually take only an hour or so of an intellectual property lawyer's time. Also that only a few sentences need be added.   I propose that we find such a  lawyer, get him or her to rewrite the license, and present the result to IEEE for approval.  I know one lawyer who _may_ be willing to take on this project.  If not, there are others available.  If we already plan to replace IEEE's license with one the group prefers and let IEEE review it in their own time frame, lets at least use a legally grounded one.

Allan
