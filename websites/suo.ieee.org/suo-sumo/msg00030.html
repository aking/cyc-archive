Title:
Adam
  I am confused about this.  Looking in CVS, you are right that the v1.52 put to vote did not have a license.  In the next version it looks like you asked Ian to insert a Teknowledge copyright and GNU license.  I found that in the current file when you left about a month and a few versions later and I took over.  I maintained it despite not being fond of GNU because I figured this had all been thought through and didn't want to get into that whole complex of issues.  Also because I knew it was your policy to put GNU into everything else.
   I got into that precise mess of legal issues partly because there were SUMO users who complained they could not use SUMO with GNU and partly because Jim decided it was time to assert IEEE's rights.  Licensing of ontologies turns out to be legally unsettled, I'd suggest we proceed conservatively.  Meaning, if IEEE cares more about the document once it became a starter doc, we should not lightly remove their license. I don't think we need to get an entire new IEEE license written, which might take months.  We might be able to get a clarification of the current license that satisfies us, and a clause for those who make derivative versions to include in their file that makes the legal status of the version explicit.  That might not take nearly as long.
Allan

Adam Pease wrote:
Allan,
  We should clarify that the "old GNU/Teknowledge" license did not exist on prior versions.  I agree that the GNU license was not appropriate.  The versions that I released when I was running the project did not have the "old GNU/Teknowledge" license, nor the new IEEE one.  My understanding was that copyright belonged to IEEE and no further license was necessary.  All versions that were submitted for a vote by the SUO list did not have a license embedded.

Adam

At 04:22 PM 6/16/2004 -0700, Allan Terry wrote:
Adam
   I defer to the Chair on that.  He requested that the old GNU/Teknowledge license be replaced with the IEEE license.  That is legally the correct stance, so I made the change.  It should carry some license rather than no license.  Releasing an unprotected copy allows people to do any of the things IEEE is trying to protect against.
   Allan

Adam Pease wrote:
Allan,
  I agree that working with the latest draft is best, but that's only
possible once it's clear that anyone can alter and use the latest draft
(although not claim conformance).  I'd suggest removing the new IEEE
license, to restore SUMO to the clearer status it once had in version
1.52.  We could work out a different license with IEEE, but, from past
experience, working with them can take a long time.  To clarify the
situation in the meantime, simply removing the license text would be
simplest I think.

Adam
