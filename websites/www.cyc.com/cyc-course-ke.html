Cycorp Course Description

Fundamentals of Knowledge Engineering with Cyc

Roles, Events, and Actors

Wednesday 9:00-9:15 -- Lecture

The purpose of this talk is to acquaint the attendee with the way different kinds of participants in situations are represented in Cyc. After introducing some examples of the many ways things can participate in situations, we introduce the representation system used in Cyc. We go over the distinction between #$actors and #$subEvents, show how #$Roles are implemented as relations between #$Situations and participants, and show how #$Roles are inferentially related to each other and to other predicates via the #$genlPreds hierarchy.


Representing Roles in Events

Wednesday 9:15-9:50 -- Exercise


Predicates and Functions

Wednesday 10:00-11:00 -- Lecture

In this section we review the basics of denotational functions in Cyc. Topics covered include non-atomic terms (NATs), arity, argument types, result types, system use of #$termOfUnit, and browsing NATs in the KB. We look at some examples of proper and improper function use. We also look at some of the specializations of #$Function-Denotational, such as #$IndividualDenotingFunction, #$CollectionDenotingFunction, and #$ReifiableFunction.


Attributes

Wednesday 11:00-11:30 -- Lecture

What are Attributes? They are intangible, individual values that are used to describe properties, qualities or characteristics of some object. #$GreenColor is an #$AttributeValue. #$Married and #$Single are #$AttributeValues. "Five inches" is also an #$AttributeValue. This lecture discusses the main types of attributes, how they are arranged into hierarchies, and how they can be ordered. Because measurable quantities are represented as attributes, an important part of this lecture concerns units of measure, measurable quantities, and comparisons of quantities.


Microtheories

Wednesday 11:30-12:00 -- Lecture

This lecture provides an overview of microtheories as they are currently (early 1999) implemented and used in the Cyc KB. Questions addressed include the following: What are microtheories? Why have them at all? What are some common types of microtheories in Cyc? What can we say about microtheories in CycL? How are microtheories used in inferencing? The rationale for covering this material at all, in light of the changes to our implementation of microtheories anticipated for this year, is that most of the people attending the course will probably soon be using a Cyc System that incorporates our current implementation. The future implementation of microtheories, in which they will be treated as specified locations in an n-dimensional vector space, will be covered in Doug's lecture on Day 4 of the course: Day 4, Friday, 4/9/99 Microtheories: Dimensions of Context Space, Fri 9:00-10:00


Rule Macro Predicates

Wednesday 1:00-1:30 -- Lecture

#$RuleMacroPredicates are predicates that have, implicit in their definition, the schema for a class of quantified formulas. A ground formula using a #$RuleMacroPredicate actually represents a more complex, quantified formula. This lecture presents some examples of RuleMacroPredicate, and discusses several ways RuleMacroPredicates can be implemented. By "implemented", we mean have the system behave so that the addition of a RuleMacroPredicate ground formula results in the conclusions that would be entailed by the more complicated formula the RuleMacroPredicate ground formula is shorthand for.


Time and Dates

Wednesday 1:30-2:15 -- Lecture

Representing and reasoning about time and dates remains one of the most challenging aspects of Cyc-based reasoning; it is also an area where some very significant KR issues are still in the process of being resolved, involving some very significant assumptions about Cyc collections and their extensions.

This section will look at the major specializations of the Cyc collection TemporalThing, at the definitions and taxonomization of the PrimitiveTemporalRelations and ComplexTemporalRelations, and at the issues surrounding temporal qualification of Cyc assertions, with particular focus being given to the entity/subabstraction distinction, temporal qualification predicates such as 'holds in', and the question of whether or not Collections in Cyc have mutable extensions. Some time will also be spent in discussing potential ramifications of the impending microtheory cleanup and implementation of context-Space-based reasoning for temporal qualification in Cyc.

Tips for Writing Efficient CycL

Wednesday 3:15-3:45 -- Lecture

CycL is a complex and featureful representation languge with multiple ways available for expressing a piece of knowledge. However, different CycL representations can differ dramatically in either their ability to be efficiently used by the inference engine or their ability to be used, understood and extended by human editors. The purpose of this talk is to provide concrete suggestions for how to write efficient CycL which will make maximal use of this power and avoid the most common pitfalls.


Mistakes to Avoid in Knowledge Entry

Wednesday 3:45-4:15 -- Lecture


KE Methodology

Wednesday 4:15-4:45 -- Lecture

This section consists of the review of a number of methods for entering knowledge into the Cyc Knowledge Base. The advantages and disadvantages of different approaches and procedures will be discussed along with the history and development of the Cyc system of knowledge acquisition.

Particular attention will be given to the balances that has been achieved between expressivity and tractability, efficiency and completeness, and application-focused and use-independent knowledge. Also included is a presentation of testing procedures and data.
