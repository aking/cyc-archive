Cycorp Course Description

Introduction to the Cyc Knowledge Server

Cyc Overview and Historical Introduction

Lecture


CycL Syntax

Lecture

The "CycL Syntax" talk will detail the rules for constructing terms, basic formulas, sentences and complex assertions in CycL. CycL is a formal language that is modelled after the language of first-order predicate calculus. It is, however, a far more expressive language, and consequently it is a more complex language. Although the syntax of CycL is very much like that of first-order logic, it differs in some important ways and even those who are well-versed in the syntax of first-order logic will benefit from this discussion. For those who have no training in formal logic, this talk will provide the necessary foundation for becoming proficient in the language of CycL.


Ontology Basics: Collections and Individuals: using #$isa and #$genls in CycL

Lecture

In this section we introduce two of the most basic (and powerful) distinctions in Cyc. First we look at the distinction between collections and individuals. Topics covered include some defining features of each, examples, how collections differ from sets, and the importance of distinguishing the collection level from the individual level in writing CycL. Second we look at the predicates #$isa and #$genls. Topics covered include the meaning and use of these predicates (including examples), the distinction between them, argument types, and transitivity.



CycL Translations, Basic Assertions

Exercise


Interface Overview and Browsing the KB

Lecture

In this section students will be introduced to the Cyc Interface: the point of contact between Cyc and the new and future Cyclists (Cyc users). Points covered will be finding constants, reading assertions, locating a constant within Cyc's ontological hierarchy, and using basic tools to ask questions and to edit the knowledge base. In addition, students will log on and off, view transcripts of their local operations, and find out how to erase transcripts they do not intend to send.


Browsing, Finding Assertions, and Editing the KB

Exercise


Major KB Partitions and The Stuff/Object Distinction

Lecture

This lecture is an introduction to the top-level distinctions in the Cyc ontology. The talk will cover three pairs of collections, each of which partition #$Thing, the topmost collection in Cyc: (1) #$SetOrCollection vs. #$Individual; (2) #$Intangible vs. #$PartiallyTangible; (3) #$TangibleThing vs. #$PartiallyIntangible. We will also discuss some partitions that Cyc doesn't have, to illustrate good vs. bad criteria for reifying collections.

This lecture also deals with the distinction between StuffType and ObjectType collections. We will talk about spatial stuff/object properties and temporal stuff/object properties. Issues of granularity will also be discussed. In addition to outlining the distinction between Stuff-like properties and Object-like properties, this lecture will give students in-depth exposure to the concept of type collections.

Fundamentals of Knowledge Engineering with Cyc

Events, Predicates, and Roles

Lecture

The purpose of this talk is to acquaint the attendee with the way different kinds of participants in situations are represented in Cyc. After introducing some examples of the many ways things can participate in situations, we introduce the representation system used in Cyc. We go over the distinction between #$actors and #$subEvents, show how #$Roles are implemented as relations between #$Situations and participants, and show how #$Roles are inferentially related to each other and to other predicates via the #$genlPreds hierarchy.


Representing Roles in Events

Exercise


Predicates and Functions

Lecture

In this section we review the basics of denotational functions in Cyc. Topics covered include non-atomic terms (NATs), arity, argument types, result types, system use of #$termOfUnit, and browsing NATs in the KB. We look at some examples of proper and improper function use. We also look at some of the specializations of #$Function-Denotational, such as #$IndividualDenotingFunction, #$CollectionDenotingFunction, and #$ReifiableFunction.


Attributes

Lecture

What are Attributes? They are intangible, individual values that are used to describe properties, qualities or characteristics of some object. #$GreenColor is an #$AttributeValue. #$Married and #$Single are #$AttributeValues. "Five inches" is also an #$AttributeValue. This lecture discusses the main types of attributes, how they are arranged into hierarchies, and how they can be ordered. Because measurable quantities are represented as attributes, an important part of this lecture concerns units of measure, measurable quantities, and comparisons of quantities.


KB Content: The Representation of "In"

Lecture

This lecture provides an overview of the many ways distinct "contained in" relations are represented in Cyc. For example, there is an ontological difference between what it is to be "contained in" something as an ingredient, and what it is to be "contained in" a package. And there's a difference between the way one is "in" when one is "in" clothing and the way one is "in" when one is "in" a swimming pool. Cyc contains dozens of specific predicates to capture the logical features of many different containment relations. This lecture provides an excellent example of just how carefully distinctions are drawn when developing Cyc knowledge base content.


The Representation of Literary, Audio, and Visual Works

Lecture

This talk provides an overview of Cyc's ontology of abstract works such as novels, movies and television series. Various difficulties arise in representing these works that do not occur when representing such concrete works as paintings or buildings. This area of the ontology was recently revised when our older, simpler, techniques proved inadequate to support various types of common sense inferences. The talk describes an example of how Cyc's ontology develops in response to the need to improve its ability to reason about objects in a particular domain.


Microtheories

Lecture

This lecture provides an overview of microtheories as they are currently (early 2000) implemented and used in the Cyc KB. Questions addressed include the following:

  • What are microtheories?
  • Why have them at all?
  • What are some common types of microtheories in Cyc?
  • What can we say about microtheories in CycL?
  • How are microtheories used in inferencing?
The rationale for covering this material at all, in light of the changes to our implementation of microtheories anticipated for this year, is that most of the people attending the course will probably soon be using a Cyc System that incorporates our current implementation.


Rule Macro Predicates

Lecture

#$RuleMacroPredicates are predicates that have, implicit in their definition, the schema for a class of quantified formulas. A ground formula using a #$RuleMacroPredicate actually represents a more complex, quantified formula. This lecture presents some examples of #$RuleMacroPredicate, and discusses several ways #$RuleMacroPredicates can be implemented. By "implemented", we mean have the system behave so that the addition of a #$RuleMacroPredicate ground formula results in the conclusions that would be entailed by the more complicated formula the #$RuleMacroPredicate ground formula is shorthand for.



Time and Dates

Lecture

Representing and reasoning about time and dates remains one of the most challenging aspects of Cyc-based reasoning; it is also an area where some very significant KR issues are still in the process of being resolved, involving some very significant assumptions about Cyc collections and their extensions.

This section will look at the major specializations of the Cyc collection TemporalThing, at the definitions and taxonomization of the PrimitiveTemporalRelations and ComplexTemporalRelations, and at the issues surrounding temporal qualification of Cyc assertions, with particular focus being given to the entity/subabstraction distinction, temporal qualification predicates such as 'holds in', and the question of whether or not Collections in Cyc have mutable extensions. Some time will also be spent in discussing potential ramifications of the impending microtheory cleanup and implementation of context-Space-based reasoning for temporal qualification in Cyc.


Tips for Writing Efficient CycL

Lecture

CycL is a complex and featureful representation language with multiple ways available for expressing a piece of knowledge. However, different CycL representations can differ dramatically in either their ability to be efficiently used by the inference engine or their ability to be used, understood and extended by human editors. The purpose of this talk is to provide concrete suggestions for how to write efficient CycL which will make maximal use of this power and avoid the most common pitfalls.



Mistakes to Avoid in Knowledge Entry

Lecture

This lecture exposes prospective ontological engineers to the kinds of knowledge entry mistakes commonly made by people new to the Cyc system. Some error-types focused on include the tendency to develop non-modular representations, the tendency to develop overly specific (hence non-use-neutral) representations, and the tendency to be overly vague.



The Cyc HPSG Parser (with demo)

Lecture and Demonstration

Conversion of English input into CycL is performed a number of different ways by a number of different tools. One of the latest of these is the Head-driven Phrase Structure Grammar English-to-CycL parsing system. This system is based on a principled English lexicon and grammar, and uses a unification chart parser to construct structures that map an input string onto a syntactic and semantic representation. A post-processing module handles tense, quantification, modals, etc., and presents the output in a variety of useful formats. The system will be described and a short demonstration given.



Rapid Knowledge Formation Tools

Lecture and Demonstration

Very few human beings are able to learn to perform the knowledge engineering (KEing) task well. A skilled knowledge engineer can spend time with a subject matter expert (SME) and rapidly extract a useful model of a domain. Studying what good knowledge engineers (KE's) do when they spend time with SME's shows them engaging in back-and-forth clarification dialogue, building up models by analogy, sketching, discussing real and hypothetical cases of interest, etc. These are all very knowledge-rich tasks. At Cycorp, we are building tools to replace the human KE in the knowledge acquisition process, in order for SME's to be able to quickly build large knowledge based systems themselves. This talk is an introduction to Cycorp's knowledge-rich KEing power tools.



The Cyc Canonicalizer: System Internals & Finding Assertions

Lecture

This talk will introduce the audience to the Cyc canonicalizer. The canonicalizer is one of the subsystems that comprise the Cyc inference engine. Its mission is to translate formulas from the conventional notation of first-order logic into canonical forms that are used by the Cyc inference engine. This target audience of this talk includes all potential users of, and contributors to, the Cyc knowledge base. The goals of this talk include:

  • explaining why and when and how formulas are canonicalized
  • identifying how the representation of formulas may be changed by the canonicalizer, and explainng how users can find formulas they have just entered in spite of these changes
  • emphasizing the dependence of canonicalization on the Cyc knowledge base



Cyc as a Knowledge Server

Lecture

Being related to large databases, Cyc as a large knowledge base must solve issues of knowledge replication and distribution. Furthermore, in order to permit collaboration both within the company and with VARs, Cyc must provide support for excising and rejoining knowledge. This lecture covers the transcript server, Cyc's knowledge replication mechanism, and the various knowledge formats for ontology excising and rejoining, such as transcripts, KE files, worlds and partitions.



Inferencing In Cyc

Lecture

The Cyc inference engine handles modus ponens and modus tollens (contrapositive) inferencing, universal and existential quantification, and mathematical inferencing. The Cyc knowledge base contains hundreds of thousands of assertions. Many approaches commonly taken by other inference engines just don't scale well to KBs of this size. As a result, the Cyc team has been forced to develop other techniques. This session is an introduction to those techniques.



HL Modules & KB Indexing

Lecture

Cyc is a large knowledge base with tens of thousands of constants interrelated by hundreds of thousands of assertions and logical deductions. The purpose of this talk is elaborate on two important mechanisms used to manage this complexity efficiently -- HL modules and KB indexing.

HL modules are the hundreds of independent code modules which perform inference in Cyc. Each module is designed to perform one specialized form of inferencing as efficiently as possible. The talk will introduce the user to HL modules, explain the motivation for them, and describe how they interact with inference and truth maintenance in Cyc.

KB indexing allows for subsets of the knowledge base with an important common property to be efficiently identified and accessed. The talk will describe the kinds of indexing currently used in Cyc and the interaction of indexing with knowledge representation and inference.



The Inference Tree Examiner

Lecture

Backward inferencing in Cyc can be considered as a search through a tree, where the nodes in the tree are CycL assertions for which bindings are sought and links between nodes are assertions in the Cyc knowledge base that help provide bindings for the nodes. The inference tree examiner is a tool that provides a hyper-linked text-based inference tree representation. It is extremely useful for debugging inferences by finding points of failure in seemingly promising paths through the tree.



Overview of Cyc NL

Lecture

This talk will introduce natural language processing using the Cyc system. Both understanding (NLU) and generation (NLG) will be covered, with the focus being on NLU. Each component of the system (lexicon, parser, grammar rules, semantic combination rules) will be explained, and we will work through a couple of examples that show how the Cyc knowledge base and inference engine are used to do natural language disambiguation.



The Cyc Parser

Lecture

Conversion of English input into CycL is performed a number of different ways by a number of different tools. One of the latest of these is the Head-driven Phrase Structure Grammar (HPSG) English-to-CycL parsing system. This system is based on a principled English lexicon and grammar, and uses a unification chart parser to construct structures that map an input string onto a syntactic and semantic representation. A post-processing module handles tense, quantification, modals, etc., and presents the output in a variety of useful formats. The system will be described and a short demonstration given.



Rapid Knowledge Formation and Natural Language

Lecture

This talk will describe how the Cyc Natural Language tools are utilized by the Rapid Knowledge Formation (RKF) system, both as they are presently implemented and as they are envisioned. We will start with an explanation and demonstration of the existing prototype system that will serve as the basis for the enhanced NL system that is envisioned for RKF. Following that, we will discuss of some of the limitations of the existing system, as well as our plans to address these problems. This will also include a discussion of how the various NL systems will work among themselves, as well as with the rest of the RKF system.



An HL Module That Uses the Web

Demonstration

This session demonstrates how Cyc can use information in a data source external to the Cyc knowledge base. For example, the evolving information on an external web site can provide bindings for variables in a Cyc-driven inference.



The Cyc API and Toolkits

Lecture -- Programmer Track

This session describes the Cyc Application Programming Interface (API), which is the external interface to Cyc. It explores the API protocols involved, and demonstrates their usage in toolkits, as well as the motivation for using them. Of interest to everyone, this session is especially valuable for Cyc integrators and for Cyc application developers.
