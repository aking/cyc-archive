Cycorp Course Description

Introduction to the Cyc Knowledge Server


Cyc Overview and Historical Introduction

Tuesday 9:00-10:00 -- Lecture


CycL Syntax

Tuesday 10:30-11:30 -- Lecture

The "CycL Syntax" talk will detail the rules for constructing terms, basic formulas, sentences and complex assertions in CycL. CycL is a formal language that is modelled after the language of first-order predicate calculus. It is, however, a far more expressive language, and consequently, it is a more complex language. Although the syntax of cycL is very much like that of first-order logic, it differs in some important ways and even those who are well-versed in the syntax of first-order logic will benefit from this discussion. For those ho have no training in formal logic, this talk will provide the necessary foundation for becoming proficient in the language of CycL.


Ontology Basics: isa/genls, Collection/Individual

Tuesday 11:30-12:15 -- Lecture

In this section we introduce two of the most basic (and powerful) distinctions in Cyc. First we look at the distinction between collections and individuals. Topics covered include some defining features of each, examples, how collections differ from sets, and the importance of distinguishing the collection level from the individual level in writing CycL. Second we look at the predicates #$isa and #$genls. Topics covered include the meaning and use of these predicates (including examples), the distinction between them, argument types, and transitivity.


Captioned Image Retrieval

Tuesday 1:15-1:45 -- Demonstration

In this demonstration, you will see one of the practical applications to which Cyc can be put. While most data retrieval systems can only retrieve items that are an exact syntactic match to the search string input, Cyc can draw inferences from that input, and return not only the syntactic matches but semantic matches as well. For example, since Cyc knows that children playing are generally happy, and Cyc knows that children are people, a request to Cyc for images of happy people will return an image with the caption "Children playing in the park."


CycL Translations, Basic Assertions

Tuesday 1:45-2:15 -- Exercise


Interface Overview and Browsing the KB

Tuesday 2:30-3:30 -- Lecture

In this section students will be introduced to the Cyc Interface: the point of contact between Cyc and the new and future Cyclists (Cyc users). Points covered will be finding constants, reading assertions, locating a constant within Cyc's ontological hierarchy, and using basic tools to ask questions and to edit the knowledge base. In addition, students will log on and off, view transcripts of their local operations, and find out how to erase transcripts they do not intend to send.


Browsing, Finding Assertions, and Editing the KB

Tuesday 3:30-4:30 -- Exercise


Cyc Ontology Basics

Tuesday 4:30-5:15 -- Lecture

This lecture is an introduction to the top-level distinctions in the Cyc ontology. The talk will cover three pairs of collections, each of which partition #$Thing, the topmost collection in Cyc: (1) #$SetOrCollection vs. #$Individual; (2) #$Intangible vs. #$PartiallyTangible; (3) #$TangibleThing vs. #$PartiallyIntangible. We will also discuss some partitions that Cyc doesn't have, to illustrate good vs. bad criteria for reifying collections.

This lecture also deals with the distinction between StuffType and ObjectType collections. We will talk about spatial stuff/object properties and temporal stuff/object properties. Issues of granularity will also be discussed. In addition to outlining the distinction between Stuff-like properties and Object-like properties, this lecture will give students in-depth exposure to the concept of type collections.
