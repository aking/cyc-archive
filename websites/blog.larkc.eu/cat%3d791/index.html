Easier plug-ins with Java 7

Monday, February 15th, 2010

(by Michael Witbrock, Cycorp Europe)

Image via Wikipedia

Image via Wikipedia

The LarKC architecture separates the platform, which provides services to support large scale inference, from the plug-ins, which implement novel methods of inference, data identification, data selection, transformation and work flow control. But maintaining this clean separation has been a little awkward, since one has to do a little bit of work to register new plug-ins with the platform or de-register them. This process looks set to get easier with Java 7, whose new watch service will make it easy to detect file system events, independent of OS.  Registering a plug-in should be as easy as dropping its JAR in a directory; De-Registering as easy as moving the JAR elsewhere.

A new concept of Where

(by Daniele Dell’Aglio)

The Where 2.0 Conference - which is an event devoted to the geospatial Web - is taking place in these days in California.

As reported by ReadWriteWeb, during the conference Yahoo! announced a new product called Placemaker. Placemaker is an open API to analyze structured or unstructured text in several languages (currently English, French, Italian, German and Spanish are supported) and finds associations to geographic information. Another interesting part of the Yahoo! Geo Techologies is GeoPlanet, which is an initiative similar to GeoNames.

Semantic Wikis Overview

Thursday, May 7th, 2009

(by Eyal Oren)

Several posts on this blog have described some general “semantic web” application areas in which LarKC operates. In this posting, I’d like to give a brief overview of semantic wikis.

Semantic wikis are extensions to traditional wikis that support some form of semantic markup, and offer improved navigation, layout, or other features. The idea is that wikis are easy to use and have a low entry barrier: anyone can contribute. By extending the wiki language a bit, people hope to keep the relatively low entry-barrier but support light-weight knowledge aqcuisition. In exchange for their semantic markup, users get improved navigation features.

The most popular semantic wiki is probably Semantic MediaWiki. This project (run mostly by AIFB in Karlsruhe) have extended the MediaWiki software to allow “typed” wiki-links, which results in RDF data. The MediaWiki software is used to run many wikis, including Wikipedia, which is the reason that their original Semantic Wikipedia article got a lot of good press.As far as I’m aware, attempts to convince the Wikipedia community to run the SMW extension haven’t been fruitful yet.

There are also lots of other semantic wikis, either as extensions of existing wiki engines or built from scratch. They differ in the supported annotation language (in expressivity and usability) and in the extra features that they offer.  Typical features are:

  • faceted browsing of pages, based on their annotations: “other events also taking place in Houston, TX”
  • querying for pages/sections and database views: “this page lists an up-to-date chronological overview of events and their locations”
  • service mashups: plotting events (annotated with location information) on an embedded map
  • reasoning support over the given annotations and some background ontologies: “this event is held in Houston, and therefore in the US”

This overview is by no means complete, and the field is constantly changing. Other systems with interesting features include IkeWiki, OntoWiki  and Freebase (which is semantic, and could to some extent be considered a wiki).

A good forum to watch for new research developments is the yearly SemWiki workshop series, with the upcoming SemWiki2009 workshop at ESWC.

Colliding with your bootstraps

Wednesday, April 29th, 2009

(by Michael Witbrock)

Cycorp Europe’s sister organisation in the US has been participating in Bootstrapped Learning, a project to enable AI systems to be instructed using lessons that build on one another over time. Now the program is starting a competition to enable AI researchers around the world to drive this sort of learning forward.  This sort of research is one of the reasons we’re building the LarKC platform - to allow people to work on interesting, difficult problems in AI without having to build all the infrastructure themselves. It’d be great to see researchers using LarKC to build entries for the BL Cup, or for  TPTP. If you’d like to get started, perhaps you’re a  LarKC early adopter.

Qualitative Spatial and Temporal Reasoning

Thursday, March 26th, 2009

(Michael Witbrock, Cycorp) Many of our most basic thoughts involve temporal and spatial reasoning: putting something in the microwave; deciding to get out of bed  tomorrow morning; assembling examples in a list. One way of reasoning about these sorts of things is to place everything at an exact set of coordinates in time and space, but this doesn’t often lead to efficient ways of combining reasoning about time an space with reasoning about the things that are in time and space. Using qualitative spatial and temporal relations is helpful, and is generally what people do: we talk about things being next to one another, or after one another. We use deliberately vague expressions like “somewhere in Slovenia” or “somewhat later”.Giving computers the ability to reason with these qualitative spatiotemporal relations has been a research areas for almost as long as giving computers the ability to reason has. And a great deal of progress has been made.I recently gave a talk on integrating this reasoning with more general, broad covering reasoning systems (like Cyc), and encouraged participants at the Symposium to wrap their algorithms as LarKC plugins.  This is exactly the sort of community LarKC is intended to address:  researchers who would like to apply their results more broadly, and evaluate them in realistic, integrated settings, but who don’t have the resources (or, really, the motivation) to spend the time to build a platform. Judging from the enthusiastic response, I think we’ll see some spatial and temporal reasoners as early plugins.
