Semantics becoming reputable Computer Science?

Monday, June 30th, 2008

by Frank

In a recent msg to the Editorial Board of the Journal of Web Semantics,
Elsevier wrote:

The Journal of Web Semantics was recently added to the list of journals indexed by Thomson Reuters (formerly Thomson Scientific and before that ISI). This is an important accomplishment in itself, since Thomson is highly regarded and selective in what journals it includes. Thompson just published their 2007 Journal Citation Report and our impact factor is given as 3.41, which is the third highest out of the 92 titles in its category — Computer Science, Information systems.

There was some speculation in emails between the board members on how to read this. The good news definitely is that such a high impact could only be achieved by getting cited by people outside the immediate Semantic Web community. That would be an indication that Semantic Web research and results are being seen as relevant and interesting by the broader CS community.

Microsoft buys into semantics?

Saturday, June 28th, 2008

by Frank

At ISWC07, Barney Pell gave one of the keynote talks, and he spoke about his new company PowerSet, which uses a mixture of natural language analysis and symbolic reasoning to improve over “Google-style” search.

In a rather significant development, recent news has it that Microsoft just bought Powerset for some $100m. That’s significant because it means that Microsoft (who is known to want to expand/improve their search business) is apparently taking semantics seriously enough to spend a bit on it.


The piece at http://venturebeat.com/2008/06/26/microsoft-to-buy-semantic-search-engine-powerset-for-100m-plus/ has a rather good write-up on the different choices that Google and Microsoft are making on this (and of course, don’t forget that Yahoo! has announced to move into semantic search as well).

For what kind of ontologies should we build LarKC?

Thursday, June 12th, 2008

(By Frank)

The widely shared feeling is that much of the action on the (current) Semantic Web is in very lightweight ontologies (light on schema, heavy on instances). An interesting paper at the recent ARea2008 workshop took this feeling to the test: they grabbed a few thousand ontologies available from the Watson search engine, and tested them on their expressivity. The results are shown below:

This exactly confirms the popular belief: very many lightweight ontologies, but each of them having very few classes, and only very few OWL DL ontologies, but each of those having very many classes per ontology. (dark purple = number of ontologies, light purple = average number classes per ontology)

Another widely shared perception is that these statistics don’t carry over to “intranet” applications (e.g. data-integration inside pharmaceutical companies, knowledge management in large corporations, e-Science in communities like bio-medical, integration of cultural heritage collections, etc.) The findings above leave that perception untouched, itcould still be either true or completely false.

The paper (”Benchmarking OWL Reasoners”) is by  Jürgen Bock, Peter Haase, Qiu Ji and Raphael Volz, all from Karlsruhe, and can be found in the on-line workshop proceedings.

Where does the business value of Semantic Technologies come from?

Wednesday, June 11th, 2008

(by Frank)

Leo Sauermann from DFKI took a close look at all the seventeen case studies of applied Semantic Web in industry settings that were collected by the W3C Semantic Web Education and Outreach SWEO Interest Group, and tabulated them on where their value-contribution was coming from.

An interesting exercise.
Details at http://www.dfki.uni-kl.de/~sauermann/2008/04/benefits,
Executive summary from Leo below

Many benefits were classified by me into “profit” - hence people use semantic web to improve their business.

  • The first five benefits of the technology are crucial: data integration accross sources, expressive queries, taxonomy/multilingual, accessible for users, navigation and serendipity. If you start a project, you should try to get benefits in these areas.
  • Inferencing was not mentioned very often - although this can be of any reason (i.e. people forgot to mention it) it can also show that inference is important, but the web-based data integration features of RDF are also worth a lot. A little amount of semantics can go a long way.
  • The last two points, the web of data, and globally unique identifiers, are going to be much more important in the future and are a growing area.
  • Focussing on the last point, I want to stress that given the Linking Open Data project (also kind-of-initiated by SWEO), you now have a very good overview of the publicly available Semantic Web data. People want automated features and to reduce workload on users, you must reuse the data that is out there and I would give the prophecy that your business value will increase if you reuse this data.

Reasoning Requirements of the Urban Computing

Thursday, June 5th, 2008

In today’s WP6 teleconference, we had an interesting discussion on the reasoning requirements of the urban computing, in particular, on that for the traffic predication and computing. The two main issues are: whether or not the Urban computing should take:

(a) closed world assumption vs. open world assumption

(b) unique name assumption vs. non-unique name assumption.

A message from Kono:

We used F-Logic because it is very helpful within specific domain. In other words, the traffic control system needed CWA based reasoning such as which road are not connected to each other. On the other hand, we were not familiar with OWA yet. In the context of LarKC, I don’t have any idea yet. I guess LarKC should handle OWA and CWA both.

The closed world assumption is the presumption that what is not currently known to be true is false.
Negation as failure is related to the closed world assumption, as it amounts to believe false every predicate that cannot be proved to be true. It seems to be well reasonable that the traffic control system should take the closed world assumption. If we do not know there is a road which links two cities directly, it is well reasonable to assume that there is no any road which links the two cities directly. Namely our knowledge of the road map is assumed to be completed.

Because of the negation as failure, we are not needed to specify the negative knowledge in our knowledge bases and ontologies. For example, we do not specify the negative information such as “there is no road which links the city X and the city Y” but positive information such as “there is a high way A2 which links the city X and the city Y”. One implication results from the closed world assumption is that: our knowledge of the traffic map is never inconsistent, because the language has no any expression which claims both a positive information and its negation. A surprise or not?

We can introduce the negation as failure explicitly in the language, like the not-operator in Prolog. The semantics of the negation not(\phi) is defined as the unprovability of its sub formula (\phi). Introducing variants of negations has been well investigated in the community of logic programming.

For the unique name assumption, we have the similar reasons to take it. When we talk about Amsterdam, it is well reasonable to assume that we know that it refers to the capital city of the Netherlands. How about the case of “Den Haag” and “’s-Gravenhage”? On the other hand, consider the case of “Washington”. That depends on the context. However, it is well reasonable to assume that the traffic control system has the sufficient knowledge of the context. Shall we assume that a driver who uses the navigator in the Washington city would never wants to go to the state of Washington?

A further question is: What are their impacts on the researches of WP1 (LarKC Conceptual Framework) and WP4 (Reasoning and Deciding)?

References

[1] the Wikipedia page on closed world assumption.

[2] the Wikipedia page on open world assumption.

[3] the Wikipedia pages on unique name assumption.

[4] Ullrich Hustadt, Do we need the closed-world assumption in knowledge representation?, Reasoning about Structured Objects Knowledge Representation Meets Databases (KRDB ‘94).

[5] Nick Drummond and Rob Shearer, The Open World Assumption, or sometimes it is nice to know what we don’t know, 2006.
