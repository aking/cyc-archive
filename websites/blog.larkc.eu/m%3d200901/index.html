The OpenKnowledge project

Saturday, January 31st, 2009

(By Frank van Harmelen)

There’s been some discussion in LarKC on whether it’s basic architecture should be service-oriented (i.e. every plugin = a service), and whether a P2P model would be the right interaction model for such services. The results of the OpenKnowledge project were suggested as one possible platform for such a P2P interaction between “service-providing plugins”. The OpenKnowledge project just got a very well-written blog-entry on ReadWriteWeb.

The HEART project (Highly Extensible & Accumulative RDF Table)

Saturday, January 31st, 2009

(by Frank van Harmelen)

The HEART project aims to build “a computing resource [that is] unlimitedly expandable to process huge set of data” (according to http://www.lifeformon.net/2008/12/30/the-three-key-technological-aspects-of-web-30/).Sounds very familiar, doesn’t it? Very similar motivtion to our very own LarKC. Very interesting to see that the general motivation is independelty popping up in different places. Their approach seems rather different though, much more a monolithic approach, not including the plugin-architecture that is crucial to LarKC. It’s all very new, and they don’t have much to show yet, but it’ll be worth watching this Korean-based initiative.

New startup on “Linked Data”

Saturday, January 31st, 2009

Image via Wikipedia

(by Frank van Harmelen) 

Many of us will agree that the Linked Data Cloud is perhaps the best expression and realisation of the Semantic Web vision to date. Interesting to see that startups are emerging based on this development. To my knowledge, Structured Dynamics is the first of these. I would be surprised if not more would follow.And again this follows a familiar pattern: the Linked Data Cloud started in Europe (with Soren Auer’s work on DBPedia being a driving force), but the first startup is from the States…

Ask.com going semantic

Sunday, January 18th, 2009

Image via Wikipedia

Ask.com was never a search engine, but always more of an “answering engine”. Since October last year, they have been steadily introducing more semantic technology in their back-end: generating replies to user-questions directly from structured data. Yet another interesting business segment that’s trialling with semantic technologies. They are reporting on all this in a recent blog-entry on their site.

Image via Wikipedia

Yet another scientific community discovers semantic search

Sunday, January 18th, 2009

by Frank

After the life-sciences (see e.g. the HCLS collection, the Obofoundry, or own Linked Life Data, and countless others), after the astronomy, after geoscience,  and probably a few I forgot or didn’t notice, now the eco-scientists are seeing the potential of semantic search: besides the already existing SEEK initiative there is now a dedicated semantic search engine (NOESIS). This chimes in nicely with our earlier blogpost of the “Digging into Data” challenges for humanities researchers. Which scientific discipline is next?

“Digging into Data” Challenge

Sunday, January 18th, 2009

Image via Wikipedia

by Frank

Although LarKC does not have a use-case in the Cultural Heritage/Humanities/Social Science domain, the “Digging into Data” Challenge <http://www.diggingintodata.org/> is nevertheless very interesting and relevant:

The idea behind the Digging into Data Challenge is to answer the question “what do you do with a million books?” Or a million pages of newspaper? Or a million photographs of artwork? That is, how does the notion of scale affect humanities and social science research? Now that scholars have access to huge repositories of digitized data — far more than they could read in a lifetime — what does that mean for research?

…

The advent of what has been called “data-driven inquiry” or “cyberscholarship” has changed the nature of inquiry across many disciplines, including the sciences and humanities, revealing new opportunities for interdisciplinary collaboration on problems of common interest.

…

to foster interdisciplinary collaboration among scholars in the humanities, social sciences, computer sciences, information sciences, and other fields, around questions of text and data analysis;

It shows that LarKC-style infrastructures are becoming useful (and even necessary) in an ever larger set of academic disciplines.
