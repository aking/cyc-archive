Nokia Point & Find, a new way to connect with information and services on the go

Friday, April 3rd, 2009

(by Daniele Dell’Aglio)

The extraction of machine-processable information from unstructured data (as text, and pictures) is one of the most active research fields. It is studied by many actors: there are a lot of different approaches and results are becoming more and more accurate.

One of the hardest parts of this kind of operations concerns the extraction of information from multimedia contents.

A first successful example of extraction of information from an image is QR codes. the idea is similar to barcodes: some information is coded in a picture (a square with white and black points) and could be decoded in real time by QR-code readers. Actually they are used for mobile applications: using a smartphone with a camera is possible to take a picture of a QR-code (from a newspaper, a poster, etc.), the reader process it, extracts a coded URL and opens in the browser.

QR-codes are a particular case of extraction of information from an image: these pictures are structured following some rules and readers know them. The analysis of any other image to get data is another problem and itâ€™s very hard to cope with.

In the near future, an interesting application is Point and Find, under development by Nokia and released recently as beta. Users that install this application to their phone (actually in US/UK only) can take photos and let Point and Find process it to get related contents. For example a user can take a picture from a movie poster and the application finds the trailer and cinemas (near the user) where he can watch the movie.

A lot of new application could be realized starting from this kind of technologies and Semantic Web has the potentiality to be very useful in their development: for example a tourist could take a photo of a painting in a museum and an application could process the photo and find information about it using the large amount of (linked) data available on the Web (thanks to projects like Linking Open Data): the author, information about the painting and its content, etc.

From the screen to the real world: mobile games in the Internet of Things era

Friday, February 27th, 2009

(By Daniele Dell’Aglio)

In the 1983 the board game Scotland Yard was released: on a map a player runs away from the others that have to catch him.

Who knows if creators of this game imagined that (about) 25 years later someone realized a “live” version of their game? Fast Foot Challenge allows to play a real version of Scotland Yard: a player has to run in an area (with a radius of a kilometer) and others have to catch him, checking the position each other using a mobile GPS-enabled phone. This game, winner of the International Mobile Game Awards (in the category “Real time games”) and one of the finalists in the Independent Games Festival Mobile (in the category “Next Great Mobile Game”) is one of the first products of a new generation of videogames that uses the real world as playing field.
Another example of this kind of games is Parallel Kingdom, a role playing game in the real world: in the map of the area around the player (located using the GPS signal) the positions of (virtual) monsters, other players, items, etc. are visualized and the player has to move to reach them in order to fight the enemies, to catch treasures, to interact with others and so on.

A possible (and probable) evolution of this category of games is the interaction of the devices with the elements of the real world, in simple ways (processing a photo get by camera, an audio signal recorded with the microphone, etc.) or in complex ones, using the Web of Things and technologies developed in the area of pervasive computing and related topics. This will introduce a new set of problems to cope with and Semantic Web could offer practices and solutions to face some of them (data integration, data retrieval, etc.).
